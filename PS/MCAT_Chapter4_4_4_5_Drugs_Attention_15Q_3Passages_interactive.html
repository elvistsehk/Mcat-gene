🧠 MCAT Practice — Chapter 4.4–4.5: Psychoactive Drugs, Reward Pathway, Attention
Drugs: depressants (alcohol, benzodiazepines, barbiturates) ↑GABA; stimulants (amphetamines, cocaine, MDMA) ↑DA/NE/5‑HT; opioids (endorphin agonists) → respiratory suppression in overdose; hallucinogens often linked to serotonin; marijuana (THC inhibits GABA, indirectly ↑dopamine). Addiction: mesolimbic reward pathway (VTA–MFB–NAc; dopamine). Attention: selective attention as a filter, cocktail party phenomenon, dichotic listening & shadowing, divided attention, controlled vs automatic processing.
15 Standalone MCAT-style Questions (Q1–Q15)
1. Alcohol’s calming effect at moderate doses is most directly explained by which cellular action?
A. Opening voltage-gated Na+ channels to increase depolarization
B. Blocking Cl− channels to reduce inhibition
C. Increasing GABA receptor activity, promoting Cl− influx and membrane hyperpolarization
D. Decreasing dopamine release in the nucleus accumbens
✔️Correct!
❌Incorrect
Explanation (click to expand/collapse)
Alcohol increases GABA receptor activity. The GABA receptor is a chloride channel; increasing Cl− conductance leads to hyperpolarization, reducing neuronal firing and arousal (a depressant effect).
  • A would increase excitability, opposite a depressant effect.
  • B would reduce inhibition, not increase it.
  • D contradicts the chapter: alcohol tends to increase dopamine (mild euphoria), not decrease it.

✔️Correct!
❌Incorrect
Explanation (click to expand/collapse)
The chapter states disinhibition occurs because alcohol depresses the neural centers that prevent inappropriate behavior. When “the brakes” are reduced, risky behavior increases.
  • B is opposite direction (executive control would be enhanced, not lost).
  • C is not the core explanation; alcohol is primarily a CNS depressant.
  • D is not how alcohol is framed here; and increasing reuptake would reduce synaptic serotonin.

📘 Passage 1 — ED Triage: Similar “Sleepy” Patients, Different Mechanisms

An emergency department quality team reviews two admissions that occurred on the same weekend. Patient 1 arrived after a party and appeared severely drowsy, with slurred speech and slowed reaction time. Friends reported that the patient drank heavily and also “took something for anxiety” that was obtained from a roommate. Earlier in the evening, witnesses described the patient as unusually bold and impulsive, repeatedly making risky choices that seemed to ignore obvious consequences.

Patient 2 arrived an hour later. Paramedics reported that the patient was found unresponsive with drug paraphernalia nearby. In the ambulance, the patient had a dangerously low respiratory rate and shallow breathing. On arrival, the patient was difficult to arouse. The resident noted that the second case appeared immediately life‑threatening even though both patients “looked sleepy.”

During handoff, a junior clinician suggested that Patient 1’s alcohol and “anxiety medication” might converge on a shared inhibitory pathway in the brain, making the combined effect more dangerous than either substance alone. The attending asks which inference best fits the data and which physiologic parameter is most urgent to stabilize in each case.

How to reason (Passage 1):
  1. Cluster symptoms: alcohol + “anxiety medication” + progressive drowsiness suggests depressant synergy; dangerously low respiratory rate suggests opioid overdose.
  2. Use mechanism anchors: alcohol/benzos/barbiturates → ↑GABA → Cl− influx → hyperpolarization → CNS inhibition; opioids → endorphin agonism → respiratory suppression.
  3. Interpret behavior clues: “bold/impulsive, ignoring consequences” fits alcohol’s disinhibition and alcohol myopia.
  4. Decide urgency: in opioid overdose, stabilizing breathing/ventilation is immediately life‑saving.

B. Cocaine that decreased monoamine reuptake, compounding alcohol’s euphoria
C. Hallucinogen that primarily increased serotonin breakdown
D. Opioid antagonist that prevented respiratory suppression
✔️Correct!
❌Incorrect
Explanation (click to expand/collapse)
The passage points to a “shared inhibitory pathway.” Alcohol increases GABA receptor activity, and benzodiazepines also increase GABA activity, producing additive CNS depression when combined.
  • B cocaine is a stimulant; it would not explain progressive drowsiness/slowed reflexes.
  • C “serotonin breakdown” is not the chapter’s hallucinogen framing.
  • D opioid antagonists are not presented as typical “anxiety medication” here.

📘 Passage 2 — Reward Circuitry: Same Pathway, Different Reinforcers

A neuroscience laboratory compares how different reinforcers recruit a dopaminergic “reward” circuit while participants perform a monotonous reaction‑time task during brain imaging. In Condition A, participants earn small tokens after an unpredictable number of correct responses (sometimes after 2 correct trials, sometimes after 12). In Condition B, participants earn the same total number of tokens, but deliveries occur at predictable time points (for example, every 60 seconds).

In Condition C, participants see emotionally salient photos of a romantic partner during short breaks, but no tokens are delivered. In Condition D, participants complete the same task after inhaling a small, controlled dose of THC; the investigators predict that THC will reduce inhibitory tone on upstream neurons that normally restrain reward signaling, thereby increasing downstream “reward” activity. Across all conditions, the imaging team focuses on a pathway described as a midbrain dopamine source nucleus connected to a ventral striatal hub by a prominent fiber tract.

The researchers report strong activation across the source nucleus, tract, and hub in Conditions A, C, and D. Condition B produces weaker activation despite identical total token payout. They argue that uncertainty in reinforcement timing and personal meaning of cues can converge on the same circuitry, strengthening approach behavior and increasing the likelihood of repeating the behavior in the future.

How to reason (Passage 2):
  1. Name the pathway: midbrain source + ventral striatum hub + connecting tract = VTA → (MFB) → NAc.
  2. Use the transmitter label: it is called dopaminergic → dopamine is primary.
  3. Connect to the chapter claim: the pathway can be activated by substances and also by experiences like falling in love (and gambling).
  4. Apply THC mechanism: THC inhibits GABA and indirectly increases dopamine → reward pathway activation.

❌Incorrect

📘 Passage 3 — ICU Multitasking: When Practice Changes Attention

A medical student begins an ICU rotation and struggles to monitor vital signs while also listening to the resident’s instructions. Early in the week, the student repeatedly misses steps because they lock onto one stream of information and lose track of the other. The resident explains that this is common when tasks are new and cognitively demanding.

After several days, the student becomes fluent at a set of routine actions—priming IV tubing, recording scheduled measurements, and navigating the electronic chart. Now the student can perform these tasks while also answering questions, noticing alarms, and tracking a conversation. During a crowded night shift, the student is speaking with a nurse when someone across the room says the student’s name and then mentions “code blue.” The student immediately stops and turns toward the sound.

Later, the resident runs a teaching demo: the student wears headphones and hears different lists of numbers in each ear. The student is instructed to repeat aloud only the left-ear stream. Afterward, the student can report the attended stream well but remembers almost nothing from the unattended ear—except that the unattended stream contained the word “blue,” which felt unusually salient.

How to reason (Passage 3):
  1. New vs routine: new/complex tasks require controlled processing and reduce multitasking success; with practice, parts become automatic.
  2. Divided attention: once one task becomes automatic, attention can be split more effectively across tasks.
  3. Cocktail party clue: your name or urgent cue (“code blue”) breaking through implies some background processing.
  4. Dichotic listening: different inputs per ear; repeating attended ear = shadowing; limited recall from unattended ear reflects filtering.

❌Incorrect
Explanation (click to expand/collapse)

  • C is drug physiology, not skill learning.
