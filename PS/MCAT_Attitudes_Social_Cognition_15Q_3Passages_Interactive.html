Standalone Questions (Q1–Q15)
1. A student reads two editorials about a new campus policy and spends an hour comparing the authors’ evidence, checking counterarguments, and evaluating assumptions before deciding. This pattern best illustrates which process in the elaboration likelihood model?
A. Peripheral route processing (low elaboration)
B. Attitude change driven primarily by slogans and source attractiveness
C. Central route processing (high elaboration)
D. Observational learning of attitudes through modeling
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: C.

Why: Central route processing involves deep scrutiny of message content (evidence, logic, assumptions). High elaboration predicts more stable attitude change when the argument is strong.

Other choices:

  • A/B: Peripheral processing relies on superficial cues (appearance, slogans, “credible source”) rather than argument quality.
  • D: Observational learning is Bandura’s social cognitive mechanism (learning by watching), not the ELM’s “route” distinction.
2. A teenager who failed several math tests says, “Math is pointless anyway,” and begins expressing a strong dislike for math to feel less embarrassed. In functional attitude theory, this attitude most directly serves which function?
A. Ego defense
B. Knowledge
C. Adaptation
D. Ego expression
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A.

Why: Ego-defensive attitudes protect self-esteem and help justify an outcome that threatens the self (“I dislike it anyway,” so failure hurts less).

Other choices:

  • B: Knowledge function organizes information and helps predict behavior, not primarily self-protection.
  • C: Adaptation is about social acceptance (“I say what’s popular to fit in”).
  • D: Ego expression communicates identity/values (e.g., “I wear this to show who I am”).
3. A participant says, “I hate those people who go to networking events,” but repeatedly attends those events and later explains, “Networking is essential for career success.” Which option best matches the cognitive component of the attitude?
A. “I hate those people who go to networking events.”
B. Attending networking events repeatedly
C. Feeling anxious before the events
D. “Networking is essential for career success.”
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: D.

Why: The cognitive component is the belief/justification (thoughts) that often supports feelings and behavior. “Networking is essential…” is a belief about the situation.

Other choices:

  • A/C: These are affective (emotional) reactions—hate or anxiety.
  • B: This is behavioral—what the person does.
4. A company pairs a neutral chime with a pleasant, uplifting jingle in repeated ads. Weeks later, employees report a more positive feeling toward the chime alone. Which learning process best explains the attitude change?
A. Observational learning
B. Classical conditioning
C. Central route processing
D. Knowledge function of attitudes
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: B.

Why: A neutral stimulus (chime) gains positive valence by repeated pairing with a positive stimulus (jingle). That is the core pattern of classical conditioning.

Other choices:

  • A: Observational learning requires watching a model.
  • C: Central route is deep evaluation of arguments, not stimulus pairing.
  • D: Knowledge function is about organizing/predicting, not conditioning.
5. Bandura’s triadic reciprocal causation (reciprocal determinism) emphasizes that behavior is shaped by interactions among:
A. Behavior, personal factors, and environment
B. Id, ego, and superego
C. Knowledge, ego expression, adaptation, and ego defense
D. Central route, peripheral route, and cognitive dissonance
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A.

Why: Bandura’s model links behavior, personal factors (cognitions/attitudes), and the environment in a two-way feedback system—each can influence the others.

Other choices:

  • B: Freud’s structural model.
  • C: Four functions in functional attitude theory.
  • D: Central/peripheral are ELM routes; “cognitive dissonance” is a separate theory not required here.

✅Correct

Why: The knowledge function provides organization and helps people predict behavior (their own and others’).

Other choices:

  • A: Ego defense protects self-esteem (e.g., “I don’t care anyway”).
  • B: Adaptation helps gain acceptance by endorsing socially approved attitudes.
  • D: Ego expression communicates identity/values.
7. A campaign poster includes almost no data, but features a beloved celebrity smiling next to a short slogan: “Choose Health. Choose Us.” Which processing route is the ad primarily targeting?

Passages (3 passages × 3 questions)
Passage 1: Two Persuasion Campaigns on the Same Topic

A university health center wants to increase uptake of a new influenza vaccine clinic. They pilot two outreach campaigns in different dorms. In Dorm A, students receive a one-page brief that summarizes a randomized trial, explains absolute risk reduction, and includes a short section anticipating common objections (e.g., “I’m healthy, so I don’t need it”). Students are encouraged to take a minute to compare the brief with what they already believe and to discuss it with a peer.

In Dorm B, the center places large posters showing a popular athlete holding a bandage with the slogan “Real Champions Protect the Team.” A QR code links to a 10-second video of the athlete saying the clinic is “the smart move,” but the video includes no statistics. Survey data show that students in Dorm A report reading and discussing the brief more often, whereas students in Dorm B mostly recall the athlete and slogan. Four weeks later, the athlete is involved in an unrelated scandal and the posters are removed.

How to reason:

  1. First, label each campaign as central-cue (argument quality, evidence) or peripheral-cue (likability, slogans, source appeal).
  2. Then predict durability: central route changes are typically more stable; peripheral changes depend on cues that can disappear or backfire.
  3. When the passage adds a new fact (e.g., scandal, removal of posters), ask: which route should be most sensitive to that fact?
  4. Pick answers that use the passage’s evidence (who read/discussed vs who recalled slogans) to justify a prediction, not a quote.
16. Based on the passage, which dorm is most likely to show more persistent pro-vaccination attitudes even after the posters are removed and the athlete’s image becomes less credible?
A. Dorm A, because the campaign relies more on central route processing
B. Dorm B, because slogans are easier to remember and therefore more stable
C. Dorm B, because the athlete’s scandal increases elaboration on the message content
D. Dorm A, because central processing depends mainly on spokesperson attractiveness
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A.

Why: Dorm A’s brief prompts analysis of evidence and counterarguments—central route cues. Central-route attitude change is typically more durable and less dependent on a single source cue.

Other choices:

  • B: Memorability is not the same as stability; peripheral-route attitudes can be fragile when cues change.
  • C: The scandal undermines the source cue; it does not supply stronger arguments.
  • D: Spokesperson attractiveness is a peripheral cue, not a driver of central processing.

D. Anticipating objections and offering rebuttals
✅Correct

Why: Peripheral route persuasion relies on cues like likability, credibility heuristics, and slogans rather than argument quality. The athlete endorsement + slogan is a textbook peripheral cue.

Other choices:

  • A/D: These invite elaboration and are central-route supportive.
  • B: Data and evidence are central-route content cues.
18. After the scandal, which outcome would most strongly suggest that Dorm B students’ attitudes were changed primarily through peripheral route processing?

Passage 2: The New Fitness Group at a Gym

A gym tracks member attendance and basic fitness measures (resting heart rate, estimated VO2 max) each month. Over five years, one subgroup consistently shows low attendance and low fitness scores, despite seasonal surges of motivation in January and late spring. When interviewed, many members in this subgroup describe frequent gym-goers as “obsessive” and “show-offs.” Several also admit that they feel embarrassed working out in front of others and worry they will be judged.

This year, the gym launches a “3–4 Days Together” initiative: members can join small teams that schedule workouts as a group and send supportive messages if someone misses a session. Team members share beginner-friendly workouts and celebrate small improvements publicly on a group chat. After two months, attendance improves for some individuals in the low-attendance subgroup, but not for all. Among those who participate, a few report that they “used to dislike gym people,” but now they find the group “less intimidating.”

How to reason:

  1. Look for language that signals why an attitude exists: protecting self-esteem → ego defense; identity signaling → ego expression; fitting in → adaptation; organizing/predicting → knowledge.
  2. When you see a behavior pattern that is consistent across years, consider whether the passage hints at a dispositional attribution (about “who they are”) rather than a one-time situational barrier.
  3. For Bandura, map evidence into the triangle: environment (new peer group), behavior (attendance), personal factors (beliefs, embarrassment, attitudes). Then ask what changes what.
  4. Prefer answer choices that require you to combine multiple clues (dislike of gym-goers + embarrassment + group change) instead of restating one sentence.

A. The pattern must be entirely due to temporary situational barriers each year
B. The pattern is best explained by peripheral route persuasion from gym advertisements
C. The pattern implies the members have already achieved self-actualization needs
D. The pattern is consistent with a dispositional attribution (stable personal factors) contributing to avoidance
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: D.

Why: A behavior that is consistent over time is often attributed to stable personal/dispositional factors (e.g., beliefs like “I’ll be judged,” anxiety, low self-efficacy), rather than a one-off situational barrier.

Other choices:

  • A: Situational barriers can matter, but the passage emphasizes a stable multi-year pattern, which points beyond “temporary” explanations.
  • B: ELM routes describe persuasion processing, not long-term attendance consistency.
  • C: Self-actualization is unrelated to gym avoidance here.

21. Which prediction best applies Bandura’s triadic reciprocal causation to members who join the new “3–4 Days Together” teams?
A. Only the environment will change; personal factors will remain completely fixed
B. Changing the environment (peer group) can shift behavior (attendance), which may gradually change personal factors (attitudes/self-beliefs)

D. Attitudes will change only if the group provides a celebrity endorsement

Passage 3: A School Program to Reduce Vaping

A high school introduces a program aimed at reducing vaping. Students who submit to weekly nicotine tests and remain negative earn points that can be exchanged for privileges (front-row seating at events, extra lab time in popular electives). The school also recruits older students who previously vaped to speak about how quitting improved their sports performance and relationships. Teachers notice that some students start repeating the speakers’ phrases (“I don’t want a product controlling me”) during peer conversations.

In follow-up interviews, one student says, “I used to think vaping was harmless, but now I’m convinced it increases my risk of poor performance.” Another says, “I still think it’s risky, but I just like how it looks—everyone thinks it’s cool.” A third student avoids vaping mainly because “my friend group would roast me if I got caught,” even though the student admits they miss the feeling of nicotine.

How to reason:

  1. Spot the learning mechanisms: points → operant conditioning (reinforcement); role models → observational learning.
  2. For attitude components (ABC), translate statements: beliefs = cognitive; feelings = affective; actions/avoidance = behavioral.
  3. For functional attitude theory, ask what the attitude is doing: fit in (adaptation), protect self-esteem (ego defense), signal identity (ego expression), or organize/predict (knowledge).
  4. Choose answers that integrate at least two clues (what the program changes + what the student says) to classify the mechanism.
22. Which program element most directly targets the behavioral component of attitudes by changing what students do through consequences?
A. Having former vapers describe how quitting improved relationships
B. Students repeating motivational phrases from the speakers
C. Awarding points for negative tests that can be exchanged for privileges
D. Students believing vaping is risky for performance
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: C.

Why: The point system is a direct operant conditioning strategy (reinforcement). It primarily changes behavior by attaching consequences to actions.

Other choices:

  • A: This targets beliefs/values via narrative persuasion and modeling, not direct behavioral consequences.
  • B: Repeating phrases suggests observational learning; it’s not the reinforcement mechanism itself.
  • D: That is the cognitive component (belief), not the behavioral lever.
23. Which additional observation would provide the strongest evidence that vaping reduction reflects reciprocal determinism rather than environment-only control?
A. Students begin choosing different social settings (e.g., avoiding vape-friendly hangouts), which reduces cravings and further decreases vaping
B. Vaping drops only in weeks when privileges are doubled, and returns immediately when points are removed
C. Vaping drops only when teachers strictly monitor bathrooms
D. Vaping drops equally across all students regardless of their beliefs or peer groups
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A.

Why: Reciprocal determinism requires two-way influence: the person selects/changes the environment, which changes internal states (personal factors) and behavior, creating a feedback loop.

Other choices:

  • B/C: These emphasize environment → behavior only (reinforcement or monitoring), not mutual influence.
  • D: If beliefs/peer groups don’t matter, you lose the “personal factors” part of the triad.
