📘 Passage 1 — Habituation, Dishabituation, and Predictability

Investigators study how repeated, low-stakes sensory input changes behavior. Healthy adults sit in a sound-attenuated room wearing headphones. Every 10 seconds, a 200 ms, 70 dB tone is presented. After each tone, participants press a key as quickly as possible when a small dot appears on a monitor (simple reaction-time task). Simultaneously, an eyelid EMG records startle magnitude. Participants also rate how “noticeable” the tone felt (1–7).

Across the first 30 tones, startle magnitude falls sharply and reaction time improves slightly. Participants report the tone as less noticeable over time. On trial 31, the tone is replaced with a single 95 dB tone of a different pitch. Immediately afterward, the original 70 dB tone returns for 10 more trials. On the first 2–3 post-loud trials, startle to the 70 dB tone increases compared with the end of the first block and reaction time briefly slows, then both measures return toward the earlier habituated levels.

In a second condition, a new group receives the same 70 dB tones, but the interval is unpredictable (randomly 4–18 seconds). In this condition, startle decreases more slowly and “noticeability” ratings remain higher. The researchers conclude that predictability accelerates tuning out and that a salient intervening stimulus can transiently restore responsiveness.

How to reason (Passage 1):
  1. Name the pattern: decreasing response to repeated identical stimulus → habituation (not fatigue by default).
  2. Spot the interrupt: a new, salient stimulus that restores response to the old one → dishabituation (resensitization).
  3. Use predictability: fixed timing makes the stimulus easier to ignore; unpredictability keeps attention engaged, slowing habituation.
  4. Link to performance: brief reaction-time slowing after the loud tone suggests arousal/orienting temporarily competes with the task.
  5. Eliminate distractors: conditioning requires pairings or consequences; here the key manipulation is repetition and novelty.

📘 Passage 2 — Classical vs Operant Conditioning and Reinforcement Schedules

A behavioral neuroscience lab trains rats in two phases. In Phase 1 (Pavlovian), a 2 kHz tone plays for 5 seconds and then a mild foot shock is delivered. The shock reliably elicits flinching and freezing. After several pairings, rats begin freezing during the tone before the shock occurs, indicating that the tone predicts an aversive event.

In Phase 2 (operant), the same rats are placed in a chamber with a lever and a food dispenser. Pressing the lever can deliver a pellet. Group FR is reinforced with one pellet for every 10 presses. Group VR is reinforced on average every 10 presses but unpredictably. Group FI is reinforced for the first press after each 30-second interval.

Over training days, Group VR presses fastest and continues pressing longest when pellets are later withheld (extinction). During extinction, the experimenter occasionally plays the tone (no shock is delivered). Immediately after tone presentations, some rats freeze and lever pressing temporarily decreases, then gradually resumes.

How to reason (Passage 2):
  1. Identify learning type: tone → shock = classical conditioning; lever press → pellet = operant conditioning.
  2. Classify schedules: ratio = number of responses; interval = time. Fixed = predictable; variable = unpredictable.
  3. Predict extinction: variable-ratio is typically most resistant to extinction.
  4. Explain suppression: a conditioned fear response (freezing) can suppress operant responding without “erasing” the operant memory.
  5. Rule out memory-list distractors: proactive/retroactive interference are about competing information sets, not pellets and levers.
6. After Phase 1 training, the tone is best classified as a(n):
A. Unconditioned stimulus
B. Conditioned stimulus
C. Unconditioned response
D. Negative reinforcement
✔️Correct!
❌Incorrect
Explanation (click to expand/collapse)
The tone starts neutral but, after pairing with shock, it predicts the aversive event and elicits freezing. That makes it a conditioned stimulus.
  • A is the shock (US), which naturally elicits fear responses.
  • C is a response; freezing becomes the conditioned response (CR).
  • D is an operant concept (removing something unpleasant to increase behavior), not a stimulus label.

  • B fixed-ratio can be high but usually extinguishes more readily than variable-ratio.
  • D variable-interval is time-based; Group VR is response-count based.

📘 Passage 3 — Encoding Depth, Context, and Retrieval Strategy

An MCAT course tests two encoding strategies for learning psychology terms. On Day 1, students learn 24 terms (Set A). The Maintenance group repeats each definition aloud and copies it three times. The Elaborative group writes a one-sentence clinical mini-case for each term and adds a personal connection (e.g., “how this could matter for my future patient interactions”).

On Day 2, all students learn a second set of 24 highly similar terms (Set B) using only repetition. On Day 3, students take a mixed exam containing: (i) free-response items requiring recall of Set A definitions, and (ii) multiple-choice items requiring recognition of Set B. Half the students test in the same classroom used on Day 1; half test in a different room with different lighting, odor, and background noise.

Results: Elaborative learners outperform Maintenance learners on Set A recall, even in the different room. Across groups, Set B recognition exceeds Set A recall. A frequent error is answering Set A questions with Set B definitions, especially when both sets share a distinctive keyword (e.g., “cue,” “reinforcement,” or “context”).

How to reason (Passage 3):
  1. Map strategies to terms: repetition/copying → maintenance rehearsal; cases + personal links → elaborative rehearsal and semantic encoding (self-reference).
  2. Use task format: free-response = recall (harder); multiple choice = recognition (easier due to cues).
  3. Use context manipulation: same environment can help via context effect; deep encoding reduces reliance on that cue.
  4. Diagnose confusion errors: Set B intruding when recalling Set A → retroactive interference.
  5. Leverage “keyword overlap”: shared cues activate multiple nodes in a semantic network, increasing competition.

✔️Correct!
❌Incorrect
Explanation (click to expand/collapse)

  • A is wrong: sensory memory lasts under a second and is unrelated to Day 3 recall.
  • B is too absolute; deep encoding can reduce reliance on weak cues but does not eliminate all interference.
  • D is incorrect: studying definitions targets explicit (declarative) memory, not procedural skills.

📘 Passage 4 — Reconstruction, Misinformation, and Source Monitoring

In a study of memory reconstruction, participants watch a 45-second video of a minor traffic incident. The video clearly shows a car stopping at a yield sign before turning. After a 10-minute filler task, participants read a written “summary” of the event. For half of participants, the summary is accurate. For the other half, the summary subtly changes one detail: it states the car stopped at a stop sign.

Participants are then interviewed. Some are asked neutrally, “How fast were the cars moving when they collided?” Others are asked with more intense wording, “How fast were the cars moving when they crashed?” Finally, participants complete two tests: (1) a forced-choice recognition test (yield vs stop), and (2) a source monitoring task asking whether each detail came from the video or the written summary.

Results: participants exposed to the misleading summary are more likely to choose “stop sign” on recognition. The “crashed” wording also increases estimated speed and increases confidence, even when estimates are inaccurate. Many participants correctly recall the sign’s appearance but misattribute the source of their memory, claiming they “saw” the stop sign in the video.

How to reason (Passage 4):
  1. Identify outside information: the written summary injects a new detail → test for misinformation effect.
  2. Separate memory content vs source: “I remember the detail but not where it came from” → source-monitoring error.
  3. Use wording manipulation: leading language changes recall judgments → reconstruction at retrieval, not pure storage.
  4. Watch for confidence traps: higher confidence does not guarantee accuracy.
  5. Choose the mechanism with direct evidence: misled group shifts toward the false detail → misinformation effect is strongly supported.

✔️Correct!
❌Incorrect
Explanation (click to expand/collapse)
The misinformation effect occurs when outside information introduced after an event becomes incorporated into memory, reducing accuracy. The misleading summary injects “stop sign,” and later responses shift toward it.
  • A concerns distributed practice across time; not relevant.
  • B is old information interfering with new; here a new narrative changes recall of the older event.
  • C sensory memory is far too brief to matter after 10 minutes.

📘 Passage 5 — Neuroplasticity and Long-Term Potentiation Across Age

To study the neurobiology of learning, researchers record synaptic responses in hippocampal slices. In young adult rats, a brief high-frequency “tetanic” stimulation of an input pathway is delivered. After tetanus, the same low-frequency test pulses evoke larger postsynaptic potentials for several hours.

In a second condition, slices are pretreated with Drug X, which decreases presynaptic neurotransmitter release probability. After tetanus, potentiation is smaller and returns toward baseline within 20 minutes. In a third condition, slices are exposed to Compound Y, which increases the availability of postsynaptic receptor sites involved in excitatory transmission. After tetanus, potentiation is larger and longer-lasting than in untreated slices.

Finally, the researchers repeat the untreated protocol in older rats. The initial potentiation appears, but it is smaller and decays faster than in young adults. The authors argue that this physiological finding helps explain why forming durable new memories can become more difficult with aging, even when basic sensation and motor function remain intact.

How to reason (Passage 5):
  1. Identify the signature: lasting increased synaptic response after tetanus = long-term potentiation (LTP).
  2. Match drugs to mechanism: lower presynaptic release → weaker LTP; more postsynaptic receptors → stronger LTP.
  3. Bridge to cognition: LTP supports consolidation by strengthening connectivity; reduced LTP implies reduced neuroplasticity.
  4. Use the aging comparison: smaller/shorter LTP aligns with age-related decline in plasticity.
  5. Ignore cognitive-distractor terms: interference/misinformation describe errors in recall, not synaptic physiology.

✔️Correct!
❌Incorrect
Explanation (click to expand/collapse)
LTP is a lasting strengthening of synaptic transmission after strong stimulation and is linked to converting short-term changes into long-term memory traces.
  • B is a decrease in responding with repetition (opposite direction).
  • C is a brief auditory sensory store, not synaptic potentiation.
  • D is a cognitive distortion phenomenon, not slice physiology.
