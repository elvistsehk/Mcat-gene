MCAT Behavioral Sciences — Self-Concept, Identity & Development (Ch. 6.1–6.2)
Standalone Questions (Q1–Q15)
1. A student describes herself as “curious and competitive,” and also says she is “a member of the Muslim community and a first‑generation college student.” According to social scientists, which part most directly reflects identity (as distinct from self‑concept)?
A. Her group-based components (e.g., religion, first‑generation status)
B. Her personality traits like curiosity and competitiveness
C. Her overall answer to “Who am I?” across time
D. Her appraisal of who she used to be and will become
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A.

Why: Identity refers to the group-related components of the self-concept (roles/memberships) that guide behavior in a given context.

Other choices:

  • B: Traits can be part of self-schema/self-concept, but identity is specifically tied to group memberships and roles.
  • C: That broad, unified answer corresponds to self-concept (one overarching self-concept).
  • D: Past/future self-appraisals are included in self-concept, not the definition of identity.
2. A person scores high on both masculinity and femininity scales. Which term best describes this gender identity profile?
A. Undifferentiated
B. Gender dysphoria
C. Androgynous
D. Binary-typical
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: C.

Why: Androgyny is defined as simultaneously scoring very high on both masculinity and femininity dimensions (not opposite ends of one continuum).

Other choices:

  • A: Undifferentiated refers to low scores on both masculinity and femininity scales.
  • B: Gender dysphoria is a diagnosis involving clinically significant distress related to gender identity, not a scale profile.
  • D: “Binary-typical” isn’t the concept tested here; the key distinction is two dimensions vs one continuum.
3. Which scenario best illustrates <b>national</b> identity (rather than ethnic identity)?
A. Feeling connected to ancestors through a shared language and cultural heritage
B. Preferring an in-group doll in a study of racial self-concept
C. Identifying strongly with a racial group that shares common ancestry
D. Feeling pride when seeing a country’s flag and recalling shared history and national symbols
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: D.

Why: National identity is grounded in political borders and shared history/media/cuisine/symbols (e.g., flags), and it need not match ethnicity or legal citizenship.

Other choices:

  • A: Shared ancestry, language, and heritage are classic features of ethnic identity.
  • B: That describes racial attitudes/self-concept in a historical methodology, not national identity.
  • C: Again, common ancestry and racial/ethnic membership indicate ethnic identity.
4. A researcher finds that college students mention gender less when describing themselves in same-gender groups than in mixed-gender groups. Which concept best explains this pattern?
A. Self-discrepancy theory
B. Hierarchy of salience (situationally activated identities)
C. Learned helplessness
D. Drive reduction theory
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: B.

Why: Identities are organized in a salience hierarchy; the social context makes certain identities (like gender in mixed-gender groups) more prominent and likely to be enacted.

Other choices:

  • A: Self-discrepancy concerns gaps between actual/ideal/ought selves causing negative affect.
  • C: Learned helplessness involves perceived lack of control after repeated uncontrollable outcomes.
  • D: Drive reduction is a motivational theory, not about context-dependent identity activation.
5. A student says, “I am not the kind of person my parents think I should be,” and feels chronic guilt and anxiety. Which framework most directly accounts for these emotions?
A. Internal locus of control
B. Reference group effects
C. Androgyny vs undifferentiated gender identity
D. Self-discrepancy theory (actual vs ideal vs ought selves)
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: D.

Why: Self-discrepancy theory proposes three selves: actual, ideal, and ought. Perceived gaps—especially between actual and ought—are linked to negative feelings like guilt/anxiety.

Other choices:

  • A: Locus of control is about attributing outcomes to internal vs external causes, not three-self comparisons.
  • B: Reference groups shift self-evaluation via comparison targets, but don’t specify actual/ideal/ought gaps.
  • C: Gender identity dimensions don’t explain guilt from mismatched expectations.
6. Which pairing is most accurate?
A. Self-esteem = how you feel about yourself; self-efficacy = belief in your ability to succeed at a task
B. Self-esteem = belief in your ability to succeed; self-efficacy = how you feel about yourself
C. Self-esteem = internal locus of control; self-efficacy = external locus of control
D. Self-esteem = ideal self; self-efficacy = ought self
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A.

Why: Self-esteem is self-worth (feelings about the self), whereas self-efficacy is confidence in one’s ability to perform successfully (often domain-specific).

Other choices:

  • B: This reverses the definitions.
  • C: Locus of control is a separate construct about what causes outcomes.
  • D: Ideal/ought selves are components in self-discrepancy theory, not definitions of self-esteem/effectiveness.
7. After repeated failures on practice tests that the student believes are “unavoidable no matter what I do,” the student stops studying and becomes depressed. Which concept best describes this pattern?
A. Approach–approach conflict
B. Learned helplessness
C. Conformity (Kohlberg stage 3)
D. Fixation (Freud)
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: B.

Why: Learned helplessness is a perceived lack of control over outcomes after repeated uncontrollable events, strongly linked to depressive symptoms and reduced motivation.

Other choices:

  • A: Approach–approach conflict is choosing between two desirable options.
  • C: Kohlberg stage 3 concerns moral reasoning based on approval/nice-person orientation.
  • D: Fixation is Freud’s concept of being stuck in a psychosexual stage due to over/under-indulgence.
8. A runner loses a race and says, “The track was wet and my shoes slipped—anyone would have lost.” This explanation reflects:
A. Internal locus of control
B. High self-efficacy
C. External locus of control
D. Self-discrepancy (actual vs ideal)
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: C.

Why: Attributing outcomes primarily to outside factors (luck, environment, other people) indicates an external locus of control.

Other choices:

  • A: Internal locus would emphasize personal controllable factors (e.g., training effort).
  • B: Self-efficacy is belief in ability, not attribution style.
  • D: Self-discrepancy involves comparing actual/ideal/ought selves, not causal attributions.
9. A teenager adjusts their clothing and speech after noticing classmates’ reactions, because they imagine how others see them. Which concept is most directly involved?
A. Looking-glass self
B. Drive reduction theory
C. Universal human ethics (Kohlberg stage 6)
D. Undifferentiated gender identity
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A.

Why: The looking-glass self is the self-concept that forms through imagining how others perceive and judge us, then reacting (modifying, accentuating, downplaying) accordingly.

Other choices:

  • B: Drive reduction concerns motivation to reduce internal tension (e.g., hunger).
  • C: Kohlberg stage 6 is abstract moral reasoning; this scenario is social self-perception.
  • D: Undifferentiated is low masculinity and low femininity on gender scales.

Other choices:

11. Which ability is most central to <b>theory of mind</b>?

12. A child is most likely to imitate a behavior after watching which model, according to observational learning principles discussed in this chapter?

Passage 1: Identity Salience on Clinical Rotation

Mina starts her first hospital rotation. During morning rounds with residents she introduces herself as “a future physician” and speaks formally, even though she is usually playful with friends. Later at lunch, she sits with a mixed‑gender group of students; she suddenly becomes self‑conscious about whether she sounds “too bossy” and notices she mentions gender when describing herself. That evening, her family calls to discuss a cultural holiday, and Mina feels especially connected to her ethnic community. After receiving feedback from an attending that she seemed “detached,” Mina replays the comment repeatedly and changes her behavior the next day by asking more empathic questions. Over the next week, Mina feels less confident when comparing her performance to a group of top‑ranked classmates, despite being rated above average by supervisors.

How to reason:

  1. List the possible identities in the scene (student, future physician, gender identity, ethnic identity, family member).
  2. Ask what the context makes most salient (mixed‑gender group → gender; family/cultural discussion → ethnic identity; hospital rounds → professional identity).
  3. Use the rule: the more salient the identity, the more a person conforms to role expectations of that identity.
  4. For self‑evaluation questions, decide whether the passage points to looking‑glass self (reacting to others’ judgments) or reference group (who she compares to).
  5. Choose the option that requires an inference from behavior, not a direct quote.
16. Which statement best predicts Mina’s behavior <i>during rounds</i> based on the concept of identity salience?
A. She will behave the same way with residents as with close friends because self-concept is singular
B. She will prioritize ethnic identity because nationality always overrides other identities
C. She will avoid any role expectations because multiple identities create paralysis
D. She will conform to professional role expectations (formal speech, task focus) because the clinical context makes that identity most salient
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: D.

Why: The hospital-rounds context makes the professional/physician-in-training identity most salient, increasing conformity to its role expectations (formal, task-oriented behavior).

Other choices:

  • A: Self-concept is one overarching construct, but people can enact different identities across contexts.
  • B: Ethnic identity can become salient in some contexts, but the passage cues professional salience during rounds.
  • C: Multiple identities do not imply paralysis; the model predicts context-dependent enactment.

Passage 2: Early Development, Modeling, and Scaffolding

Noah is 2 years old and is learning to use the toilet. When accidents happen, his caregiver scolds him harshly and prevents him from trying again. Noah begins to avoid new tasks and often says “I can’t.” At preschool, he watches an older same‑gender sibling receive praise for calmly sharing toys, and Noah starts copying that sharing behavior. Later, Noah tries to ride a bicycle; alone he can balance briefly but cannot pedal and steer at the same time. With an adult holding the seat and giving short prompts, he succeeds and gradually needs less help.

How to reason:

  1. For Erikson questions, match the child’s age to the stage (1–3 years → autonomy vs shame and doubt).
  2. Look for caregiver style: harsh control/criticism → shame/doubt and a more external sense of control.
  3. For observational learning, identify the model (similarity, reinforcement/praise) and the imitated behavior.
  4. For Vygotsky, ask: can the child almost do it alone but needs guidance? If yes → ZPD + more knowledgeable other.
  5. Choose answers that integrate multiple cues (age + behavior + social context).

Correct Answer: B.

Why: At ages 1–3, Erikson’s key conflict is autonomy vs shame and doubt. Excessive control/criticism tends to produce shame/doubt and reduced initiative to act independently.

Other choices:

  • A: Trust vs mistrust is infancy (0–1).
  • C: Industry vs inferiority is 6–12 and centers on competence in school-age tasks.

Passage 3: Reasoning Through a Moral Dilemma

A community clinic has a policy that an expensive medication can only be dispensed after a long approval process. A volunteer discovers that a patient will likely die if treatment is delayed. The volunteer considers taking a dose without permission. In interviews, Participant X argues: “Rules exist to keep the clinic functioning; if everyone broke policy when they felt like it, the system would collapse.” Participant Y argues: “Policies are important, but they’re agreements meant to protect people’s rights—when a rule threatens someone’s life, we should revise it.” A researcher notes that the interview sample came only from one country and mostly included men.

How to reason:

  1. Ignore whether the act is “right” or “wrong.” Kohlberg focuses on why the person justifies it.
  2. Map statements to stages: law/order emphasizes social order; social contract emphasizes rights and revisable rules; universal ethics emphasizes abstract principles.
  3. Use the passage wording as evidence: “system would collapse” → law/order; “agreements meant to protect rights” → social contract.
  4. For critique questions, look for sampling/cultural bias (individualistic vs collectivist) and gender-biased sampling.
  5. Choose the answer that matches the reasoning structure, not the action.
