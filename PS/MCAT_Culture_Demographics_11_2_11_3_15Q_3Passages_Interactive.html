Standalone Questions (Q1–Q15)
1. A museum curator studies how a nation’s flag is used at parades, in classrooms, and on government buildings to reinforce shared identity and loyalty. In this analysis, the flag is best treated as:
A. a norm that defines acceptable behavior in public spaces
B. a ritual that must follow a prescribed order of events
C. an artifact of material culture that embodies symbolic culture
D. evidence of cultural relativism applied to national identity
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: C.

Why: A flag is a tangible object (material culture) that carries shared meaning (symbolic culture such as patriotism and belonging). Material culture often serves as the physical embodiment of nonmaterial ideas.

Other choices:

  • A: Norms are rules/expectations for behavior; the flag itself is an object, not a rule.
  • B: Rituals are formalized ceremonies (e.g., a pledge ceremony); the flag can be used in rituals but is not itself the ritual.
  • D: Cultural relativism is a method for evaluating cultures on their own terms; it isn’t a category for classifying a cultural object.
2. A student moves to a new country and learns that refusing a host’s second serving of food is considered rude. The student’s initial confusion is best explained by differences in:
A. norms that define the boundaries of acceptable behavior
B. values, because values are formalized ceremonies
C. beliefs, because beliefs are the physical objects of a culture
D. rituals, because rituals are always informal and spontaneous
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A.

Why: The “you should accept a second serving” rule is a norm—an informal expectation for appropriate behavior that provides social control.

Other choices:

  • B: Values are what is deemed important (e.g., hospitality), but the specific behavioral rule is a norm.
  • C: Beliefs are accepted truths/assumptions; they’re not physical objects.
  • D: Rituals are formalized ceremonies with a prescribed order; this is everyday etiquette.
3. A researcher notes that smartphones rapidly changed how people share photos, but social expectations about privacy changed much more slowly, creating conflict about what “should” be posted online. This pattern is best described as:
A. symbolic ethnicity
B. cultural relativism
C. ethnography
D. culture lag
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: D.

Why: Culture lag occurs when material culture (technology, artifacts) changes faster than symbolic/nonmaterial culture (values, norms, beliefs), producing tension and adjustment problems.

Other choices:

  • A: Symbolic ethnicity is selective, occasional expression of ethnic identity (e.g., one holiday).
  • B: Cultural relativism is studying a culture on its own terms instead of judging it by one’s own.
  • C: Ethnography is a method for studying cultures; it doesn’t name the mismatch between tech change and value change.
4. A hospital adds multilingual signage and trained interpreters after repeated misunderstandings about consent procedures among recent immigrants. These misunderstandings are best categorized as:
A. gender stratification

Correct Answer: B.

Why: A cultural barrier occurs when differences in beliefs, values, or language impede effective interaction—here, communication about consent and autonomy.

Other choices:

  • A: Gender stratification is unequal access to resources based on gender.
  • C: Symbolic ethnicity is intermittent celebration of heritage.
  • D: Ageism is prejudice or discrimination based on age.

Correct Answer: C.

Why: If a culture relies heavily on cattle farming and milk consumption, a mutation allowing lactase persistence can improve nutrition and survival, increasing reproductive success and being selected for.

D. Stage 2: dropping death rates with continued high birth rates

Correct Answer: D.

Why: In Stage 2, death rates drop first (health, nutrition, sanitation improve) while birth rates remain high, producing rapid population growth.

Other choices:

  • B: Stage 4 has both rates low and population relatively constant.
  • C: Stage 3 is when birth rates begin to fall substantially.

Passage-Based Questions (Q16–Q24)
Passage 1: Culture, Technology, and Healthcare Communication

At Riverside Medical Center, a new wearable device automatically translates spoken language into subtitles visible to staff through augmented-reality glasses. Administrators hope the device will reduce misunderstandings during informed consent conversations, especially when family members translate for patients.

After deployment, some clinicians enthusiastically record brief clips of the subtitles to “prove” what was said during difficult encounters. Patients and families react negatively, arguing that recording—even without audio—violates privacy and trust. The hospital’s older staff members also express discomfort, insisting that sensitive conversations should happen “face-to-face, without devices.”

In response, the hospital bans recording and creates a new ceremony for admission: a short scripted introduction where staff explain how translation works, what will and will not be stored, and how patients can opt out.

How to reason:

  1. Identify which details describe new technology or artifacts (material culture) vs values/norms about privacy and interaction (symbolic culture).
  2. If the technology changes faster than expectations about privacy or “proper” interaction, consider culture lag.
  3. Decide whether the scenario is about an informal rule (norm) or a formalized, ordered ceremony (ritual).
  4. For healthcare decision dilemmas, look for belief/value conflicts (e.g., autonomy, privacy, family decision-making) that create a cultural barrier.
16. Which interpretation best explains why the translation glasses triggered conflict even though they improved communication?
A. The glasses increased ethnocentrism by forcing staff to judge other cultures more harshly
B. The glasses reduced symbolic culture by eliminating the need for language
C. Material culture changed quickly while privacy and interaction norms changed slowly, producing culture lag
D. The conflict occurred because rituals always change faster than technology
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: C.

Why: The device is a material innovation. Discomfort and backlash centered on symbolic culture—privacy expectations and norms for sensitive conversations. When material change outpaces symbolic adjustment, culture lag creates conflict.

Other choices:

  • A: Ethnocentrism is judging other cultures by one’s own standards; the conflict here is mainly about privacy/recording and interaction norms.
  • B: Language remains central; translation tools don’t remove symbolic culture.
  • D: Rituals are usually slower-moving symbolic practices; the issue is tech vs norms, not rituals outpacing tech.

Passage 2: Demographic Transition and Age Structure

In the country of Karuma, a public health campaign rapidly expands access to clean water and vaccines. Over a decade, the annual mortality rate drops substantially, while the birth rate remains high. The population pyramid begins to widen at the base as more children survive into adolescence.

Ten years later, contraception becomes more accessible and more girls remain in school through secondary education. Families increasingly report that raising children is more expensive because young people require longer education before becoming economically productive. The birth rate begins to fall.

Meanwhile, Karuma’s leaders debate whether to invest first in elementary schools, job creation, or elder-care infrastructure.

How to reason:

  1. Translate story details into birth-rate vs death-rate changes over time.
  2. Match the pattern to demographic transition stages: death rate drops first (Stage 2), then birth rate drops (Stage 3), and eventually both are low (Stage 4).
  3. Use the population pyramid and age structure to infer the dependency ratio and which public resources are under strain.
  4. Keep units straight: fertility rate is children per woman per lifetime; birth/mortality rates are per 1000 people per year.

C. Stage 3, because birth rates are already falling
D. Stage 2, because death rates decline before birth rates
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: D.

Why: Stage 2 is defined by falling death rates from improvements in healthcare/sanitation while birth rates remain high, causing population growth.

Other choices:

  • A: Stage 1 keeps death rates high.
  • B: Stage 4 requires low birth rates as well.
  • C: Stage 3 is characterized by declining birth rates, which occurs later in Karuma.

Passage 3: Race, Ethnicity, and Social Movements

In a large coastal city, a clinic launches an outreach program for newly arrived immigrants. The program hires clinicians who speak Spanish and partners with community leaders to increase screening for chronic diseases. Some staff initially describe the target population as a single “Hispanic race,” assuming the group shares the same customs, language preferences, and health beliefs.

During an intake interview, one patient explains: “I’m Guatemalan; my first language is K’iche’. I also speak Spanish, but I prefer medical information in my indigenous language when possible.” Another patient says she feels deeply Irish on St. Patrick’s Day, but otherwise rarely thinks about her ancestry.

At the same time, a local organization holds rallies opposing immigration and calling for stricter border policies, arguing that newcomers are changing the city too quickly.

How to reason:

  1. Separate race (social labels based on perceived phenotypic traits) from ethnicity (shared culture/language/national origin).
  2. Look for clues about symbolic ethnicity: intermittent, selective expression of heritage via symbols/holidays.
  3. For social movements, ask: does the movement promote change (proactive) or resist change (reactive)? Then connect motivations to perceived threats or relative deprivation.
  4. In healthcare examples, consider how simplifying labels can create cultural barriers and reduce effective communication.
