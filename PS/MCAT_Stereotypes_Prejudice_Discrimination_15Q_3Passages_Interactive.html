Standalone Questions (Q1–Q15)
1. A student believes that members of Group X are “naturally bad at public speaking.” Before meeting a new classmate from Group X, the student already feels uneasy and dislikes the classmate. Which construct is best represented by the student’s uneasy dislike?
A. Stereotype
B. Prejudice
C. Discrimination
D. Cultural relativism
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: B.

Why: Prejudice is the affective (emotional/attitudinal) response toward a group prior to direct experience—here, uneasy dislike.

Other choices:

  • A: The belief “Group X is bad at public speaking” is a stereotype (cognitive expectation), but the question asks about the feeling.
  • C: Discrimination is behavior (actions), not feelings.
  • D: Cultural relativism is evaluating cultures on their own terms, not liking/disliking a group.

D. Discrimination
✅Correct
❌Incorrect

Correct Answer: D.

Why: Discrimination is the behavioral component—differential treatment of groups (refusing interviews).

Other choices:

  • A: “Unsafe neighborhood” belief is a stereotype, but the stem asks about the action.
  • B: Anxiety is prejudice (affect), but again the question asks about behavior.
  • C: Ethnocentrism is judging other cultures by one’s own culture.

A. Self-fulfilling prophecy
B. Cultural relativism
C. Ethnocentrism
D. Institutional discrimination
✅Correct
❌Incorrect

Correct Answer: A.

Why: A self-fulfilling prophecy occurs when expectations create conditions that make the expectation come true (reduced opportunities → reduced performance).

Other choices:

  • B/C: These are about judging cultures, not expectation-driven performance outcomes.
  • D: Institutional discrimination is discrimination by an institution’s structure; this stem focuses on an interpersonal expectation cycle. (It could contribute, but the mechanism tested is self-fulfilling prophecy.)

Passage 1: “Don’t Mess Up the Knots”

During the first week of a surgery clerkship, a supervising resident tells new students, “Third-years are always slow at knot-tying.” Some students laugh nervously and begin practicing in the skills lab late into the night.

On the first day in the operating room, one student, Mina, hears the comment repeated by a scrub nurse. Mina becomes hyperaware of every hand movement and worries that any error will confirm the stereotype. She notices her heart racing and repeatedly checks whether others are watching her hands. Her performance is shaky, and she drops the suture twice.

Afterward, the resident concludes, “See? Third-years really can’t do it,” and assigns Mina fewer opportunities to practice in the operating room. Over the next two weeks Mina improves more slowly than her peers who received more practice time.

How to reason:

  1. First, label what is belief vs feeling vs behavior. Stereotypes = beliefs/expectations; prejudice = emotions; discrimination = actions.
  2. Identify whether anxiety is about confirming a negative stereotype → that’s stereotype threat.
  3. Then check if the authority’s expectation changes the environment (fewer opportunities), which can make the expectation come true → self-fulfilling prophecy.
  4. Pick the answer that best matches the specific mechanism described by the evidence, not just the topic word.

❌Incorrect

Correct Answer: A.

Why: The passage shows anxiety about confirming a negative stereotype, plus distraction and reduced performance—textbook stereotype threat.

  • C/D: These relate to judging cultures, not performance under stereotype-related pressure.

Passage 2: Hiring “Fit” and Promotion Pipelines

A midsize company prides itself on being “merit-based.” In practice, entry-level hires come largely from a few elite universities, and promotions heavily depend on informal mentorship by senior staff. New hires who did not attend the elite schools report fewer invitations to networking events where senior staff choose mentees.

During interviews, some managers casually describe certain candidates as “not a culture fit” without specifying job-related reasons. These managers rarely express overt hostility, but a pattern emerges: candidates from non-elite universities are less likely to receive offers, and employees from those schools are less likely to be promoted.

In response, a few employees create a slideshow arguing that non-elite graduates are “less disciplined” and that the company’s outcomes “prove” this. The slideshow is circulated widely to justify continuing the current system.

How to reason:

  1. Separate individual actions from institutional structures. Policies/practices that produce group disparities without one “bad actor” are often institutional discrimination.
  2. Identify whether a claim is a belief (stereotype), a feeling (prejudice), or an action/outcome (discrimination).
  3. Look for how power, prestige, and class operate: elite pipelines and informal networks can preserve unequal access.
  4. When justification materials appear, ask: are they amplifying stereotypes to rationalize inequality (a common function of prejudice/discrimination)?

Passage 3: Study Abroad, “Normal” Rules, and Group Images

A group of students from Country A studies abroad in Country B. During a local festival in Country B, one host family avoids direct criticism and instead uses subtle hints and long pauses to signal disagreement. Several visitors from Country A complain, “They should just say what they mean—this is so dishonest,” and begin avoiding conversations with hosts.

One visitor, Lian, takes a different approach: she asks the host family to explain how disagreement is typically expressed in their community and learns that direct confrontation is considered disrespectful. Lian starts interpreting pauses as a polite form of refusal rather than deception.

Back on campus, students debate a campus group that is high-status and competes for limited scholarships. Some students describe this group as “capable but cold,” while another campus group that does not compete for resources is described as “nice, but not very competent.”

How to reason:

  1. When someone calls another culture “dishonest” because it violates their own norms, that’s ethnocentrism (judging by one’s own culture).
  2. When someone tries to understand a practice within that culture’s context, that’s cultural relativism.
  3. For the campus groups, map competition → warmth (competition lowers perceived warmth) and status → competence (status raises perceived competence).
  4. Use the stereotype content labels: warm+incompetent = paternalistic; cold+competent = envious; warm+competent = admiration; cold+incompetent = contemptuous.

Correct Answer: B.

Why: They judge another culture’s communication norms using their own culture’s standards (“should just say what they mean”), which is ethnocentrism.

Other choices:

  • C: Stereotype threat involves anxiety about confirming a stereotype.
  • D: Self-fulfilling prophecy involves expectations shaping outcomes through changed conditions.
