Standalone Questions (Q1–Q15)
1. Which pair correctly lists the major components of the central nervous system (CNS)?
A. Cranial nerves and spinal nerves
B. Brain and spinal cord
C. Autonomic nerves and somatic nerves
D. Ganglia and peripheral nerves
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: B.

Why: The CNS is composed of the brain and spinal cord; all other nerve fibers and ganglia are classified as part of the PNS.

Other choices:

  • A: Cranial and spinal nerves are part of the PNS (even though they connect to the CNS).
  • C: Somatic and autonomic divisions are subdivisions of the PNS, not the CNS.
  • D: Ganglia and peripheral nerves lie outside the brain/spinal cord and are PNS.

Passage 1: Pain, Reflexes, and Circuit Organization

During a lab practical, a student accidentally steps on a sharp tack. The student immediately withdraws the affected foot and shifts body weight onto the other leg. A moment later, the student reports feeling a sharp pain and looks down to inspect the injury.

The instructor notes that several neuron types are involved in the sequence. A receptor in the skin detects the noxious stimulus and sends a signal toward the spinal cord. Within the spinal cord, the incoming signal is relayed through a local circuit that can activate muscles in both legs.

The instructor emphasizes that the rapid motor response can occur before the conscious perception of pain is fully processed in the brain. However, information about the injury still travels upward so that the student can make a voluntary decision about what to do next.

How to reason:

  1. Direction matters: receptor → CNS uses afferent neurons; CNS → muscle uses efferent neurons.
  2. Look for fast control: the withdrawal happens via a spinal reflex arc using interneurons.
  3. Timing clue: movement occurs before conscious report → reflex precedes cortical processing.
  4. Avoid traps: skeletal muscle is controlled by somatic motor neurons, not autonomic output.

Passage 2: Autonomic Balance in Two Clinical Scenarios

Two patients present with contrasting physiologic states. Patient A is brought to the clinic immediately after narrowly avoiding a car accident. Their heart rate is elevated, pupils are dilated, and they report reduced appetite and slowed bowel activity during the event.

Patient B arrives for a follow-up appointment shortly after eating a large meal. Their heart rate is lower than baseline, they feel relaxed and sleepy, and bowel sounds are prominent on exam with increased digestive activity.

A resident explains that the autonomic nervous system contains two branches that often produce opposite effects. The resident predicts that medications shifting autonomic tone can change both cardiovascular and gastrointestinal function in predictable directions.

How to reason:

  1. Classify each state: acute stress signs → sympathetic; relaxed post-meal digestion → parasympathetic.
  2. Match effects: sympathetic ↑ heart rate, ↓ digestion; parasympathetic ↑ peristalsis/secretions and lowers heart rate.
  3. Use antagonism: the branches are often oppositional on the same organ systems.
  4. Check for fight-or-flight markers: pupil dilation and glucose mobilization align with sympathetic tone.

Passage 3: Mapping Structures to CNS vs PNS

A neuroscience demonstration uses a simplified wiring diagram. The instructor labels the brain and spinal cord as the processing center and highlights bundles of nerves leaving the spinal cord to reach peripheral tissues. The instructor also notes cranial nerves that emerge directly from the brain.

A student points out that some cranial nerves have special structural relationships to the CNS. The instructor agrees, explaining that certain cranial nerves are outgrowths of CNS tissue but are still classified within the peripheral nervous system because they lie outside the brain and spinal cord proper.

To test understanding, the instructor asks the class to categorize several structures as CNS or PNS and to predict whether those structures would primarily carry incoming sensory information, outgoing motor commands, or local processing within circuits.

How to reason:

  1. Use the definition: CNS = brain + spinal cord; nerves and ganglia outside are PNS.
  2. Special case: optic/olfactory nerves are CNS outgrowths but still counted as PNS cranial nerves.
  3. Then map roles: sensory input to CNS is afferent; commands from CNS are efferent; interneurons are mainly CNS.
  4. If unsure, ask: is it inside the brain/spinal cord (CNS) or a nerve outside (PNS)?
