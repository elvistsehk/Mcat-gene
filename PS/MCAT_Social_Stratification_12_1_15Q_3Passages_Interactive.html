Standalone Questions (Q1–Q15)
1. A public health study classifies participants’ socioeconomic status (SES). Which combination is most directly used to determine SES?
A. Religious affiliation, marital status, and neighborhood density
B. Personality traits, political ideology, and family size
C. Income, educational attainment, and occupational prestige
D. Age, gender identity, and skin color
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: C.

Why: SES is typically operationalized using indicators like income/wealth, education, and occupation because these capture access to material resources and life opportunities.

Other choices:

  • A: These variables can correlate with SES but are not the core, standard indicators used to define SES.
  • B: Traits and ideology are not structural resource measures; they may influence outcomes but do not define SES.
  • D: These are largely ascribed statuses; they can affect opportunities through discrimination, but they do not define SES itself.
2. A student is treated differently in school because of her skin color, even though her grades and work ethic are strong. In the language of status, skin color is best classified as:
A. Ascribed status
B. Achieved status
C. Role strain
D. Cultural capital
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A.

Why: Ascribed status is involuntary and based on identifiable characteristics (e.g., age, gender, race/skin color) that can shape life chances regardless of merit.

Other choices:

  • B: Achieved status is earned through individual effort (e.g., education level, job title).
  • C: Role strain is difficulty meeting expectations within a single role; it is not a type of status.
  • D: Cultural capital refers to skills/knowledge that can yield advantages (e.g., mannerisms, credentials), not a physical characteristic.
3. A physician is described as “a doctor first, everything else second,” and this identity strongly shapes how others treat her and how she sees herself across settings. This best illustrates:
A. Horizontal mobility
B. False consciousness
C. Weak-tie influence
D. Master status
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: D.

Why: A master status is a status that pervades many aspects of a person’s life and dominates social identity and interactions (e.g., being a physician, being a parent, being incarcerated).

Other choices:

  • A: Horizontal mobility is a job/lifestyle change that stays within the same class.
  • B: False consciousness is misperceiving one’s class position and interests in a way that prevents collective action.
  • C: Weak ties are superficial connections that can provide new information; this is about identity dominance, not networks.
4. A city council member can influence zoning decisions by threatening to block funding unless businesses comply. Which concept is most directly being demonstrated?
A. Prestige
B. Power
C. Cultural capital
D. Social cohesion
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: B.

Why: Power is the ability to influence others’ behavior through real or perceived rewards and punishments, often rooted in unequal control of valued resources.

Other choices:

  • A: Prestige is the positive regard or respect associated with a person or occupation; it is not necessarily coercive influence.
  • C: Cultural capital is advantage from knowledge/skills; it does not describe coercive leverage over others.
  • D: Social cohesion is solidarity and connectedness among groups, not leverage over decisions.

A. A socioeconomic gradient in health and development
B. An example of horizontal mobility
C. Evidence of a caste system
D. A marker of anomie
✅Correct
❌Incorrect

Correct Answer: A.

Why: The socioeconomic gradient refers to the stepwise improvement in health outcomes as SES increases, not just a difference between the poorest and richest.

Other choices:

  • B: Horizontal mobility is an occupation/lifestyle shift within the same social class, unrelated to population health gradients.
  • C: A caste system is ascribed and rigid; the finding is about health outcomes across SES levels, not a stratification system type.
  • D: Anomie is norm breakdown and weakened social bonds; it can relate to deviance, not directly to a graded health pattern.
6. According to conflict theory, what most directly prevents the working class from organizing to challenge the capitalist class?
A. High levels of social capital among the proletariat
B. Intergenerational mobility that guarantees equal opportunity
C. False consciousness about one’s true class position and shared interests
D. The existence of weak ties across social networks
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: C.

Why: Marx argued that false consciousness—misperceiving one’s exploitation or failing to recognize shared interests—blocks development of class consciousness and collective action.

Other choices:

  • A: High social capital could facilitate coordination and collective action, not prevent it.
  • B: High mobility would reduce class rigidity; it is not the key barrier described by conflict theory.
  • D: Weak ties can spread information and opportunities; they are not the central mechanism blocking class consciousness.
7. A community experiences rapid economic change, rising inequality, and weakening shared norms. Over time, rates of rule-breaking behaviors increase. Which pairing best fits this scenario?
A. Social cohesion → cultural capital
B. Anomie → strain theory leading to deviance
C. Meritocracy → upward mobility
D. World-systems theory → suburbanization

❌Incorrect

Correct Answer: B.

Why: Anomie is a breakdown of widely accepted norms and social bonds; strain theory links these anomic conditions to increased deviance as individuals adapt to blocked goals and social disorganization.

Other choices:

  • A: Social cohesion is the opposite of the described norm breakdown; cultural capital is skill/knowledge advantage, not deviance.
  • C: Meritocracy describes mobility based on talent/achievement; it does not explain norm breakdown and deviance.
  • D: World-systems theory explains global division of labor (core/periphery); suburbanization is a migration pattern, not the primary framework here.

Passages (3 passages × 3 questions)
Passage 1: Urban Renewal, Cost of Living, and Health

Riverside is an aging urban neighborhood located two blocks from a large industrial corridor. For decades, many residents worked hourly jobs nearby and rented apartments in older buildings. A recent city report shows that a higher-income district across town has far lower asthma and emergency-room visit rates than Riverside, even though both areas are within the same city limits.

After a new tech campus opens nearby, investors begin buying and renovating Riverside properties. Over two years, coffee shops and boutique gyms appear, and median rent increases sharply. Some longtime tenants who earn slightly above the federal poverty threshold say that, despite being “above the line,” they cannot cover rent, food, and childcare in the now higher-cost area. Several families relocate to the city’s outskirts where rent is cheaper but public transit is limited.

At the same time, the city proposes expanding a waste-processing facility in the industrial corridor. Community members attend hearings, but many report that they lack the time, money, and political connections to influence the decision compared with residents from wealthier neighborhoods.

How to reason:

  1. Skim for the big mechanisms: urban renewal/gentrification, poverty line vs cost of living, and environmental justice.
  2. When a question asks for an example of gentrification, look for higher-SES newcomers + renovation + rising costs + displacement (not just “neighborhood improvement”).
  3. When a question mentions the poverty line, connect it to geographic cost differences (rent in a city vs rural town).
  4. For environmental justice, focus on hazard placement and political power: low-SES areas often have less influence and more exposure.
  5. Choose the option that requires using passage evidence to infer the sociological mechanism, not just restate a sentence.

Passage 2: Networks, Weak Ties, and Opportunity

A community college launches a career program for adults who have been unemployed for over a year. Participants learn interview skills, but the program also pushes them to attend industry meetups and build professional contacts. Lina, a participant with strong family support, relies heavily on close relatives and best friends for encouragement, but none work in the industry she wants to enter.

At a conference, Lina speaks briefly with a recruiter and later meets a consultant who mentions an opening at a firm Lina had not considered. The consultant introduces Lina to two people in that firm, and within a week Lina receives an interview invitation. Another participant, Omar, has similar training and income but rarely attends events and knows few people outside his immediate circle.

Program staff notice that a small group of attendees repeatedly serve as “connectors” who introduce others across industries; referrals tend to pass through them. The staff debate whether unequal interview outcomes are mostly about who has more money and credentials, or about where individuals sit within the network.

How to reason:

  1. Identify the key vocabulary: social capital, strong vs weak ties, and situational vs positional inequality.
  2. Strong ties = close friends/family (high support, fewer new leads). Weak ties = acquaintances (more bridges to new information).
  3. Situational inequality is about socioeconomic advantage (resources) that improves opportunities. Positional inequality is about centrality/gatekeeping in the network.
  4. For questions, map each answer choice to a network mechanism: bridging, redundancy, gatekeepers, or SES.
  5. Prefer answers that explain why the network structure changes opportunity, not just that “connections help.”

Passage 3: Merit, Credentials, and Social Reproduction

Two high schools in the same state send graduates to competitive universities. School A is in an affluent suburb with advanced placement courses, college counselors, and frequent alumni networking events. School B is in a low-income urban area with larger class sizes, fewer electives, and frequent teacher turnover.

During the admissions cycle, two applicants—Maya (School A) and Jae (School B)—earn similar test scores and grades. Maya’s family pays for expensive test-prep, and Maya receives an interview referral through a legacy alumni connection. Jae works part-time to help cover household expenses and has limited access to tutoring or unpaid internships.

An admissions officer argues that the system is merit-based because everyone takes the same standardized exam. A sociologist counters that differences in schooling, time, and connections can reproduce advantage across generations, even when two students appear equally “talented” on paper.

How to reason:

  1. Separate merit (achievement) from ascribed/resource advantages (legacy ties, paid prep, school quality).
  2. When asked to critique meritocracy, look for outcomes that change despite similar performance—that signals non-merit factors.
  3. For mobility questions, connect education to credentials → job access (structural mobility).
  4. For intergenerational persistence, look for structural channels: schools, housing, neighborhood resources, political influence (social reproduction).
  5. Pick the choice that best matches the passage’s causal story rather than a definition in isolation.
