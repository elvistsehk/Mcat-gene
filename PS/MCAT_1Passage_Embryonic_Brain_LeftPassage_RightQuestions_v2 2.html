Embryonic Brain
Timer 12:00
Passage 1 (Questions 1–8)

In prenatal development, the central nervous system begins as the neural tube. Early in development, three major swellings appear along this tube: the prosencephalon, mesencephalon, and rhombencephalon. Although these swellings are simple in the earliest stage, they provide the framework from which more specialized brain regions later emerge.

The figure shows both a straighter early view of the neural tube and a later flexed embryonic view. Comparing the two is useful because the tube bends as development proceeds, yet the same anterior-to-posterior relationships must still be tracked. The prosencephalon remains at the rostral end, the mesencephalon remains intermediate, and the rhombencephalon remains caudal to the midbrain and continuous with the developing spinal cord.

As development continues, the forebrain and hindbrain each subdivide further, producing the familiar five-vesicle pattern studied in developmental neurobiology. The midbrain does not split in this simplified scheme. For MCAT-style reasoning, the important step is not memorizing isolated labels, but connecting each embryonic region to the kinds of structures and functions it will later support.

The flexed embryo image also shows landmarks that help with inference. The optic vesicle emerges from the anterior region, supporting the idea that specialized sensory outgrowths appear while broad embryonic divisions are still visible. Cranial ganglia and spinal ganglia develop in association with the nervous system but should not be confused with the primary brain vesicles themselves. Likewise, flexures indicate bending of the neural tube rather than the creation of entirely new major divisions.

Use this quick approach for developmental neuroanatomy questions:

  • Start with orientation: identify which side of the figure is rostral (prosencephalon) and which side continues into the spinal cord.
  • Track the three primary swellings: prosencephalon, mesencephalon, and rhombencephalon are the starting map of the embryonic brain.
  • Connect embryonic to later function: when a question describes a later deficit, first decide which embryonic vesicle would have produced the affected adult structures.
  • Use landmarks carefully: optic vesicle, cranial ganglia, spinal ganglia, and flexures help you infer location; they are not interchangeable boundaries.
  • Watch for developmental logic: the forebrain and hindbrain subdivide further, while the midbrain stays as one major vesicle in this simplified pattern.
A developmental toxin selectively disrupts formation of the prosencephalon early in gestation. Which later deficit would most directly follow from the passage and figure?
Explanation
The best answer is C. The figure places the prosencephalon at the rostral end and shows the optic vesicle emerging from this region. The passage states that the embryonic forebrain later divides into multiple forebrain structures, so an early prosencephalon defect would be expected to compromise higher-order forebrain development and related outgrowths.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • A: The medulla is a hindbrain derivative, not a prosencephalic derivative.
  • B: Cerebellar coordination points to metencephalic/hindbrain dysfunction, not a primary forebrain defect.
  • D: Spinal ganglia are shown outside the central brain swellings and do not best capture the most direct consequence of selective prosencephalon disruption.
A researcher notes that one embryonic swelling does not subdivide further, whereas two others later split into additional regions. Which swelling is most consistent with that observation?
Explanation
The best answer is A. The passage explains that the neural tube first forms three swellings: hindbrain, midbrain, and forebrain. It then states that the hindbrain and forebrain later divide into two swellings each, implying that the midbrain, or mesencephalon, is the one that remains undivided in this simplified scheme.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • B: The rhombencephalon is one of the swellings that later divides further.
  • C: The prosencephalon is also described as one that later divides further.
  • D: The spinal cord is not one of the three original brain swellings.
If a mutation prevented the normal increase in DNA synthesis and cell proliferation specifically in the posterior swelling shown in the diagram, which adult system would most likely be affected first?
Explanation
The best answer is D. The posterior swelling in the figure is the rhombencephalon, or hindbrain. The background material on brain subdivisions links the hindbrain to functions such as balance, coordination, and vital autonomic control. A developmental problem there would therefore be expected to impair those functions most directly.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • A: Visual relay structures are more closely associated with forebrain and midbrain pathways than with the hindbrain as the primary answer here.
  • B: Hypothalamic derivatives come from the forebrain, not the hindbrain.
  • C: Olfactory structures are forebrain-associated, not posterior hindbrain derivatives.

  • A: The presence of the spinal cord does not specifically demonstrate early sensory specialization.
  • C: Flexures indicate bending of the neural tube, not sensory specialization by themselves.
  • D: External ganglia suggest peripheral organization, but the optic vesicle is the clearest direct evidence of early sensory outgrowth.
During dissection, a student mistakes the cephalic flexure for the boundary between forebrain and hindbrain. Based on the figure, why is that interpretation incorrect?
Explanation
The best answer is A. In the figure, the cephalic flexure is positioned around the developing midbrain region rather than serving as the dividing line between the anterior prosencephalon and the posterior rhombencephalon. The student is therefore using the wrong landmark for that boundary.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • B: The spinal cord is caudal to the rhombencephalon, not what the cephalic flexure marks in the figure.
  • C: The figure depicts the cephalic flexure during embryonic development, so it clearly is not a postnatal feature.
  • D: Flexures are bends of the developing neural tube, not exclusive features of peripheral nerves.
A clinician suspects a congenital lesion in tissue that develops from the middle primary vesicle. Which additional finding would best strengthen that suspicion?
Explanation
The best answer is D. The middle primary vesicle is the mesencephalon. If that specific embryonic region were affected, the strongest support would be a pattern showing abnormalities centered on midbrain-associated functions while structures derived mainly from the forebrain and hindbrain remain relatively spared.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • A: That pattern points more strongly to forebrain pathology than to a selective mesencephalic lesion.
  • B: Respiratory centers are associated with hindbrain derivatives, especially the medulla.
  • C: Spinal ganglia are peripheral structures and do not best identify a lesion limited to the middle brain vesicle.
Why is it useful, from an MCAT reasoning standpoint, to compare the straight neural tube diagram with the flexed embryo diagram rather than memorize labels from only one view?
Explanation
The best answer is A. The purpose of using both views is to reason through how the same embryonic regions can still be identified after the neural tube bends. That helps a student preserve orientation and connect early developmental anatomy with the later arrangement seen in the embryo.
Why the other choices are not as good

  • C: Both diagrams show continuity with the spinal cord rather than independence from it.
  • D: Both views are useful; the figure pair is valuable precisely because each contributes complementary spatial information.
An investigator wants to trace how the original three-vesicle stage gives rise to the mature five-vesicle stage. Which pattern best matches the passage?
Explanation
The best answer is C. The passage explicitly states that the embryonic brain begins with three swellings and that the forebrain and hindbrain later divide, producing a total of five swellings. That means the mesencephalon remains singular while the prosencephalon and rhombencephalon each split further.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • A: This reverses the passage logic; the mesencephalon is the one that does not divide in this scheme.
  • B: The rhombencephalon does not regress completely; it subdivides.
  • D: Ganglia are not replacements for the primary brain vesicles in the developmental sequence described.
