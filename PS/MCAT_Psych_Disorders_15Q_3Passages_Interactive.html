Standalone Questions (15)
1. A patient’s depression is evaluated by considering genetic vulnerability, maladaptive thought patterns, and limited social support. Which model is the clinician using?
A. Biomedical model
B. Type theory of personality
C. Biopsychosocial model
D. Behaviorist model
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: C.

Why: The biopsychosocial model explicitly integrates biological (e.g., genetics), psychological (thoughts/emotions/behaviors), and social (support, stressors, stigma) contributors to a disorder and often guides both direct and indirect interventions.

Other choices:

  • A: The biomedical approach focuses primarily on biological disturbances and symptom reduction (e.g., medication) without systematically incorporating social/psychological context.
  • B: Type theories classify personality into categories (e.g., Type A/B) rather than modeling causes of mental disorders.
  • D: Behaviorism emphasizes learning histories (reinforcement/conditioning) and does not inherently require biological + social integration.
2. A clinician uses the DSM-5 to diagnose a disorder by matching a patient’s presentation to a checklist of features. The DSM-5 primarily classifies disorders based on:
A. Descriptions of symptoms rather than etiology
B. Response to a specific medication
C. Family systems dynamics
D. Underlying causes and mechanisms
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A.

Why: The DSM-5 is a descriptive diagnostic system: clinicians match a patient’s presentation to symptom criteria rather than diagnosing based on the presumed cause.

Other choices:

  • B: Medication response is not the organizing principle of DSM-5 diagnoses.
  • C: Family dynamics may be relevant clinically, but DSM-5 categories are not built around family-system explanations.
  • D: That would be an etiologic classification scheme; DSM-5 generally avoids committing to specific causes.
3. Rosenhan’s pseudopatient study most strongly suggests that once someone receives a psychiatric label, clinicians may:
A. Be unable to make any accurate diagnoses at all
B. Diagnose only mood disorders, not psychotic disorders
C. Rely entirely on biological tests to confirm diagnoses
D. Interpret subsequent behaviors through the lens of the diagnosis (labeling/confirmation bias)
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: D.

Why: Rosenhan showed that once a person is labeled with a mental illness, observers may reinterpret ambiguous or even normal behavior as evidence of pathology, and the label can be difficult to shed.

Other choices:

  • A: The study critiques context and labeling effects, not the claim that diagnosis is impossible in principle.
  • B: Rosenhan’s pseudopatients were diagnosed with psychotic or mood disorders; the key point is the persistence of labels.
  • C: The study highlights reliance on contextual interpretation, not a shift to biological confirmation tests.
4. Which of the following is a classic negative symptom of schizophrenia?
A. Auditory hallucinations
B. Loose associations
C. Delusions of grandeur
D. Avolition
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: D.

Why: Avolition is a classic negative symptom: reduced motivation and decreased engagement in goal-directed behavior (a loss of normal function).

Other choices:

  • A: Hallucinations are <b>positive symptoms</b> (added experiences).
  • B: Disorganized thought/speech is a <b>positive/disorganized</b> symptom dimension, not negative.
  • C: Delusions are also <b>positive symptoms</b>.
5. A patient insists that the news anchor on TV is sending him secret messages specifically meant for him. This belief is best described as a delusion of:
A. Thought insertion
B. Grandeur
C. Reference
D. Persecution
✅Correct
❌Incorrect

Correct Answer: C.

Why: Delusions of reference involve the belief that neutral events (TV, news, strangers’ actions) are specifically directed at the person.

Other choices:

  • A: Thought insertion is believing thoughts are being placed into one’s mind by an external agent.
  • B: Grandiose delusions involve exaggerated self-importance (e.g., being uniquely powerful or famous).
  • D: Persecutory delusions focus on being harmed/targeted by a plot; the stem emphasizes “messages meant for me.”
6. A clinician notes that a patient’s speech jumps between unrelated topics in a way that is difficult to follow. This is most consistent with:
A. Affective flattening
B. Loosening of associations (disorganized thought)
C. Echolalia
D. Somatic delusions
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: B.

Why: Rapid topic shifts with an untraceable train of thought reflect loosening of associations, a hallmark of disorganized thought (sometimes progressing to word salad).

Other choices:

  • A: Flat affect is a <b>negative symptom</b> involving reduced emotional expression, not disorganized speech.
  • C: Echolalia is <b>repeating another person’s words</b> (a motor/behavioral phenomenon).
  • D: Somatic delusions are false beliefs about bodily functions/health (e.g., organs rotting).
7. To meet DSM-5 criteria for schizophrenia, the disturbance must persist for at least 6 months, including at least 1 month of:
A. Obsessions and compulsions
B. Catatonia only
C. Positive symptoms (delusions, hallucinations, or disorganized speech)

Correct Answer: C.

Why: Schizophrenia requires ≥6 months of disturbance, including ≥1 month of core positive/disorganized symptoms such as delusions, hallucinations, or disorganized speech.

  • A: Those define OCD-related disorders, not schizophrenia.
  • B: Catatonia can occur, but schizophrenia diagnosis requires characteristic psychotic symptoms; catatonia alone is insufficient.

8. A major depressive episode is defined as at least 2 weeks of 5 or more depressive symptoms, including either depressed mood or:
A. Delusions of reference
B. Anhedonia
C. Flight of ideas
D. Grandiosity
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: B.

Why: A major depressive episode must include either depressed mood or anhedonia (loss of interest/pleasure) as one of the symptoms present among the required ≥5.

Other choices:

  • A: Delusions are psychotic symptoms; they are not required for a major depressive episode.
  • C: Flight of ideas/racing thoughts are manic symptoms.
  • D: Grandiosity is a manic symptom (DIG FAST), not a defining depressive criterion.
9. Persistent depressive disorder (dysthymia) requires depressed mood on most days for at least:
A. 2 weeks
B. 6 months
C. 2 years
D. 4 days
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: C.

Why: Persistent depressive disorder (dysthymia) is defined primarily by duration: depressed mood for most of the day, more days than not, for ≥2 years (≥1 year in children/adolescents).

Other choices:

  • A: Two weeks is the minimum duration for a major depressive episode, not PDD.
  • B: Six months is used in some anxiety diagnoses (e.g., GAD), not PDD.
  • D: Four days is a hypomania duration threshold, not a depressive disorder duration.
10. The single feature that most strongly distinguishes bipolar I disorder from bipolar II disorder is the presence of:
A. Seasonal onset of symptoms
B. Hypomanic episodes only
C. A manic episode
D. A major depressive episode
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: C.

Why: Bipolar I is defined by the presence of at least one manic episode (often with depression but not required). Bipolar II requires hypomania + major depression and no mania.

Other choices:

  • A: Seasonal onset is a specifier and not the defining difference between bipolar I and II.
  • B: Hypomania without mania points toward bipolar II (if MDE present) or cyclothymia (if subthreshold), not bipolar I.
  • D: MDE is required for bipolar II but not for bipolar I; both may have depression so it is not the key separator.

Why: Cyclothymic disorder involves chronic fluctuation (≥2 years) of hypomanic and depressive symptoms that remain subthreshold for full episodes.

Passage 1: Mood Episodes, Treatment Choice, and the Biopsychosocial Lens

Jordan, a 28-year-old graduate student, reports that over the last month he has alternated between two distinct patterns. For about two weeks, he felt persistently “down,” had low energy, slept 11–12 hours per day, and stopped enjoying hobbies he normally loved. He lost about 6% of his body weight because he “couldn’t be bothered to eat,” and he struggled to concentrate on lab work. After this period, Jordan experienced 6 days of unusually elevated mood with racing thoughts and a surge of goal-directed activity: he started three new projects, spoke more quickly than usual, and slept only 3–4 hours per night while still feeling energized. During those 6 days he also made several impulsive online purchases, but he continued attending class and did not require hospitalization. Jordan’s father has a history of recurrent “mood swings,” and Jordan reports that he has been under increased academic stress and has felt isolated since moving away from friends. His clinician recommends a mood stabilizer, structured sleep routines, and a weekly peer support group while educating Jordan’s roommates on warning signs of mood escalation.

How to reason:

  1. Step 1: Classify the symptom clusters. Separate depressive vs manic/hypomanic features (duration + impairment).
  2. Step 2: Apply the diagnostic thresholds. MDE = ≥2 weeks + 5/9 (including mood or anhedonia). Hypomania = ≥4 days, ≥3 DIG FAST, not severe enough for marked impairment; Mania = ≥7 days or severe impairment.
  3. Step 3: Use context as evidence. Family history and social support can inform a biopsychosocial formulation and treatment planning.
  4. Step 4: Predict consequences of interventions. Antidepressants can precipitate mania/hypomania in vulnerable patients—link to episode history.

Passage 2: Rituals, Personality Style, and Diagnostic Boundaries

Mei, a 32-year-old accountant, spends 2–3 hours each evening washing her hands and wiping kitchen surfaces in a strict sequence. She reports intrusive thoughts that she might contaminate her younger sibling with germs and that “something terrible will happen” if she does not complete the ritual exactly. Mei recognizes the fear is excessive, yet when she tries to stop she becomes intensely anxious and cannot focus on anything else until she completes the routine. She has attempted to reduce the ritual multiple times, but the anxiety spikes and she returns to the sequence. At work, Mei is organized and likes clear procedures, but she does not describe her organizational style as a problem. In therapy, she asks, “Why can’t I just let this go?” and says the thoughts feel like they are “intruding” into her mind. She denies hearing voices, denies believing coworkers are poisoning her, and states she would be relieved if the thoughts and rituals disappeared. Her clinician is considering several diagnoses and wants to distinguish obsessive thoughts from fixed false beliefs.

How to reason:

  1. Step 1: Identify the core pattern. Look for intrusive thoughts vs fixed beliefs, and whether behaviors are performed to reduce anxiety.
  2. Step 2: Check insight (ego-dystonic vs ego-syntonic). OCD is typically ego-dystonic; personality disorders often feel “right” to the person.
  3. Step 3: Separate disorder categories. Psychosis involves delusions/hallucinations; OCD involves obsessions/compulsions; OCPD is a lifelong style.
  4. Step 4: Use impairment/time cost as evidence. Hours of rituals and functional impairment support OCD diagnosis.

Passage 3: Psychosis, Phases, and the Power of Labels

Samuel, a 21-year-old college student, is brought to the emergency department by his roommate, who reports a decline over the last 7 months. During the first several months, Samuel withdrew from friends, stopped attending classes, and began acting “odd,” including laughing inappropriately during serious conversations and neglecting basic hygiene. In the last 5 weeks, Samuel has become convinced that strangers on the subway are part of a plot to monitor him, and he reports hearing a voice commenting on his actions when he is alone. His speech sometimes shifts abruptly between unrelated topics, and he has missed multiple rent payments because he forgets deadlines. Urine toxicology is negative, and there is no evidence of a neurological condition explaining his symptoms. Samuel’s family is worried that once he is “labeled,” clinicians will dismiss alternative explanations and that Samuel will be treated differently by peers and employers. They also report that Samuel recently lost his part-time job and has struggled to maintain housing stability since his symptoms worsened.

How to reason:

  1. Step 1: Track the timeline. Identify prodromal vs active symptoms and check the DSM-5 duration requirement.
  2. Step 2: Classify symptoms. Positive (added experiences like delusions/hallucinations) vs negative (loss of normal function).
  3. Step 3: Use functional impairment as evidence. Disorganized behavior and decline in role functioning support a psychotic disorder diagnosis.
  4. Step 4: Consider sociological implications. Labels can bias interpretation (Rosenhan) and schizophrenia can worsen SES (downward drift hypothesis).

D. Residual phase

❌Incorrect

Correct Answer: A.

Why: The prodromal phase involves deterioration, social withdrawal, role impairment, and odd behavior prior to prominent positive symptoms.

  • C: Catatonia describes specific motor abnormalities; it is not the typical named ‘phase’ preceding diagnosis.
  • D: Residual (recovery) follows an active episode, with reduced positive symptoms and lingering deficits.
