Standalone Questions (Q1–Q15)
1. Which meningeal layer is directly connected to the skull?
A. Arachnoid mater
B. Choroid plexus
C. Dura mater
D. Pia mater
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: C.

Why: The dura mater is the outermost meningeal layer and is attached to the inner surface of the skull.

Other choices:

  • A: Arachnoid mater is the middle, weblike layer.
  • B: Choroid plexus produces cerebrospinal fluid; it is not a meningeal layer.
  • D: Pia mater is the delicate inner layer adherent to brain tissue.

B. Dura mater
C. Pituitary stalk
D. Cerebellar cortex
✅Correct
❌Incorrect

Correct Answer: A.

Why: CSF is produced by specialized cells lining the ventricles (via the choroid plexus).

  • D: Cerebellar cortex coordinates movement but does not produce CSF.

Passages (3 passages × 3 questions)
Passage 1: Emergency Neurology and Brainstem Signs

A 62-year-old patient is brought to the emergency department after a sudden collapse. On arrival, the patient has irregular breathing and episodes of bradycardia. The neurological exam shows that the patient is difficult to arouse, and gag and cough reflexes are weak.

CT imaging rules out a large cortical hemorrhage, but the clinician suspects damage near the junction where the brain meets the spinal cord. The clinician explains to trainees that this region contains structures essential for basic survival functions such as breathing, heart rate control, and vital reflexes like vomiting and coughing.

The team also notes that higher cortical functions (language and planning) appear less immediately affected than autonomic stability, suggesting a lesion in a more primitive, lower brain region rather than in the cerebral cortex.

How to reason:

  1. Match symptoms to region: abnormal breathing/heart rate + weak vital reflexes → points to the medulla (hindbrain/brainstem).
  2. Use the hierarchy idea: brainstem controls survival functions; cortex supports higher cognition.
  3. Rule out distractors: cerebellum problems are coordination/balance, not primary respiration/HR failure.
  4. Pick the structure that best explains the mechanism implied by the clinical evidence.

Passage 2: Awake Surgery and Cortical Mapping

During a neurosurgical procedure to remove a tumor near the cortex, the patient is kept awake under local anesthesia. The surgeon applies a small electrode to the exposed cortical surface in short pulses while the patient performs simple tasks and reports subjective experiences.

When the electrode is placed over one region, the patient’s hand briefly moves without voluntary intent. When the electrode is moved to another region, the patient reports seeing brief flashes of light, even though the operating room lighting is unchanged.

The surgeon explains that mapping functional regions helps avoid removing tissue critical for movement or perception. Because the brain tissue itself lacks pain receptors, local anesthesia at the scalp is sufficient for the mapping portion of the procedure.

How to reason:

  1. Translate stimulation → function: hand movement suggests motor cortex; flashes of light suggest visual cortex.
  2. Use the method logic: electrical stimulation causes neurons to fire, revealing the functional map of nearby cortex.
  3. Don’t overreach: this is cortical mapping, not deep nuclei or brainstem reflex testing.
  4. Choose the option that matches the specific effect produced by stimulation.

Passage 3: Choosing a Brain-Mapping Tool for a Study

A research team wants to study brain activity during REM sleep and also wants to identify which brain regions increase activity during a music-listening task. Because the team is working with human participants, they prioritize noninvasive methods whenever possible.

For the sleep study, they need a method that can record broad patterns of neural activity over time and can be used repeatedly without damaging tissue. For the music study, they want a method that can compare activity between brain regions based on physiological signals that correlate with neuronal activation.

The team debates options including placing electrodes on the scalp, injecting a radioactive tracer, measuring blood flow changes, and creating experimental lesions in animals. They also note that some techniques provide better temporal resolution while others provide better spatial localization.

How to reason:

  1. Match question to tool: broad, time-resolved electrical patterns in sleep → EEG.
  2. For regional activation, use blood-flow coupling: increased neural activity → increased regional blood flow (rCBF/fMRI).
  3. Be careful with constraints: “noninvasive” rules out lesioning; “radioactive tracer” suggests PET or rCBF.
  4. Pick the method whose measurement matches the evidence you need (electrical vs blood flow vs structure).
