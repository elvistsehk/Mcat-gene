MCAT Behavioral Sciences — Health Disparities & Healthcare (Ch. 12.2)
Standalone Questions (Q1–Q15)
1. In a town of 10,000 adults, 200 people were newly diagnosed with type 2 diabetes in 2025. At the end of 2025, 1,100 adults in the town were living with type 2 diabetes (including the new cases). Which pair of measures is correctly matched to the data?
A. Incidence = 1,100/10,000 in 2025; Prevalence = 200/10,000 in 2025
B. Incidence = 200/1,100 in 2025; Prevalence = 1,100/200 in 2025
C. Incidence = 200/10,000 in 2025; Prevalence = 1,100/10,000 in 2025
D. Incidence = 200/(10,000−1,100) in 2025; Prevalence = 1,100/(10,000−200) in 2025
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: C.

Why: Incidence counts new cases per population at risk over a time interval; prevalence counts total existing cases (new + preexisting) per total population over that same interval. Here, 200 are new in 2025, and 1,100 total have diabetes by year end.

Other choices:

  • A: This swaps incidence and prevalence: 1,100 is the total burden (prevalence), not new cases.
  • B: These ratios do not use the relevant population denominators and invert the meaning of the measures.
  • D: You are not given the number at risk at the start of 2025, so subtracting cases from the denominator creates an unjustified calculation; MCAT-style interpretation uses the provided total population.
2. A report states: “Community X has a high burden of illness from chronic respiratory disease, but deaths from the disease are relatively uncommon.” Which interpretation best matches this statement?
A. Chronic respiratory disease has high morbidity but relatively low mortality in Community X
B. The disease has high mortality but low morbidity in Community X
C. The statement is about prevalence only; morbidity and mortality cannot be inferred
D. The disease has low incidence because deaths are uncommon
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A.

Why: Morbidity refers to the degree/burden of illness, while mortality refers to deaths caused by the disease. A condition can be disabling and common without being a leading cause of death.

Other choices:

  • B: This reverses the terms: the passage describes lots of illness burden but few deaths.
  • C: The statement explicitly contrasts illness burden (morbidity) with deaths (mortality), not just case counts.
  • D: Low deaths do not imply low incidence; you can have many new cases with effective treatment or nonfatal disease courses.

D. How does a mutation in CFTR alter chloride transport in airway epithelium?
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: C.

Why: Social epidemiology examines how social advantages and disadvantages (e.g., SES, segregation, social capital) relate to population patterns of health and disease.

Other choices:

  • A: This is a cellular/molecular physiology question, not a social determinants-of-health question.
  • B: This focuses on pharmacologic efficacy in a lab setting rather than social patterning of disease.
  • D: This is a genetic/physiologic mechanism question rather than a social-structural question.
4. A public health team observes that as household income rises, average life expectancy rises in roughly stepwise fashion across each income bracket (not just the poorest vs everyone else). This pattern is best described as:
A. A socioeconomic gradient in health and development
B. An example of the ecological fallacy
C. Evidence that achieved status fully determines health outcomes
D. A demonstration of genetic determinism
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A.

Why: A socioeconomic gradient means health outcomes improve proportionally as SES increases—across the whole distribution, not only between extremes.

Other choices:

  • B: Ecological fallacy is drawing individual-level conclusions from group-level data; the description is a population gradient pattern.
  • C: The gradient pattern does not show that merit/achieved status is the only determinant—social and structural factors can drive it.
  • D: A stepwise SES-health relationship is not evidence that genes alone determine health.
5. Howard Waitzkin’s “second sickness” is best captured by which scenario?
A. An infectious outbreak spreads because a new pathogen mutates to become more virulent
B. Despite medical advances, preventable disease worsens in a marginalized group because social injustice limits access to resources and care
C. A patient experiences side effects from a medication that was incorrectly dosed
D. A city’s average life expectancy increases after sanitation infrastructure improves
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: B.

Why: Waitzkin’s “second sickness” describes the exacerbation of health outcomes that results from social injustice—for example, unequal access, discrimination, and poverty producing avoidable morbidity/mortality even when effective medicine exists.

Other choices:

  • A: This is a biological/epidemiologic mechanism (pathogen virulence), not an injustice-driven amplification of outcomes.
  • C: A dosing error is an iatrogenic event, not a population-level injustice mechanism.
  • D: This is an example of socioeconomic improvement improving health, the opposite direction of “second sickness.”
6. A student wants to replicate John Snow’s reasoning during the 1854 cholera outbreak. Which approach most directly mirrors Snow’s key methodological move?

C. Sequence bacterial genomes to show transmission chains between households
D. Survey residents about stress levels and correlate stress with diarrhea
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: B.

Why: Snow used spatial mapping to identify clustering and inferred a common source exposure (a contaminated pump), demonstrating how geography can reveal disease transmission pathways.

Other choices:

  • A: Randomized assignment is not what Snow did; it is often unethical/impossible during outbreaks.
  • C: Genomic sequencing can help today, but it is not the classic spatial-epidemiologic approach Snow used.
  • D: Stress surveys do not test the waterborne exposure hypothesis driving cholera transmission.

Passage 1: Two Neighborhoods, One City

Riverbend is a midsize city with two adjacent neighborhoods: Harborview and East Junction. Harborview has stable housing, low violent crime, and many community organizations that regularly coordinate neighborhood events. East Junction has higher residential turnover, more interpersonal violence, and residents report low trust in local institutions.

In 2025, public health workers tracked new diagnoses of uncontrolled hypertension (HTN) and also counted how many residents were living with HTN by the end of the year. Harborview has 8,000 adults; 160 adults received a new diagnosis of uncontrolled HTN in 2025, and 960 adults were living with uncontrolled HTN by year end. East Junction has 8,000 adults; 240 adults received a new diagnosis in 2025, and 1,040 adults were living with uncontrolled HTN by year end.

A city council member argues that because both neighborhoods have clinics within a 3‑mile radius, the remaining difference must be “mostly personal choices.” A community advocate counters that clinic proximity does not guarantee access, and that chronic stressors and distrust change whether people seek care early or adhere to treatment.

How to reason:

  1. Underline the numbers that represent new cases vs total cases.
  2. Translate those into the formulas: incidence (new/at‑risk/time) and prevalence (total/total pop/time).
  3. Then connect the social details (trust, violence, organizations) to social capital, barriers to care, and the idea of a “second sickness.”
  4. Pick the answer that explains the pattern using both the data and the social context—not just one.
16. Based on the data, which statement is most accurate about uncontrolled HTN in 2025?
A. Harborview has a higher incidence but a lower prevalence than East Junction
B. East Junction has both a higher incidence and a higher prevalence of uncontrolled HTN than Harborview
C. Both neighborhoods have identical incidence because the population sizes are the same
D. East Junction has higher prevalence, but Harborview has higher incidence
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: B.

Why: Incidence compares new diagnoses: 240/8000 > 160/8000. Prevalence compares total cases: 1,040/8000 > 960/8000. Both measures are higher in East Junction for 2025.

Other choices:

  • A: Harborview has fewer new cases and fewer total cases given equal denominators.
  • C: Same denominators do not imply identical incidence; the numerators (new cases) differ.
  • D: This reverses the incidence comparison; East Junction has more new diagnoses.
17. Suppose Riverbend introduces a citywide guideline that standardizes hypertension treatment protocols across clinics, yet East Junction’s mortality from stroke remains higher over the next decade. Which interpretation best reflects Waitzkin’s “second sickness” in this context?
A. The guideline proves that biology is the primary cause of stroke disparities in East Junction
B. Because treatment protocols are standardized, prevalence must fall to zero in both neighborhoods
C. East Junction residents must have higher incidence only because they ignore physician advice, independent of social environment
D. Medical improvements alone did not remove injustice-linked barriers (e.g., distrust, chronic stress, access constraints), so inequities in outcomes persisted or worsened
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: D.

Why: “Second sickness” emphasizes that social injustice can keep outcomes worse for marginalized groups even when effective medicine exists—through mechanisms like unequal access, discrimination, stress exposure, and reduced adherence shaped by structural conditions.

Other choices:

  • A: The pattern is not proof of biology as the primary driver; it is consistent with persistent social barriers.
  • B: Standardization does not eliminate disease; prevalence cannot be assumed to drop to zero.
  • C: This ignores how environment and trust shape help-seeking, adherence, and exposure to risk factors.

Passage 2: Gender, Morbidity, and Help-Seeking

A primary care network reviews 50,000 patient records over five years. Women in the network schedule more annual checkups and receive more preventive services per visit. Men in the same age range are less likely to come in for routine care, but they account for a disproportionate share of emergency visits after accidents.

When the analysts stratify by diagnosis, women show higher rates of reported chronic pain syndromes and inflammatory bowel disease, while men show higher death rates from heart disease and suicide. A subgroup analysis suggests that men who endorse statements like “real men don’t need doctors” are the least likely to follow up after abnormal lab results.

A clinician proposes that increasing clinic hours alone will close the gender gap. Another argues that norms about risk and toughness must be addressed alongside access.

How to reason:

  1. Separate morbidity (burden of illness) from mortality (deaths).
  2. Use the vignette’s behaviors (routine checkups vs accidents, “tough it out”) to infer sociological mechanisms.
  3. Ask: does the answer explain why men die more but women report more illness in many conditions?
  4. Eliminate choices that confuse access (clinic hours) with help‑seeking and adherence norms.

B. The findings show women have lower morbidity because they use more preventive services
C. The findings imply incidence and prevalence are interchangeable measures
D. The findings demonstrate that gender differences disappear once clinics offer longer hours
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A.

Why: Women’s greater service use and higher rates of many nonfatal conditions align with higher morbidity, while men’s higher deaths from major causes and higher accident-related emergencies align with higher mortality. The “tough it out” norm supports a sociological mechanism affecting help-seeking and adherence.

Other choices:

  • B: More preventive services does not mean lower morbidity; the data explicitly reports higher rates of several conditions among women.
  • C: Incidence/prevalence are different measures and are not the focus of these gender findings.
  • D: Longer hours may help access, but the vignette highlights norms and behaviors that also drive the disparity.

Passage 3: Access, Bias, and Continuity of Care

A community health center serves a low-income, racially diverse population. Many patients are covered by Medicaid, and some are uninsured. The center refers patients to nearby specialists, but several specialty practices do not accept Medicaid panels or have long waitlists for new Medicaid patients.

A quality audit finds that patients with obesity are less likely to receive recommended colorectal cancer screening, and chart notes frequently attribute fatigue or shortness of breath to weight without documenting alternative workups. Patients who report feeling dismissed are more likely to switch providers, leading to gaps in records and fragmented follow-up.

The center pilots two changes: (1) a navigation team to schedule referrals and arrange transportation, and (2) clinician training focused on bias reduction and culturally responsive communication. Six months later, the audit shows improved screening rates and fewer missed follow-up appointments.

How to reason:

  1. Identify the barriers: insurance acceptance, waitlists, transportation, trust, and provider bias.
  2. Ask whether the question is about access (can you get care?) vs quality (what happens when you do?).
  3. Use the pilot results to infer what mechanism was operating (navigation → access; training → quality/trust).
  4. Choose the option that explains the observed changes without overclaiming causality.

B. Clinicians documenting symptoms as “due to weight” without full workups
C. Specialty practices’ limited acceptance of Medicaid, creating access bottlenecks despite coverage
D. Individuals switching providers after negative experiences
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: C.

Why: Structural barriers are embedded in institutional arrangements (e.g., insurance acceptance policies, provider distribution, waitlists) that limit access independent of an individual’s motivation.

Other choices:

  • A: Frustration is an interpersonal experience; it may affect utilization but is not itself an institutional access gate.
  • B: This is a quality-of-care/bias issue within clinical encounters, not the access gate described by structural policy.
  • D: Switching providers is a behavioral response to mistrust; it is downstream of access/quality problems.
