Standalone Questions (Q1–Q15)
1. A resident meets a new attending who is curt during introductions. For weeks afterward, the resident interprets the attending’s neutral comments as “rude,” while overlooking moments of kindness. Which concept best explains this selective interpretation after the initial categorization?
A. Recency effect
B. Just-world hypothesis
C. Primacy effect
D. Attribute substitution
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: C.

Why: The primacy effect emphasizes that first impressions disproportionately shape later judgments. After the initial “curt → rude” categorization, the perceiver then selectively notices cues that confirm it and discounts disconfirming cues.

Other choices:

  • A: Recency effect would mean the most recent behavior dominates impressions.
  • B: Just-world involves believing people “deserve” outcomes.
  • D: Attribute substitution is using an easier judgment for a complex one (heuristics), not “first impression locks in.”
2. A supervisor initially thinks an employee is disorganized, but after observing a month of highly efficient work, the supervisor revises the impression and now primarily relies on the most recent evidence. This shift best illustrates:
A. Recency effect
B. Self-serving bias
C. Fundamental attribution error
D. Just-world hypothesis
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A.

Why: The recency effect occurs when the most recent information weighs most heavily in impression formation, overriding earlier impressions.

Other choices:

  • B: Self-serving bias concerns how people explain their own successes/failures.
  • C: Fundamental attribution error is overemphasizing dispositional causes for others’ behavior.
  • D: Just-world is about deserved outcomes and victim blaming.
3. A hiring manager thinks, “She seems warm and friendly, so she must also be highly competent,” despite limited job-relevant evidence. Which bias best describes this inference?
A. Self-serving bias
B. Fundamental attribution error
C. Attribute substitution
D. Halo effect
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: D.

Why: The halo effect occurs when a broad positive impression (warm/friendly) biases more specific judgments (competent) without sufficient evidence.

Other choices:

  • A: Self-serving bias is about explanations for one’s own outcomes.
  • B: Fundamental attribution error concerns dispositional vs situational attributions for behavior.
  • C: Attribute substitution is answering an easier question than the one asked (e.g., “Do I like her?” instead of “Is she competent?”). Here the definition in the text is better captured by halo’s trait spillover.

  • D: Consensus cues compare behavior to others’ behavior, not moral deservingness.

  • D: Out-group bias is harsher judgment of those outside one’s group.

Passage 1: The New Intern

A hospital unit welcomes a new intern, Sara. On her first day, she arrives ten minutes late to rounds and speaks very little during introductions. One resident immediately labels her “unreliable and unfriendly.” Over the next two weeks, that resident notices each time Sara forgets to bring a printout, but rarely notices when Sara stays late to help nurses complete discharge paperwork.

During a later morning, Sara arrives late again. Another resident learns that Sara’s commute includes a bus route with frequent delays and that multiple interns from different neighborhoods have been late on days when that route runs behind schedule.

By contrast, on weekend shifts Sara is consistently early, and she is on time to all afternoon conferences. A month later, Sara gives a well-organized presentation. Some team members still describe her as “probably lucky,” while others revise their overall impression.

How to reason:

  1. Identify impression formation versus attribution: first impressions (primacy) can lock in a category that shapes later attention.
  2. Look for attribution cues embedded in the story: consistency (over time), consensus (others too), distinctiveness (only in certain settings).
  3. When asked about a bias, ask: are they over-blaming disposition (FAE), letting a global vibe spill to specifics (halo), or crediting/deservingness (just-world)?
  4. Choose the answer that best matches the evidence pattern, not a single detail.

Passage 2: The Scholarship Interview

A university committee interviews finalists for a competitive scholarship. Candidate A is impeccably dressed, speaks confidently, and smiles often. Several committee members later say that A is “obviously honest” and “probably a reliable teammate,” even though the interview questions were not designed to test honesty or teamwork directly.

Candidate B arrives after running from a distant bus stop. B’s answers are brief and somewhat disorganized, but B later submits a detailed follow-up document with carefully reasoned plans. Some committee members claim B is “careless,” while others highlight the follow-up as evidence that B may simply have faced situational constraints.

After the final decision, a committee member explains the winner’s success as “pure talent,” but describes the non-winner’s outcome as “a consequence of not working hard enough,” even though the committee never measured effort.

How to reason:

  1. Spot when a global impression (confidence/appearance) drives unrelated trait judgments → halo effect.
  2. For B, weigh situational evidence (running from bus stop) against dispositional labels (“careless”). If observers ignore context, think FAE.
  3. For post-decision explanations, notice whether people are using simple internal stories without evidence (a kind of attribution bias).
  4. Choose answers that best match the type of error described: impression spillover vs dispositional overreach vs heuristic substitution.

Passage 3: The Optics Demo and the “Easy Answer”

In a perception lab, participants view two birds placed in a photograph with strong perspective cues. Many report that the bird “farther away” looks larger, even after being told the birds are identical in size. The instructor explains that people often rely on three-dimensional context to judge two-dimensional size.

Next, participants are shown a shaded checkerboard image. They insist that two squares labeled A and B are different colors, even after the instructor provides a measurement showing the squares reflect the same amount of light.

Finally, participants answer a short word problem: “A pencil and an eraser cost $1.10 together. The pencil costs one dollar more than the eraser. How much does the eraser cost?” Most answer “10 cents” rapidly. After the instructor walks through the math, many realize they substituted an easier problem for the one asked.

How to reason:

  1. For illusions, ask what feature is being judged (size/color) and what context cue might be substituted (3D perspective, shadows).
  2. Connect that to the chapter concept: attribute substitution—using an easier attribute (apparent 3D size, perceived illumination) instead of the true attribute (2D size, actual color).
  3. For the word problem, identify the “fast” intuitive answer and why it’s tempting (it matches the “$1 more” cue) but inconsistent when checked.
  4. Select the answer that explains both the illusion judgments and the word-problem mistake via the same cognitive shortcut.
