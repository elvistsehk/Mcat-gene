Standalone Questions (Q1–Q15)
1. A researcher argues that once an emotion is experienced, the body has biologically prepared expressions (e.g., facial muscle patterns), but that an individual’s interpretation of the situation helps determine which emotion is experienced in the first place. This view is most consistent with the:
A. Social construction model
B. Purely behavioral model with no cognition
C. Appraisal model
D. Claim that emotional expression has no biological component
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: C.

Why: The appraisal model accepts biologically prepared expressions once an emotion occurs, but adds a cognitive antecedent (appraisal/interpretation) that shapes emotional experience and expression.

Other choices:

  • A/D: The social construction model denies a biological basis; the stem explicitly includes biology.
  • B: The stem explicitly includes cognition (interpretation/appraisal), so a no-cognition model does not fit.
2. A student in a new culture learns that showing frustration in public is considered disrespectful and begins suppressing anger during group meetings. These culturally learned expectations are best described as:
A. Display rules
B. Network norms
C. The generalized other
D. Role exit
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A.

Why: Display rules are cultural expectations about which emotions can be expressed and to what degree in a given setting.

Other choices:

  • B: “Network norms” is not the key term here; the stem is about emotion expression rules.
  • C: Generalized other is Mead’s concept for imagining society’s expectations, not specifically cultural emotion rules.
  • D: Role exit is changing identities (dropping one role for another).
3. In Goffman’s dramaturgical perspective, which behavior best illustrates the back-stage self?
A. A cashier smiling and using scripted greetings with customers
B. A student carefully choosing professional clothing before an interview
C. A candidate rehearsing “polished” answers while waiting in the lobby
D. The same candidate relaxing at home and speaking candidly with close friends, unconcerned with image
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: D.

Why: The back stage self appears when one is hidden from the audience and no longer feels pressure to maintain a public performance.

Other choices:

  • A/B/C: These are front-stage behaviors—scripted, audience-facing, and image-managed.
4. A person says, “I’m a premedical student,” primarily to establish how they should be seen by someone they just met. This is the impression management strategy of:
A. Ingratiation
B. Self-disclosure
C. Aligning actions
D. Alter-casting
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: B.

Why: Self-disclosure involves giving information about oneself to establish an identity.

Other choices:

  • A: Ingratiation uses flattery or conforming to win someone over.
  • C: Aligning actions involves making questionable behavior acceptable through excuses/justifications.
  • D: Alter-casting imposes an identity onto someone else.

  • D: That is aligning actions (excuses/justifications for questionable behavior).

9. A person feels furious but deliberately maintains a neutral facial expression and steady voice while speaking to a supervisor. This is best described as emotional management by:

Why: Neutralizing means displaying no emotional expression despite having an internal emotional state.

10. Which statement best distinguishes nonverbal from verbal communication as described in this chapter?

B. Nonverbal communication can occur without words and may be unintentional, whereas verbal communication relies on words (spoken, written, or signed)
C. Nonverbal communication is always conscious, whereas verbal communication is always unconscious
D. Nonverbal communication cannot convey emotion or attitudes
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: B.

Why: Verbal communication uses words (spoken, written, signed). Nonverbal communication conveys information without words (facial expression, posture, tone, eye contact) and can be intentional or unintentional.

  • C: Either type can be conscious or unconscious.
  • D: Nonverbal cues often communicate emotions, attitudes, and personality.

Passages (3 passages × 3 questions)
Passage 1: The Interview “Performance”

Leah is applying for a research internship. The day of the interview, she wears a conservative suit, checks her posture in the mirror, and practices a calm tone of voice even though she feels anxious. In the lobby, she repeats a few “safe” stories that highlight teamwork and perseverance. During the interview, she notices the interviewer casually mentions a famous scientist and Leah quickly says, “I’ve always admired that lab’s work,” although she has only skimmed one of their papers.

After the interview, Leah meets a close friend for coffee and admits she felt overwhelmed and uncertain. She jokes about how “scripted” her answers sounded and says she would never talk that way in her everyday life.

How to reason:

  1. Mark which behaviors are audience-facing (front stage) versus private (back stage).
  2. When you see props/clothing/controlled tone, think managing appearances. When you see flattery/agreement to be liked, think ingratiation.
  3. Map “selves”: authentic (who she is), ideal (who she wants to be), tactical (who she markets herself to be to meet expectations).
  4. Choose answers that combine at least two passage cues (what she did + why/when she did it), not just a single quoted phrase.
16. Leah saying “I’ve always admired that lab’s work” after only skimming one paper is best interpreted as which impression management strategy?
A. Self-disclosure (establishing identity)
B. Aligning actions (excusing questionable behavior)
C. Alter-casting (assigning an identity to the interviewer)
D. Ingratiation (agreeing/flattering to win favor)
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: D.

Why: Leah strategically aligns herself with the interviewer’s interest to be liked and appear compatible—classic ingratiation (flattery/conforming to expectations).

Other choices:

  • A: Self-disclosure is sharing identity facts (e.g., “I’m premed”), not flattering the interviewer.
  • B: Aligning actions is making a questionable act acceptable via excuses (not happening here).
  • C: Alter-casting assigns a role to the other person (e.g., “As a responsible mentor, you…”).

Why: Back stage involves being hidden from the formal audience, allowing more candid behavior that may contradict the public performance.

18. Leah’s “safe” stories and controlled tone most directly reflect which of the three selves used in impression management language?
A. Authentic self
B. Tactical self
C. Ideal self

Passage 2: Emotion Rules Across Contexts

Two medical students, Hana and Miguel, rotate through clinics in two different cultural contexts. In Clinic X, staff strongly discourage public displays of anger; supervisors describe anger as “disruptive” and rarely express it themselves. When Hana feels irritated after a patient repeatedly interrupts, she keeps a neutral expression and speaks softly. Later she admits she “felt angry but tried to show nothing.”

In Clinic Y, staff treat open frustration as acceptable when used to emphasize safety concerns. During rounds, Miguel raises his voice, gestures emphatically, and frowns while arguing that a medication order is unsafe. Afterward, he says he was “more upset than I looked,” because he intentionally toned down his reaction to remain professional.

How to reason:

  1. Identify what the passage is really contrasting: not “who is angry,” but how culture/context shapes expression (display rules).
  2. Classify emotion management: neutralizing (show nothing), deamplifying (show less), amplifying (show more), masking (show a different emotion).
  3. When asked to choose a model, ask whether the passage supports a strong biological universality or emphasizes situational/cultural learning.
  4. Use the passage’s evidence (neutral face, toned-down reaction, norms described by staff) to justify your inference.
19. Which inference best fits the passage’s contrast between Clinic X and Clinic Y?
A. Anger cannot be experienced in Clinic X because it is not displayed publicly
B. Facial expressions of anger are never influenced by cultural expectations
C. Cultural context can shape how emotions are expressed, even when the internal emotion is similar
D. The passage proves that emotions have no internal component
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: C.

Why: Both students report feeling anger/irritation, but they modulate expression differently due to local norms—evidence that culture shapes display (display rules) without denying internal experience.

Other choices:

  • A: The passage explicitly says Hana felt angry but showed nothing.
  • B: The passage suggests the opposite: expression varies by setting.
  • D: The passage does not eliminate internal emotion; it shows regulation of expression.
20. Miguel saying he was “more upset than I looked” because he toned down his reaction is best classified as:
A. Masking anger with happiness
B. Neutralizing emotional expression
C. Amplifying emotional expression
D. Deamplifying emotional expression
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: D.

Why: Deamplifying means showing the emotion less intensely than it is felt internally (“more upset than I looked”).

Other choices:

  • A: Masking would show a different emotion (e.g., smiling).
  • B: Neutralizing would show no emotion at all, which fits Hana more than Miguel.
  • C: Amplifying would make the expression stronger than the internal emotion.

Passage 3: Signals Without Words

A behavioral ecology team observes communication in multiple species. They record a colony of bees and note that returning foragers perform patterned movements near the hive entrance. The researchers also study prairie dogs and find that alarm calls differ depending on whether a hawk, coyote, or unfamiliar object is nearby. In a nearby wetland, male birds display brightly colored plumage during mating season and perform short dances toward females.

To compare chemical signaling, the team documents that skunks release a strong odor when threatened and that ants follow scent trails to food sources. Finally, in a lab setting, a primate uses a standardized set of hand signs to request food and name objects while interacting with a trainer.

How to reason:

  1. Classify each signal type: movement/visual display (dance, plumage), scent (odor trails, pheromones), vocalization (alarm calls).
  2. For the primate using standardized hand signs, ask whether the system functions like words with agreed meaning → verbal classification in this chapter.
  3. When asked “which is nonverbal,” remember: verbal = words (spoken/written/signed); nonverbal = posture, facial expression, prosody, scent, etc.
  4. Prefer answers that map the passage evidence to the correct communication category.
