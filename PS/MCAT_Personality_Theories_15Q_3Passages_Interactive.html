Standalone Questions (Q1–Q15)
1. A researcher argues that an employee’s consistent tendency to feel tense during deadlines reflects a stable disposition that can be summarized along a few broad dimensions. Which perspective is this researcher most directly using?
A. Behaviorist perspective
B. Trait perspective
C. Humanistic perspective
D. Psychoanalytic perspective
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: B.

Why: Trait theorists describe personality as relatively enduring patterns of behavior and emotion that can be summarized with broad dimensions (e.g., PEN or the Big Five).

Other choices:

  • A: Behaviorists emphasize learned behaviors shaped by reinforcement, not stable trait dimensions.
  • C: Humanistic approaches emphasize conscious experience, growth, and self-realization rather than factor-derived trait dimensions.
  • D: Psychoanalytic theories focus on unconscious drives/conflicts (id/ego/superego), not trait dimensions.
2. A student feels an urge to yell at a classmate ("I want to snap right now"), but instead stays quiet because she knows it would damage her reputation and could lead to consequences. Which structure is primarily enforcing the delay of gratification?
A. Id
B. Superego
C. Conscience
D. Ego
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: D.

Why: The ego operates on the reality principle: it delays immediate gratification from the id to match objective constraints and consequences.

Other choices:

  • A: The id seeks immediate gratification (pleasure principle) and would drive the urge to yell.
  • B: The superego is the perfectionist moral judge; it can create guilt/pride but is not the main "reality-testing" mediator.
  • C: The conscience is a component of the superego (punished behaviors), not the main mediator with external reality.
3. After a traumatic car accident, a patient reports having no memory of the crash details, despite having been awake at the scene. Which defense mechanism best explains this pattern?
A. Repression
B. Suppression
C. Reaction formation
D. Rationalization
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A.

Why: Repression is an unconscious process that removes distressing thoughts or memories from conscious awareness, often described after trauma.

Other choices:

  • B: Suppression is <b>conscious</b> choosing not to think about something ("I’m not going to think about that now").
  • C: Reaction formation converts an impulse into its opposite (e.g., acting overly kind toward someone you dislike).
  • D: Rationalization is giving socially acceptable reasons for behavior rather than the true motive.
4. A resident who feels insecure about her clinical skills becomes convinced that the attending "doesn’t like me" and "wants me to fail," despite neutral feedback. Which defense mechanism is most consistent with this description?
A. Displacement
B. Sublimation
C. Projection
D. Regression
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: C.

Why: Projection attributes one’s own unacceptable feelings (e.g., insecurity/hostility) to someone else ("They dislike me").

Other choices:

  • A: Displacement redirects emotion to a safer target (e.g., angry at attending → snaps at roommate).
  • B: Sublimation channels unacceptable urges into socially acceptable behavior (e.g., aggression → competitive sports).
  • D: Regression is reverting to earlier developmental behaviors under stress (e.g., tantrums, clinginess).
5. In Jung’s framework, the set of qualities a person displays at a job interview—polished, agreeable, and carefully controlled—would be best described as the:
A. Persona
B. Shadow
C. Anima/animus
D. Collective unconscious
✅Correct
❌Incorrect

Correct Answer: A.

Why: Jung’s persona is the "mask" presented to the public—an adaptive social face that emphasizes socially valued traits.

Other choices:

  • B: The shadow contains socially unacceptable or repressed thoughts/impulses.
  • C: Anima/animus refers to gender-atypical qualities (as Jung framed them), not the public role-mask.
  • D: The collective unconscious is a shared reservoir of archetypes; it is not the outward performance in a specific situation.
6. A therapist avoids giving direct advice and instead reflects a client’s feelings, aiming to help the client reconcile a gap between the real self and ideal self. This approach is most consistent with:

B. Behaviorist therapy emphasizing token economies
C. Trait theory emphasizing OCEAN scoring

Why: Rogers’s person-centered (nondirective) therapy uses empathy and unconditional positive regard to reduce incongruence between real and ideal selves.

Other choices:

  • A: Freudian therapy emphasizes unconscious conflict and techniques like free association/interpretation.
  • B: Behaviorist approaches target observable behaviors via reinforcement schedules and skill training.
  • C: Trait theory describes personality dimensions but is not a therapeutic method focused on congruence.
7. Maslow suggested that some self-actualized individuals have rare, deeply meaningful episodes that can produce lasting shifts in perspective and well-being. These episodes are called:
A. Functional autonomies
B. Peak experiences
C. Primary processes

Why: Maslow used the term peak experiences for profound, transformative moments often reported by self-actualizers.

Correct Answer: C.

Why: Lewin emphasized the person’s current field: opposing forces in the present that either facilitate or hinder goal pursuit.

Other choices:

  • A: Archetypes and the collective unconscious are Jungian concepts.
  • B: Fixed trait levels describe trait perspectives, not Lewin’s situational "field" focus.
  • D: Reinforcement history is central to behaviorism but Lewin is not limited to reinforcement contingencies.

A. Secondary trait
B. Cardinal trait

Passage 1: Perfectionism, Guilt, and Therapy Style

Elena, a 24-year-old research assistant, is described by her peers as relentlessly productive. When she takes a day off, she reports a surge of guilt and a harsh inner voice insisting she is "lazy" and "worthless." At work, Elena repeatedly tells coworkers she "can’t stand" a new colleague, yet she checks that colleague’s messages first and feels unusually nervous before meetings with them. After receiving mild feedback from her supervisor, Elena spends hours replaying the conversation and then stays late to "make up for" any perceived mistake. In therapy, Dr. Patel does not give direct advice; instead, she reflects Elena’s feelings, validates her experience, and emphasizes acceptance while Elena explores why perfection feels necessary. Elena mentions vivid daydreams in which she is praised for flawless performance, and she notes that imagining this praise briefly relieves her tension, but the guilt returns when she looks at her actual schedule.

How to reason:

  1. Identify which theory the question is pointing to (Freud’s structure/defenses vs humanistic therapy).
  2. For defense mechanisms, map behavior to the pattern: opposite expression (reaction formation), attributing to others (projection), redirecting target (displacement), etc.
  3. For Freud’s structure, link emotions: guilt → superego, reality-testing/delay → ego, immediate urges → id.
  4. For therapy style, look for key cues: nondirective reflection + acceptance → Rogers (person-centered).
  5. Choose the answer that requires an inference from behaviors, not a direct quote.

Passage 2: Personality at Work—Traits, Types, and Context

A consulting firm evaluates employees using multiple personality frameworks to predict long-term well-being and performance. One team member, Marcus, is described as calm during tense client calls, rarely anxious, and quick to recover after criticism. Another employee, Dana, is intensely time-urgent, becomes irritated when meetings run long, and treats even minor delays as personal failures. During an annual review period, the firm also administers a PEN-based questionnaire and finds that a subset of employees shows strong physiological arousal and emotional reactivity when discussing mistakes, even when the consequences are minimal. The firm debates whether Dana’s style is best viewed as a stable personality pattern or as a product of the firm’s competitive culture, which publicly ranks employees each week. Notably, when rankings are temporarily removed, Dana’s irritability decreases somewhat but does not disappear.

How to reason:

  1. Translate descriptions into trait labels: calm/resilient → low neuroticism; time-urgent/competitive → Type A pattern.
  2. If a question references PEN, pick among P (deviance), E (social stimulation), N (stress reactivity).
  3. If a question references Big Five, map to OCEAN; emotional stability is the opposite of neuroticism.
  4. For type theory, recall: Type A → competitive/time-urgent → higher heart disease risk.
  5. When culture changes reduce—but do not erase—behavior, infer an interaction between disposition and situation rather than a single cause.

Passage 3: Behavior Change in a Clinic Program

An inpatient clinic designs a program to improve daily functioning in adolescents with school avoidance. Staff award points for specific behaviors (arriving on time, completing assignments, participating in group sessions), and points can be exchanged for privileges such as extra recreation time. At first, Jordan participates only when prompted, but after earning privileges, Jordan begins initiating tasks earlier in the day. Jordan also starts choosing quieter rooms to study in, reporting that this setting helps concentration; over time, Jordan studies more and earns more points, which further reinforces the choice of environment. Another patient, Sam, watches older peers receive praise for calm conflict resolution and begins imitating their phrasing during disagreements. When Sam has a setback, Sam says, "Nothing I do matters—my mood is controlled by brain chemistry and bad luck," and disengages from the program for a day.

How to reason:

  1. If a system uses points exchanged for rewards, identify token economy → operant conditioning → behaviorism.
  2. To test reciprocal determinism, look for a feedback loop: person chooses environment → thoughts/feelings change → behavior changes → environment selection increases.
  3. Observational learning cues: imitation of models, especially socially rewarded behavior (Bandura).
  4. For locus of control, listen for attribution language: "nothing I do matters"/luck → external locus vs effort/strategy → internal locus.
  5. Pick answers that use the passage evidence to make a prediction or classify a mechanism, not merely restate a sentence.
