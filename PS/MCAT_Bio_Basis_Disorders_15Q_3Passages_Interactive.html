Standalone Questions (Q1–Q15)
1. An antipsychotic medication reduces hallucinations in a patient with schizophrenia by blocking dopamine receptors. Which change is the most direct therapeutic mechanism for reducing the patient’s positive symptoms?
A. Increased acetylcholine release in the nucleus basalis
B. Increased dopaminergic signaling in the nigrostriatal pathway
C. Decreased dopaminergic signaling in the mesolimbic pathway
D. Decreased serotonin reuptake in the raphe nuclei
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: C.

Why: Schizophrenia is strongly associated with excess dopamine signaling, and many antipsychotics (neuroleptics) work primarily by antagonizing dopamine receptors. Reducing dopamine activity in the mesolimbic pathway is most closely linked to improvement in positive symptoms (hallucinations, delusions).

Other choices: B: ACh loss is more classically tied to Alzheimer’s disease. C: Increasing nigrostriatal dopamine would counter Parkinson-like side effects but isn’t the antipsychotic mechanism for positive symptoms. D: Decreasing serotonin reuptake is an SSRI mechanism used for depression/anxiety, not the primary dopamine-blocking antipsychotic action.

Passage 1: Passage 1: Dopamine Pathways, Psychosis, and Parkinsonism

A neurology–psychiatry consult service follows two patients receiving medications that alter dopamine signaling. Patient X was admitted for new-onset persecutory delusions, auditory hallucinations, and disorganized speech. The team starts Drug D, a medication that blocks dopamine receptors, and within a week Patient X’s hallucinations become less frequent.

Patient Y has a several-year history of resting tremor, bradykinesia, and cogwheel rigidity. Imaging suggests degeneration of dopaminergic neurons that normally project to motor circuits. The team starts Drug L, a dopamine precursor that can be converted to dopamine in the brain. Patient Y’s gait improves, but after dose escalation the patient begins reporting vivid visual hallucinations.

A resident proposes using Drug D to treat Patient Y’s hallucinations. Another resident worries that this may worsen Patient Y’s motor function. The attending asks the team to justify which symptoms would be expected to improve or worsen based on dopamine’s role in different brain pathways.

How to reason:

  1. Identify the core dopamine problem in each condition: schizophrenia is linked to dopamine excess; Parkinson’s to dopamine deficit (substantia nigra).
  2. Map symptom type to pathway: positive psychotic symptoms improve with dopamine blockade; Parkinson motor symptoms worsen with dopamine blockade and improve with dopamine replacement.
  3. Use medication logic: L-DOPA can overshoot and cause psychosis; antipsychotics can cause parkinsonism by blocking dopamine in motor pathways.

Passage 2: Passage 2: Alzheimer Genetics—Chromosomes 14, 19, and 21

The counselor notes that one gene implicated in Alzheimer’s disease is located on chromosome 21, and that gene dosage changes can influence disease risk.

  3. Use the Down syndrome clue: trisomy 21 increases APP gene dosage, linking to Alzheimer risk.

Passage 3: Passage 3: Depression Biomarkers, Brain Circuits, and Diagnostic Shifts

A research team recruits adults with a history of recurrent major depressive episodes and matched controls. Participants undergo functional neuroimaging while viewing emotionally negative images, and blood samples are collected at several time points across the day.

Compared with controls, the depressed group shows higher glucose metabolism in the amygdala during the task. Their bloodwork reveals persistently elevated cortisol levels across the day. Structural MRI additionally shows smaller hippocampal volume in participants who report the longest cumulative duration of depressive symptoms.

After 8 weeks of treatment with a medication that increases synaptic serotonin, many participants report improved mood. However, a subset develops abnormally elevated energy, decreased need for sleep, and increased goal-directed activity, prompting clinicians to reconsider the initial diagnosis.

How to reason:

  1. Link each brain region to the marker described: amygdala ↔ emotional salience/negative affect; hippocampus ↔ memory and stress-related atrophy over time.
  2. Connect elevated cortisol to HPA-axis activation and chronic stress physiology in depression.
  3. Use treatment response: SSRIs raise serotonin; emergence of mania-like symptoms suggests an underlying bipolar spectrum rather than unipolar depression.
