Standalone Questions (Q1–Q15)
1. Which statement best captures the sociological meaning of a status?
A. A set of behaviors an individual is expected to perform in a given situation
B. A perceived position in society used to classify an individual relative to others
C. A pattern of social relationships mapped by nodes and ties
D. A complex secondary group designed to accomplish specific goals
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: B.

Why: A status is a socially recognized position (e.g., student, physician) that gains meaning in relation to other statuses.

Other choices:

  • A: This describes a role (expected behaviors tied to a status).
  • C: This is a network definition.
  • D: This describes an organization (often a complex secondary group).
2. A person becomes a licensed physician after years of training and exams. This is best categorized as which type of status?
A. Ascribed status
B. Master status
C. Status set
D. Achieved status
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: D.

Why: Achieved statuses are gained through effort and choices (training, exams, career path).

Other choices:

  • A: Ascribed statuses are involuntary (often at birth).
  • B: Master status is the status most central to identity/perception; “physician” can become a master status, but the question asks about how it is obtained.
  • C: Status set is the collection of statuses a person holds, not a type of status itself.

C. Network redundancy in overlapping ties
D. Interaction process analysis (SYMLOG)

❌Incorrect

Correct Answer: A.

Why: A master status is the status by which someone is most identified, and it can lead to pigeonholing—viewing the person only through that lens.

Other choices:

  • B: Role strain is difficulty meeting multiple demands of one role (not being reduced to one identity).
  • C: Network redundancy is repeated/overlapping connections, not identity reduction.
  • D: SYMLOG is a framework to classify interactions (dominance, friendliness, expressiveness).

Other choices:

  • A: Role set is the collection of roles tied to one status (e.g., “doctor” includes roles with patients, nurses, admin).
  • B: Master status is the single status most central to identity/perception.

Passages (3 passages × 3 questions)
Passage 1: Identity in the Clinic

Dr. Rivera is in the first year of residency at a large teaching hospital. In addition to being a physician-in-training, Rivera is also the first person in their family to finish college, and many relatives frequently introduce Rivera at gatherings as “our doctor.” Rivera notices that even distant acquaintances ask for medical advice at weddings and family parties, and some people seem surprised when Rivera mentions hobbies unrelated to medicine.

At work, Rivera’s day includes rounding with attending physicians, coordinating with nurses and pharmacists, answering pages from patients’ families, and supervising medical students. Rivera feels pulled in different directions: students want detailed teaching, while the attending expects speed and efficiency. Rivera also feels pressure when a family member calls during a shift asking for help scheduling a specialist appointment.

How to reason:

  1. Separate status (position) from role (expected behaviors). Then identify which status dominates how others see the person → master status.
  2. When one status comes with multiple interaction partners, list them and label this as a role set (roles linked to one status).
  3. For stress, ask: are demands coming from one role (strain) or two roles (conflict)? Use passage clues to justify your choice.
  4. Pick answers that integrate multiple details (who expects what, where, and why), not ones that simply quote a line.

D. Groupthink within a primary group
✅Correct

Why: The passage shows Rivera being most identified as “the doctor,” across contexts, with others discounting other traits—classic master status effects and pigeonholing.

  • C: Networks are not described; this is about identity perception.
  • D: No decision-making consensus failure is described.

Passage 2: The Student Council Vote

A student council committee of eight members must decide whether to spend most of its annual budget on a single large concert or distribute funds across smaller events. Two popular members strongly advocate for the concert and repeatedly say that “a big splash is what students want.” During meetings, several quieter members avoid offering alternative ideas, even though they privately worry that weather or low turnout could waste the budget. After the vote, a few members admit they “didn’t want to be the only one pushing back.”

In the following weeks, the committee works efficiently by assigning each person a narrow task (ticketing, promotion, security coordination). Members follow a checklist and avoid deviating from the plan. When a new member suggests surveying students to reassess interest, the group dismisses the idea as “too slow” and insists on sticking to the original schedule.

How to reason:

  1. Identify why dissent disappears: fear of social cost → conformity; agreement-at-all-costs + poor evaluation → groupthink.
  2. Look for passage evidence of self-censorship, ignored risks, and limited alternatives to support groupthink.
  3. When the passage describes standardized checklists and narrow tasks, consider whether it resembles bureaucratic/efficiency-oriented organization patterns.
  4. Choose answers that require you to connect multiple clues (popular leaders + silence + post-vote admissions), not just restate one sentence.

Passage 3: Scaling a Community Clinic

A community clinic that began as a small neighborhood initiative grows rapidly after receiving a large grant. To handle increased demand, leadership creates formal job titles, a rigid chain of command, and detailed rules for how staff should schedule patients and document visits. New employees are hired based on credentials and training, and promotions require passing internal competency exams. Over time, staff complain that even small changes require multiple approvals and that “the system feels slower than when we were small.”

Although the clinic initially advertised itself as egalitarian, a small group of long-tenured administrators gradually gains control over key decisions (budget, staffing, and policy). The clinic also introduces standardized “quick-visit” templates that prioritize measurable counts (number of visits per hour) and consistent scripting for patient education. Some clinicians worry that the new approach reduces flexibility and makes patient encounters feel more like a predictable routine.

How to reason:

  1. Match passage features to definitions: credentials + fixed rules + hierarchy + exams → bureaucracy.
  2. When a small leadership group increasingly controls decisions, think iron law of oligarchy (power centralizes).
  3. When the system emphasizes efficiency, predictability, calculability, and control (e.g., scripts, quotas), label it McDonaldization.
  4. Answer choices should be supported by multiple passage clues (not just “it’s a clinic”).
