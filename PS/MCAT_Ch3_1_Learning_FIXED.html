🧠 MCAT Practice — Learning (Chapter 3.1) (15Q + 3 Passages × 3Q)
Standalone Questions (15)
1. A student startles strongly the first time they hear a cadaver saw, but after several lab sessions the sound barely affects them. Which learning-related process best describes this change?
A Habituation
B Sensitization
C Spontaneous recovery
D Avoidance learning
✅ Correct
❌ Incorrect
Show explanation

Habituation is a decreased response after repeated exposure to the same stimulus. In the passage you provided, repeated exposure to an intense stimulus (e.g., cadaver lab sights/sounds) leads to a reduced reaction over time—this is the definition of habituation.

Why the others are wrong: Sensitization is the opposite pattern (responses grow stronger with repeated exposure). Spontaneous recovery is the reappearance of an extinguished conditioned response after time. Avoidance learning is an operant conditioning phenomenon (a behavior performed to prevent an unpleasant stimulus).

2. A stimulus is presented at such a low intensity that it fails to elicit any detectable response from the organism. In the terminology of the passage, this stimulus is best described as:
A a conditioned stimulus
B a subthreshold stimulus
C an unconditioned stimulus
D a discriminative stimulus
✅ Correct
❌ Incorrect
Show explanation

The text explicitly notes that a stimulus “too weak to elicit a response” is a subthreshold stimulus. This is before we even get to conditioning labels like CS/UCS.

Why the others are wrong: A CS is a previously neutral stimulus that now triggers a learned response; a UCS naturally triggers a reflex. A discriminative stimulus signals that reinforcement is available in operant conditioning.

3. During a long highway drive, a driver becomes less aware of the engine noise (habituation). After taking an exit ramp and then merging back onto a similar highway, the engine noise suddenly feels noticeable again. This renewed response to the original engine noise is best termed:
A Generalization
B Dishabituation
C Extinction
D Negative reinforcement
✅ Correct
❌ Incorrect
Show explanation

Dishabituation is the recovery of a response to the original stimulus after habituation, often triggered by introducing a second, different stimulus. The exit ramp changes sensory input and interrupts habituation, restoring attention to the original highway stimuli.

Why the others are wrong: Generalization is responding similarly to similar conditioned stimuli; extinction is loss of a conditioned response when CS is presented without UCS; negative reinforcement is operant (removing an unpleasant stimulus increases a behavior).

4. In a Pavlovian setup, a tone is paired repeatedly with an air puff to the eye, which reflexively causes blinking. Eventually, the tone alone elicits blinking. In this paradigm, the air puff is the:
A Conditioned stimulus (CS)
B Conditioned response (CR)
C Unconditioned stimulus (UCS)
D Neutral stimulus (NS)
✅ Correct
❌ Incorrect
Show explanation

An unconditioned stimulus (UCS) is any stimulus that naturally/innately produces a reflexive response. An air puff naturally causes blinking (the UCR) without learning. The tone begins as a neutral stimulus and becomes the CS after pairing.

Key MCAT rule from the passage: identify the stimulus that triggers the response—UCS → UCR, CS → CR.

5. Pavlov repeatedly rings a bell immediately before presenting food. Over trials, the bell changes from neutral to capable of eliciting salivation on its own. The process of turning a neutral stimulus into a conditioned stimulus by pairing it with a UCS is called:
A Extinction
B Acquisition
C Shaping
D Latent learning
✅ Correct
❌ Incorrect
Show explanation

Acquisition is the learning phase in classical conditioning where the neutral stimulus is repeatedly paired with the UCS, and the association is formed so that the neutral stimulus becomes a CS.

Why the others are wrong: Extinction is loss of CR when CS is presented without UCS. Shaping is operant (rewarding successive approximations). Latent learning is learning without reinforcement, expressed later when reinforcement appears.

6. After conditioning, a dog stops salivating to the bell when the bell is presented many times without food. Two days later, the bell briefly elicits salivation again before fading. Which pairing of concepts best matches these observations?
A Generalization then discrimination
B Extinction then spontaneous recovery
C Sensitization then habituation
D Avoidance learning then escape learning
✅ Correct
❌ Incorrect
Show explanation

Presenting the CS (bell) repeatedly without the UCS (food) produces extinction—the conditioned response weakens. Later, the reappearance of a weak CR after time is spontaneous recovery.

MCAT trap: extinction doesn’t necessarily erase the association; spontaneous recovery shows the learning can persist even when performance disappears temporarily.

7. A child is conditioned to fear a specific white rat paired with a loud noise. Later, the child shows fear toward a white rabbit and a man with a white beard. This illustrates:
A Stimulus discrimination
B Generalization
C Negative reinforcement
D Operant conditioning
✅ Correct
❌ Incorrect
Show explanation

Generalization is a “broadening effect” in which stimuli sufficiently similar to the CS also evoke the CR. The original conditioned fear spreads from the white rat to other similar white, furry/white stimuli.

Why A is wrong: discrimination is the opposite—learning to respond to one stimulus but not to similar ones. C and D are operant concepts, not classical stimulus–stimulus pairing.

8. A driver hears an annoying seat-belt alarm that stops only after buckling up. Over time, the driver starts buckling before the car moves to prevent the alarm. The initial learning (buckling to stop the alarm once it starts) is best classified as:
A Positive punishment
B Escape learning via negative reinforcement
C Avoidance learning via positive reinforcement
D Extinction
✅ Correct
❌ Incorrect
Show explanation

The alarm is an unpleasant stimulus. Buckling removes it, which increases the likelihood of buckling in the future—this is negative reinforcement (removal of something unpleasant strengthens behavior). When the unpleasant stimulus has already begun and the behavior stops it, that is escape learning.

Why others are wrong: Positive punishment would be adding something unpleasant to reduce buckling (opposite direction). Avoidance learning is when the driver buckles before the alarm occurs (later stage). Extinction pertains to weakening of a conditioned response.

9. Which scenario best exemplifies <i>negative punishment</i> rather than negative reinforcement?
A A child loses phone privileges after breaking curfew, and curfew-breaking decreases
B A person takes aspirin and is more likely to take it again when they have a headache
C A rat receives a food pellet after pressing a lever, increasing lever pressing
D A driver buckles a seat belt to stop an alarm, increasing buckling
✅ Correct
❌ Incorrect
Show explanation

Negative punishment means removing a desirable stimulus to decrease a behavior. Taking away phone privileges removes something the child likes, reducing curfew-breaking.

Why the others are reinforcement: Aspirin removes pain (negative reinforcement). Food pellet adds something pleasant (positive reinforcement). Buckling removes an unpleasant alarm (negative reinforcement).

10. A dog trainer pairs a clicker sound with food until the clicker itself increases the dog’s trick performance. In operant conditioning terms, the clicker is best described as a:
A Primary reinforcer
B Conditioned (secondary) reinforcer
C Unconditioned stimulus
D Neutral stimulus that remains neutral
✅ Correct
❌ Incorrect
Show explanation

The dog responds to food naturally, making food a primary reinforcer. The clicker becomes reinforcing only after it is associated with food via classical conditioning, so it becomes a conditioned (secondary) reinforcer.

Why A is wrong: primary reinforcers have innate value (food, water). UCS/NS labels are classical-conditioning stimulus labels, but the question asks for operant terminology.

11. A student receives extra credit immediately after answering questions correctly in class and begins volunteering answers more frequently. This consequence is best classified as:
A Positive reinforcement
B Negative reinforcement
C Positive punishment
D Negative punishment
✅ Correct
❌ Incorrect
Show explanation

Behavior (volunteering answers) increases because a pleasant stimulus (extra credit) is added. Adding a positive consequence that increases a behavior is positive reinforcement.

Quick MCAT grid: Reinforcement increases behavior; punishment decreases behavior. Positive adds a stimulus; negative removes a stimulus.

12. Which reinforcement schedule typically produces the <b>fastest response rate</b> and the <b>greatest resistance to extinction</b>?
A Fixed-interval (FI)
B Fixed-ratio (FR)
C Variable-interval (VI)
D Variable-ratio (VR)
✅ Correct
❌ Incorrect
Show explanation

The passage emphasizes that variable-ratio (VR) schedules (e.g., gambling/slot machines) create very rapid responding because each response might be the winning one, and they are the most resistant to extinction.

Why the others are less effective: Interval schedules limit reinforcement by time, often lowering response rate. Fixed schedules produce more predictable patterns and typically show post-reinforcement pauses.

13. A lab rat receives one food pellet the <i>first time</i> it presses a lever after 60 seconds have passed since the previous pellet. Presses during the 60 seconds have no effect. This schedule is:
A Fixed-interval (FI)
B Variable-interval (VI)
C Fixed-ratio (FR)
D Variable-ratio (VR)
✅ Correct
❌ Incorrect
Show explanation

This is classic fixed-interval: reinforcement becomes available after a fixed time period, and the first response after that interval is reinforced. The 60-second window resets after reinforcement.

Contrast: FR/VR depend on number of responses; VI varies the time requirement; VR varies the number of responses required.

14. A trainer wants a bird to peck a keyboard key. First, the bird gets food for stepping toward the keyboard, then only for standing near it, then only for touching the key, and finally only for pecking it. This procedure is best described as:
A Latent learning
B Stimulus generalization
C Shaping
D Extinction
✅ Correct
❌ Incorrect
Show explanation

Shaping rewards successive approximations to a desired behavior—reinforcement criteria become increasingly specific as the animal gets closer to the target response.

Why others are wrong: latent learning is learning without reward shown later; generalization is responding similarly to similar stimuli; extinction is loss of a conditioned response when reinforcement/UCS is removed.

15. Raccoons are trained to drop coins into a piggy bank, but after initial success they begin rubbing the coins together and repeatedly dipping them into the bank instead of depositing them. This failure is most consistent with:
A Preparedness (biological predisposition)
B Instinctive drift (reversion to innate behavior)
C Spontaneous recovery
D Dishabituation
✅ Correct
❌ Incorrect
Show explanation

The passage describes instinctive drift as reverting to an instinctive behavior after learning a similar behavior—especially when the trained behavior conflicts with the animal’s natural tendencies. The raccoon returns to its food-washing/rubbing instinct.

Why A is tempting but wrong: preparedness is being predisposed to learn certain behaviors more easily; drift is the re-emergence of instinct that disrupts conditioning after learning initially seemed to work.

Passage-Based Questions (3 passages × 3)
Passage 1: Eyeblink conditioning

How to reason (Classical conditioning)

  1. Label what is reflexive. Identify the stimulus that naturally triggers a response (that’s the UCS) and the reflex itself (the UCR).
  2. Track what changes with learning. A previously neutral cue becomes the CS once pairing causes the cue to elicit a learned response (CR).
  3. Use time-course clues. Decline in CR with CS-alone presentations → extinction. Reappearance after a delay → spontaneous recovery.
  4. Check for spread vs precision. Responding to similar cues → generalization. Responding only to the trained cue after differential training → discrimination.

A researcher studies classical conditioning of a startle-like eyeblink in adults. Participants wear a headset that can deliver a brief air puff to the eye and can also play tones of different pitches. At baseline, the air puff reliably produces an eyeblink, while tones alone do not.

During training, a 1000-Hz tone is played for 1 second immediately before each air puff. After 20 pairings, many participants begin blinking during the tone, even when the air puff is withheld on occasional probe trials.

Next, the researcher repeatedly presents the 1000-Hz tone without any air puff. Eyeblinks during the tone gradually decline. The following week, participants return and hear the 1000-Hz tone again; a weak tone-evoked eyeblink reappears in several participants.

Finally, the researcher tests tones at 900 Hz, 1000 Hz, and 1100 Hz. Participants blink most strongly to 1000 Hz, but some blinking occurs to nearby pitches as well.

16. In the training phase, which pairing correctly identifies the tone and the air puff?
A Tone = UCS; Air puff = CS
B Tone = UCR; Air puff = UCS
C Tone = CS; Air puff = NS
D Tone = CS; Air puff = UCS
✅ Correct
❌ Incorrect
Show explanation

The air puff naturally triggers eyeblinking, so it is the UCS. The tone starts neutral but, after pairing, comes to elicit blinking on its own, making it the CS. Thus: tone = CS, air puff = UCS.

Why C is wrong: once conditioning occurs, the air puff cannot be a neutral stimulus because it already elicits a reflexive response.

17. The decline in tone-evoked blinking when the tone is presented repeatedly without the air puff is best explained as evidence of:
A Extinction due to CS-alone presentations
B Generalization to new stimuli
C Avoidance learning
D Positive punishment
✅ Correct
❌ Incorrect
Show explanation

Extinction occurs when the CS is repeatedly presented without the UCS, weakening the conditioned response. Here, tone-alone trials reduce tone-evoked blinking over time.

Why the others are wrong: generalization is spread to similar stimuli; avoidance learning and punishment are operant concepts involving consequences of voluntary behavior.

18. The weak reappearance of tone-evoked blinking one week later (after extinction training) most directly supports which conclusion?
A The UCS has become a conditioned reinforcer
B The original association was not necessarily erased by extinction
C Participants developed stimulus discrimination during training
D Tone-evoked blinking was a subthreshold response
✅ Correct
❌ Incorrect
Show explanation

Spontaneous recovery—reappearance of a weak conditioned response after time—suggests that extinction reduces performance of the CR but may not completely erase the underlying CS–UCS association. The memory can re-emerge after a delay.

Why C is wrong: some blinking to 900/1100 Hz indicates generalization, not strong discrimination.

Passage 2: Consequences and schedules

How to reason (Operant conditioning)

  1. Decide: reinforcement or punishment? Ask whether the consequence makes the behavior more frequent (reinforcement) or less frequent (punishment).
  2. Decide: positive or negative? Positive = adding a stimulus; negative = removing a stimulus. Combine with step 1 (e.g., remove unpleasant + behavior increases = negative reinforcement).
  3. Classify schedules by what varies. Ratio = based on number of responses; interval = based on time. Fixed = predictable; variable = unpredictable.
  4. Predict extinction. Variable-ratio schedules typically persist the longest when reinforcement stops.

A clinic tests two behavioral interventions to increase medication adherence. In Program A, a smartwatch emits a loud buzz every 10 minutes until the patient logs that they took the pill; once logged, the buzzing stops. In Program B, patients earn a raffle entry each day they take the pill; a weekly drawing awards gift cards.

In a separate animal study, rats press a lever for sugar water. Group 1 receives sugar water every 5 presses. Group 2 receives sugar water after a changing number of presses (e.g., 2, then 8, then 4, then 6), with a similar average payoff.

After training, the researchers stop delivering sugar water entirely. Group 2 continues pressing longer before giving up.

Finally, the researchers add a mild air puff to the cage whenever a rat presses the lever. Lever pressing drops sharply.

19. Program A (buzzing stops only after logging the pill) most directly uses which learning principle to increase adherence?
A Positive punishment
B Negative reinforcement
C Negative punishment
D Stimulus discrimination
✅ Correct
❌ Incorrect
Show explanation

Logging the pill-taking behavior removes an unpleasant stimulus (the loud buzz), which increases the likelihood of logging/taking pills in the future. That is negative reinforcement.

Why A is wrong: positive punishment would add something unpleasant to reduce a behavior; here the behavior is strengthened.

20. In the rat study, Group 2’s reinforcement schedule is best classified as:
A Fixed-ratio
B Fixed-interval
C Variable-ratio
D Variable-interval
✅ Correct
❌ Incorrect
Show explanation

Reinforcement depends on the number of presses (ratio), and the required number changes unpredictably while maintaining an average (variable). Therefore, Group 2 is on a variable-ratio schedule.

Why not VI: no time requirement is described—only counts of responses.

21. When the researchers add an air puff every time a rat presses the lever and lever pressing decreases, this consequence is best described as:
A Negative reinforcement
B Negative punishment
C Positive punishment
D Positive reinforcement
✅ Correct
❌ Incorrect
Show explanation

The air puff is an added unpleasant stimulus contingent on the behavior, and the behavior decreases. Adding an aversive consequence to reduce a behavior is positive punishment (also called aversive conditioning).

Common trap: “positive” does not mean “good”—it means the stimulus is added.

Passage 3: Models, consequences, and constraints

How to reason (Observational + biological constraints)

  1. Observational learning needs a model. Look for behavior changes after watching someone else, especially when consequences to the model (reward/scolding) shift the observer’s behavior.
  2. Separate imitation from learning rules. If observers adjust behavior based on whether the model is rewarded or punished, that’s stronger evidence for observational learning than simple mimicry.
  3. Use neural evidence carefully. Neurons that fire during both doing and observing an action match the definition of mirror neurons.
  4. Check biology vs training. If a learned behavior collapses into an instinctive pattern over time, consider instinctive drift rather than “poor reinforcement.”

A daycare investigates how children learn new behaviors by watching peers. In Week 1, children watch an older child calmly place toys back on a shelf while an adult smiles and says, “Nice job.” In free-play afterward, many observers also begin tidying toys.

In Week 2, a different group watches an older child hit a large inflatable clown toy. When the adult laughs and gives the child a sticker, observers later show more hitting behaviors toward the clown. However, when the adult instead scolds the model after hitting, observers show fewer hitting behaviors.

Researchers also record brain activity in a subset of children. They find neurons in frontal and parietal regions that fire when a child performs a reaching-and-grasping action and also when the child watches another person perform the same action.

Finally, in an animal-training side project, trainers teach raccoons to place small disks into a container for food rewards. After initial learning, many raccoons begin rubbing the disks and repeatedly dipping them into the container before pulling them out.

22. The Week 2 finding that observers hit the clown more when the model is rewarded (but less when the model is scolded) most strongly supports that the observers are engaging in:
A Latent learning
B Habituation
C Observational learning influenced by consequences to the model
D Extinction
✅ Correct
❌ Incorrect
Show explanation

Observers change their behavior based on what happens to the model (reward vs scolding). That pattern matches observational learning (learning by watching), and it shows it is not mere imitation—consequences to the model alter the observer’s behavior.

Why not extinction: extinction is reduction of a conditioned response when reinforcement/UCS is removed, not learning from others.

23. The neurons that fire both when a child performs an action and when the child watches another perform the same action are most consistent with:
A Feature detector neurons
B Mirror neurons
C Binocular neurons
D Nociceptors
✅ Correct
❌ Incorrect
Show explanation

The passage defines mirror neurons as neurons (noted in frontal and parietal cortex) that fire both during action execution and action observation. That is exactly what the recordings describe.

Why others are wrong: feature detectors are visual-cortex cells for specific visual features; binocular neurons compare inputs from both eyes; nociceptors detect pain.

24. The raccoons’ tendency to revert to rubbing/dipping disks after learning to place them into a container most closely parallels which concept from the learning chapter?
A Preparedness
B Stimulus generalization
C Spontaneous recovery
D Instinctive drift
✅ Correct
❌ Incorrect
Show explanation

This resembles the classic description of instinctive drift: after conditioning seems successful, the animal gradually reverts to an innate behavior pattern that interferes with the trained response (raccoon “food-washing” motions).

Why A is not best: preparedness is being predisposed to learn some behaviors easily; drift is the post-learning re-emergence of instinct that disrupts the conditioned behavior.
