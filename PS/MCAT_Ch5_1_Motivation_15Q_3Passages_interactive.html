MCAT Behavioral Sciences — Chapter 5.1: Motivation
Standalone Questions (Q1–Q15)
1. A toddler automatically grasps a caregiver’s finger when the palm is stroked. Which theory of motivation most directly frames this as an innate, fixed behavioral pattern?
A. Drive reduction theory
B. Instinct theory
C. Incentive theory
D. Expectancy–value theory
Show explanation

Correct Answer: B. Instinct theory

Why: Instinct theory explains certain behaviors as evolutionarily programmed, innate patterns that can appear early in life (e.g., primitive reflexes).

Why the other choices are not as good:

  • A. (Correct)
  • C. Incentive theory emphasizes external rewards/punishments, not innate reflex patterns.
  • D. Expectancy–value concerns beliefs about success and value of goals, not newborn reflexes.
2. A student drinks coffee before an exam because they feel under-stimulated and want to be more alert. Which theory best explains this behavior?
A. Arousal theory
B. Instinct theory
C. Maslow’s hierarchy
D. Opponent-process theory
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A. Arousal theory

Why: Arousal theory proposes people act to maintain an optimal level of arousal—raising arousal when it is too low and lowering it when it is too high.

Why the other choices are not as good:

  • B. Instinct theory focuses on innate, fixed patterns of behavior, not adjusting alertness levels.
  • C. Maslow describes prioritized needs; it does not specifically explain optimizing arousal states.
  • D. Opponent-process theory explains tolerance/withdrawal with repeated drug use, not simple arousal optimization.
3. According to the Yerkes–Dodson law, performance is typically BEST when arousal is:
A. Very low
B. Moderate (intermediate)
C. Very high
D. Independent of arousal level
Show explanation

Correct Answer: B. Moderate (intermediate)

Why: Yerkes–Dodson describes a U-shaped relationship: extremely low or high arousal impairs performance, while an intermediate level is optimal.

Why the other choices are not as good:

  • A. This option does not best fit the theory or evidence described.
  • C. Excessive arousal can produce anxiety/panic and impair performance.
  • D. The law explicitly ties performance to arousal level.
4. Which arousal level is generally most optimal for a highly cognitive, complex task (e.g., reasoning through a difficult passage)?
A. Higher arousal than for simple tasks
B. Lower arousal than for simple tasks
C. The highest possible arousal
D. Arousal level does not matter
Show explanation

Correct Answer: B. Lower arousal than for simple tasks

Why: For complex, highly cognitive tasks, too much arousal tends to disrupt performance; lower arousal is often optimal compared with simple tasks.

Why the other choices are not as good:

  • A. This option does not best fit the theory or evidence described.
  • C. Extremely high arousal tends to impair complex cognition (test anxiety).
  • D. Yerkes–Dodson predicts arousal matters.
5. Which statement best captures drive reduction theory?
A. Motivation is driven by the pursuit of external rewards.
B. Motivation is driven primarily by evolutionarily programmed instincts.
C. Motivation is driven by reducing internal tension and restoring homeostasis.
D. Motivation is determined by the expectation of success times the value of the goal.
Show explanation

Correct Answer: C. Motivation is driven by reducing internal tension and restoring homeostasis.

Why: Drive reduction theory treats drives as uncomfortable internal states that motivate behavior to reduce the tension and re-establish homeostasis.

Why the other choices are not as good:

  • A. This option does not best fit the theory or evidence described.
  • B. That describes instinct theory.
  • D. That describes expectancy–value theory.
6. Which example best illustrates a PRIMARY drive rather than a secondary drive?
A. Feeling cold and seeking warmth
B. Studying to earn a high MCAT score
C. Desiring social status in a club
D. Wanting praise from a supervisor
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A. Feeling cold and seeking warmth

Why: Primary drives support basic biological needs (food, water, warmth). Secondary drives are learned/emotional goals like achievement or status.

Why the other choices are not as good:

  • B. Achievement toward school goals is a learned/secondary drive.
  • C. Status and belonging are secondary drives/needs, not basic physiological drives.
  • D. Praise is an external incentive and not a primary biological drive.

Why the other choices are not as good:

9. Self-determination theory (SDT) emphasizes which three universal needs?
A. Autonomy, competence, and relatedness
B. Arousal, drive, and instinct
C. Safety, esteem, and self-actualization
D. Punishment avoidance, reward pursuit, and fear

Correct Answer: A. Autonomy, competence, and relatedness

Why: SDT proposes that motivation and psychological growth depend strongly on autonomy (control), competence (effectiveness), and relatedness (connection).

Why the other choices are not as good:

  • B. Those are broad categories of motivation theories, not SDT’s three needs.
  • C. Those are levels from Maslow, not SDT’s core needs.
  • D. That framing is closer to behaviorist incentive/punishment language than SDT.

12. A child who loved drawing begins drawing less after being promised prizes for every picture. This phenomenon is best described as:
A. Drive reduction
B. Opponent-process theory
C. Cognitive dissonance
D. Reduced intrinsic motivation due to external reward (overjustification)
Show explanation

Correct Answer: D. Reduced intrinsic motivation due to external reward (overjustification)

Why: Introducing external rewards can shift motivation from intrinsic to extrinsic, decreasing the inherent enjoyment and persistence (often called the overjustification effect).

Why the other choices are not as good:

  • A. This option does not best fit the theory or evidence described.
  • B. Opponent-process concerns repeated drug use leading to tolerance/withdrawal.
  • C. Cognitive dissonance involves discomfort from inconsistent beliefs/behaviors.
13. Opponent-process theory predicts that repeated use of a depressant (e.g., alcohol) will most likely lead to withdrawal characterized by:
A. Deep sedation (same as the drug effect)
B. Anxiety and jitteriness (opposite the drug effect)
C. No physiological change after cessation
D. Improved coordination and attention
Show explanation

Correct Answer: B. Anxiety and jitteriness (opposite the drug effect)

Why: The body counters repeated drug effects; when the drug wears off, the compensatory response persists, producing withdrawal opposite to the drug’s acute effect.

Why the other choices are not as good:

  • A. This option does not best fit the theory or evidence described.
  • C. Opponent-process predicts a compensatory response, not ‘no change.’
  • D. Withdrawal symptoms are typically unpleasant and opposite to the drug effect.

Correct Answer: C. Tolerance and withdrawal

  • A. This option does not best fit the theory or evidence described.
  • B. Maslow concerns prioritization of needs.
  • D. Infant reflexes are examples of instincts.
15. A person repeatedly seeks extreme sports and is described as a ‘sensation seeker.’ The passage notes this can be associated with low monoamine oxidase (MAO). Low MAO would most directly be expected to:

Passage-Based Questions (3 Passages × 3 Questions)
Passage 1: Rewards, Autonomy, and Task Engagement

A school tests ways to increase reading among 10-year-olds. Students are randomly assigned to one of three conditions for four weeks.

Group R receives a prize each time they finish a book; Group F receives praise emphasizing effort and choice (e.g., ‘You chose a challenging book and stuck with it’); Group C receives no special feedback.

After the program ends, prizes and praise stop. Over the next month, Group R’s voluntary reading time drops below Group C, while Group F’s voluntary reading time remains above Group C.

Researchers also note that students in Group F report feeling more ‘in control’ of what they read and more confident they can handle difficult books.

How to reason:

  1. Identify what changed after the intervention: rewards/praise removed, but behavior persists differently across groups.
  2. Map outcomes to motivation types: a drop when rewards stop suggests extrinsic control; persistence suggests internalization/intrinsic motivation.
  3. Link clues to SDT: ‘in control’ → autonomy; ‘confident’ → competence; both support intrinsic motivation.
  4. Choose answers that explain behavior AFTER rewards are removed (the key diagnostic moment).
16. Which interpretation best explains why Group R read LESS than controls after prizes were removed?
A. The students reduced physiological drives for sleep by reading less.
B. Instincts for reading extinguished with age.
C. External rewards undermined intrinsic motivation, so behavior declined when the incentive disappeared.
D. High arousal produced by prizes permanently improved reading performance.
Show explanation

Correct Answer: C. External rewards undermined intrinsic motivation, so behavior declined when the incentive disappeared.

Why: When behavior is driven mainly by external incentives, removing the incentive can reduce persistence; this is consistent with reduced intrinsic motivation (overjustification).

Why the other choices are not as good:

  • A. This option does not best fit the theory or evidence described.
  • B. Instinct theory does not explain a reward-dependent drop in reading after an intervention.
  • D. Yerkes–Dodson concerns performance vs arousal during tasks, not long-term persistence after incentives stop.
17. The pattern in Group F is MOST consistent with which SDT needs being supported by the feedback?
A. Autonomy and competence
B. Safety and self-actualization
C. Physiological needs and safety
D. Relatedness and withdrawal
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A. Autonomy and competence

Why: Feedback emphasizing choice (‘in control’) supports autonomy, and emphasizing ability to handle difficult books supports competence—both strengthen intrinsic motivation.

Why the other choices are not as good:

  • B. Those are Maslow terms and do not match the study’s explicit clues.
  • C. These are basic survival needs and are not implicated by reading-choice feedback.
  • D. ‘Withdrawal’ is opponent-process language, not SDT; relatedness isn’t the primary clue given.
18. If the school wants reading to persist long-term without ongoing prizes, which strategy is most supported by the passage’s results?
A. Design activities that increase autonomy and competence rather than relying on tangible rewards.
B. Increase prize size to maximize extrinsic motivation.
C. Add time pressure to raise arousal as high as possible.
D. Use punishment for not reading to strengthen intrinsic motivation.
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A. Design activities that increase autonomy and competence rather than relying on tangible rewards.

Why: Group F maintained higher voluntary reading even after feedback ended, implying internalization/intrinsic motivation supported by autonomy/competence cues.

Why the other choices are not as good:

  • B. Bigger prizes may increase short-term reading but can still reduce persistence once removed.
  • C. Extremely high arousal can impair complex tasks and does not target intrinsic persistence.
  • D. Punishment can increase compliance but often undermines intrinsic motivation and can create avoidance.
Passage 2: Arousal, Task Complexity, and Performance

A lab measures performance on two tasks: Task S is simple (copy a short symbol pattern quickly), and Task C is complex (solve multi-step logic puzzles).

Participants are assigned to one of three arousal conditions before testing: Low (quiet room), Moderate (mild time awareness), or High (loud countdown plus audience observation).

Results show Task S accuracy peaks in the High-arousal condition, while Task C accuracy peaks in the Moderate-arousal condition; both tasks perform poorly in the Low-arousal condition.

The lab asks students to explain how the same arousal manipulation can improve one task yet harm another.

How to reason:

  1. Identify the theory: a U-shaped arousal–performance relationship (Yerkes–Dodson).
  2. Use task type as a modifier: complex cognitive tasks peak at lower/moderate arousal; simpler tasks can tolerate or benefit from higher arousal.
  3. Look for the ‘peak’ condition for each task and match to the law’s prediction.
  4. Avoid overgeneralizing: ‘more arousal is always better’ is not what the data show.
19. Which statement best explains why Task C performance decreased in the High-arousal condition?
A. Complex tasks require the lowest possible arousal to be completed at all.
B. High arousal can push complex cognitive performance past the optimal point, impairing reasoning and attention.
C. High arousal eliminates all extrinsic motivation.
D. Performance on any task is independent of arousal once practice occurs.
Show explanation

Correct Answer: B. High arousal can push complex cognitive performance past the optimal point, impairing reasoning and attention.

Why: Yerkes–Dodson predicts complex tasks have an optimal arousal that is not too high; excessive arousal can produce anxiety and cognitive interference.

Why the other choices are not as good:

  • A. This option does not best fit the theory or evidence described.
  • C. Arousal level is not equivalent to motivation type; extrinsic motivation is not the mechanism here.
  • D. The passage explicitly shows performance changes across arousal conditions.
20. Which theoretical framework is MOST directly supported by the pattern that both tasks were poor at Low arousal but peaked at different intermediate levels?
A. Maslow’s hierarchy of needs
B. Drive reduction theory
C. Arousal theory with the Yerkes–Dodson law
D. Opponent-process theory
Show explanation

Correct Answer: C. Arousal theory with the Yerkes–Dodson law

Why: A U-shaped arousal–performance curve (with task-dependent optimal arousal) is the hallmark of Yerkes–Dodson within arousal theory.

Why the other choices are not as good:

  • A. This option does not best fit the theory or evidence described.
  • B. No internal-tension/homeostasis variable is being reduced; arousal is manipulated externally.
  • D. Opponent-process is about repeated drug use and compensatory physiological changes.

Passage 3: Drug Use, Tolerance, and Withdrawal

A 22-year-old begins using a sedative recreationally to ‘feel relaxed.’ Initially, one dose produces calmness and sleepiness for several hours.

After repeated use over months, the same dose produces much less calmness. When the person stops abruptly, they experience anxiety, jitteriness, and irritability that persist longer than the original calming effect used to last.

They report taking the drug again mainly to stop the unpleasant feelings rather than to feel euphoric.

A clinician asks which motivation theory best explains the pattern and what type of reinforcement is operating when the person uses the drug to end withdrawal symptoms.

How to reason:

  1. Match the time-course: reduced effect with repeated use → tolerance; opposite symptoms after stopping → withdrawal.
  2. Identify the theory: opponent-process explains compensatory responses that outlast the drug.
  3. Classify reinforcement: taking a drug to remove an aversive state is negative reinforcement (behavior increases because something bad is removed).
  4. Separate ‘why they take it now’ (avoid withdrawal) from ‘why they first took it’ (positive effects).
22. Which theory best explains both the decreased drug effect over time and the opposite withdrawal symptoms?
A. Instinct theory
B. Opponent-process theory
C. Maslow’s hierarchy
D. Expectancy–value theory
Show explanation

Correct Answer: B. Opponent-process theory

Why: Opponent-process theory predicts compensatory physiological changes with repeated use, producing tolerance and withdrawal opposite the drug’s acute effects.

Why the other choices are not as good:

  • A. This option does not best fit the theory or evidence described.
  • C. Maslow is about needs prioritization, not pharmacologic adaptation.
  • D. Expectancy–value can influence decisions to use, but it does not explain the physiological opponent withdrawal pattern.
23. When the person takes the drug primarily to stop the unpleasant withdrawal symptoms, this behavior is best classified as:
A. Positive reinforcement
B. Positive punishment
C. Negative punishment
D. Negative reinforcement
Show explanation

Correct Answer: D. Negative reinforcement

Why: The drug use increases because it removes an aversive internal state (withdrawal). Removing something unpleasant reinforces the behavior: negative reinforcement.

Why the other choices are not as good:

  • A. This option does not best fit the theory or evidence described.
  • B. Punishment decreases behavior; here the behavior (drug taking) is increasing.
  • C. Negative punishment is removing something desirable to reduce a behavior (e.g., taking away privileges).
24. Which change would MOST directly reduce the motivation to take the drug under the ‘withdrawal-relief’ pattern described?
A. Focusing only on self-actualization goals without addressing physiology
B. Increasing audience observation during daily activities
C. Raising arousal as high as possible to improve cognition
D. Reducing withdrawal severity during cessation so the aversive state is smaller
Show explanation

Correct Answer: D. Reducing withdrawal severity during cessation so the aversive state is smaller

Why: If drug-taking is maintained by negative reinforcement (relief), decreasing the aversive withdrawal state reduces the reinforcing ‘payoff’ of taking the drug.

Why the other choices are not as good:

  • A. This option does not best fit the theory or evidence described.
  • B. Audience observation is an arousal manipulation and does not target withdrawal reinforcement directly.
  • C. This misapplies Yerkes–Dodson and does not address the withdrawal mechanism driving behavior.
