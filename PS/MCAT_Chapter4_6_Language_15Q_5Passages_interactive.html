🗣️ MCAT Practice — Chapter 4.6: Language, Development Theories, Aphasia
Components: phonology (sounds/phonemes), morphology (morphemes), semantics (meaning), syntax (word order), pragmatics (context/prosody).
Milestones: babbling (9–12 mo), ~1 word/month (12–18 mo), naming explosion + overextension + word combining (18–20 mo), longer sentences + overregularization errors (2–3 yr), rules largely mastered (~5 yr).
Theories: nativist (Chomsky; LAD; sensitive/critical period before puberty), behaviorist (Skinner; operant conditioning + reinforcement), social interactionist (bio + social interaction; circuits reinforced/pruned).
Cognition link: Whorfian/linguistic relativity (language can shape perception to some degree).
Brain: Broca (inferior frontal gyrus; production), Wernicke (superior temporal gyrus; comprehension), arcuate fasciculus (connection); aphasias: Broca (nonfluent, comprehension intact), Wernicke (fluent but nonsensical, comprehension impaired), conduction (can speak + understand, cannot repeat).

❌Incorrect
Explanation (click to expand/collapse)

  • C syntax = word order in sentences, not word meaning.

📘 Passage 1 — Clinic Screening: A “Late Talker” With Uneven Skills

On a hospital stroke unit, a clinician performs a rapid bedside language exam on three patients. Patient A answers yes/no questions appropriately and follows commands like “close your eyes,” but speaks in short, effortful phrases with long pauses. The patient appears visibly frustrated and frequently gestures as if the “right word” is just out of reach.

Patient B speaks fluently with normal rhythm and intonation but frequently produces incorrect word combinations that do not convey clear meaning. When asked to point to common objects named by the clinician, the patient often selects the wrong item and insists the task is being done correctly. Patient B also struggles to follow spoken instructions and seems surprised when the clinician repeats directions more slowly.

Patient C converses normally and follows complex commands. However, when the clinician says a simple sentence and asks the patient to repeat it exactly, Patient C repeatedly fails—substituting words, reordering phrases, or stopping mid-sentence. Patient C becomes annoyed because they can explain what the sentence meant but cannot reproduce it verbatim, even after several attempts.

How to reason (Passage 3):
  1. Use the 3-part aphasia screen: (1) comprehension, (2) fluency/production, (3) repetition.
  2. Patient A: comprehension intact + nonfluent/frustrated production → Broca’s (expressive) aphasia (inferior frontal gyrus).
  3. Patient B: fluent but meaningless + poor comprehension/insight → Wernicke’s (receptive) aphasia (superior temporal gyrus).
  4. Patient C: comprehension + fluency intact but repetition impaired → Conduction aphasia (arcuate fasciculus connection).

Explanation (click to expand/collapse)

  • A would produce fluent speech with poor comprehension.
  • C would primarily impair repetition with relatively intact production/comprehension.
  • D is not a language center.

📘 Passage 4 — Bilingual Input: Phoneme Boundaries, Context, and Labels

At a bilingual daycare, researchers track how infants’ speech perception changes across the first year. At 6 months of age, infants from both monolingual and bilingual homes can discriminate several consonant contrasts that are not used in the local community language. By 10 months, many monolingual infants show a drop in sensitivity to non-native contrasts, but infants with daily exposure to two languages maintain discrimination for a wider set of contrasts.

To better understand why, the team observes adult–child interactions. Caregivers in both settings respond more strongly to vocalizations that resemble words used in their own speech, but the bilingual caregivers alternate which sound patterns are emphasized depending on who is speaking to the child and which activity is occurring (storybook time vs snack time). As the children become toddlers, teachers notice that the same child may phrase a request differently depending on whether they are speaking to an unfamiliar adult visitor, a familiar teacher, or a close playmate, even when the goal of the request is identical.

In a follow-up test at age 4, the team gives children a fine-grained color sorting task. Some children use a single familiar label for multiple shades, whereas others spontaneously use distinct labels they have learned at home, and those children tend to draw sharper boundaries between the shades on the task.

How to reason (Passage 4):
  1. Identify what changes with age: the passage contrasts 6-month vs 10-month performance, implying a developmental shift rather than a one-time deficit.
  2. Link the shift to exposure: monolinguals lose sensitivity to non-native contrasts; bilingual exposure preserves it → suggests experience-dependent tuning of phoneme categories.
  3. Separate “sound” vs “social use”: discrimination of consonant contrasts is phonology/categorical perception, while changing request style with audience is pragmatics.
  4. Use labels as evidence: children who know more distinct words for shades draw sharper boundaries → consistent with linguistic relativity (language can shape perception to some degree).

✔️Correct!
❌Incorrect
Explanation (click to expand/collapse)
The passage suggests infants start broadly sensitive to speech sounds, then become tuned to the sounds that matter in their environment. Bilingual infants receive regular input from two systems, so their phoneme categories remain broader and less “narrowed,” preserving discrimination of contrasts that monolingual infants stop prioritizing.
  • A grammar mastery is far later; this is a speech-sound (phonology) finding.
  • B arcuate fasciculus relates to repetition (conduction aphasia), not infant phoneme discrimination.
  • D overextension is about word meaning/category labeling in toddlers, not 10-month phoneme tuning.

📘 Passage 5 — Late Exposure and Lesions: Limits, Pathways, and Practice

A community clinic receives a referral for a teenager who experienced prolonged social isolation in early childhood and had minimal meaningful language exposure until early adolescence. After two years of intensive intervention, the teen can learn a substantial vocabulary and can often understand simple spoken instructions, but struggles with many “rule-like” aspects of grammar. For example, the teen can communicate basic needs (“eat,” “water,” “go home”) but has persistent difficulty applying consistent tense marking and producing complex sentence structures. Therapists note that progress is strongest when sessions involve real conversational back-and-forth with a supportive partner, rather than rote repetition drills.

In a separate case discussed at the same clinic, an adult patient is evaluated after a stroke. The patient speaks fluently and follows complex commands, and they can explain the meaning of a sentence they just heard. However, when asked to repeat the sentence exactly, they repeatedly fail—substituting words or rearranging phrases—despite clearly understanding what was said. The clinician explains that language comprehension and speech production can be intact while the pathway connecting them is disrupted.

The clinic team debates how to interpret the teen’s long-term limitations: whether to treat language as primarily learned through reinforcement, as an innate system with time-limited plasticity, or as a skill that depends on both biological maturation and social use that continually strengthens some circuits and lets others fade.

How to reason (Passage 5):
  1. Match each case to a mechanism: the teen case is about timing of exposure and how that affects later mastery of rules; the stroke case is about which pathway is disrupted.
  2. Use the nativist time-window idea carefully: the chapter discusses a critical/sensitive period before puberty; late exposure can allow some learning but leaves persistent rule deficits.
  3. For the stroke patient, use the repetition triad: comprehension intact + fluent speech + repetition impaired → conduction aphasia → arcuate fasciculus.
  4. Decide which theory fits best: improvement with real conversation (not rote drills) supports models that emphasize social use and interaction (and not only reinforcement of isolated outputs).
