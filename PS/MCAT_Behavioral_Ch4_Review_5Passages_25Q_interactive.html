🧠 MCAT Practice — Cognition, Problem Solving, Consciousness, Drugs, Attention & Language (5 Passages × 5 Questions)
📘 Passage 1 — Cognitive Development in a Science Museum

Researchers at a children’s science museum compare how children of different ages solve problems that require either concrete manipulation or abstract reasoning. In one station, children see a toy mouse “hide” under one of three cups, then the cups are moved behind a screen and swapped twice. The child is asked to point to the cup that still contains the mouse.

In a second station, children see two identical beakers with the same amount of colored water. The experimenter then pours one beaker into a taller, thinner cylinder and asks which container has “more water.” The experimenter also asks children to explain their choice. Finally, in a third station, children are shown a pendulum rig where they can vary string length, bob mass, and release angle. They are told: “Find what changes how fast it swings.” They may perform as many trials as they want, and their strategy is recorded.

Results: Children ages 18–24 months often search for the mouse where it was last seen before the screen. Children ages 3–5 years frequently say the tall cylinder has more water and justify it by pointing to the height of the water line. Children ages 8–10 years usually answer that the amounts are equal but struggle to isolate one variable at a time in the pendulum task. Adolescents (12–14 years) are most likely to hold two variables constant while systematically changing one, and they correctly identify string length as the key determinant.

How to reason (Passage 1):
  1. Map age → Piaget stage: 0–2 sensorimotor; 2–7 preoperational; 7–11 concrete operational; 11+ formal operational.
  2. Match task → hallmark: hidden object search → object permanence; water-in-different-shape → conservation; variable isolation → hypothetical/scientific reasoning.
  3. Use explanations: “It’s taller” suggests centration (focus on one dimension).
  4. Predict limitations: concrete operational kids can do logic with concrete info, but struggle with abstract variable control.
  5. Eliminate distractors: Don’t confuse with memory capacity; this is about developmental stage features.

Q1. A 20-month-old repeatedly searches for the mouse in the location where it was last seen before the cups moved behind the screen. This behavior most strongly suggests the child has not fully developed:

A. Object permanence
B. Conservation
C. Hypothetical reasoning
D. Crystallized intelligence
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Explanation

Correct answer: A — Object permanence

Explanation:

  • ✅ A. Object permanence (sensorimotor milestone) is understanding that objects continue to exist when out of view. Searching where it was last seen (and failing to track hidden switches) is classic pre–object permanence behavior.
  • ❌ B. Conservation concerns quantity staying the same despite shape change; that’s the water task, not the hidden-object task.
  • ❌ C. Hypothetical reasoning is a formal-operational skill for isolating variables; it’s tested by the pendulum task in older children/adolescents.
  • ❌ D. Crystallized intelligence is knowledge-based problem solving and isn’t the main explanation for a 20‑month‑old’s hidden-object error.

Q2. A 4-year-old insists the tall cylinder has more water after the pour, and explains: “Look, it’s higher.” Which cognitive feature best explains this error?

A. Egocentrism
B. Centration
C. Object permanence
D. Deductive reasoning
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Explanation

Correct answer: B — Centration

Explanation:

  • ❌ A. Egocentrism is difficulty taking another person’s perspective; it doesn’t directly explain failing conservation judgments.
  • ✅ B. Centration is focusing on one salient aspect (here, height) while ignoring others (width/overall volume), producing conservation errors in the preoperational stage.
  • ❌ C. Object permanence is an earlier sensorimotor milestone about hidden objects, not quantity transformations.
  • ❌ D. Deductive reasoning applies general rules to derive necessary conclusions; the child isn’t applying a logical rule set—they’re over-weighting one perceptual cue.

Q3. Adolescents in the pendulum station hold bob mass and release angle constant while changing string length across trials, and then correctly infer what determines swing frequency. This pattern of testing best reflects:

A. Trial-and-error without a strategy
B. A mental set preventing new solutions
C. Formal operational hypothetical reasoning
D. Proactive interference from earlier tasks
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Explanation

Correct answer: C — Formal operational hypothetical reasoning

Explanation:

  • ❌ A. Randomly changing variables would be trial-and-error; the passage describes a controlled, methodical strategy.
  • ❌ B. A mental set can lock someone into a familiar approach; here they are using an effective experimental approach, not being stuck.
  • ✅ C. Systematically holding variables constant and manipulating one variable at a time is hallmark hypothetical/scientific reasoning, characteristic of the formal operational stage.
  • ❌ D. Proactive interference is old information disrupting new learning; it doesn’t explain variable control strategies.

Q4. After several demonstrations, a child who initially said the tall cylinder had more water begins to say the amounts are equal and explains, “It just looks different.” In Piagetian terms, this change is best described as:

A. Assimilation (fitting new info into an unchanged schema)
B. Overconfidence (inflated certainty)
C. A mental set (reusing the same approach)
D. Accommodation (modifying a schema to incorporate new information)
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Explanation

Correct answer: D — Accommodation (modifying a schema to incorporate new information)

Explanation:

  • ❌ A. Assimilation would keep the old schema and squeeze the new experience into it; the child’s explanation shows the schema itself changed.
  • ❌ B. Overconfidence is a judgment bias about certainty; it doesn’t describe learning a new cognitive rule.
  • ❌ C. A mental set is a habitual problem-solving approach; the key idea here is schema change, not a stuck approach.
  • ✅ D. Accommodation occurs when an existing schema is modified to incorporate information that doesn’t fit—here, updating the ‘taller means more’ intuition to a conservation-consistent understanding.

Q5. Which age group in the museum would be expected to correctly judge conservation of water but still struggle to isolate variables in the pendulum task?

A. 8–10 years
B. 18–24 months
C. 3–5 years
D. 12–14 years
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Explanation

Correct answer: A — 8–10 years

Explanation:

  • ✅ A. Children around 7–11 years (concrete operational) typically understand conservation but often have difficulty with abstract, multi-variable hypothesis testing.
  • ❌ B. 18–24 months are typically in sensorimotor; conservation is not yet present and object permanence is still developing.
  • ❌ C. 3–5 years are typically preoperational and commonly fail conservation because of centration.

📘 Passage 2 — Diagnoses, Heuristics, and Belief Perseverance

A hospital trains new interns using computer-based diagnostic cases. Each case begins with a short vignette (symptoms + a few labs). Interns choose an initial diagnosis, then decide what single additional test to order next. After seeing the test result, they may revise the diagnosis. Interns also estimate (0–100%) how confident they are in their final diagnosis.

In one module, a rare condition (Disease R) occurs in only 1 out of 200 cases in the training set, while a common condition (Disease C) occurs in 1 out of 5 cases. Disease R and Disease C can produce similar chest pain. A subset of interns repeatedly choose Disease R after reading vivid stories about Disease R during orientation (including a dramatic near-death narrative), even when the base rates are displayed on screen. When additional test results contradict Disease R, these interns often order a second confirmatory test for Disease R rather than switching to Disease C. They remain highly confident even when their final answer is incorrect.

In a second module, interns are given a classic “candle problem” variant: they receive a candle, a book of matches, and a box of thumbtacks. They must mount the candle so wax won’t drip on a table. Most interns initially try to tack the candle directly or melt wax to glue it. Only after a hint (“Think about the box”) do many solve it quickly.

How to reason (Passage 2):
  1. Start with base rates: if a disease is rare (1/200), it should be the default low probability unless strong evidence overrides.
  2. Spot the trap: vivid stories → availability heuristic; using “prototype” over numbers → base rate fallacy.
  3. Watch what they do after disconfirming data: ordering more supportive tests instead of switching → confirmation bias + failure to apply the disconfirmation principle.
  4. Confidence ≠ accuracy: high confidence despite errors → overconfidence and belief perseverance.
  5. Object-use rigidity: candle task failure until re-framing object function → functional fixedness.

Q6. Interns choose the rare Disease R after hearing vivid orientation stories, even when base rates are displayed showing it is uncommon. This is best described as:

A. Retroactive interference
B. Base rate fallacy influenced by the availability heuristic
C. The spacing effect improving recall of Disease R
D. An algorithm guaranteeing correct diagnosis
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Explanation

Correct answer: B — Base rate fallacy influenced by the availability heuristic

Explanation:

  • ❌ A. Retroactive interference is new information disrupting older memories; here it’s a decision bias, not a memory mix-up.
  • ✅ B. They overweight vivid examples (availability heuristic) and ignore numerical prevalence (base rates), which is the base rate fallacy.
  • ❌ C. Spacing effect is better retention with distributed practice; it doesn’t explain ignoring prevalence data.
  • ❌ D. An algorithm is a step-by-step procedure that reliably solves a problem; the interns are not following a guaranteed procedure.

Q7. After a test result contradicts Disease R, some interns order additional tests mainly aimed at “proving” Disease R instead of revising their diagnosis. This behavior most strongly reflects:

A. Inductive reasoning
B. Functional fixedness
C. Confirmation bias with poor use of the disconfirmation principle
D. State-dependent memory
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Explanation

Correct answer: C — Confirmation bias with poor use of the disconfirmation principle

Explanation:

  • ❌ A. Inductive reasoning builds generalizations from specific observations; the problem is biased evaluation, not the reasoning direction.
  • ❌ B. Functional fixedness is using objects only in their typical way; that’s relevant to the candle task, not diagnostic testing.
  • ✅ C. They seek supportive evidence and discount disconfirming results—classic confirmation bias—and they fail to discard a hypothesis when evidence refutes it (disconfirmation principle).
  • ❌ D. State-dependent memory is better recall in the same mental state; it’s not the main driver of selective test ordering.

Q8. In the candle variant, most interns fail until they reframe the matchbox as something other than a container. The main barrier before the hint is:

A. Mental set
B. Availability heuristic
C. Algorithmic thinking
D. Functional fixedness
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Explanation

Correct answer: D — Functional fixedness

Explanation:

  • ❌ A. Mental set can contribute, but the classic explanation for Duncker-style object-use tasks is functional fixedness.
  • ❌ B. Availability heuristic concerns judging likelihood by ease of recall; it’s not about object use.
  • ❌ C. Algorithms are stepwise procedures; the issue isn’t ‘too algorithmic’ but constrained object representation.
  • ✅ D. Functional fixedness is the inability to see an object’s nontraditional uses (using the box as a platform/holder).

Q9. A redesigned training module tells interns: “If symptom pattern X plus lab Y, then order Test Z; if Test Z is positive, diagnose Disease C.” This approach is best characterized as:

A. An algorithm
B. Trial-and-error problem solving
C. Belief perseverance
D. Representativeness heuristic
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Explanation

Correct answer: A — An algorithm

Explanation:

  • ✅ A. It’s a defined step-by-step procedure intended to produce the correct result given the inputs—an algorithm.
  • ❌ B. Trial-and-error involves trying options until one works, usually without a guaranteed procedure.
  • ❌ C. Belief perseverance is holding a belief despite contrary evidence; here the module is an instruction set.
  • ❌ D. Representativeness heuristic is judging based on stereotypes/prototypes rather than data; the module is explicitly data-driven.

Q10. Interns who report 95% confidence even when they choose incorrect diagnoses are most clearly demonstrating:

A. Proactive interference
B. Overconfidence bias
C. Semantic priming
D. Deductive reasoning
✅Correct
❌Incorrect

  • ❌ A. Proactive interference is old information interfering with new learning; not primarily about confidence calibration.
  • ✅ B. Overconfidence is overestimating the accuracy of one’s beliefs or decisions.
  • ❌ C. Semantic priming is improved processing after related cues; it doesn’t explain inflated certainty.
  • ❌ D. Deductive reasoning can be correct or incorrect; ‘high confidence in wrong answers’ is a bias pattern.
📘 Passage 3 — Sleep Architecture, Circadian Hormones, and Memory

A sleep lab studies how different sleep stages relate to memory. Adult participants learn two tasks at 9:00 PM: (1) a word-pair list (declarative memory) and (2) a mirror-tracing skill task (procedural memory). They then sleep overnight with EEG monitoring. Half the participants keep a normal schedule with dim lights after 9:30 PM. The other half are exposed to bright blue-enriched light until midnight.

EEG scoring shows typical NREM stages early in the night with slow-wave sleep (SWS) concentrated in the first third of sleep, and more REM toward morning. The bright-light group falls asleep later, has reduced early-night SWS, and shows a delayed melatonin rise. Morning cortisol levels are similar between groups, but the bright-light group reports feeling “less sleepy” at midnight despite taking longer to fall asleep.

Memory testing at 8:00 AM shows: the normal-light group improves more on word-pair recall, while the bright-light group shows smaller gains on word pairs but similar or slightly better improvement on mirror tracing. A small subset of participants with obstructive sleep apnea shows frequent brief awakenings and reduced REM duration; they perform worse on the procedural task despite adequate total time in bed.

How to reason (Passage 3):
  1. Decode EEG/stages: SWS (deep NREM) is linked to declarative consolidation; REM is linked more to procedural consolidation.
  2. Use circadian cues: less evening light → more melatonin → easier sleep onset; bright light delays melatonin and can shift sleep.
  3. Separate feeling vs performance: “less sleepy” doesn’t guarantee better cognition; sleep deprivation can reduce insight into impairment.
  4. Apply disorder logic: sleep apnea fragments sleep → can reduce REM and impair tasks tied to REM/procedural consolidation.
  5. Pick the strongest inference: connect stage changes (SWS/REM) to which memory outcome changes.

Q11. The bright-light group shows reduced early-night SWS and smaller improvements in word-pair recall. The most plausible explanation is that SWS is especially important for consolidating:

A. Procedural memories
B. Primacy effects
C. Declarative memories
D. Implicit conditioning responses
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Explanation

Correct answer: C — Declarative memories

Explanation:

  • ❌ A. Procedural skill consolidation is more strongly linked to REM in many models.
  • ❌ B. Primacy effect is a list-position phenomenon; it isn’t specifically consolidated by SWS.
  • ✅ C. Slow-wave sleep (deep NREM) is strongly associated with consolidation of declarative/explicit information such as word pairs.
  • ❌ D. Implicit conditioning can involve many circuits; the pattern (word pairs vs skill) points to declarative consolidation.

✅Correct

Correct answer: D — Retinal input to the hypothalamus can suppress pineal melatonin release in bright light

Explanation:

  • ❌ A. CRF→ACTH→adrenal cortex describes cortisol physiology, not melatonin.
  • ❌ B. Melatonin generally promotes sleepiness rather than wakefulness.
  • ❌ C. Cortisol is regulated by the HPA axis and tends to rise toward morning; it does not require pineal darkness.
  • ✅ D. Light detected by the retina signals the hypothalamus, which influences the pineal gland; brighter light in the evening delays melatonin secretion.

📘 Passage 4 — Drugs, Synapses, and the Reward Pathway

In a rodent study, researchers compare how different drug classes alter neural signaling and behavior. One group receives a moderate dose of ethanol. Another receives a benzodiazepine. A third receives cocaine. A fourth receives morphine. Animals are tested on (a) a maze requiring flexible planning, (b) a simple lever-press task for food, and (c) respiratory rate monitoring.

Neural recordings show that ethanol and the benzodiazepine increase inhibitory signaling in cortical circuits and reduce overall firing, while cocaine increases catecholamine signaling in a reward-related circuit and produces high locomotor activity. Morphine produces strong analgesia and euphoria-like behavior but markedly slows breathing at high doses. In an additional condition, animals receive ethanol plus the benzodiazepine, leading to profound sedation and occasional loss of righting reflex. Cocaine-treated animals show repeated lever pressing even when food rewards are made less frequent; their behavior is difficult to extinguish.

To probe addiction circuitry, the team measures dopamine in the nucleus accumbens after each drug. Dopamine rises for cocaine and also rises (more modestly) after ethanol. When dopamine signaling between the ventral tegmental area and nucleus accumbens is pharmacologically blocked, cocaine no longer produces strong approach behavior toward drug-paired cues.

How to reason (Passage 4):
  1. Match drug class → neurotransmission: depressants (alcohol, benzos, barbiturates) ↑GABAA effects → CNS inhibition.
  2. Look for synergy: two GABAergic depressants together → additive CNS/respiratory depression risk.
  3. Stimulant signature: cocaine blocks reuptake of DA/NE/5-HT → high arousal + reinforcement.
  4. Opiate risk: opioid receptor agonism can suppress brainstem breathing drive → overdose via respiratory depression.
  5. Addiction pathway: VTA → (MFB) → NAc, dopamine; blocking that link reduces cue-driven approach.

C. They antagonize opioid receptors in the brainstem
D. They enhance GABA_A receptor–mediated chloride influx, increasing neuronal inhibition
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Explanation

Correct answer: D — They enhance GABA_A receptor–mediated chloride influx, increasing neuronal inhibition

Explanation:

  • ❌ A. Direct dopamine receptor activation is not the primary benzodiazepine mechanism.
  • ❌ B. Serotonin reuptake blockade is typical of SSRIs; benzodiazepines act through GABA_A.
  • ❌ C. Opioid antagonism would reduce opioid effects; it does not explain benzodiazepine sedation.
  • ✅ D. Benzodiazepines enhance GABA_A effects (often by increasing channel opening frequency), allowing Cl⁻ influx and hyperpolarization → CNS inhibition and sedation.

📘 Passage 5 — Selective Attention Meets Language

A cognitive neuroscience lab studies how attention and language processing interact. Adults perform a dichotic listening task: different stories play in each ear. Participants must shadow (repeat aloud) the story in the right ear while ignoring the left. During the task, the unattended (left-ear) story occasionally includes the participant’s name or a sudden loud clap. Separately, participants complete a driving simulator where they must keep a car centered while either (a) repeating a list of random digits (new task) or (b) speaking in their native language about their weekend (highly practiced).

In the dichotic listening test, most participants later report hearing their name despite being told to ignore the left ear; they also show brief shadowing errors immediately after the loud clap. In the driving simulator, lane deviation is much larger during digit repetition than during casual weekend speech. The researchers conclude that selective attention behaves more like a filter that can still allow salient stimuli through, and that automatic processing can support divided attention better than controlled processing.

In a clinical extension, a patient with a left frontal stroke speaks in short, effortful phrases but follows complex spoken instructions accurately. Another patient with a left temporal lesion speaks fluently but produces nonsensical sentences and cannot follow verbal commands. A third patient can understand and speak fluently but cannot repeat simple phrases.

How to reason (Passage 5):
  1. Selective attention ≠ perfect block: cocktail party effect (own name) shows some unattended processing.
  2. Controlled vs automatic: new/effortful tasks consume resources; practiced tasks allow better divided attention.
  3. Use symptom patterns: effortful nonfluent speech + intact comprehension → Broca’s aphasia.
  4. Fluent but meaningless + poor comprehension → Wernicke’s aphasia.
  5. Can’t repeat with intact production/comprehension → conduction aphasia (arcuate fasciculus).

B. Selective attention is an all-or-nothing gate that completely blocks unattended input
C. Attention depends only on working memory capacity (7±2) and not stimulus meaning
D. Unattended stimuli are never processed, but are reconstructed later by confabulation
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Explanation

Correct answer: A — Selective attention functions like a filter that can still allow highly salient stimuli to be processed

Explanation:

  • ✅ A. The cocktail party effect (detecting your name) shows unattended input can still be processed to some degree when it is salient/meaningful.
  • ❌ B. All-or-nothing blocking is contradicted by detecting the name.
  • ❌ C. Capacity matters, but salience/meaning clearly influences breakthrough of unattended stimuli.
  • ❌ D. Confabulation is a clinical memory-fabrication phenomenon; it’s not needed to explain consistent name detection.

  • ❌ C. Delirium tremens is severe alcohol withdrawal delirium; it’s not the focal language pattern described.
  • ✅ D. Fluent speech with poor comprehension and meaningless output is classic Wernicke’s aphasia (superior temporal gyrus, dominant hemisphere).

Q25. A third patient can understand and speak fluently but cannot repeat simple phrases. The most likely site of damage is:

A. The arcuate fasciculus connecting Broca’s and Wernicke’s areas
B. Broca’s area in the inferior frontal gyrus
C. The hippocampus
D. The pineal gland
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Explanation

Correct answer: A — The arcuate fasciculus connecting Broca’s and Wernicke’s areas

Explanation:

  • ✅ A. Conduction aphasia results from damage to the arcuate fasciculus, disrupting the link between comprehension and production needed for repetition.
  • ❌ B. Broca’s damage would primarily impair speech production/fluency.
  • ❌ C. Hippocampus damage affects memory consolidation, not selective repetition with intact comprehension/fluency.
  • ❌ D. Pineal gland relates to melatonin and circadian rhythms, not language repetition.
