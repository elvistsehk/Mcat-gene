MCAT Behavioral Sciences — Social Processes & Culture (8.1)
Standalone Questions (1–15)
1. During a lab practical, an experienced student performs a well-practiced pipetting routine faster while classmates watch. A novice student makes more errors when watched. This pattern best illustrates:
A. Deindividuation caused by anonymity and loss of self-awareness
B. Social loafing due to diffusion of responsibility in groups
C. Social facilitation explained by the Yerkes–Dodson relationship between arousal and performance
D. Groupthink driven by pressure for harmony in decision-making
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: C.

Why: Social facilitation means the mere presence of others raises arousal. By the Yerkes–Dodson law, higher arousal improves performance on simple or well-learned tasks but impairs complex/novel tasks.

Other choices: Social loafing is reduced effort in groups on shared tasks; deindividuation is loss of self-awareness and increased antinormative behavior; groupthink is faulty group decision-making driven by harmony.

2. A medical student who knows a procedure poorly is asked to demonstrate it in front of a large audience. Which change is most likely to explain a performance drop?
A. Reduced arousal lowers performance on simple tasks regardless of skill level
B. Increased arousal shifts the student past the optimal point on the Yerkes–Dodson curve for complex/novel tasks
C. Anonymity in the crowd eliminates evaluation and improves complex task performance
D. Peer pressure increases accuracy by strengthening internal locus of control
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: B.

Why: For difficult or unfamiliar tasks, too much arousal pushes performance down the right side of the inverted-U curve (over-arousal → errors).

Other choices: Arousal does not universally lower simple-task performance; anonymity tends to reduce evaluation apprehension rather than create it; peer pressure is about conforming to peers’ norms, not fixing skill deficits.

3. A masked crowd at a rivalry game begins throwing objects and vandalizing property. Which factor most directly increases the likelihood of this behavior?
A. Anonymity within a cohesive group that reduces personal accountability (deindividuation)
B. Low arousal that reduces risk-taking and impulsive behavior
C. Strong internal locus of control that increases personal responsibility
D. High individual self-awareness and private self-reflection
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A.

Why: Masks/uniforms + a cohesive crowd increase anonymity and diffuse personal responsibility, producing deindividuation and more antinormative behavior.

Other choices: High self-awareness typically restrains behavior; internal locus of control is about attributing outcomes to oneself; low arousal is not the driver of mob-like escalation described.

4. In a subway station, multiple people see someone collapse, but no one calls for help for several minutes. Which mechanism best accounts for this delay?
A. Assimilation pressures bystanders to adopt the victim’s cultural behaviors
B. Social facilitation improves emergency response because observers increase arousal
C. Group polarization makes individuals adopt more extreme helping norms
D. Diffusion of responsibility and social cues from others reduce perceived need to act (bystander effect)
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: D.

Why: With many bystanders, each person feels less personal responsibility (diffusion of responsibility) and may look to others for cues (pluralistic ignorance), delaying help.

Other choices: Group polarization is about becoming more extreme in beliefs after discussion; social facilitation is performance on tasks, not emergency helping; assimilation concerns cultural merging.

5. A professor notices that students contribute less in large group projects than when graded individually. Which intervention would most directly reduce the effect?
A. Add a competitive leaderboard to trigger deindividuation
B. Increase individual accountability by making each member’s contribution identifiable
C. Encourage members to remain anonymous to reduce evaluation apprehension
D. Increase group size so responsibility is shared more evenly
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: B.

Why: Social loafing is reduced effort when individual output is not identifiable. Making contributions measurable and personally accountable directly counters it.

Other choices: Larger groups usually worsen loafing; anonymity reduces accountability; competition may increase effort but the choice given mislabels mechanisms and does not address identifiability as directly.

✅Correct
❌Incorrect

Correct Answer: D.

Why: The teen first conforms to avoid rejection, then resolves the uncomfortable mismatch between behavior and prior preferences by adopting the group’s standards as their own—an identity shift that reduces cognitive dissonance.

Other choices: Self-handicapping creates excuses for failure; displacement redirects emotion to a safer target; learned helplessness involves giving up after uncontrollable outcomes.

Passages (3 passages × 3 questions)
Passage 1: Performance Under Observation in EMT Training

A fire department evaluates new EMT recruits on two stations. Station A is a simple, well-rehearsed sequence: assemble an oxygen mask and start oxygen flow. Station B is a complex scenario: interpret changing vital signs, choose an intervention, and communicate a plan while a supervisor asks rapid follow-up questions.

Recruits first practice alone in a quiet room. One week later, they repeat both stations in a public skills lab while ten peers watch from behind a glass wall. For Station A, average completion time drops from 55 s (alone) to 45 s (observed), with no change in error rate. For Station B, average completion time is similar, but decision errors rise from 8% (alone) to 22% (observed).

After the observed session, several recruits report that they felt "amped up" and hyper-aware of being evaluated, even though the peers behind the glass did not speak.

How to reason:

  1. Label task type: Station A is simple/well-learned; Station B is complex/novel.
  2. Map arousal → performance: social presence can raise arousal; the inverted-U predicts improvement for simple tasks but impairment for complex tasks.
  3. Use the numbers: faster time + stable errors suggests facilitation; higher error rate on complex decisions suggests over-arousal.
  4. Beware distractors: peer pressure changes beliefs/behavior to fit norms; here the peers are silent—think evaluation apprehension and arousal.
16. Which concept best explains why Station A improved while Station B showed more errors when peers were present?
A. Social facilitation: increased arousal enhances simple tasks but impairs complex tasks (Yerkes–Dodson)
B. Groupthink: consensus-seeking that reduces critical evaluation
C. Assimilation: adopting the dominant culture’s behaviors
D. Social loafing: reduced effort because others share responsibility
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A.

Why: The passage contrasts a simple, practiced task (time improves) with a complex decision task (errors increase) under observation—classic social facilitation via the Yerkes–Dodson curve.

Other choices: Social loafing would predict less effort on a shared group task; groupthink is faulty group decision-making; assimilation is cultural merging.

D. Recruits report stronger agreement with peers’ medical opinions after the session
✅Correct
❌Incorrect

Correct Answer: A.

Why: The mechanism is arousal under perceived evaluation. A direct biomarker of sympathetic arousal aligns with Yerkes–Dodson.

18. Suppose the peers behind the glass were replaced by a single expert evaluator who provides immediate written scoring. What change would you most predict for Station A?
A. Lower effort on Station A due to diffusion of responsibility among bystanders
B. Worse performance on Station A because any evaluation necessarily decreases arousal

Why: Evaluation generally raises arousal more than mere presence. For a simple/well-practiced task, moderate extra arousal can improve speed/precision—until over-arousal.

Passage 2: Online Committee, Extremes, and Consensus Pressure

A hospital forms an online committee to decide whether to adopt a new triage protocol. Before discussion, most members privately rate the protocol as "somewhat helpful" but note limitations. Over three days, members post messages in a shared forum that is visible to the whole group.

As the thread grows, members who favor adoption share success stories and upvote one another. A smaller number of skeptics post concerns about staffing and unintended consequences, but those posts receive little engagement. One senior member repeatedly replies, "We don’t need to overthink this," and summarizes the discussion by highlighting only pro-adoption arguments.

At the final vote, the group chooses an extremely aggressive adoption plan with minimal pilot testing. Later, an external auditor notes that several major risks were never seriously evaluated.

How to reason:

  1. Separate two group phenomena: moving to more extreme beliefs = group polarization; making a poor decision because dissent is suppressed = groupthink.
  2. Look for groupthink markers: pressure for conformity, self-censorship, illusion of unanimity, and mindguards (filtering info).
  3. Use passage evidence: upvotes amplify one side (polarization), and a senior member filters/discounts concerns (groupthink).
  4. Predict interventions: add structured dissent, outside review, or independent subgroups to reduce groupthink.

Passage 3: Neighborhood Change: Enclaves, Assimilation, and Subcultures

A city tracks outcomes for newcomers from Country X. Many families initially settle in a neighborhood where grocery stores, clinics, and after-school programs operate primarily in the heritage language. Over the next decade, two patterns emerge.

Pattern 1: Some residents remain concentrated in the neighborhood, marry within the community, and consume mostly heritage-language media. Their children often become bilingual but report feeling "at home" mainly inside the neighborhood.

Pattern 2: Other residents move throughout the city, predominantly use the majority language at work and school, and have higher rates of intermarriage. Their children report fewer heritage practices but feel comfortable navigating multiple social settings.

In the same city, a yearly festival celebrates multiple cultural traditions side-by-side. Meanwhile, a youth group adopts distinctive music and fashion to stand out, but they still endorse most mainstream rules and goals.

How to reason:

  1. Tag each pattern: concentrated neighborhood + heritage institutions → ethnic enclave; spreading out + intermarriage/language shift → assimilation.
  2. Use the four assimilation indicators mentioned in the chapter: socioeconomic status, geographic distribution, language attainment, intermarriage.
  3. Identify multiculturalism: public celebration of coexisting cultures (a mosaic).
  4. For youth groups: distinctive symbols without rejecting society → subculture; active opposition → counterculture.

Correct Answer: B.
