MCAT Behavioral Sciences — Chapter 5.2: Emotion
Standalone Questions (Q1–Q15)
Q1. A student feels their heart racing, notices their hands are shaking, and only then thinks, “I must be anxious about this exam.” Which element of emotion is being used to label the feeling?
A. Cognitive response (subjective interpretation of arousal).
B. Behavioral response (facial expression/body language).
C. Universal emotion expression (facial muscle pattern shared across cultures).
D. Physiological response (autonomic arousal itself).
Show explanation

The **cognitive response** is the *interpretation/label* you apply to what you’re experiencing. Here, the student observes arousal and then **labels** it as anxiety.

- **Physiological response** is the heart racing and shaking (the body changes), not the labeling.
- **Behavioral response** would be observable actions/expressions (posture, face, gestures).
- **Universal emotion expression** refers to cross-cultural facial expressions (Ekman/Darwin), not internal labeling.

Q2. Which option best matches one commonly taught set of seven universal facially expressed emotions in the passage?
A. Happiness, pride, shame, guilt, surprise, fear, and anger.
B. Happiness, sadness, contempt, surprise, fear, disgust, and anger.
C. Happiness, sadness, anxiety, jealousy, fear, disgust, and anger.
D. Joy, trust, anticipation, surprise, fear, disgust, and sadness.
Show explanation

The passage lists **seven universal facially expressed emotions** as: happiness, sadness, contempt, surprise, fear, disgust, and anger.

- **Pride/shame/guilt** are often considered *social emotions* and are not in this basic facial-expression set.
- **Trust/anticipation** appear in some other emotion taxonomies, not the Ekman-style list here.
- **Anxiety/jealousy** are not in the classic universal facial-expression list.

Q3. A researcher finds that severing peripheral sensory feedback from the body does NOT eliminate the conscious experience of fear in an animal model. Which theory does this finding most directly support?
A. James–Lange theory.
B. Cannon–Bard theory.
C. Instinct theory of motivation.
D. Schachter–Singer (two-factor) theory.
Show explanation

**Cannon–Bard** proposes that **physiological arousal and conscious emotion occur simultaneously**, so removing peripheral feedback should not abolish emotion.

- **James–Lange** predicts emotion depends on *peripheral feedback* (arousal first → label), so this finding challenges it.
- **Schachter–Singer** requires arousal + cognitive appraisal; it is not mainly a claim about peripheral feedback being necessary for emotion.
- **Instinct theory** is a motivation framework, not a mechanistic sequencing model for emotion.

Q4. Which sequence best describes the James–Lange theory of emotion?
A. Stimulus → conscious emotion → physiological arousal → action.
B. Stimulus → physiological arousal + cognitive appraisal → conscious emotion.
C. Stimulus → physiological arousal → conscious experience/label of emotion.
D. Stimulus → simultaneous arousal and conscious emotion → action.
Show explanation

In **James–Lange**, the body responds first (autonomic arousal), and the brain then **labels** that bodily state as an emotion.

- “Stimulus → emotion → arousal” reverses the James–Lange order.
- “Arousal + cognitive appraisal” is **Schachter–Singer**.
- “Simultaneous arousal and conscious emotion” is **Cannon–Bard**.

Q5. In the Cannon–Bard framework described, which brain structure is emphasized as sending sensory information simultaneously to the cortex and to the sympathetic nervous system?
A. Hippocampus.
B. Amygdala.
C. Ventromedial prefrontal cortex.
D. Thalamus.
Show explanation

The Cannon–Bard description highlights the **thalamus** as a relay that sends sensory information to both **cortex** and **sympathetic nervous system** at the same time.

- **Amygdala** is strongly tied to fear/attention and emotional learning, but it is not the primary relay described here.
- **Hippocampus** supports explicit memory/context.
- **Ventromedial prefrontal cortex** helps regulate emotional responses and decision-making.

Q6. A participant experiences unexplained physiological arousal and then adopts the emotion displayed by people around them. Which theory best explains this pattern?
A. James–Lange theory.
B. Schachter–Singer (two-factor) theory.
C. Drive reduction theory.
D. Cannon–Bard theory.
Show explanation

**Schachter–Singer** requires **physiological arousal + a cognitive label** derived from environmental cues. If arousal is unexplained, people may attribute it to the situation and adopt the displayed emotion.

- **James–Lange** emphasizes labeling arousal but does not center **context-driven appraisal/misattribution** as the defining feature.
- **Cannon–Bard** does not require cognitive labeling from context.
- **Drive reduction** is a motivation theory about relieving internal tension.

Q7. A subject shows a wrinkled nose and raised upper lip while viewing spoiled food. Which universal emotion and cue pairing is most consistent with the table?
A. Contempt — one corner of the mouth pulled upward.
B. Disgust — nose wrinkling and/or raising of the upper lip.
C. Surprise — jaw opens with widened eyes.
D. Fear — lips pulled toward the ears.
Show explanation

The described cue (nose wrinkling/upper lip raise) is classic for **disgust**.

- **Fear** often includes widened eyes and lips pulled back, but the nose-wrinkle is more disgust.
- **Surprise** includes widened eyes and open jaw.
- **Contempt** is typically a unilateral mouth-corner raise.

Q8. After bilateral amygdala damage, a person shows intact episodic memory for a traumatic event but fails to develop autonomic fear conditioning to similar cues later. This pattern most strongly implicates the amygdala in:
A. Primary formation of long-term explicit memories.
B. Generating physiological arousal via the adrenal medulla.
C. Routing all sensory input to the cortex.
D. Implicit emotional learning (fear conditioning) rather than explicit episodic recall.
Show explanation

The passage notes that amygdala damage impairs **fear conditioning**—a form of **implicit emotional memory/learning**—while explicit “story-like” memory is more hippocampal.

- **Explicit long-term memory formation** is primarily hippocampus/medial temporal lobe.
- **Sensory routing to cortex** is largely thalamic.
- **Adrenal medulla** helps generate sympathetic output; the amygdala can trigger responses but is not the peripheral effector.

Q11. A lab uses galvanic skin response (skin conductance) to index sympathetic activation. Based on the passage, the best interpretation is that skin conductance:
A. Directly measures cognitive appraisal of the stimulus.

Show explanation

Skin conductance correlates with **sympathetic arousal**, but multiple emotions can share arousal patterns; it is **not emotion-specific**.

- Cognitive appraisal is a mental process; not what conductance measures.
- Conductance is not primarily parasympathetic-driven.

Passage 1: Misattributed Arousal in a Waiting Room

A behavioral lab recruits volunteers for a “vitamin and attention” study. Participants receive an injection that is either saline or epinephrine. Half of the participants are accurately told that the injection may cause a racing heart and sweaty palms; the other half are told the injection has no side effects. While waiting, each participant sits with a confederate who behaves either extremely cheerful (telling jokes, laughing) or visibly irritated (complaining, slamming a clipboard). Researchers later ask participants to rate how happy or angry they felt during the wait and record heart rate throughout.

Reasoning guide
1) Identify the key evidence: **physiological arousal** (epinephrine → ↑HR) plus **environmental cues** (cheerful vs irritated confederate).
2) Ask which theory requires *both* arousal and a *cognitive label from context*.
3) Predict the strongest emotional report when arousal is **present but unexplained**, making misattribution to context more likely.
4) Map group patterns to theory (two-factor vs James–Lange vs Cannon–Bard).
Q16. (Passage 1) Which group should report the most intense emotion that matches the confederate’s mood (highest happiness with cheerful confederate, highest anger with irritated confederate)?
A. Saline + informed, because expectation of arousal creates the strongest emotions.
B. Epinephrine + uninformed about side effects (told “no side effects”), because arousal is unexplained and gets labeled from context.
C. Saline + uninformed, because there is no arousal to misattribute.
D. Epinephrine + informed, because knowing the cause of arousal increases emotion labeling from context.
Show explanation

Two-factor (Schachter–Singer) predicts strongest emotion when **arousal is high** and the person lacks a clear explanation, so they **attribute** it to the environment. Thus, **epinephrine + uninformed** should show the biggest context-dependent ratings.

- Saline groups have less arousal, so less to misattribute.
- Informed epinephrine participants can attribute arousal to the injection, reducing context-driven emotion ratings.
- Expectation without arousal (saline informed) does not create strong emotion in the same way.

Q17. (Passage 1) The overall pattern described is most consistent with which theory of emotion?
A. Schachter–Singer (two-factor) theory.
B. James–Lange theory.
C. Opponent-process theory.
D. Cannon–Bard theory.
Show explanation

The design tests whether **physiological arousal** plus **cognitive appraisal of the environment** determines the emotion reported—this is classic **Schachter–Singer**.

- James–Lange emphasizes arousal-first labeling but not the defining misattribution mechanism.
- Cannon–Bard does not require cognitive labeling from context.
- Opponent-process addresses tolerance/withdrawal from repeated drug use.

Q18. (Passage 1) Which added manipulation would most cleanly test whether participants are using environmental cues to label arousal (rather than merely reacting to the confederate)?
A. Replace heart-rate measurement with a multiple-choice quiz about emotion theory.
B. Include an epinephrine group that waits alone (no confederate), then compare emotion ratings to those with a confederate.
C. Use only saline injections so arousal is eliminated.
D. Tell everyone the injection causes intense nausea to increase placebo effects.
Show explanation

To isolate **contextual labeling**, keep arousal present but **remove the emotional cue** (no confederate). If ratings become more neutral, that supports labeling from context.

- Saline removes the key arousal variable.
- Nausea expectations confound appraisal.
- A quiz tests knowledge, not mechanism.

Passage 2: Afferent Feedback and Emotional Experience

A clinician evaluates two patients after different neurological injuries. Patient A has a spinal cord injury that markedly reduces bodily feedback from below the neck, but vision and hearing are intact. Patient B has no spinal injury but shows abnormal thalamic processing on imaging. In a lab, both patients watch a virtual-reality snake rapidly approach. Heart rate, facial expression, and self-reported fear are measured. Patient A shows a fearful facial expression and reports strong fear despite a blunted autonomic response. Patient B shows normal autonomic arousal but reports a muted conscious fear experience.

Reasoning guide
1) Separate the three elements: **physiology** (HR/ANS), **behavior** (face), **cognition** (reported fear).
2) Ask what each theory predicts when **peripheral feedback is reduced** (James–Lange would expect reduced emotion).
3) Use the thalamus role in Cannon–Bard: it helps send sensory info to cortex and ANS.
4) Match each patient’s pattern to the theory’s bottleneck (peripheral vs central).
Q19. (Passage 2) Patient A’s strong reported fear despite blunted autonomic feedback most directly challenges which theory?
A. Need-based theories of motivation.
B. Schachter–Singer theory.
C. Cannon–Bard theory.
D. James–Lange theory.
Show explanation

James–Lange predicts that **peripheral arousal precedes and enables** the conscious emotion label. If bodily feedback is reduced, James–Lange would expect **less** emotion. Patient A still reports strong fear, challenging that prediction.

- Cannon–Bard allows emotion without requiring peripheral feedback.
- Schachter–Singer can still yield emotion via appraisal even if arousal is altered.
- Need-based theories address motivation, not emotion sequencing.

Q20. (Passage 2) Patient B’s normal autonomic arousal but muted conscious fear most strongly implicates disruption in which processing step emphasized in the passage’s emotion theories?
A. Central routing of sensory information to conscious emotional experience (thalamic relay to cortex).
B. The patient’s facial muscles cannot express fear.
C. Peripheral organs generating sympathetic arousal (adrenal medulla) are failing.
D. The hippocampus cannot store implicit emotional memories.
Show explanation

Patient B has abnormal **thalamic processing** and shows arousal but reduced *conscious* fear. That points to disruption in **central processing/relay to cortex**.

- Autonomic arousal is intact, so peripheral generation is not failing.
- Facial impairment is not described.
- Implicit emotional memory is more amygdala-linked than hippocampal.

Q21. (Passage 2) Which theory best predicts that conscious fear and autonomic arousal can occur simultaneously, such that altering peripheral feedback alone need not eliminate fear?
A. James–Lange theory.
B. Instinct theory of motivation.
C. Schachter–Singer theory (requires misattribution).
D. Cannon–Bard theory.
Show explanation

**Cannon–Bard**: stimulus → **simultaneous** arousal and conscious emotion (via central pathways), then action. Peripheral feedback removal does not necessarily abolish the emotion.

- James–Lange relies on arousal-first sequencing.
- Schachter–Singer is defined by arousal + appraisal, not simultaneity as the core claim.
- Instinct theory is not a sequencing model for emotion processing.

Passage 3: Emotion, Memory, and Regulation in PTSD

A PTSD clinic uses fMRI while patients view neutral scenes and then scenes that resemble their trauma context (same street layout and sounds). Compared with controls, PTSD patients show heightened amygdala activity during trauma-like scenes, reduced hippocampal engagement during narrative recall of the event, and reduced ventromedial prefrontal cortex (vmPFC) activity during attempts to calm down. In a separate task, they are slower and less accurate at recognizing subtle facial expressions, especially when faces are presented briefly in the left visual field.

Reasoning guide
1) Map structures to functions: **amygdala → fear/emotional learning**, **hippocampus → explicit context/story**, **vmPFC → regulating amygdala-driven responses**.
2) PTSD logic: strong implicit emotional responses can coexist with fragmented explicit narrative.
3) Facial-expression task: left visual field projects to **right hemisphere**, and the passage notes right hemisphere dominance for facial processing.
4) Predict behavior from the imaging findings.
Q22. (Passage 3) The combination of heightened amygdala activity and reduced hippocampal engagement best predicts which clinical profile?
A. Strong conditioned emotional reactions to cues with a fragmented or less coherent explicit narrative of the trauma.
B. Complete loss of all emotional experience with preserved autonomic arousal.
C. Normal fear responses but perfect detailed episodic recall of the trauma.
D. Improved facial-expression recognition with reduced emotional reactivity.
Show explanation

Amygdala hyperactivity suggests strong **implicit emotional memory/conditioning**, while reduced hippocampal engagement suggests weaker **explicit contextual narrative**. That combination fits intense cue-triggered reactions plus a less coherent “story.”

- The other options contradict the structure–function mapping described.

Q23. (Passage 3) Reduced vmPFC activity during attempts to calm down most directly suggests impairment in:
A. Generation of sympathetic arousal by peripheral organs.
B. Primary sensory relay from the retina to the cortex.
C. Top-down regulation of emotional responses generated by limbic structures like the amygdala.
D. Long-term storage of episodic memories.
Show explanation

The passage notes **vmPFC** helps control/shape emotional responses from the **amygdala**. Reduced vmPFC activity would impair **regulation** (top-down control).

- Sensory relay is thalamic/visual pathways.
- Episodic memory storage is hippocampal.
- Peripheral sympathetic arousal is ANS/endocrine.

Q24. (Passage 3) Patients are especially impaired when faces are presented in the left visual field. Based on the passage, the most likely explanation is:
A. Facial-expression recognition depends primarily on the cerebellum, which is damaged in PTSD.
B. The left hemisphere is specialized for negative emotions only, so it blocks facial processing.
C. Left visual field inputs project to the right hemisphere, which is more active in discerning facial expressions.
D. Left visual field inputs bypass the thalamus and go directly to the amygdala.
Show explanation

Information from the **left visual field** projects to the **right hemisphere**, and the passage states the right hemisphere is more active for **facial-expression discrimination**. Therefore, a right-hemisphere-linked deficit would show up strongly for left-field presentations.

- Inputs do not bypass the thalamus.
- The hemisphere specialization described is not “left blocks faces.”
- Cerebellum is not the primary facial-expression interpretation center here.
