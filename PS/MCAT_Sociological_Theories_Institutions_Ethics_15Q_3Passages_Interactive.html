Standalone Questions (Q1–Q15)
1. A sociologist notes that the “peace sign” originally represented nuclear disarmament, but later came to symbolize peace and freedom more broadly. Which theoretical approach is most directly being applied?
A. Rational choice (exchange) theory
B. Conflict theory
C. Social constructionism
D. Structural-functionalism
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: C.

Why: Social constructionism emphasizes that meanings are not inherent in objects or symbols; they are created and maintained by social agreement and can change over time.

Other choices:

  • A: Rational choice focuses on individuals maximizing perceived rewards vs costs in decisions.
  • B: Conflict theory focuses on power struggles and inequality at the macro level.
  • D: Structural-functionalism asks how institutions contribute to social stability and cohesion, not how meanings evolve.
2. A resident considers whether to volunteer for an extra clinic shift. They list potential “rewards” (praise from the program director, stronger evaluations) and “costs” (lost free time, fatigue), then choose the option with the best balance. This reasoning best exemplifies:
A. Rational choice (exchange) theory
B. Symbolic interactionism
C. Feminist theory
D. Social constructionism
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A.

Why: Rational choice (social exchange) theory models social actions as transactions where people weigh perceived benefits and costs (social approval, prestige, sanctions, etc.).

Other choices:

  • B: Symbolic interactionism focuses on interpreting symbols and meanings in interaction.
  • C: Feminist theory critiques gendered power structures and institutional disadvantages.
  • D: Social constructionism examines how societies collectively construct concepts (e.g., money, norms).
3. A sociologist studies the U.S. healthcare system by asking which groups (patients, hospitals, insurers, pharmaceutical companies, government) control resources and shape policies. Which approach best matches this analysis?
A. Symbolic interactionism
B. Social constructionism
C. Structural-functionalism
D. Conflict theory
✅Correct
❌Incorrect

Correct Answer: D.

Why: Conflict theory (macro-level) emphasizes power differentials, control of resources, and how groups with power preserve the status quo while less powerful groups organize for change.

Other choices:

  • A: Symbolic interactionism is micro/meso and focuses on meaning-making via symbols.
  • B: Social constructionism focuses on how concepts are socially agreed upon (e.g., “disease”), not primarily on resource control.
  • C: Structural-functionalism emphasizes cohesion and the functions of institutions in maintaining equilibrium.

B. Symbolic interactionism

❌Incorrect

Correct Answer: B.

Why: Symbolic interactionism explains human interaction by analyzing symbols and the meanings people attach to them through social interaction and interpretation.

Other choices:

  • A: Conflict theory emphasizes inequality and group struggle.
  • C: Structural-functionalism emphasizes how institutions maintain social stability.
  • D: Feminist theory centers gendered power structures.
5. A sociologist argues that formal schooling does more than teach math and reading. It also creates peer networks and reinforces social stratification through tracking. From a structural-functional perspective, the peer-networking and stratification effects are best labeled as:
A. Latent functions
B. Manifest functions
C. Dysfunctions
D. Social constructs
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A.

Why: In structural-functionalism, manifest functions are intended outcomes; latent functions are unintended but beneficial outcomes. Peer socialization and identity formation are classic latent functions of schooling.

Other choices:

  • B: Teaching academic skills is the manifest function described in the chapter.
  • C: Dysfunctions are negative consequences; “reinforcing stratification” can be argued as harmful, but the stem frames the effects as additional roles of schooling. On the MCAT, “peer socialization” is the clean latent-function choice.
  • D: “Social constructs” are collectively created ideas; not the functionalist label tested here.

8. A hospital allocates a scarce ICU bed. Two patients have similar medical need, but the team prioritizes one based on celebrity status. Which medical ethics tenet is most directly violated?
A. Beneficence
B. Nonmaleficence
C. Respect for autonomy
D. Justice
✅Correct
❌Incorrect

Correct Answer: D.

Why: Justice requires treating similar patients similarly and distributing resources fairly. Prioritizing based on fame is unfair allocation.

Other choices:

  • A: Beneficence is acting in an individual patient’s best interest; the issue here is fairness between patients.
  • B: Nonmaleficence is avoiding harm; the stem emphasizes biased distribution rather than iatrogenic harm.
  • C: Autonomy concerns respecting patient choices; it does not address allocation fairness.

Passages (3 passages × 3 questions)
Passage 1: The White Coat, the “Code,” and the Chart

On an inpatient service, a new medical student notices that staff members behave differently depending on what the student is wearing. When the student wears a short white coat, nurses offer fewer corrections and families frequently address the student as “Doctor.” When the student wears plain scrubs without a coat, staff members speak more casually and patients ask fewer questions.

The student also overhears a team member call out, “Code blue!” Immediately, several people run toward a room. Later, the student learns that the same words may trigger very different responses in other hospitals because some sites use different “codes” for emergencies.

Finally, the hospital’s quality committee recently expanded the definition of “medication error” to include delayed documentation. Some clinicians argue this change will improve safety culture; others argue it will inflate the hospital’s error statistics without changing patient outcomes.

How to reason:

  1. Identify whether the passage is about meaning in interaction (symbols) vs society constructing categories (social constructs) vs cost–benefit choices.
  2. Clues for symbolic interactionism: clothing/labels/words that change behavior because of shared meanings.
  3. Clues for social constructionism: a committee changes a definition/category; the “thing” changes because people agree to define it differently.
  4. When asked about decisions, look for explicit weighing of social rewards/punishments → rational choice.
16. The change in how staff and families respond to the student depending on whether the white coat is worn is best explained by:
A. Conflict theory
B. Structural-functionalism
C. Feminist theory
D. Symbolic interactionism
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: D.

Why: The white coat functions as a symbol that carries socially learned meaning (authority/physician identity), altering interactions. That is the core of symbolic interactionism.

Other choices:

  • A: Conflict theory could discuss hierarchy and power broadly, but the mechanism highlighted is meaning attached to a symbol.
  • B: Functionalism focuses on how institutions maintain stability; it does not focus on micro-level symbol interpretation.
  • C: Feminist theory centers gendered power structures; not indicated here.
17. The quality committee’s decision to expand the definition of “medication error” is most directly an example of:
A. Social constructionism
B. Conflict theory
C. Symbolic interactionism
D. Rational choice theory
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A.

Why: A social construct is an idea/category defined by social agreement. Changing what “counts” as a medication error changes the construct via institutional agreement—classic social constructionism.

Other choices:

  • B: Conflict theory could apply if the focus were power struggles between groups; the stem emphasizes category definition.
  • C: Symbolic interactionism emphasizes the meaning of symbols in interaction; here the emphasis is institutional redefinition.
  • D: Rational choice would emphasize individuals weighing costs/benefits in decisions, not redefining categories.

Passage 2: A School District’s “Improvement Plan”

A city implements a new districtwide education initiative aimed at improving standardized test scores. The official plan includes additional tutoring hours, new textbooks, and teacher training sessions. Within months, students report forming new friendships in tutoring groups and spending more time socializing on campus.

Meanwhile, teachers are instructed to identify “high-potential” students for advanced tracks. Some teachers assume students from one neighborhood are “not academic,” set lower expectations, and provide less challenging feedback. Those students’ performance later declines, which administrators cite as evidence that the neighborhood “needs stricter discipline.”

Parents from the affected neighborhood form an advocacy coalition, attend school board meetings, and lobby for equal funding and transparent criteria for placement in advanced tracks.

How to reason:

  1. When asked about manifest vs latent, separate the stated purpose from unintended benefits.
  2. When you see expectation → changed treatment → outcome that “confirms” the expectation, recognize a self-fulfilling prophecy (teacher expectancy).
  3. For theory selection: conflict theory looks for power/resource inequality and collective action by disadvantaged groups; functionalism looks for social cohesion and roles.
  4. Use passage evidence (who holds power? what resources? what actions?) to justify your choice.

✅Correct

Passage 3: Promotions, “Leadership Fit,” and Care Ethics

A large hospital system announces a goal of “leadership diversity” but continues to promote employees through informal sponsorship. Several women physicians report being praised as “excellent clinicians” yet repeatedly told they are “not quite leadership material,” even when their performance metrics match male colleagues. In one department, a male physician receives frequent invitations to leadership meetings and is promoted within a year despite expressing little interest in management.

Separately, the system’s ethics committee debates whether a costly new therapy should be offered only to patients with private insurance. Committee members note that the therapy could help some patients, but that limiting it by insurance status may worsen disparities.

In internal discussions, executives argue that “leadership material” is an objective trait; critics argue it is shaped by shared expectations of what leaders “look like” and “sound like” in medicine.

How to reason:

  1. For feminist theory questions, look for institutional power structures that disadvantage women (promotion barriers, biased sponsorship).
  2. Differentiate glass ceiling (barriers for women reaching top roles) from glass escalator (men propelled upward in female-typed settings).
  3. For ethics, decide which principle is being tested: justice (fair distribution), beneficence (best interest), nonmaleficence (avoid harm), autonomy (respect choices).
  4. For “objective trait vs shared expectations,” consider whether “leadership material” is a social construct shaped by agreement and norms.

D. Rational choice (purely individual cost–benefit decisions)

❌Incorrect

Correct Answer: C.

Why: Feminist theory critiques institutional and cultural power structures that sustain gender stratification (e.g., biased promotion norms, “leadership fit” gatekeeping).

Other choices:

  • A: Functionalism focuses on cohesion; it is not the best tool for analyzing inequality mechanisms.
  • B: Symbolic interactionism can discuss meanings of labels, but the core issue here is structural disadvantage by gender.
  • D: Rational choice alone does not explain consistent gendered barriers when metrics are equal.
