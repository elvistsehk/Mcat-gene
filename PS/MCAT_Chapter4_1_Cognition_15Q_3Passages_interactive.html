15 Standalone MCAT-style Questions (Q1–Q15)
1. A student remembers the steps of glycolysis after weeks of studying, but during the exam they “blank” even though they can recognize the right answer when they see it later. The most likely breakdown during the exam is in:
A. Retrieval of stored information
B. Sensory memory formation
C. Object permanence
D. Primary circular reactions
✔️Correct!
❌Incorrect
Explanation (click to expand/collapse)
The information-processing model emphasizes encoding → storage → retrieval. If the student can recognize the answer later, the knowledge was likely stored, but couldn’t be accessed on demand during the exam—this is a retrieval problem.
  • B sensory memory lasts seconds or less and is not the bottleneck for learned biochemical steps.
  • C object permanence is an infant milestone (sensorimotor stage).
  • D primary circular reactions are repetitive infant behaviors, not exam performance.
2. A lecturer provides both a diagram of cardiac blood flow and a spoken explanation. According to Paivio’s dual-coding theory, the main advantage is that:
A. Visual encoding always replaces verbal encoding
B. Two representational codes create redundancy, improving later access with different cues
C. Short-term memory capacity increases beyond 7 ± 2
D. Retrieval becomes automatic and emotion-free
✔️Correct!
❌Incorrect
Explanation (click to expand/collapse)
Dual-coding proposes that verbal associations + visual images can store similar content, creating redundancy. If one cue fails (words), another cue (image) may still trigger retrieval.
  • A is incorrect: dual-coding uses both, not replacement.
  • C confuses coding strategy with STM capacity limits.
  • D memory is influenced by context/emotion; dual-coding doesn’t remove that.
3. A clinician applies a successful communication strategy from one patient encounter to a new patient but modifies it for different cultural expectations. This best reflects:
A. Centration
B. Egocentrism
C. Situational modification (extrapolating and adjusting prior decisions)
D. Primary circular reactions
✔️Correct!
❌Incorrect
Explanation (click to expand/collapse)
The model highlights that decisions can be extrapolated and adjusted to solve new problems—this is situational modification. The clinician transfers a strategy but adapts it to context.
  • A centration is a preoperational limitation (focus on one feature only).
  • B egocentrism is inability to take others’ perspectives (also preoperational).
  • D is an infant sensorimotor pattern, unrelated to adult problem solving.
4. A 4-year-old insists that a tall, thin glass has “more juice” than a short, wide glass even after watching the same juice be poured between them. Piaget would attribute this error primarily to:
A. Object permanence
B. Hypothetical reasoning
C. Conservation mastery
D. Centration during the preoperational stage
✔️Correct!
❌Incorrect
Explanation (click to expand/collapse)
Preoperational children often fail conservation because of centration—they focus on one salient feature (height) and ignore width/volume.
  • A object permanence is sensorimotor (0–2 years).
  • B hypothetical reasoning is formal operational (≈11+ years).
  • C is the ability for concrete operational kids, but the child shows the opposite (no conservation).
5. A child repeatedly throws a spoon from a high chair and smiles when a parent picks it up, then immediately throws it again. Piaget would classify this pattern as:
A. A secondary circular reaction in the sensorimotor stage
B. Egocentrism in the preoperational stage
C. Hypothetical reasoning in the formal operational stage
D. Crystallized intelligence in late adulthood
✔️Correct!
❌Incorrect
Explanation (click to expand/collapse)
Secondary circular reactions are repetitive behaviors focused on the external environment (dropping/throwing objects) because the action produces an interesting response.
  • B is a preoperational limitation about perspective-taking, not repetitive sensorimotor exploration.
  • C refers to systematic variable control, far beyond infancy.
  • D is an aging/intelligence concept, not early development.
6. A toddler laughs when an adult “disappears” behind hands during peek-a-boo. The developmental concept most directly involved is:
A. Conservation
B. Object permanence (a sensorimotor milestone)
C. Centration
D. Delirium tremens
✔️Correct!

Explanation (click to expand/collapse)
Peek-a-boo is fun for very young infants because they may not yet fully grasp object permanence—the idea that objects/people continue to exist when out of view.
  • A conservation is mastered in concrete operational (≈7–11 years).
  • C centration is a preoperational error (≈2–7 years).
  • D is a severe alcohol-withdrawal delirium, unrelated to infants.
7. A child calls every four-legged animal “dog.” After seeing a cat, the child updates their categories so that “dog” and “cat” are separate. This update is best described as:
A. Assimilation of the cat into the existing “dog” schema
B. Egocentrism

D. Fluid intelligence crystallizing into procedural memory

❌Incorrect

Accommodation occurs when existing schemata are modified to incorporate information that doesn’t fit neatly (splitting “dog” into dog vs cat).
  • A would be labeling the cat as a dog (fitting new info into the old schema without changing it).
  • B is a perspective-taking limitation, not category restructuring.
  • D mixes unrelated concepts; Piaget’s terms are about schemata, not intelligence types.
8. A 9-year-old can solve “If you have 12 apples and give away 5, how many remain?” but struggles with “What is justice?” Piaget would say the child is most likely in the:

Concrete operational children (≈7–11) can reason logically about concrete, directly manipulable information but have limited abstract reasoning.

📘 Passage 1 — Dual Coding, Context, and Aging

A medical school designs a training module to teach 30 unfamiliar clinical symbols (icons used on a hospital dashboard). Students are randomly assigned to one of two encoding conditions. In the Verbal-only condition, each symbol is paired with a spoken definition and a written label. In the Dual-code condition, the same verbal information is provided, but students also see a vivid image-based story that visually links the icon to its meaning (for example, an icon resembling a droplet is paired with a short image-story of a “drip” filling an IV bag).

Two days later, students complete two tests. Test 1 is a free-response task: for each icon, students must write its meaning (recall). Test 2 is multiple-choice: students choose the correct meaning from four options (recognition). Half of each encoding group takes the test in the same classroom used for studying, while the other half tests in a different room with different lighting, background noise, and seating.

The study includes two age cohorts: a younger cohort (ages 22–26) and an older cohort (ages 65–72). Across cohorts, Dual-code students outperform Verbal-only students on the free-response test. The room change slightly reduces free-response performance for Verbal-only students, but produces minimal change for Dual-code students. On the multiple-choice test, the performance difference between groups is smaller than on free-response. In addition, older adults show a larger gap between learning brand-new icons and applying well-practiced clinical routines that they have used for years.

How to reason (Passage 1):
  1. Identify the theory: “verbal + image-based story” points to dual-coding → redundancy in retrieval routes.
  2. Use test format: free-response = recall (harder); multiple-choice = recognition (easier because cues are provided).
  3. Use context manipulation: same vs different room tests the context effect. Richer encoding can reduce reliance on external context cues.
  4. Interpret aging pattern: difficulty with novel learning suggests fluid intelligence declines more than crystallized intelligence.
  5. Eliminate distractors: Piaget stages/circular reactions are childhood development labels, not adult encoding experiments.
16. The finding that Dual-code students outperform Verbal-only students on free-response is best explained by:
A. Dual coding prevents all interference by eliminating competing memories
B. Dual coding increases sensory memory duration from seconds to days
C. Dual coding converts explicit knowledge into procedural memory
D. Dual coding creates redundant retrieval paths (verbal + visual), increasing access during recall
✔️Correct!
❌Incorrect
Explanation (click to expand/collapse)
Dual-coding theory proposes that using both verbal and visual codes increases the chance that some cue will successfully activate the stored meaning—especially important for recall, which requires generating the answer without options.
  • A is too absolute: dual coding can help but does not eliminate interference.
  • B confuses short-lived sensory stores with long-term learning.
  • C is wrong: meanings of icons are declarative/explicit, not a motor skill habit.
17. The smaller performance drop from changing rooms in Dual-code students most strongly suggests that their recall is:
A. Less dependent on context cues because the memory has richer internal associations
B. Stronger only because older adults have superior object permanence
C. Driven by centration on a single feature of each icon
D. An example of delirium-related fluctuation in cognition
✔️Correct!
❌Incorrect
Explanation (click to expand/collapse)
A context effect means recall improves when the retrieval environment matches encoding. If dual-coding builds stronger internal links, recall relies less on external context cues—so changing rooms hurts less.
  • B object permanence is an infant milestone, irrelevant to adult testing rooms.
  • C centration is a preoperational limitation, not a mechanism for improved adult recall.
  • D delirium is acute medical confusion; nothing suggests that here.

Learning novel icons is a new problem (fluid intelligence), while applying familiar routines relies on acquired knowledge (crystallized). The chapter describes fluid as declining earlier, with crystallized remaining more stable.

📘 Passage 2 — Schemata, Conservation, and Hypothesis Testing

A developmental psychologist evaluates children’s reasoning using two tasks. Task A is a liquid conservation task: two identical cups contain equal amounts of colored water. The child agrees the amounts are the same. The researcher then pours the water from one cup into a taller, narrower cylinder and asks which container has more water and why.

Task B is a “mini pendulum” task: children can change (i) string length, (ii) weight, and (iii) how hard they push. They are told to find what determines how fast the pendulum swings. The researcher observes whether children hold variables constant and test one factor at a time.

Child X (age 5) says the tall cylinder has more water because it is “higher.” In Task B, Child X changes multiple variables at once and concludes, “Heavier weights always swing faster,” even when their own trials do not consistently show this. Child Y (age 10) says the water is the same amount and explains, “It’s just spread out differently.” In Task B, Child Y is more systematic but still occasionally changes two variables together when impatient.

After the tasks, the researcher shows Child X a cat for the first time. Child X calls it a “dog.” The researcher points out differences (meow, whiskers, and ear shape), and Child X begins using the word “cat” for cats while still calling dogs “dog.”

How to reason (Passage 2):
  1. Use hallmark errors: conservation failure + “height” reasoning suggests preoperational centration (≈2–7).
  2. Conservation success: “same amount, spread differently” suggests concrete operational (≈7–11).
  3. Pendulum logic: one-variable testing is formal operational (≈11+); partial systematic behavior at age 10 fits late concrete ops.
  4. Schema update: calling a cat “dog” is assimilation; splitting cat vs dog after feedback is accommodation.
  5. Pick evidence-based options: match the choice to what the child actually says/does.

  • B would predict the child says the amounts are the same.
  • D would predict systematic one-variable hypothesis testing, not random changes plus biased conclusions.

📘 Passage 3 — Rapid Confusion vs Progressive Decline

An emergency department evaluates two older patients who arrive with family members. Patient A’s family reports that over the last 18 months the patient has gradually become more forgetful, has difficulty managing finances, and sometimes gets lost while driving familiar routes. The family also notes subtle personality changes and increasing need for help with activities of daily living. Today, the patient seems “about the same as last week.”

Patient B’s family reports the patient was functioning independently yesterday morning. By yesterday evening, the patient became disoriented, could not maintain attention during conversation, and alternated between agitation and drowsiness. On exam today, the patient’s level of alertness fluctuates from minute to minute. Vital signs show fever, and labs reveal an elevated white blood cell count. The family notes the patient has had poor oral intake for two days and recently started a new medication.

The clinician must decide which pattern is likely reversible, which broad category of causes is most likely, and which cognitive steps are most disrupted when attention cannot be sustained. The clinician also recalls that some cognitive skills remain strong with age, especially those based on long-practiced knowledge and routines.

How to reason (Passage 3):
  1. Time course is everything: months/years progressive decline suggests dementia; hours–days with fluctuation suggests delirium.
  2. Look for medical triggers: fever, high WBC, dehydration, new meds point to medical causes (infection/metabolic/drug reaction).
  3. Map to information processing: inability to sustain attention commonly disrupts encoding (new info can’t be reliably “put into memory”).
  4. Reversibility: delirium is often reversible if the cause is treated; dementia is generally progressive.
  5. Age + skills: preserved well-learned routines relate to crystallized intelligence.

A. Patient B shows a gradual decline over years with stable attention
B. Patient B shows rapid, fluctuating cognition likely linked to medical causes and often reversible
C. Patient B is best explained by preoperational centration
D. Patient B is best explained by object permanence failure
✔️Correct!
❌Incorrect
Explanation (click to expand/collapse)
Patient B’s acute onset, fluctuating alertness, and medical signs (fever, high WBC, dehydration, new meds) match delirium, which is often reversible when the medical cause is treated. Patient A’s gradual course fits dementia.
  • A describes dementia more than delirium.
  • C and D are child development concepts unrelated to acute medical confusion.
23. Patient B’s inability to maintain attention most directly disrupts which information-processing step, making new learning unreliable?

✔️Correct!
❌Incorrect
Explanation (click to expand/collapse)
The information-processing model requires attention to encode stimuli into meaningful neural signals. If attention fluctuates, new information is inconsistently encoded, so later memory appears “lost” even if storage systems are intact.
  • A and B are developmental milestones, not information-processing steps.
  • D is an infant behavior pattern, not an information-processing stage.
