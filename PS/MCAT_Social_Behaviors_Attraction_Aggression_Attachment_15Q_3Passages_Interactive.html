Passage 1: A Dorm, a Study Group, and a “Liked” Signal

Ravi transfers to a new university and is assigned a room in a large dormitory. During the first week, he repeatedly sees two students—Mina and Jo—near the laundry room and elevator, and they begin exchanging quick greetings. By midsemester, Ravi ends up sitting next to Mina in an evening review session because the only open seats are in the front row. Over the next month, Mina and Ravi walk back to the dorm together after class several times a week and often talk about how overwhelmed they feel by exams.

One day Ravi overhears Jo telling a friend, “Ravi is actually pretty cool; I like studying with him.” Ravi later notices that when Jo invites him to join a weekend project, Ravi feels unexpectedly excited to accept—even though the project topic is not especially interesting to him.

At the end of the semester, Ravi is surprised to realize he now prefers sitting near Mina and Jo during lectures, even when other seats are available.

How to reason:

  1. Split the passage into attraction mechanisms: proximity (physical closeness), mere exposure (repeated contact), self-disclosure (sharing vulnerabilities), reciprocity/reciprocal liking (belief others like you).
  2. For each question, identify which cue is the strongest causal lever (e.g., “overheard they like me” → reciprocal liking).
  3. When two cues both fit, ask which is more specific to the behavior change described (excitement to accept invitation vs general familiarity).
  4. Choose the answer that explains the behavior shift without requiring extra assumptions.

Passage 2: Heat, Hunger, and “Brakes” on Threat

A city installs temporary cooling stations during a summer heat wave. A public health team compares two neighborhoods that are similar in population density and traffic volume. In Neighborhood A, the stations are placed near bus stops and distribute water and shade. In Neighborhood B, logistical delays prevent the stations from opening until late afternoon.

During the heat wave, researchers record a higher rate of hostile honking, shouting between drivers, and threatening gestures in Neighborhood B. At the same time, they note that many residents in Neighborhood B report skipped meals due to long work hours and that a popular sports bar is promoting discounted alcohol drinks each day.

In a separate clinical study, patients with damage affecting prefrontal functioning show increased impulsive reactions to ambiguous social cues that they interpret as “provocative,” even when no physical confrontation occurs.

How to reason:

  1. First decide whether the behaviors qualify as aggression (intent to harm/dominance) or just negative emotion. Threat gestures and hostile shouting can count as aggression.
  2. Map situational triggers (heat, hunger, pain, frustration) to the cognitive neoassociation model.
  3. Layer in biology: amygdala signals threat; prefrontal cortex can inhibit impulsive reactions (the “brakes”).
  4. When multiple risk factors appear (heat + hunger + alcohol), avoid single-cause answers that ignore the passage’s converging evidence.

Passage 3: Helping, Fitness, and Strategy

On an isolated island, a biologist studies a bird species in which adults sometimes feed chicks that are not their own. In one nesting area, a female’s sibling dies during a storm. Over the next week, the female brings extra food to the sibling’s chicks, reducing the time she spends foraging for herself. The chicks survive the week and later fledge successfully.

In another area, two unrelated adults compete for a limited feeding territory. One adult frequently escalates to physical fights. The other initially displays threat postures but retreats once the conflict intensifies, then returns later when the fighter is away. The biologist records that the payoff for winning the territory is high, but the risk of injury is also high.

A third observation involves group hunting: young birds improve hunting success after watching skilled hunters, and the group follows a rule that certain individuals feed first after a successful hunt. Over time, the population maintains both “fight” and “avoid-fight” strategies depending on seasonal changes in food availability.

How to reason:

  1. Identify whether the helping behavior is costly to the helper → that is key for altruism.
  2. Ask whether the recipient shares genes with the helper; if yes, connect to inclusive fitness.
  3. For the territory competition, map “fight” vs “avoid-fight” to the Hawk–Dove logic: payoff depends on reward value vs injury cost.
  4. When asked about stability in a population, think evolutionary stable strategy (ESS): selection can maintain a mix when no alternative strategy can easily invade given the payoffs.
