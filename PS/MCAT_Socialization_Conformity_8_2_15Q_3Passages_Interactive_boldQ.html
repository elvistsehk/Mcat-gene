Standalone Questions (1–15)
Q1. During childhood, Maya learns to say “please” and “thank you” because her caregivers model the behavior and praise her for it. Years later, she learns to use formal email salutations after starting a corporate internship. The second change is best described as:
A. Cultural diffusion
B. Resocialization
C. Secondary socialization
D. Primary socialization
✅ Correct
❌ Incorrect
Explanation (click to expand)

Correct Answer: C

Why this is correct: Secondary socialization is learning the norms and expected behaviors of specific subgroups or new social settings outside the home (e.g., school, workplace).

Why the other choices are not correct:

  • A: Cultural diffusion = spread of cultural elements between cultures, not learning within one setting.
  • B: Resocialization = discarding old behaviors via intensive retraining in a total institution.
  • D: Primary socialization = early childhood learning of basic norms at home.
Q2. A new recruit in the military rapidly abandons previous habits and adopts strict routines after intensive training designed to produce unquestioning adherence to orders. This process most directly illustrates:
A. Primary socialization
B. Identification
C. Anticipatory socialization
D. Resocialization
✅ Correct
❌ Incorrect
Explanation (click to expand)

Correct Answer: D

Why this is correct: Resocialization involves unlearning prior norms/roles and adopting new ones through intensive retraining, often in total institutions (e.g., the military).

Why the other choices are not correct:

  • A: Primary socialization = earliest learning in childhood, not adult retraining.
  • B: Identification = surface-level acceptance without internal attitude change.
  • C: Anticipatory socialization = preparing for a future role before entering it.
Q3. A city introduces a fine for littering and a public “clean block” award for neighborhoods with the lowest litter rates. From a sociological perspective, these are examples of:
A. Stigma
B. Formal sanctions
C. Folkways
D. Informal sanctions
✅ Correct
❌ Incorrect
Explanation (click to expand)

Correct Answer: B

Why this is correct: Fines and official awards are enforced/recognized by formal institutions (government), making them formal sanctions (negative and positive, respectively).

Why the other choices are not correct:

  • A: Stigma is social disapproval of a person/group, not a sanction category.
  • C: Folkways are polite customs, not reward/punishment mechanisms.
  • D: Informal sanctions are peer-enforced (teasing, praise), not government fines/awards.
Q4. After being repeatedly called “a troublemaker” at school, a student begins skipping class and joining peers who shoplift, eventually seeing delinquency as part of who they are. This trajectory is most consistent with:
A. Strain theory
B. Social facilitation
C. Labeling theory leading to role engulfment
D. Cultural transmission
✅ Correct
❌ Incorrect
Explanation (click to expand)

Correct Answer: C

Why this is correct: Labeling theory predicts that being labeled deviant can shape self-image and others’ responses; internalizing the label can lead to role engulfment where the label dominates identity.

Why the other choices are not correct:

  • A: Strain theory explains deviance via blocked access to culturally valued goals.
  • B: Social facilitation is performance change in presence of others, unrelated to labels.
  • D: Cultural transmission is passing norms across generations, not identity takeover by a label.
Q5. A teenager who spends most evenings with friends who vandalize property begins to view vandalism as normal and later participates. This pattern is best explained by:
A. Multiculturalism
B. Obedience
C. Differential association theory
D. Foot-in-the-door technique
✅ Correct
❌ Incorrect
Explanation (click to expand)

Correct Answer: C

Why this is correct: Differential association proposes deviance is learned through interactions; when deviant associations are more frequent/intense than normative ones, deviant behavior becomes more likely.

Why the other choices are not correct:

  • A: Multiculturalism concerns coexisting cultures, unrelated to deviance learning.
  • B: Obedience is responding to authority, not adopting vandalism from peers.
  • D: Foot-in-the-door is a persuasion technique, not a deviance-learning theory.
Q6. A student privately agrees that cheating is wrong and stops cheating after realizing their friends value academic integrity. Another student stops cheating only when a proctor is present, but still believes cheating is acceptable. The first student’s change reflects:
A. Identification
B. Internalization
C. Obedience
D. Projection
✅ Correct
❌ Incorrect
Explanation (click to expand)

Correct Answer: B

Why this is correct: Internalization is conforming while also adopting the group’s beliefs internally; the student genuinely changes their attitude about cheating.

Why the other choices are not correct:

  • A: Identification is outward conformity without private agreement.
  • C: Obedience involves authority’s command, not peer group influence.
  • D: Projection is attributing one’s own unwanted feelings to others.
Q7. In a role-play study, participants assigned as “guards” begin acting harshly toward “prisoners,” and later report surprise at their own behavior because they felt the role became real. This is most consistent with:
A. Internalization of social roles
B. Door-in-the-face compliance
C. Cultural diffusion
D. Social loafing
✅ Correct
❌ Incorrect
Explanation (click to expand)

Correct Answer: A

Why this is correct: Zimbardo-style findings are commonly interpreted as participants internalizing social roles and associated norms, altering both behavior and self-perception.

Why the other choices are not correct:

  • B: Door-in-the-face relies on rejecting a big request first; no initial big request here.
  • C: Cultural diffusion is cross-cultural spread, not within-lab role behavior.
  • D: Social loafing is reduced effort in groups, not role adoption.
Q8. A charity volunteer first asks a passerby to sign a small petition supporting nutrition programs. After the person signs, the volunteer asks for a $20 donation. This sequence best exemplifies:
A. Foot-in-the-door technique
B. Door-in-the-face technique
C. Obedience
D. Lowball technique
✅ Correct
❌ Incorrect
Explanation (click to expand)

Correct Answer: A

Why this is correct: Foot-in-the-door starts with a small request to gain compliance, increasing the likelihood of agreeing to a larger request later.

Why the other choices are not correct:

  • B: Door-in-the-face starts with a large request first; here it starts small.
  • C: Obedience requires authority; this is a peer request.
  • D: Lowball increases the cost after commitment, not request size.
Q9. A salesperson asks a customer to buy a premium appliance warranty for $400. When the customer refuses, the salesperson offers a basic warranty for $80, which the customer accepts. This is best described as:
A. Foot-in-the-door technique
B. That’s-not-all technique
C. Door-in-the-face technique
D. Internalization
✅ Correct
❌ Incorrect
Explanation (click to expand)

Correct Answer: C

Why this is correct: Door-in-the-face begins with an unreasonably large request that is likely to be rejected, followed by a smaller request that seems reasonable by comparison.

Why the other choices are not correct:

  • A: Foot-in-the-door starts small then escalates; here the first request is huge.
  • B: That’s-not-all sweetens an offer before acceptance, not after refusal.
  • D: Internalization is attitude change, not a request sequencing strategy.
Q10. A manager convinces an employee to chair a committee by emphasizing it will require only one short meeting per month. After the employee agrees, the manager reveals there will also be weekly reports and extra meetings. This is an example of:
A. Informal sanction
B. Identification
C. That’s-not-all technique
D. Lowball technique
✅ Correct
❌ Incorrect
Explanation (click to expand)

Correct Answer: D

Why this is correct: Lowball obtains initial commitment, then increases the cost (time/effort/money) after the person has psychologically committed.

Why the other choices are not correct:

  • A: Informal sanction is peer-enforced social reward/punishment, not a persuasion technique.
  • B: Identification is surface-level acceptance; not about escalating costs after commitment.
  • C: That’s-not-all adds bonuses/discounts before decision, not hidden extra work after yes.
Q11. An infomercial offers a blender for $29.99, and before you can decide, the host adds “and we’ll include a second set of blades for free!” This persuasion strategy is:
A. That’s-not-all technique

C. Lowball technique
D. Door-in-the-face technique
✅ Correct
❌ Incorrect
Explanation (click to expand)

Correct Answer: A

Why this is correct: That’s-not-all improves the offer (adds bonuses or discounts) before you make a decision, increasing perceived value and compliance.

Why the other choices are not correct:

  • B: Obedience requires authority; infomercials use persuasion, not authority.
  • C: Lowball raises costs after commitment; here costs decrease via extra bonus.
  • D: Door-in-the-face requires an initial large request and a rejection.

Q15. A community strongly endorses the “American dream” as a social goal, but offers limited access to quality education and high-paying jobs. Some residents turn to theft to obtain wealth. This example aligns most closely with:

Passage 1: School Culture, Labels, and Identity

At Northbridge High, first‑year students attend a two‑day “campus culture” program. On Day 1, older student leaders demonstrate expected behaviors: how to address teachers, how to join clubs, and even where to sit during lunch to avoid social conflict. New students who follow the program’s rules receive public shout‑outs during announcements; those who break rules (for example, eating in a restricted hallway) are required to stay after school for a brief clean‑up duty.

During the semester, one student, Lina, is repeatedly teased as “weird” because she eats lunch alone and avoids eye contact. Over time, she starts sitting with a group that prides itself on being “outsiders.” The group jokes about being ‘bad influences’ and begins skipping assemblies together. Lina reports that being called weird used to bother her, but now she feels ‘like an outsider is who I really am.’ Teachers notice that Lina’s grades and attendance decline after she becomes close with this group.

1) Identify the *social mechanism* described (socialization, sanctions, labeling). 2) Separate what is enforced by institutions (formal) vs by peers (informal). 3) Use the passage’s timeline: what changed first, and what changed after the label/peer group appeared? 4) Pick the choice that best explains both the behavior change *and* the identity shift described.

Q16. Which observation would most strongly support the claim that the “campus culture” program is functioning as **secondary socialization** rather than primary socialization?

B. Students adopt new behaviors only after an intensive, isolating boot‑camp style retraining.
C. Students learn to speak their first language during the program.
D. Students imitate only their parents’ behavior during early childhood.
✅ Correct
❌ Incorrect
Explanation (click to expand)

Correct Answer: A

Why this is correct: Secondary socialization refines behavior for specific settings outside the home. The program teaches school‑specific norms on top of earlier childhood learning.

Why the other choices are not correct:

  • B: Intensive isolating retraining that replaces prior norms is resocialization.
  • C: Learning a first language is classic primary socialization in childhood.
  • D: Imitating parents in early childhood is primary socialization, not secondary.

  • B: Resocialization/anticipatory/internalization is about role prep or retraining, not stigmatizing labels.

Passage 2: Designing a Vaccination Sign‑Up Campaign

A public health team wants to increase vaccination sign‑ups at a community clinic. In Week 1, volunteers stand outside the clinic and ask pedestrians to answer a one‑question survey: “Do you support protecting the community from preventable disease?” Most people say yes. In Week 2, the same volunteers approach many of the same pedestrians and say, “Since you support protecting the community, can you schedule your vaccination appointment today?”

In a second condition, volunteers begin by asking pedestrians to commit to a two‑hour weekend shift at the clinic. Most refuse. Volunteers then respond, “Okay—could you at least share this clinic link with one friend?” Many pedestrians agree to the smaller request. In a third condition, a volunteer says, “Appointments take only 10 minutes,” gets a yes, and then adds, “Actually, because of paperwork it may take closer to 45 minutes, but you’re already scheduled.”

Across conditions, researchers track two outcomes: (1) immediate agreement to the request, and (2) whether the person later reports they genuinely believe scheduling was the ‘right thing to do’ rather than ‘something I did to avoid awkwardness.’

1) Match each condition to a compliance technique by focusing on the *order and size* of requests. 2) Use the second outcome to separate attitude change (internalization) from surface-level acceptance (identification). 3) When two choices sound plausible, ask: does the technique involve adding benefits, increasing costs, or changing request size after refusal?

❌ Incorrect

Why this is correct: A small initial request (one-question survey) is used to gain agreement, increasing the likelihood of agreeing to a larger request later (scheduling).

Why the other choices are not correct:

  • B: That’s-not-all improves the deal by adding extras, not by escalating request size later.

Passage 3: Explaining Vandalism—Peers vs. Structure

A city council is deciding how to address a rise in late‑night vandalism in a neighborhood where unemployment recently increased. Interviews with teenagers show two common themes. Group A describes spending most evenings with the same small peer group; several members boast about graffiti as ‘our signature,’ and newcomers are praised when they vandalize a highly visible wall. Group A members report that older peers teach them which streets have cameras and which store owners ‘never call the police.’

Group B teens report little connection to any one peer group. Instead, they describe feeling ‘stuck’ because they see social success defined as owning expensive items, yet they believe there are few legitimate ways to achieve that goal. Some report stealing spray paint or breaking into storage sheds because ‘it’s the only way to get what people respect.’

The council considers two interventions: (1) a mentorship program that pairs teens with employed adults who model norm‑abiding behavior, and (2) expanded job training and scholarships aimed at increasing access to education and stable work.

1) Decide whether the passage emphasizes *learning deviance from peers* (differential association) or *goal–means mismatch* (strain theory). 2) Use the proposed interventions: mentorship changes associations; job training changes access to legitimate means. 3) For each question, point to the specific evidence (peer teaching vs blocked opportunity) that supports the best theory.

Q22. Which pairing of teen group and theory is best supported by the passage?
A. Group A: labeling theory; Group B: cultural diffusion
B. Group A: strain theory; Group B: differential association
C. Group A: differential association; Group B: strain theory
D. Group A: obedience; Group B: social loafing
✅ Correct
❌ Incorrect
Explanation (click to expand)

Correct Answer: C

Why this is correct: Group A emphasizes learning techniques/norms of vandalism through close peer interactions (differential association). Group B emphasizes blocked legitimate means to culturally valued goals (strain).

Why the other choices are not correct:

  • A: Labeling theory would require being formally labeled deviant as the driver; diffusion is cross-cultural spread.
  • B: Group A shows peer teaching/reinforcement (fits differential association), not goal–means mismatch; Group B fits strain.
  • D: Obedience and social loafing do not explain learned vandalism norms or blocked opportunities.
