George Miller argued that immediate memory is limited to about seven pieces of information, plus or minus two. The investigators in this study noted, however, that a backward digit-span task places a heavier demand on working memory than simple storage because the participant must hold a sequence, mentally reverse it, and then report it in the correct backward order. They also emphasized that a score above seven should not automatically be taken as proof that typical working-memory capacity exceeds Miller’s estimate; such a claim would require many participants to cluster near that range, not just one or two exceptional performers.

Chunking can raise performance when separate items are reorganized into larger, meaningful units. In the present task, the digit strings were read at one digit per second and were composed of random numbers, so semantic chunking was limited. During debriefing, only a small minority of participants reported deliberately grouping digits into temporary mini-sequences. The researchers therefore cautioned that unusually high averages might reflect strategy discovery rather than a general increase in capacity shared by the whole sample.

Twenty subjects completed five sessions over the course of a year, with five backward-span trials per session. Table 1 reports each subject’s average longest correctly reversed string across all 25 trials. If Miller’s upper range generalized broadly to this task, the investigators expected many subjects to center near seven or above. Instead, they predicted that most scores would fall below that level because backward recall requires manipulation as well as storage, and because random digits provide relatively weak support for meaningful chunking.

In planning a follow-up study, the investigators debated whether the highest performers should be interpreted as evidence of a larger universal capacity or as outliers who had adopted an effective strategy. They proposed that explicitly marking chunk boundaries during presentation should help average performers more than merely slowing the digit rate slightly, because chunking changes the unit being remembered rather than only adding rehearsal time.

Treat the passage as a research argument about what the table means, not as a definition list.

  • First, identify the investigators’ claim: backward digit span is meant to test working-memory manipulation, so performance can fall below Miller’s classic range even when immediate memory is intact.
  • Next, inspect the distribution in Table 1: ask whether the values cluster across many subjects or whether only a few high performers are pulling attention upward.
  • Then use the passage’s warning about chunking: the researchers think some unusually high means may reflect strategy discovery, especially because the digit strings were random and meaningful grouping was limited.
  • Finally, test the answer choice causally: the best choice should explain how the table pattern supports or weakens the researchers’ interpretation, or predict which manipulation would distinguish general capacity from strategy-based improvement.

Why the other choices are not as good

  • B: Means summarize a distribution; they do not replace individual data when the passage explicitly highlights heterogeneity.
  • C: The problem is not that p-values are restricted to forward span. The problem is misinterpreting a summary statistic as an individual-level claim.
