Emotional Memory

Passage 1 (Questions 1–7)

Researchers who study emotional memory often distinguish between the factual details of an event and the meaning later attached to it. Two people may encounter broadly similar danger yet differ substantially in the way the event is remembered, integrated into later behavior, and used when imagining similar future situations.

To examine this issue, investigators recruited adults who had previously experienced an attempted street robbery while alone at night. Some participants were physically robbed, whereas others escaped before the robbery was completed. Rather than recreate danger in the laboratory, the researchers used structured interviews to measure how participants now evaluated the event and whether they believed they would respond similarly in a comparable situation. The investigators were interested in whether emotional memory differed as a function of the event’s outcome.

Table 1 Emotional-memory responses for participants who were robbed versus those who avoided the robbery.

The robbed group was less likely to say they would handle a similar situation the same way. They were also much more likely to rank the episode among the worst experiences of their lives, to report that the event changed routine daily behavior, and to believe that the outcome might have been avoidable. The avoided-robbery group showed the same pattern in direction but with lower intensity.

The authors cautioned that these results do not prove that one group remembered the event more accurately than the other. Instead, the data suggest that emotional significance, counterfactual thinking, and future behavioral planning may all be shaped by outcome-dependent emotional memory, even when the original threat context was broadly similar.

Use a three-step approach.

  • First identify the shared context: both groups faced a similar threat, but the outcome differed.
  • Then compare the pattern across all four rows. Ask whether the robbed group shows consistently greater emotional impact, stronger counterfactual thinking, and more behavioral change.
  • Finally connect the table to the authors’ claim: the study is about how events are later interpreted and integrated into memory, not direct proof of what happened second by second during the robbery.
Question 1
Which interpretation is best supported by the passage and Table 1?
The best answer is A. Across multiple rows, the robbed group reports stronger downstream impact: they more often rank the event among the worst experiences of life, more often say daily behavior changed, and more often believe the event could have been avoided. That pattern supports greater long-term emotional significance, not greater objective accuracy.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • Choice B confuses reduced aftermath with proof of better accuracy, which the passage explicitly avoids claiming.
  • Choice C introduces a pre-existing difference not measured in the study.
  • Choice D ignores the consistent group differences shown across the table.
Question 2
Which row in Table 1 most directly supports the idea that emotional memory influences future behavioral planning rather than only retrospective emotional intensity?
The best answer is C. Reporting changed routine daily behavior indicates that the memory of the event influenced future conduct, not just how bad the event felt in retrospect. That is the most direct evidence in the table for downstream planning or adaptation.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • Choice A concerns hypothetical future handling, but it is less direct than actual reported behavioral change.
  • Choice B mainly reflects emotional intensity or salience.
  • Choice D reflects counterfactual interpretation more than day-to-day behavioral planning.
Question 3
If the two groups were otherwise comparable before the event, which conclusion is most justified by the difference in the avoidable row when interpreted alongside the rest of Table 1?
The best answer is A. The robbed group is much more likely to say the event could have been avoided, and the rest of the table shows greater emotional impact overall. Together, those findings support stronger counterfactual thinking in the robbed group. The data do not prove objective preventability or absence of fear in the other group.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • Choice B is far too strong and contradicted by the avoided group’s substantial emotional responses.
  • Choice C is unsupported because the table does not measure fear during the event directly.
  • Choice D mistakes retrospective belief for objective fact.
Question 4
Which follow-up finding would most weaken the authors’ interpretation that outcome shapes emotional memory rather than merely reporting style?
The best answer is B. If the robbed group already had a much stronger general tendency to endorse catastrophic or extreme responses, then the differences in Table 1 could reflect reporting style rather than the emotional-memory effects of robbery outcome. That would directly weaken the authors’ preferred interpretation.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • Choice A would actually strengthen the emotional-memory interpretation by reducing concern about style differences.
  • Choice C addresses factual recall of place, not the emotional meaning attached to the event.
  • Choice D is compatible with the existing results and does not explain them away.
Question 5
Which statement best distinguishes the among worst experiences row from the changed routine daily behavior row?
The best answer is A. Ranking an event among the worst experiences captures emotional salience and subjective impact. Reporting changed routine daily behavior goes one step further by indicating that the remembered event altered later habits or decisions.
Why the other choices are not as good

  • Choice C introduces accuracy versus distortion, which the passage says cannot be concluded from these data.
  • Choice D is unsupported and too absolute.
Question 6
A researcher claims that because 74% of the avoided-robbery group said they would handle a similar situation the same way, their memory must be less influenced by emotion than the robbed group’s memory. Which response is best justified?
The best answer is B. The avoided-robbery group generally reports less intense aftermath, but the table still shows meaningful emotional consequences in that group. Lower impact is not the same as no emotional shaping of memory.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • Choice A is too strong; repeating a strategy does not prove low emotion.
  • Choice C uses one row to make a stronger inference than the data support.
  • Choice D contradicts the entire logic of the passage.

The best answer is A. If both groups remember basic facts equally well yet still differ in perceived impact, avoidability, and behavioral change, the difference is more likely to involve emotional meaning and integration than simple forgetting of event details.
Why the other choices are not as good
