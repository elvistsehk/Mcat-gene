Passage 1 (Questions 1–7)

Sleep is divided into non-REM and REM stages. The earliest non-REM stages are associated with progressively lower levels of body activity, whereas REM sleep is paradoxical: the sleeper often shows physiology that resembles wakefulness, including relatively elevated pulse and body temperature, yet voluntary skeletal muscle activity is strongly reduced. As a result, dream content during REM is not usually translated into large body movements.

Sleep stages can be distinguished with electroencephalography and motion monitoring. Wakefulness and REM are both associated with relatively fast, low-amplitude EEG activity, whereas deep non-REM sleep is characterized by high-amplitude, low-frequency delta waves. Because REM is accompanied by marked reduction of voluntary movement, accelerometers placed on the wrists and ankles can help distinguish REM from quiet wakefulness.

Investigators monitored 120 healthy participants from infancy through adulthood for a full 24-hour period. Each participant wore scalp EEG electrodes and wrist- and ankle-mounted accelerometers. Figure 1 summarizes the mean total sleeping hours and REM sleeping hours observed for each age group.

Figure 1 Mean total hours of sleep and mean REM hours of sleep across age groups

In a second experiment, three individuals from each age group were observed for one week while the laboratory light cycle was delayed by one hour each day. Infants showed little change in the timing of sleep onset, whereas older children, adolescents, and adults gradually fell asleep later each day. The investigators argued that the infant pattern reflected weaker entrainment of sleep timing to the imposed light schedule rather than an inability to generate REM sleep.

Finally, 12 healthy men between 25 and 35 years of age underwent a separate overnight laboratory session. Electrodes and accelerometers were attached at 20:00, lights were turned off at 22:00, and the subjects were awakened at 8:00. Body temperature, pulse rate, respiration, and EEG were recorded throughout the night. Figure 2 shows body temperature measurements from one representative subject.

Figure 2 Body temperature over a 12-hour laboratory recording from one adult subject
