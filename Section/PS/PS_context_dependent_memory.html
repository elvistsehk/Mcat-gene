Context-Dependent Memory
Timer 13:00
Passage 1 (Questions 1–8)

Investigators studying memory retrieval proposed that stress at encoding may impair later recall, but that some of this impairment might be offset if retrieval occurs in the same external context in which learning took place. They emphasized that a benefit produced by a repeated environmental cue should not automatically be confused with restoration of the learner’s internal physiological state.

In Experiment 1, healthy adults were randomly assigned to either a control group or a stress group. Both groups studied a list of 30 neutral nouns for a delayed free-recall test administered 24 hours later. During encoding, the stress group simultaneously completed a socially evaluated cold pressor procedure, whereas the control group encoded the words without that stress manipulation. The researchers used Experiment 1 to confirm that stress present during encoding would reduce later recall for a simple verbal task. The results are shown in Figure 1.

Figure 1 Results of Experiment 1

In Experiment 2, a new sample of participants was again assigned to control and stress groups. This time, participants learned the locations of 12 pairs of objects on a digital grid while the laboratory was infused with an orange-blossom scent. No additional scent was introduced anywhere else in the building. Twenty-four hours later, half of each group completed the location test in a room with the same scent, whereas the remaining participants were tested in an unscented room. The researchers deliberately did not repeat the stress manipulation during retrieval, because they wanted to isolate the role of an external contextual cue rather than recreate the original internal stress state.

The authors also warned students not to compare the raw score differences in Experiment 1 and Experiment 2 as if they measured the same capacity. The tasks differed in both format and maximum possible score, so direct numerical comparisons across experiments could be misleading. Instead, they argued that the critical question was whether reinstating the encoding context changed the size of the stress-related deficit within Experiment 2. They further noted that if the scent simply increased alertness at test, both groups should improve by about the same amount; by contrast, if the scent acted as a retrieval cue linked to encoding, the benefit should be disproportionately larger in the stress group. The results of Experiment 2 are shown in Figure 2.

Figure 2 Results of Experiment 2

How to reason through this passage

  • First identify the investigators’ main claim: stress at encoding hurts later memory, but a matching external cue at retrieval may reduce that deficit.
  • Keep separate the ideas of context-dependent memory and state-dependent memory. The passage says retrieval was not done under renewed stress, so any benefit in Figure 2 must come from the external cue, not recreation of the internal stress state.
  • Use Figure 1 to confirm the baseline stress-related impairment for delayed recall, then use Figure 2 to ask whether the same scent changes the size of that impairment within Experiment 2.
  • When evaluating answer choices, watch for overgeneralization. The passage explicitly warns that raw score gaps cannot be compared across experiments as though the tasks were identical.
  • Strong answers usually combine a passage caution with a specific data pattern from one or both figures.

Explanation
The best answer is B. The passage states that if the scent simply increased alertness, both groups should improve by about the same amount. Figure 2 instead shows that reinstating the scent produces a much larger benefit in the stress group than in the control group, which supports a cue-specific retrieval account rather than a general alertness effect.

Which conclusion is best supported when Figure 1 is considered together with Figure 2?
Explanation
The best answer is B. Figure 1 establishes that stress during encoding lowers later recall. In Figure 2, the stress-related deficit is clearly smaller when the same scent is present again at retrieval than when it is absent. Together, the figures support the idea that reinstating an external encoding context can mitigate, though not necessarily eliminate in every setting, a stress-related memory disadvantage.

Which additional finding would most strongly weaken the investigators’ preferred explanation that Figure 2 reflects context-dependent retrieval?
Explanation
The best answer is D. The investigators argue that the scent helps because it reinstates a cue that was present during encoding. If participants who were not exposed to that scent during encoding show the same benefit when the scent appears only at retrieval, then the improvement cannot be attributed specifically to context reinstatement. That result would point instead to some more general effect of the scent at test.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • A: Awareness differences could matter, but they do not directly overturn the encoding-context account.
  • B: That result would actually fit the idea that cue identity matters for context reinstatement.
  • C: This does not address whether the scent benefit in Experiment 2 depends on matching encoding and retrieval context.
A reviewer writes, “Figure 2 shows that stress becomes irrelevant as long as the test room recreates the original context.” Which evidence best qualifies that statement?
Explanation
The best answer is A. Figure 2 suggests that a matched context can reduce the stress-related deficit in that particular task, but Figure 1 still demonstrates a clear impairment after stressed encoding when no special retrieval cue is restored. Taken together, the data support a conditional mitigation account, not the blanket claim that stress becomes irrelevant whenever memory is tested.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • B: Figure 2 does not justify such a universal testing recommendation.
  • C: Figure 1 clearly shows a stress-related decrement, so the manipulation in Experiment 1 was not obviously unsuccessful.
  • D: The unscented condition is essential because it shows what happens when the encoding cue is not reinstated.
Which follow-up design would best distinguish a context-dependent memory explanation from a simpler “same room” familiarity explanation for Experiment 2?
Explanation
The best answer is C. The passage identifies the orange scent as the critical external cue. To separate cue-specific context reinstatement from a simpler familiarity-with-the-room account, the investigators should hold the room constant and manipulate only whether the scent is present again at retrieval. That way, any performance difference can be attributed more specifically to the reinstated cue itself.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • A: Changing retention interval does not isolate the role of the scent cue.
  • B: Returning everyone to the original room confounds room familiarity with scent unless the cue itself is independently manipulated.
  • D: Changing the response format may alter task demands, but it does not directly distinguish scent-specific context from room familiarity.
Which overall pattern best supports the idea that stress in these studies primarily disrupted memory encoding rather than some task-specific motor or response requirement at test?
Explanation
The best answer is B. Figure 1 shows poorer later recall after stressed encoding in a verbal free-recall task. Figure 2 shows that, when the matching cue is absent, stressed participants also underperform in an object-location task with a different kind of response. Because the deficit appears across two different test formats, the combined pattern more strongly suggests an encoding-related problem than a motor or task-specific retrieval problem.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • A: Figure 2 also shows a stress-related disadvantage in the unscented condition.
  • C: The scented condition narrows the gap, but it does not by itself prove that all effects of stress disappear.
  • D: Strong control performance alone does not identify the source of the stress-related deficit.
