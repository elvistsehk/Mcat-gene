Broca’s Area / Wernicke’s Area / Arcuate Fasciculus

Passage 1 (Questions 1–6)

A cognitive neurology service evaluated patients after a small ischemic stroke involving the language-dominant hemisphere. The attending physician warned trainees not to classify aphasias by a single feature alone. Fluent speech does not guarantee intact comprehension, and intact comprehension does not guarantee intact repetition. Instead, the pattern must be assembled from several observations: spontaneous speech, comprehension, repetition, naming, and awareness of errors. In most right-handed adults, Broca’s area in the dominant inferior frontal gyrus supports speech production, Wernicke’s area in the posterior superior temporal region supports language comprehension, and the arcuate fasciculus connects the two regions so that heard language can be transformed into an accurately repeated spoken response.

The team designed a bedside protocol around three tasks. In the comprehension task, the examiner gave multistep spoken commands such as “close your eyes, point to the ceiling, then touch your left ear.” In the repetition task, the patient repeated phrases of increasing complexity, including low-frequency words and grammatically unusual sentences. In the spontaneous speech task, the patient described a picture showing a child reaching for cookies while water overflowed from a sink. The attending emphasized another common pitfall: patients with severe motor weakness of the tongue or face may sound dysarthric, but dysarthria alone is not aphasia because the language plan itself can remain intact.

The attending then compared bedside data with network logic. Patient A struggled to initiate and sequence speech but followed commands well and recognized many of the errors, suggesting a frontal expressive deficit rather than a comprehension deficit. Patient B produced fluent but content-poor language and did not reliably follow spoken commands, suggesting impaired comprehension. Patient C understood conversation and could generate fluent spontaneous speech, yet repetition was disproportionately impaired, suggesting a disconnection syndrome rather than destruction of either major cortical language area. Patient D sounded abnormal because articulation was impaired, but grammar, comprehension, and repetition remained intact; the team used this case to remind students that dysarthria can mimic aphasia superficially while sparing the language network itself.

In follow-up discussion, the neurologist noted that lesion location matters for naming as well as repetition. A patient with Broca-type aphasia may know the intended word but produce halting, effortful output. A patient with Wernicke-type aphasia may speak fluently yet select incorrect words and fail to detect that the listener is confused. A patient with damage to the arcuate fasciculus may understand the target phrase and even object that the attempted repetition “sounds wrong,” but still be unable to reproduce it accurately because transmission between comprehension and production systems is disrupted.

Start with the bedside pattern, not the label. Compare speech fluency, comprehension, repetition, and self-awareness before deciding where the lesion most likely lies.

  • Broca pattern: nonfluent output with relatively preserved comprehension and impaired repetition.
  • Wernicke pattern: fluent but content-poor speech with poor comprehension and poor repetition.
  • Arcuate fasciculus / conduction pattern: fluent speech and intact comprehension with disproportionately poor repetition.
  • Do not confuse dysarthria with aphasia. If grammar, comprehension, and repetition remain intact, think motor articulation rather than language loss.
