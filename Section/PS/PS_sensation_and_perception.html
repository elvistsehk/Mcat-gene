Sensation and Perception
Timer 13:00
Passage 1 (Questions 1–8)

Psychologists distinguish sensation from perception. Sensation refers to the registration of physical input by the sensory organs, whereas perception involves interpreting that input and assigning it meaning. Because threatening stimuli can require rapid action, the brain may favor quick categorization over exhaustive analysis when deciding whether something is dangerous.

To study how brief visual input can support judgments of danger, researchers recruited 21 adults with normal or corrected vision. Participants fixated on the center of a screen while grayscale photographs of animals were flashed for 30 ms and then immediately masked. Half of the images depicted animals commonly judged dangerous, and half depicted nonthreatening animals matched as closely as possible for size and contrast. Participants held a response device and were instructed to press a button whenever an image seemed dangerous. Each participant judged 200 unique images. The distribution of all response times across button-press events is shown in Figure 1.

Figure 1. Pooled response times across danger judgments.

Researchers also computed each participant’s mean response time and overall classification accuracy across all 200 trials. Those participant-level values are plotted in Figure 2. The team cautioned students not to treat the two figures as interchangeable: Figure 1 pools single responses from the whole sample, whereas Figure 2 summarizes each person’s average speed and accuracy. The investigators also warned against claiming that slower observers had “better sensation.” Because every image was visible for the same 30 ms, especially long latencies were more likely to reflect additional perceptual decision time and response caution after stimulus offset than prolonged sensory exposure.

Figure 2. Participant mean response time versus overall accuracy.

The working hypothesis was that brief sensation can be sufficient for rapid threat-related judgments, but that more cautious perceptual processing should improve accuracy. If that idea is correct, most responses should cluster within a few hundred milliseconds, with a smaller right-sided tail of slower trials, and participants who respond more slowly on average should tend to classify the images more accurately.

How to reason

  • First identify whether a question is asking about pooled trials (Figure 1) or participant averages (Figure 2). They answer different kinds of claims.
  • Use the passage to separate brief sensory exposure from later perceptual decision time. The 30 ms presentation does not mean the full response must occur in 30 ms.
  • When a question mentions whether fast responding is “better,” check Figure 2 for the speed–accuracy relationship rather than assuming that quicker always means more accurate.
  • If a choice makes a claim about individual participants, ask whether the evidence comes from a participant-level figure or from pooled events.
  • For follow-up experiments, ask which design would isolate danger categorization from simpler sensory detection or motor responding.

The best answer is C. The passage states that every image was presented for the same brief 30 ms and then masked, while Figure 1 shows most responses occurring hundreds of milliseconds later. Together, these facts support the idea that sensation was brief but perceptual decision making and response execution continued after stimulus offset.
Why the other choices are not as good

  • B: Each trial used a unique image, not repeated presentations before a single decision.
  • D: Figure 1 shows all danger-judgment response times, not only errors.
A student argues that because rapid responding to danger is adaptive, the fastest participants should also be the most accurate. Which evidence most directly contradicts that claim?
Explanation
The best answer is A. The passage frames a possible tradeoff between speed and careful perceptual analysis, and Figure 2 shows a positive relationship between mean response time and accuracy. That directly contradicts the claim that the fastest participants were necessarily the most accurate.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • B: This describes the pooled distribution of response times but says nothing about whether faster participants were more accurate.
  • C: This is true, but it does not by itself refute the student's claim about speed and accuracy.
  • D: Stimulus duration alone does not show how accuracy varies across participants.

Explanation
The best answer is D. The passage explicitly warns that Figure 1 and Figure 2 are not interchangeable. Figure 1 combines individual response events from everyone, so it cannot identify which specific person was most accurate; Figure 2 provides the participant-level averages needed for that kind of inference.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • A: Figure 1 includes the response times for the danger judgments that participants actually made; it is not described as excluding dangerous images.
  • B: Figure 1 is not a plot of average participant response times; it is a pooled histogram of events.
  • C: Even if the task involves perception, that does not explain why Figure 1 cannot identify the most accurate individual.
Considering both figures and the investigators’ hypothesis, which conclusion is best supported?
Explanation
The best answer is B. Figure 1 shows that many responses clustered within a few hundred milliseconds even though the images were extremely brief, supporting rapid judgments. Figure 2 then shows that participants with slower average responses tended to be more accurate, consistent with more cautious perceptual processing improving performance.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • A: The images were visible for only 30 ms, yet participants still made many responses; the data do not require several hundred milliseconds of stimulus exposure.
  • C: The figures do not show that faster participants were more accurate; Figure 2 suggests the opposite trend.
  • D: The passage warns that long response times are more likely to reflect additional decision time than failure to perceive the image at all.
A reviewer claims that the slow end of Figure 1 is due entirely to some people having sluggish motor responses, not to perceptual decision demands. Which existing result most weakens that claim?
Explanation
The best answer is A. If slower latencies were only a motor problem, there would be little reason to expect those slower participants to classify the images more accurately. The positive association in Figure 2 fits the passage’s idea of more cautious perceptual decision making, not merely sluggish movement.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • B: This describes the shape of the histogram, but it does not specifically separate motor delay from perceptual processing.
  • C: Sample size alone does not address the reviewer's proposed mechanism.
  • D: That is true of the histogram, but it does not explain why slower participants were also more accurate.
If the same images were presented for 60 ms instead of 30 ms, which outcome would be most consistent with the current data and the researchers’ caution?
Explanation
The best answer is D. The passage emphasizes that long latencies are not simply prolonged sensory exposure, and Figure 1 shows response times far greater than the 30 ms display duration. Doubling the display time could help somewhat, but the data imply that much of the total latency comes from later perceptual judgment and response selection.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • A: A fixed 30 ms rightward shift assumes response time is driven almost entirely by display length, which the passage argues against.
  • B: Nothing in the current figures requires the relationship in Figure 2 to reverse.
  • C: The existing data do not suggest two distinct processing modes producing a bimodal distribution.
A new participant completes the task with an average response time of 390 ms and an accuracy of 88%. Relative to the original sample in Figure 2, which interpretation fits best?
Explanation
The best answer is B. Figure 2 places the faster participants toward the left side of the graph and the lower-accuracy participants toward the bottom. A point near 390 ms and 88% would fall in the lower-left region, fitting the passage’s idea that emphasizing speed can come at the expense of accuracy.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • A: A 390 ms mean response time is relatively fast, not slow, and 88% is below the higher-accuracy points on the graph.
  • C: The plotted points show a positive trend, so this location would not support the idea that speed and accuracy are unrelated.
  • D: Figure 2 includes participants with average response times below 400 ms.
Which follow-up finding would most strongly support the claim that the speed–accuracy relationship in Figure 2 reflects perceptual categorization of danger rather than raw visual sensation?
Explanation
The best answer is C. The passage argues that the current relationship reflects additional perceptual decision time in categorizing danger, not simply sensory exposure. If the same visual input no longer produces the same speed–accuracy pattern when the categorization demand is simplified, that would strongly support a danger-specific perceptual explanation.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • A: A general brightness effect could reflect easier sensory registration, but it would not isolate categorization of danger as the source of the original relationship.
  • B: If the same pattern remained during simple image detection, that would argue against a danger-categorization explanation.
  • D: A longer right tail could mean the new images are harder or more ambiguous, but it would not specifically distinguish perception of danger from raw sensation.
