Passage 1 (Questions 1–7)

Clinical psychologists often note that a stressful history can alter how ambiguous cues are interpreted in the present. In some individuals, repeated exposure to danger may sharpen detection of genuine threats; in others, it may bias perception so that neutral or mixed stimuli are labeled as dangerous. Distinguishing adaptive vigilance from maladaptive threat bias is therefore important when evaluating trauma-related symptoms.

Researchers designed a laboratory task to compare adults with a documented history of repeated trauma exposure to matched controls without such exposure. Participants sat in front of a monitor and speakers and viewed short audiovisual clips constructed to be deliberately ambiguous. After each clip, each participant selected which of two descriptions best fit the stimulus. One option in every pair was classified by the investigators as threatening and the other as nonthreatening. For example, an abrupt cracking sound might be labeled either gunfire or fireworks. After the task, participants also reported whether the session had raised their stress, lowered their stress, or had little effect on it.

Figure 1 Percentage of respondents in each group who selected the threatening interpretation, reported raised stress, or reported little effect on stress.

The trauma-exposed group chose the threatening option far more often than controls. They also more often reported that the task increased stress and less often reported little effect on stress. The investigators cautioned, however, that the graph alone cannot determine whether the higher rate of threatening interpretations reflects trauma-linked hypervigilance, greater familiarity with dangerous stimuli, or a more general tendency to endorse intense response options.

To address that limitation, the researchers noted that a true general response-bias account would predict the trauma-exposed group should endorse both stress-related options more indiscriminately. Instead, the trauma-exposed participants were more likely to report raised stress and less likely to report little effect. The authors therefore argued that the pattern is more consistent with a specific threat-appraisal bias than with a simple tendency to choose any dramatic response.

Use a three-step approach.

  • First identify the exact variables: the black bars measure threatening interpretations, while the gray and white bars measure two different stress judgments.
  • Then compare the direction of the differences across groups. Ask whether the trauma-exposed group is higher on every intense response or whether the pattern is more selective.
  • Finally connect that pattern to the competing explanations in the passage: hypervigilant threat appraisal, familiarity with danger, or a general response bias.

Which interpretation of Figure 1 is most consistent with the authors’ argument against a general tendency to endorse any intense response option?
The best answer is A. The passage says a pure general response-bias account would predict indiscriminate endorsement of intense options. Figure 1 instead shows a selective pattern: the trauma-exposed group is much higher on threatening interpretations and raised stress, but lower on little effect. That pattern fits a specific bias toward threat/stress appraisal rather than a blanket tendency to choose dramatic responses.

The best answer is B. The new result would show two things at once: better detection of genuine danger and excessive threat labeling under ambiguity. Figure 1 already shows that the trauma-exposed group chose threatening interpretations more often and reported more stress. Together, those findings would support both heightened vigilance for real threat and a bias in ambiguous settings.
Why the other choices are not as good

Question 3
Which additional finding would most weaken the claim that Figure 1 reflects a threat-appraisal bias rather than simple familiarity with dangerous stimuli?
The best answer is A. The passage presents familiarity with danger as one competing explanation. If the difference appeared only for war- or danger-specific cues and disappeared for ordinary ambiguous stimuli, the graph’s pattern would be easier to explain by stimulus familiarity than by a broader threat-appraisal bias.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • Choice B could actually fit stronger emotional engagement and does not distinguish familiarity from appraisal bias.
  • Choice C is not closely tied to the mechanism behind threatening interpretations.
  • Choice D does not address why one group labeled more clips as threatening.
Question 4
Which statement about the relationship between the threatening-choice bar and the raised-stress bar is best justified by Figure 1 and the study design?
The best answer is B. The graph shows a group-level association: the trauma-exposed group has both more threatening interpretations and more raised-stress reports. But the study presents percentages at the group level, not paired individual data showing that each person who chose the threatening option also reported higher stress. So a direct individual-level causal claim cannot be made from Figure 1 alone.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • Choice A overstates the data by converting a group-level association into certain individual causation.
  • Choice C is incorrect because a smaller difference does not mean no relationship.
  • Choice D is wrong because many controls still reported raised stress.
Question 5
If the investigators wanted to test whether the trauma-exposed group simply preferred negative wording regardless of content, which modification would be most informative when interpreted alongside Figure 1?
The best answer is A. The question asks whether the group prefers negative wording in general, not threat specifically. A positive-versus-neutral version would let the researchers see whether the same respondents systematically choose emotionally loaded language even when the valence is positive rather than threatening. Figure 1 already gives the threat-specific pattern; the added task would test whether the bias is broad or threat-focused.
Why the other choices are not as good

  • Choice C would remove one of the useful converging measures rather than test the wording hypothesis.
  • Choice D eliminates the comparison group needed to interpret the pattern.

Question 7
Which follow-up result would most strongly support the idea that Figure 1 reflects top-down interpretation rather than a purely sensory difference between groups?
The best answer is A. Top-down interpretation means contextual expectations shape how an ambiguous stimulus is labeled. If adding a neutral context sentence substantially reduces the group difference for the exact same sound, that would show that meaning assignment—not basic sensory detection alone—is driving much of the effect seen in Figure 1.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • Choice B points toward a sensory explanation rather than supporting a top-down account.
  • Choice C concerns confidence, which is not the same as the mechanism of interpretation.
  • Choice D provides no direct evidence about whether the effect is interpretive or sensory.
