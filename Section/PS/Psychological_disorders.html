Parkinson's Disease
Timer 13:00
Passage 1 (Questions 1–7)

Parkinson's disease is a neurodegenerative disorder in which dopaminergic neurons in the substantia nigra gradually degenerate, reducing dopaminergic input to the basal ganglia. Patients commonly develop bradykinesia, rigidity, resting tremor, and postural instability. Because the basal ganglia are especially important for internally generated movement sequences, some neurologists have proposed that externally timed sensory cues may help patients initiate gait even when automatic motor programs are impaired.

To test that idea, investigators followed 900 adults with mild Parkinson's disease whose medication regimens had been stable for at least three months. Participants either enrolled in a twice-weekly class built around metronome-paced stepping, turning, and partner movement, or they received a home stretching booklet without rhythmic cueing. The researchers did not randomize assignment; instead, patients selected the program they preferred. The primary outcome was the percentage of each age group that developed frequent freezing-of-gait episodes severe enough to interfere with daily walking over the next 18 months. Figure 1 summarizes the results.

Figure 1 Percent of patients in each age group who developed frequent freezing of gait during follow-up

The authors noted two possible explanations for the lower percentages in the class participants. First, rhythmic auditory cues may recruit cortical motor planning pathways that partially compensate for impaired basal ganglia output. Second, a structured group class may improve adherence because the intervention is social as well as physically demanding. The authors cautioned that the figure alone cannot distinguish these explanations. They also emphasized that patients in the cue-training group still showed more freezing with increasing age, suggesting that the intervention may delay progression rather than eliminate it.

Medication effects were monitored throughout the study. Large levodopa dose escalations were uncommon and occurred at similar rates in both groups. In a separate chart review performed at the same clinic, vivid visual hallucinations were much more strongly associated with dopaminergic dose increases than with Parkinson's disease itself.

How to reason through this passage

  • Start with the study design: patients were not randomized, so any causal claim must consider possible self-selection or adherence differences.
  • Then inspect what the graph actually measures: it shows the percentage in each age band who developed frequent freezing of gait, not overall symptom severity and not medication side effects.
  • Compare groups using both absolute differences and overall trends with age. The cue-trained group is lower in every age band, but risk still rises with age inside both groups.
  • Use the passage to connect the pattern to mechanism: external rhythmic cues could help movement initiation, but the social nature of the class could also improve adherence.
  • When a question mentions hallucinations or levodopa, remember the passage separates gait freezing from dopaminergic side effects.
