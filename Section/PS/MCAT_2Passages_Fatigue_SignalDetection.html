Passage 1 (Questions 1–6)
Based on the functions of Regions A–C and the reported activation patterns, which interpretation best explains subjective motor fatigue in patients with MS?
Explanation
The best answer is B. Patients performed the task as well as controls, so the study does not point to gross movement failure. Instead, fatigue scores in MS tracked altered cerebellar activity, suggesting increased sensorimotor effort, while healthy controls—but not patients—showed adaptive premotor upscaling across blocks. That pattern supports a model in which subjective fatigue emerges when compensatory processing rises but top-down support for sustaining action does not scale appropriately.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • A: Performance remained intact, so a global relay failure is too strong and is not supported by the putamen findings.
  • C: The passage reports the opposite pattern: healthy controls showed the clearer premotor upscaling response.
  • D: The task was designed to be non-fatiguing behaviorally, so peripheral muscle exhaustion is not the main inference.
Suppose a follow-up study found that cerebellar activity in patients with MS rose further during later grip blocks while grip accuracy still remained normal. Which conclusion would be most consistent with the original authors’ interpretation?
Explanation
The best answer is A. The study argues that cerebellar upscaling in MS reflects increased sensorimotor processing that helps maintain performance even as fatigue is experienced subjectively. Preserved accuracy alongside increasing cerebellar demand therefore fits compensation, not absence of fatigue.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • B: The original study does not justify an absolute statement about total putamen involvement.
  • C: Stable performance does not rule out fatigue; the study distinguishes subjective fatigue from overt behavioral decline.
  • D: This is opposite the reported pattern, which emphasized reduced premotor upscaling in patients.
Why is the fact that patients and controls performed the grip task equally well especially important for interpreting the imaging results?
Explanation
The best answer is A. Because overt task performance was matched, the observed neural differences are less likely to be explained by basic motor failure. That allows the investigators to interpret the imaging patterns as correlates of subjective fatigue and compensatory processing.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • B: MS can still affect motor systems even if performance on one easy task is preserved.
  • C: Nothing in the passage challenges the cerebellum’s role in coordination.
  • D: The findings concern motor-control networks, not sensory deprivation alone.
If Region A were experimentally stimulated so that patients with MS could increase its activity across task blocks in a more control-like manner, which change would most plausibly follow?
Explanation
The best answer is B. Region A includes premotor and dorsomedial prefrontal areas associated with planning, control, and motivational support. Because patients were described as impaired at upscaling activity in these regions as fatigue increased, restoring that adaptive response would most reasonably reduce the internal burden of sustained movement rather than abolish all cerebellar processing.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • A: False alarms are part of signal detection theory, not the motor-fatigue paradigm in Passage 1.
  • C: Cerebellar contributions to coordination would still be expected.
  • D: The study implies that inadequate, not excessive, premotor upscaling contributes to fatigue.
Which finding would most directly weaken the claim that putamen dysfunction is the primary cause of subjective motor fatigue in this study?
Explanation
The best answer is C. If putamen activity is not meaningfully different between high-fatigue patients and controls, then putamen dysfunction is less likely to explain the observed differences in subjective fatigue. The stronger evidence instead points toward cerebellar and premotor/prefrontal patterns.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • A: A general increase in reported fatigue does not isolate the putamen as the mechanism.
  • B: This supports a premotor interpretation but is less direct than showing no relevant putamen difference.
  • D: This is true background knowledge but does not directly test the putamen claim.
Taken together, the findings suggest that subjective fatigue in MS is best described as which type of problem?
Explanation
The best answer is D. The passage argues for a combined mechanism: increased cerebellar sensorimotor demand plus reduced premotor/prefrontal upscaling as fatigue rises. That is a systems-level mismatch, not a purely peripheral, purely motivational, or globally akinetic disorder.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • A: The passage explicitly supports a sensorimotor contribution from the cerebellum.
  • B: Patients still executed the task successfully.
  • C: The task was non-fatiguing behaviorally, making a purely peripheral account too narrow.
A participant is told that her name will probably be called during a noisy task, but the actual auditory stimulus intensity is unchanged. Compared with an uninformed participant, which pattern is most likely if expectation makes her more willing to answer “yes”?
Explanation
The best answer is A. A more liberal decision criterion means the participant says “yes” more readily. That usually increases both correct detections when the signal is truly present and false alarms when the signal is absent.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • B: That pattern is more consistent with a conservative criterion.
  • C: A liberal criterion should not selectively reduce hits.
  • D: Signal detection theory was developed precisely because expectation can change reports even when sensory input is constant.
In a hospital screening context, a physician chooses a threshold that sacrifices some specificity so that dangerous cases are less likely to be overlooked. In signal detection terms, which tradeoff is the physician most directly accepting?
Explanation
The best answer is B. When missing a true case is especially costly, the decision criterion is often shifted in a liberal direction. That reduces misses but usually increases false alarms.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • A: This would move in the opposite direction and make detection less sensitive to true cases.
  • C: That is the tradeoff chosen when false alarms are more costly, not when misses are more dangerous.
  • D: Again, that would not match the stated goal of avoiding overlooked dangerous cases.
A researcher increases the loudness of the target tone while leaving instructions and expectations unchanged. Which outcome pattern would most strongly suggest improved sensory sensitivity rather than merely a changed response bias?
Explanation
The best answer is C. Making the signal clearer should improve true detectability, so presented signals are more often reported and less often missed. Unlike a purely criterion shift, this does not require a matching increase in false alarms.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • A: That pattern could reflect a more liberal response bias instead of clearer perception.
  • B: This suggests a more conservative criterion, not improved sensitivity.
  • D: Rising false alarms without fewer misses does not indicate better sensory sensitivity.
During a trial with no stimulus present, a subject reports hearing the signal. According to Figure 2, this response is classified as a:
Explanation
The best answer is D. A false alarm occurs when the signal is absent but the subject still responds “yes.”
Why the other choices are not as good
  • A: A miss requires that the signal was actually present.
  • B: A correct negative is a correct “no” when the signal is absent.
  • C: A hit requires both signal presence and correct detection.
Two participants hear the same set of near-threshold tones. Participant X reports “yes” very rarely and has few false alarms but many misses. Participant Y reports “yes” more often and has more hits along with more false alarms. Which inference is most justified?
Explanation
The best answer is A. The response pattern for Participant X—few false alarms but many misses—matches a conservative criterion. Participant Y’s greater willingness to say “yes” produces more hits but also more false alarms, consistent with a more liberal criterion.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • B: The pattern can arise from response bias alone, so receptor quality is not a necessary conclusion.
  • C: The prompt states that the tones are the same for both participants.
  • D: More hits plus more false alarms suggests criterion differences, not automatically lower sensitivity.
Which scenario best illustrates a shift caused primarily by nonsensory factors rather than by a stronger physical stimulus?
Explanation
The best answer is C. Expectation is a nonsensory factor. If the physical stimulus is unchanged yet reports increase because subjects now anticipate the signal, the shift is best explained by a change in decision criterion rather than improved sensory input.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • A: Brighter light is a stronger physical stimulus.
  • B: Higher concentration is also a physical stimulus change.
  • D: Reducing background noise changes external sensory conditions rather than only internal expectation.
