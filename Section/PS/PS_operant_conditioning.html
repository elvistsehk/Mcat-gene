Applications of operant conditioning

Passage 1 (Questions 1–7)

At a municipal animal-enrichment lab, trainer Elena works with two shelter dogs, Rocket and Basil. Rocket eagerly chases a flirt pole but becomes overaroused and jumps on Elena’s sleeves near the end of play bouts. Basil is far less active; he prefers lying on a foam mat and often ignores toys altogether. Elena wants Basil to spend more time in voluntary play, and she wants Rocket to finish play bouts with all four paws on the floor. She chooses an operant-conditioning approach because she wants to change the consequences of behavior rather than treating the dogs’ differences as fixed traits.

Elena reminds her interns that several terms are commonly confused. Delivering a food pellet after a target response is positive reinforcement if the goal is to increase that response. Adding or removing a consequence after an unwanted behavior can count as punishment if the goal is to decrease that behavior. Negative reinforcement, in contrast, increases a behavior by removing something aversive after the target response. Simply putting Basil’s mat away before a session is not itself reinforcement; it only changes the setting in which training occurs.

During pilot sessions, edible rewards increased desired behavior much more reliably than verbal praise. Elena also warns the interns not to judge a schedule only by the first few trials. More predictable schedules often produce rapid acquisition but can also produce pauses immediately after reward delivery. Less predictable schedules usually produce steadier responding and better persistence if a reward is delayed or omitted. Some schedules depend on elapsed time, whereas others depend on the number of target responses; interns often confuse these two ideas.

For Basil, the target response is continued toy pursuit. For Rocket, the target response is a completed play bout that ends without jumping. Elena emphasizes that if Rocket jumps and still receives attention or a treat, the jumping may be accidentally strengthened. In a short pilot test, Basil began engaging slightly faster under the predictable schedule assigned to him, but when one reward was delayed, the less predictable schedule maintained play longer. Rocket reduced jumping more quickly under the schedule that rewarded every successful calm bout, but after a dispenser malfunction produced several unrewarded calm bouts, the less predictable schedule maintained calm behavior better.

Use the passage like an MCAT experiment summary, not like a vocabulary list.

  • First, identify the trainer’s goal: Basil’s behavior should increase, while Rocket’s jumping should decrease by reinforcing an alternative response.
  • Next, map each schedule to the table: decide whether reward depends on elapsed time or on the number of completed responses, and whether the schedule is predictable or less predictable.
  • Then connect the schedule to Elena’s evidence: the passage tells you which kind of schedule helps initial acquisition, which one causes post-reward pauses, and which one better resists delayed or omitted reinforcement.
  • Finally, test the causal claim: if a choice rewards the wrong behavior, confuses punishment with reinforcement, or ignores the specific dog listed in the table, it is probably not the best answer.

After a day when the treat dispenser occasionally pauses for several minutes, which schedule would most likely keep Basil playing despite the delay, and why?
Explanation
The best answer is D. The passage says that less predictable schedules produce better persistence when a reward is delayed or omitted. Table 1 shows that for Basil, the less predictable schedule is B, where the treat follows an unpredictable duration of continued toy pursuit.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • A: A is Basil’s predictable schedule, and the passage explicitly says predictable schedules are weaker when reinforcement is delayed.
  • B: Schedule C applies to Rocket, not Basil, and it reinforces calm non-jumping bouts rather than sustained toy pursuit.
  • C: D is also for Rocket, and the passage never says variable schedules always produce the fastest acquisition. In fact, Elena reports faster early learning under more predictable schedules.
Question 2 of 7
An intern argues that yelling “Off!” at Rocket each time he jumps is equivalent to schedule C because both are meant to reduce jumping. Which reply best fits the passage and Table 1?
Explanation
The best answer is B. Table 1 shows that schedule C delivers a treat after every calm play bout that ends without jumping, so it reinforces an alternative response. The passage also warns that if Rocket jumps and still gets attention, the jumping may be accidentally strengthened. Yelling occurs after the problem behavior and may not teach the calm response Elena wants.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • A: Nothing in the passage indicates that yelling removes an aversive stimulus after the target response. This mislabels the contingency.
  • C: The passage does not claim punishment is always faster or superior. Elena’s own pilot data support reinforcement of calm behavior.
  • D: This ignores the central logic of operant conditioning in the passage, which is precisely about modifying consequences to change behavior.

Table 1 maps those schedules to A and B, respectively. For Rocket, the passage says calm behavior persists better under the less predictable schedule after unrewarded calm bouts, and Table 1 shows that schedule is D, not C.

  • C: The passage argues the opposite for persistence: less predictable schedules resist extinction better.
  • D: Merely noting that both schedules are positive reinforcement ignores the evidence that predictability still matters.
Question 5 of 7
A new volunteer claims schedule B is a ratio schedule because Basil can get more treats by making more rapid toy contacts. Which prediction would most clearly show the volunteer is misreading Table 1 in light of the passage?
Explanation
The best answer is D. The passage specifically warns that interns often confuse schedules based on elapsed time with schedules based on the number of responses. Table 1 shows that schedule B depends on an unpredictable duration of continuous pursuit, not on a count of discrete contacts. So Basil could respond many times and still not be reinforced until enough time has passed.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • A: This concerns Rocket’s schedule D and does not directly expose the misreading of Basil’s time-based schedule B.
  • B: The passage explicitly says putting the mat away is not itself reinforcement, so this option compounds two conceptual errors.
  • C: Even if a schedule is less predictable, the key mistake here is confusing time-based and response-count contingencies. This option does not address that problem as directly as D does.

Question 7 of 7
After several sessions, an intern proposes giving Rocket a treat at the end of a play bout even if he jumped midway through it, as long as he is calm at the instant the treat appears. Based on the passage and Table 1, which concern is most justified?
Explanation
The best answer is A. Table 1 defines Rocket’s target contingency in both C and D as a calm, non-jumping play bout. The passage explicitly warns that if Rocket jumps and still receives attention or a treat, the jumping may be accidentally strengthened. Rewarding at the end of a bout that included jumping would therefore blur the contingency and reinforce the wrong behavioral chain.
Why the other choices are not as good

  • C: Changing the success criterion does not convert Rocket’s schedule into Basil’s time-based schedule B.
  • D: Variable schedules are not immune to accidental reinforcement. If the wrong behavior is followed by reward, it can still be strengthened.
