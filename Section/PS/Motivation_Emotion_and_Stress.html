Response to Monetary Compensation
Timer 10:00
Passage 1 (Questions 1–7)

A social psychologist studying norms of reciprocity wanted to understand why people from different cultural backgrounds respond differently when an unexpected cash payment is offered after a task. Her lab had already found that some people view extra payment as a courteous acknowledgment of time, whereas others see it as creating an obligation to reciprocate. She warned her students not to confuse these two ideas: a participant might accept money because it feels appropriate, or refuse it because accepting would create discomfort about indebtedness.

To build groups for a follow-up experiment, the researchers first surveyed a large pool of volunteers about tipping, gifts, and repayment obligations. People who consistently described unsolicited payment as normal and polite were placed in Group A. People who said such payment could be acceptable but depended strongly on context were placed in Group B. People who described unsolicited payment as socially awkward unless it could be repaid were placed in Group C. The groups were then matched as closely as possible for age and sex distribution.

All participants completed what they believed would be a two-hour advertising study. In fact, the session lasted two hours and twenty minutes. At the end, the same researcher apologized for the extra time and offered each participant an additional cash payment. A camera recorded facial behavior during the offer. The investigators then coded whether the participant accepted the money, refused the money, verbally thanked the researcher, or offered to return some or all of the payment. Table 1 summarizes the proportion of each group showing each response.

Before examining the data, the research team discussed two competing interpretations. One interpretation was that group differences would mainly reflect internalized social norms about gratitude and reciprocity. A second interpretation was that any visible differences would simply reflect who liked the researcher more, regardless of payment norms. The team noted that if the first interpretation were correct, measures tied to repayment concern should diverge especially strongly across groups.

How to reason

  • Start by separating each group’s baseline norm belief from its later behavior. The passage tells you what each group believed before the cash offer.
  • Use Table 1 to decide whether the observed behavior fits comfort with acceptance, conditional acceptance, or reciprocity discomfort. One row by itself is often not enough.
  • Pay attention to whether a question is asking about acceptance/refusal or about what participants did after the offer, such as thanking or offering to return the money.
  • When judging competing explanations, ask which pattern would be expected if behavior came from payment norms rather than a generic positive feeling toward the researcher.
  • For prediction questions, change only the feature named in the stem and then infer which group’s behavior should shift most based on the passage’s logic and the table’s pattern.
Which finding in Table 1 most strongly supports the researchers’ claim that group differences reflect reciprocity norms rather than merely general liking of the researcher?
Explanation
The best answer is B. The passage states that the key alternative explanation is simple liking of the researcher, whereas the preferred interpretation concerns norms about obligation and repayment. The pattern that most directly points to reciprocity concern is not just lower acceptance, but the combination that Group C both refused the money most often and offered to return it most often. That links behavior specifically to discomfort about indebtedness rather than to a generally colder attitude toward the researcher.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • A: Thanking could occur for many reasons, including politeness or liking, so by itself it is less diagnostic of reciprocity concern.
  • C: Being intermediate is descriptive, but it does not isolate the mechanism as clearly as the repayment-related pattern in Group C.
  • D: This only shows that no group was uniform; it does not distinguish between the two competing interpretations.
Suppose the researchers repeated the study but told participants in advance that the session might run long and that extra compensation would automatically be added if it did. Based on the passage and Table 1, which outcome would most likely change the most?
Explanation
The best answer is D. Group C is defined in the passage as seeing unsolicited payment as awkward unless it can be socially justified or reciprocated. If the extra payment were announced in advance as part of the study arrangement, it would feel less like an unexpected gift and more like a standard compensation rule. That change should reduce the reciprocity discomfort most strongly in Group C, making acceptance more likely.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • A: Group A already views such payment as normal and polite, so there is little reason to expect a large rise in returning it.
  • B: The passage does not suggest that advance notice would especially suppress gratitude in Group B.
  • C: Advance notice should make the payment less awkward, not more awkward, for Group C.
Which follow-up observation from the video recordings would best strengthen the idea that Group B treated the payment as condition-dependent rather than simply splitting randomly between the attitudes of Groups A and C?
Explanation
The best answer is A. The passage describes Group B as believing that extra payment can be acceptable in some contexts. Evidence that Group B’s comfort changes when the offer is framed more sincerely would directly support a context-sensitive interpretation. This uses both the passage’s description of Group B and the idea that the video can reveal reactions during the offer.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • B: This compares Groups A and C, not whether Group B is specifically condition-dependent.
  • C: This again mostly contrasts Groups C and A and says little about the contextual nature of Group B’s judgments.
  • D: Posture during the task itself is too remote from the compensation offer to test the proposed interpretation.
A critic argues that the table does not distinguish gratitude from norm compliance. Which additional result would best address that criticism while remaining consistent with the study design described in the passage?
Explanation
The best answer is C. The table reports observable responses, but the criticism is about whether those behaviors reflected gratitude or compliance with a social rule. Written justifications collected immediately after the offer would connect the observed behavior to participants’ own reasoning. That directly addresses the distinction raised in the passage.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • A: Ad ratings concern task performance, not the meaning participants assigned to the payment.
  • B: Travel time might affect fatigue, but it does not clarify whether the response reflected gratitude or norm compliance.
  • D: Prior experiment experience could matter as a control, but it does not directly separate gratitude from norm-based reasoning.
Which inference is best supported by combining the passage’s group definitions with the table values for acceptance and offering to return the money?
Explanation
The best answer is B. Group B accepted the money at a moderate rate, yet still showed a nontrivial rate of offering to return it. Group C showed this pattern even more strongly. Together, these values indicate that acceptance does not necessarily mean complete social comfort; some people accept while still trying to reduce a sense of obligation.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • A: Their rates differ across multiple rows, so identical interpretation is not supported.
  • C: The table suggests both behaviors can occur within the same group-level pattern, so exclusivity cannot be concluded.
  • D: Thanking rates are high in Groups A and B despite substantial acceptance, so this claim is contradicted by the table.
If the researchers wanted to test the alternative explanation that differences were caused mainly by how positively each group viewed the specific researcher, which modification would provide the strongest check?
Explanation
The best answer is A. The alternative explanation concerns reactions to the specific researcher, not to reciprocity norms. Rotating several researchers while holding the wording constant would test whether the same group pattern persists regardless of who makes the offer. If it does, the norm explanation becomes stronger.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • B: A larger amount changes the incentive but does not isolate the effect of the particular researcher.
  • C: Removing the apology would also change the social meaning of the offer, making interpretation harder, not cleaner.
  • D: Narrowing the age range may reduce variance, but it does not directly address whether the researcher herself drove the effect.
Which conclusion about Group A is most justified by the passage and Table 1?
Explanation
The best answer is A. The passage defines Group A as viewing tips or gifts for time and effort as normal and polite, and the table shows the highest acceptance and highest thanking rates, with relatively low offering-back behavior. Together, these suggest that Group A generally saw the payment as appropriate acknowledgment rather than as a burdensome obligation.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • B: The passage gives no evidence that Group A performed better on the rating task, and task performance is not tied to the compensation response.
  • C: Group A actually thanked the researcher more often than Group B.
  • D: The entire pattern across groups suggests the opposite: norms appear to shape behavior substantially.
