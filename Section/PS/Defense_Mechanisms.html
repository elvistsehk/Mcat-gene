Defense Mechanisms
Timer 11:00
Passage 1 (Questions 1–7)

In professional settings, feedback can shape learning, but it can also threaten self-concept. Psychodynamic accounts propose that when a disappointing evaluation creates ego threat, some people may rely on defense mechanisms. One possibility is projection, in which a person attributes an unwanted trait or motive to the evaluator. Another is rationalization, in which the person produces a respectable-sounding explanation that redirects attention away from personal shortcomings. The researchers emphasized that a statement can be consistent with a defense mechanism without proving that an unconscious defense has occurred.

Participants with similar workplace training completed the same timed case-analysis task and were judged with the same rubric. After outlier performances were removed, one group privately assigned itself a score from 1 to 5 and submitted its explanation on a computer. The remaining participants received either a supervisor score of 2 or a supervisor score of 5 from a blinded evaluator and then discussed that score briefly with the evaluator face-to-face. All participants next completed a survey about how they interpreted the evaluation and what they planned to do before the next task.

The survey produced the two summary outcomes shown in Figure 1. One outcome was the percentage of participants who endorsed supervisor-trait criticism, such as describing the evaluator as insecure, jealous, or careless. The other outcome was the percentage who generated a concrete improvement plan, such as changing study strategy, rehearsing a skill, or requesting additional practice. The authors argued that a shift toward trait-based criticism after a low externally delivered score would be consistent with defensive responding under evaluative threat. However, they cautioned that the figure alone cannot distinguish projection from accurate criticism of the evaluator, nor can it fully separate projection from rationalization based on genuine problems with the task or scoring context.

Figure 1. Percentage of participants endorsing supervisor-trait criticism or generating a concrete improvement plan after different evaluation conditions.

How to reason

  • Start with the study design: everyone completed the same task and rubric, but only some participants received an external authority rating.
  • Use the bars to identify the response pattern, not just the highest value. The key contrast is between supervisor-trait criticism and concrete planning.
  • For defense-mechanism questions, ask whether the response sounds more like projection (assigning one’s own unwanted quality to the evaluator) or rationalization (giving a plausible external excuse).
  • Do not overclaim. The passage explicitly says the data are consistent with defensive responding, but they do not prove unconscious defense use.
  • When a question asks about an intervention or a follow-up study, combine the group pattern in the figure with the passage’s explanation of evaluative threat.

The best answer is A. The figure shows that the supervisor score 2 group is where defensive responding is most likely to be inferred. Complaints about task setup or timing, rather than about the supervisor’s personality, would therefore move the interpretation toward rationalization.

A hospital educator wants to reduce defensive responding after criticism while preserving motivation to improve. Based on the passage and Figure 1, which intervention should be prioritized first?

The best answer is C. Figure 1 shows that the supervisor score 2 group has the strongest supervisor-trait criticism and the weakest concrete improvement planning. The passage suggests this pattern is consistent with defensive responding under evaluative threat. An intervention that makes feedback more private, more behavior-specific, and more explicitly linked to an action plan best addresses that problem while still supporting learning.
