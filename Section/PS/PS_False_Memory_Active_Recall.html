False Memory and Active Recall
Timer 13:00
Passage 1 (Questions 1–8)

A graduate student studying memory distortion designed an experiment to test whether actively trying to retrieve a recent experience can improve true memory while also increasing false memory. Her faculty mentor warned that a rise in false alarms after retrieval practice could mean two very different things: participants might be reconstructing the theme of the list, or they might simply become more willing to circle any word that seems familiar.

Participants listened to four audio lists built around hidden seed words. Each list contained one seed word and fifteen associates arranged from stronger to weaker semantic association. The seed word itself was never spoken. For each list, the participant heard eight words randomly chosen from the first eleven associates. Immediately afterward, the participant either spent two and a half minutes writing down as many list words as possible in any order (recall review) or spent the same amount of time completing a pattern-matching task that prevented rehearsal. The assignment of recall review or pattern-matching was randomized across lists.

Table 1. Hidden seed words and associated word lists ordered from stronger to weaker association.

After all four lists were completed, participants received a recognition sheet containing seventy-two words in random order. The sheet included the thirty-two words that had actually been heard. It also included twelve critical lures: the four seed words plus two unpresented words from the first eleven associates of each list. Twelve non-critical lures were the final three, most weakly related associates from each list. Sixteen unrelated lures were drawn from categories not represented in any of the four lists. Participants circled every word they believed had appeared in the audio lists.

The student predicted that if active retrieval strengthened memory for the general meaning of a list, recall review should increase endorsement of critical lures more than unrelated lures. By contrast, if recall review merely made participants more liberal in saying they had heard an item, endorsement should rise across all lure categories, including unrelated lures.

Table 2. Proportion of each word type reported as heard after recall review or pattern-matching.

The student also examined the order in which spoken words were remembered. Because each list contained only eight actually heard words, she plotted the probability of correctly recognizing a heard word as a function of serial position within the spoken list.

Figure 1. Proportion of heard words correctly recognized as a function of serial position.

The student concluded that active retrieval helped participants recognize more studied words, but that it also strengthened the semantic gist of each list strongly enough to produce additional false memories for related items that had not actually been presented. She suggested that the findings may be relevant to situations in which people repeatedly try to reconstruct a recently experienced event.

How to reason

  • First identify the study’s comparison: after each list, participants either did recall review or a pattern-matching control.
  • Then sort the recognition items into heard, critical lure, non-critical lure, and unrelated lure. Many questions hinge on why those categories were separated.
  • Use Table 1 to determine whether a specific word is the hidden seed, a stronger associate, or one of the weakest final associates.
  • Use Table 2 to test competing explanations. If active recall caused a general “yes” bias, unrelated lures should rise too. If it strengthened semantic reconstruction, the increase should center on related lures.
  • Use Figure 1 when a question asks about serial position, especially primacy and recency. The graph applies only to heard words, not to lure items.
  • For the strongest MCAT answer, combine method + category definition + data pattern rather than relying on any single sentence or figure.
A teaching assistant argues that recall review probably just made participants more willing to circle any word on the recognition sheet. Which finding most directly undermines that interpretation?
Explanation
The best answer is B. The passage explicitly sets up two competing explanations: semantic reconstruction versus a general willingness to say “heard.” Table 2 shows that recall review raised endorsement of related lures (critical and non-critical), but the unrelated lure rate stayed essentially unchanged and was not statistically significant. That pattern is inconsistent with a broad yes-response bias.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • A: Higher recognition of heard words shows a memory benefit, but by itself it does not rule out a general yes bias.
  • C: This highlights the strength of false memory, but it still does not isolate whether the mechanism is gist-based reconstruction or indiscriminate responding.
  • D: The list structure makes false memory possible, but the question asks which finding undermines the assistant’s interpretation.
On one trial, the spoken list came from the sleep category, but the word mattress was not presented. Based on Table 1 and the recognition-sheet rules, mattress should be classified as which kind of item?
Explanation
The best answer is D. Table 1 shows that mattress is near the bottom of the sleep list. The passage says the final three weakest associates from each list were designated non-critical lures. So if mattress was not presented, it would appear on the recognition sheet as a non-critical lure.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • A: Merely belonging to a category does not make a word heard; it had to be one of the eight spoken items.
  • B: Critical lures were the hidden seed words plus omitted items from the first eleven stronger associates, not the final three weakest ones.
  • C: Unrelated lures came from categories not represented in any of the four lists.
Which evaluation of the student’s conclusion that active retrieval “improves memory accuracy” is most justified by the passage and Table 2?
Explanation
The best answer is A. Table 2 shows a real gain in memory for studied items: heard-word recognition rises from 0.63 to 0.76 after recall review. But accuracy is not purely improved, because endorsement of critical and non-critical lures also rises. The data support improved retrieval of true items together with increased distortion for related items.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • B: Endorsing more items in multiple categories does not automatically mean greater accuracy; some of those extra endorsements are false memories.
  • C: This overstates the problem. The heard-word rate still improves with recall review.
  • D: Low unrelated-lure rates help reject indiscriminate guessing, but they do not erase the increase in related false memories.
Suppose the student wanted to choose spoken words most likely to remain recognizable despite later interference from many related lures on the recognition sheet. Based on Figure 1 and the study design, which serial positions would be the best candidates?
Explanation
The best answer is C. The recognition sheet contained many related lure items, so the student would want spoken words with the strongest genuine memory trace. Figure 1 shows a clear primacy and recency pattern: recognition is highest for the first and last spoken words. That makes positions 1 and 8 the best candidates.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • A: The middle of the list has poorer, not stronger, recognition.
  • B: Positions 4 and 5 are actually the lowest points on the curve.
  • D: Being near the average does not make those positions the strongest protected from interference.
A participant hears nurse, clinic, patient, hospital, surgery, exam, and cure from the doctor list, but the word doctor is never spoken. Later, after a recall-review trial, the participant circles doctor. Which interpretation best fits the passage and data?
Explanation
The best answer is D. The word doctor is the hidden seed for that list, so it is a critical lure, not a studied word. Table 2 shows that critical lures are endorsed often, especially after recall review. The most coherent interpretation is that retrieval practice strengthened the list’s semantic theme enough to create a convincing false memory.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • A: Doctor is not unrelated; it is the central seed of that category.
  • B: Primacy refers to memory for actual spoken items at early serial positions, not to a seed that was never presented.
  • C: Organizing the meaning of the list does not make the item truly heard.
Why was it important for the recognition sheet to include the final three weakest associates from each list as a separate category?
Explanation
The best answer is B. Table 1 orders words by association strength, and the passage defines the last three items as non-critical lures. Including them lets the experiment separate false memory for the most conceptually powerful unpresented items (critical lures) from false endorsement of only weakly related associates. That comparison helps interpret the pattern in Table 2.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • A: The serial-position curve applies only to the eight words actually spoken, not to extra lure items added later.
  • C: The task concerns semantic association, not sound-based similarity.
  • D: Non-critical lures are still related to the category, so they are not unrelated controls.
If recall review had strengthened only memory for actually heard words, with no reconstructive effect on list meaning, which result would be expected but is contradicted by Table 2?
Explanation
The best answer is C. The key phrase is only memory for actually heard words. Under that hypothesis, recall review should improve recognition of the studied items without increasing false recognition of unpresented related items. Table 2 contradicts that prediction because critical-lure endorsement also rises substantially.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • A: This is a descriptive pattern, not the specific prediction of a no-reconstruction account.
  • B: A no-reconstruction account does not predict a rise in unrelated-lure responding.
  • D: Figure 1 describes the observed ordering effect for heard words, but the central contradiction comes from lure behavior in Table 2.
The student suggests that the findings may matter when people repeatedly reconstruct a recent real-world event. Which limitation is most important when evaluating that generalization?
Explanation
The best answer is A. The study convincingly shows that active retrieval can increase both true recognition and related false recognition in a tightly controlled word-list task. But a real event contains emotion, perception, context, and social feedback that are not captured by semantically grouped words. That limits how directly the exact pattern can be generalized.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • B: The low unrelated-lure rate does not prevent conclusions; it helps show that the distortion is selective rather than random.
  • C: Serial-position effects concern memory for heard words; they do not invalidate the false-memory findings.
  • D: This ignores the simultaneous increase in related false memories shown in Table 2.
