At a pediatric behavioral medicine clinic, investigators are designing a short training workshop for newly hired staff. Their goal is not merely to memorize vocabulary, but to classify real consequences that follow voluntary actions. The director reminds the group that operant conditioning concerns how consequences change the future frequency of voluntary behavior. The matrix in Figure 1 is introduced as a decision tool rather than a list of definitions: trainees must decide whether the target behavior becomes more likely or less likely, and whether the consequence adds a stimulus or removes one.

During workshop practice, several examples create confusion. In one case, a child fastens a seat belt and the loud safety alarm stops immediately; several trainees call this punishment because the sound itself is unpleasant. In another case, a teenager completes each step of a relaxation routine and receives a token that can later be exchanged for music time. In a third case, a patient who shouts during check-in loses ten minutes of tablet access. The director emphasizes that words such as reward, relief, privilege, and unpleasant do not determine the label by themselves. What matters is the direction of behavior change and whether something was added or removed.

To test understanding, the clinic runs a simulation. Staff members first classify cases using emotional language alone, then repeat the task using the two-step framework from Figure 1. Accuracy improves most when trainees first identify the target behavior and ask whether it increased or decreased after the consequence. Only then do they ask whether the consequence involved presentation or removal of a stimulus. The director warns that the most common mistake is to confuse negative reinforcement with punishment, especially when the removed stimulus is aversive.

At the end of the session, the clinic introduces a final scenario: during physical therapy, a patient keeps a difficult stretching posture for the full prescribed interval, and the therapist ends the exercise set early on future trials once the patient complies consistently. The trainees are told to classify the therapist’s strategy only after deciding which behavior is being strengthened, not after deciding whether the exercise felt good or bad.

Use the figure as a two-step classification map.

  • First identify the target voluntary behavior named in the passage scenario.
  • Next ask whether that behavior increases/continues or decreases/stops after the consequence.
  • Then use the figure to decide whether the consequence adds or removes a stimulus.
  • Do not classify from emotion words such as reward, relief, or unpleasant alone; the passage explicitly says that this causes errors.
  • When two labels seem close, ask which one best matches both the passage’s stated behavior change and the quadrant shown in the figure.
Figure 1. Ebbinghaus’s retention function showing that recall drops sharply soon after learning and then declines more gradually.

A cognitive psychology laboratory asks volunteers to memorize a 50-word list and tests recall over the following month. The researchers are interested in decay: memory loss that occurs as the neurochemical trace of a short-term memory fades over time. They use Ebbinghaus’s curve of forgetting as their guiding model. As shown in Figure 1, recall falls steeply during the first one to two days after learning, then the curve begins to level off, indicating that the rate of loss becomes smaller with time.

The lab compares several review schedules. One group reviews the list 24 hours after learning, another reviews it on Day 5, and a third receives no review until Day 25. The investigators argue that if a single review session is meant to prevent the greatest amount of decay, it should be placed where the slope of the curve is steepest rather than where recall is already changing slowly. They also note that the later, flatter portion of the graph does not mean memory is strong; instead, it means additional forgetting is occurring more gradually.

In a follow-up manipulation, participants are either asked to restudy the same list briefly or are given retrieval cues without extra study time. The researchers predict that restudy should primarily combat decay by strengthening the memory trace, whereas cues mainly help when stored information is still present but temporarily inaccessible. They therefore caution students not to confuse a plateau in the curve with complete preservation of the original memory.

Before publishing, the investigators consider how to advise students preparing for cumulative exams. Their draft conclusion is that spaced review is valuable, but the first review should occur before the steep early loss has already passed. They also warn that waiting until much later may produce only a modest gain because the most rapid forgetting has already occurred by then.

Read the graph for shape and slope, then connect it to the lab’s reasoning.

  • Use the passage to identify whether the researchers care about preventing decay, recovering inaccessible information, or both.
  • Then inspect the figure: the curve is steep early and flatter later.
  • A steeper slope means a faster rate of forgetting, not necessarily a lower absolute amount remembered.
  • Questions about timing usually require combining the passage’s goal with the figure’s largest early drop.
  • If an answer ignores either the experimental purpose from the passage or the curve’s trend from the figure, it is probably incomplete.
