Passage 1 (Questions 1–5)
Figure 1 summarizes the main behavioral outcomes after temporary forebrain manipulations. The visual rule-switch required animals to stop following a previously rewarded light cue and instead adopt a new rule. The odor version used the same reward contingency change but replaced the light cue with scent cues.

A neuroscience group investigated whether two common teaching statements about the forebrain are overgeneralized by students. The first statement is that the thalamus relays incoming sensory information to cortex. The second is that the hypothalamus controls homeostatic drives such as feeding. The authors noted that students often remember the first statement so rigidly that they forget the major exception: olfactory information reaches cortex without first using the thalamus as its primary relay.

Rats were trained on two versions of a rule-switch task. In the visual version, a lamp predicted reward; after criterion performance was reached, the lamp contingency was reversed. In the odor version, a peppermint versus vanilla cue replaced the lamp, but the switch logic was identical. A separate fasting-and-feeding assay measured how much food was consumed during the first 15 minutes after a 12-hour fast.

One group underwent temporary silencing of the mediodorsal thalamus immediately before testing. Another group received patterned activation of the ventromedial hypothalamus (VMH), a manipulation predicted to suppress feeding without directly changing sensory relay. The authors warned students not to confuse poor task performance caused by drowsiness with poor performance caused by misrouting of the relevant sensory signal. For that reason, all animals were tested during the same circadian window, and general locomotion was unchanged across groups.

The team argued that a manipulation affecting the thalamic relay should selectively disrupt the task that depends on thalamocortical processing, whereas a manipulation affecting satiety circuits should alter feeding more strongly than sensory set-shifting. They also emphasized that the odor task was included specifically to challenge the common mistake that all sensory modalities depend equally on the thalamus.

  • Step 1: Identify which part of the passage defines the purpose of each manipulation and which part explains the odor exception.
  • Step 2: Read the figure by comparing visual-task errors, odor-task errors, and meal size across groups rather than looking at only one bar.
  • Step 3: Choose the answer that best links the forebrain structure to the specific pattern of deficits described in the passage, not just to a memorized definition.

Passage 2 (Questions 6–10)
Figure 2 compares how different midbrain interventions affected rapid orienting to a flash or tone and the latency to initiate a learned lever press. All animals had intact peripheral hearing and vision.

A laboratory studying rapid orienting responses focused on three midbrain systems that students often memorize separately and then fail to integrate. The superior colliculus helps direct orienting to visual events, the inferior colliculus participates in auditory processing and reflexive responses to sound, and dopaminergic neurons in the substantia nigra pars compacta (SNpc) influence movement initiation through basal ganglia circuits.

To force students to reason from patterns rather than labels, the researchers used three reversible interventions. Cooling the superior colliculus was expected to slow head-turning toward light flashes. Cooling the inferior colliculus was expected to interfere more with responses to tones. Blocking dopamine release from SNpc was not expected to abolish perception of either stimulus, but it was expected to make initiation of a learned action sluggish even when the animals detected the cue correctly.

Each rat underwent two orienting tests: one requiring a rapid head turn toward a brief flash and another requiring a head turn toward a tone burst. In a separate operant chamber, the same animals pressed a lever after a warning cue to obtain sucrose. The authors reminded students that delayed movement after a cue does not necessarily mean the animal failed to sense the cue; the bottleneck may lie in transforming detected information into an initiated motor act.

The team predicted that a manipulation of sensory collicular processing would produce a modality-specific orienting delay, whereas an SNpc manipulation would show a broader movement-initiation problem with comparatively preserved sensory orienting. Figure 2 summarizes the results.

Use this approach:

  • Step 1: Separate the passage into sensory orienting roles versus movement-initiation roles.
  • Step 2: Use Figure 2 to compare flash latency, tone latency, and lever-press initiation across all four groups.
  • Step 3: Pick the answer that explains the pattern of selective versus generalized slowing, not just the name of one midbrain structure.

Passage 3 (Questions 11–15)
Figure 3 shows three outcome measures after selective hindbrain lesions. The REM muscle-tone index rises when normal REM atonia is lost. All values were obtained after recovery from surgery and during matched circadian periods.

A sleep-and-balance clinic collaborated with a hindbrain physiology lab to design a teaching case around three structures students frequently confuse: the cerebellum, the medulla, and the pons. The cerebellum fine-tunes coordination and error correction during movement. The medulla contains vital autonomic nuclei that help regulate respiratory rhythm and cardiovascular reflexes. The pons participates in sleep-state regulation and normally contributes to the muscle atonia of REM sleep.

To create a pattern-recognition exercise, the lab generated three lesion groups in rodents: a cerebellar vermis lesion, a small medullary lesion affecting respiratory centers, and a pontine tegmentum lesion. Animals then completed a rotarod coordination task, overnight respiratory monitoring, and electromyography during REM sleep. Students were warned not to treat “hindbrain” as one functional block; the same broad region can contain circuits for posture, breathing, and REM motor suppression that dissociate sharply in experiments.

The clinicians paired the rodent findings with a patient who reported dream-enactment behaviors, intermittent nocturnal breathing pauses, and mild gait unsteadiness. They told students that a good answer would not simply list every hindbrain structure but would weigh which structure best explains each measured abnormality. Figure 3 summarizes the lesion data that students used for comparison.

The authors emphasized one final point: the most compelling lesion assignment should account for the strongest abnormality in the symptom domain of interest while remaining consistent with the relative sparing of the other domains. In other words, the best answer usually comes from matching a pattern, not from memorizing one function in isolation.

Use this approach:

  • Step 1: Sort the passage into three symptom domains: coordination, autonomic breathing, and REM motor suppression.
  • Step 2: Compare the figure across all three measures so you can see which lesion produces the largest selective abnormality in each domain.
  • Step 3: Match the clinical or experimental pattern to the lesion that best explains the whole profile, not just one isolated sign.
