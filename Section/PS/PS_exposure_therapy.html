Exposure Therapy
Timer 13:00
Passage 1 (Questions 1–8)

Exposure therapy is a behavioral treatment in which a patient is repeatedly confronted with a feared stimulus in a controlled setting until distress begins to decline. A neuroscience team examined whether this decline in arachnophobia reflects a broad reduction in threat responding or simply fatigue from repeatedly viewing the same images in the scanner.

Figure 1 fMRI results of the treatment and waitlist groups before and after the 3-hour interval.

Twenty-four adults with clinically significant spider fear were randomly assigned either to an exposure group or to a waitlist group. All participants first underwent fMRI while viewing neutral baseline screens and then spider images. The exposure group then completed a three-hour session of graded spider-image viewing; participants remained with each image set until their self-reported distress fell before advancing to the next set. The waitlist group spent the same interval resting quietly and returned for a second scan. To limit common misinterpretations, investigators kept the image order, scanner duration, and timing of the post-session scan identical across groups. Figure 1 shows mean percent signal change from baseline in the dorsomedial prefrontal cortex, amygdala, anterior cingulate cortex, and parahippocampal gyrus for the treatment group before therapy (TB), treatment group after therapy (TA), waitlist group before the interval (WB), and waitlist group after the interval (WA).

The investigators noted that lower post-session distress scores were most closely associated with reduced activity in the amygdala and parahippocampal gyrus. They also cautioned that a small residual signal in the anterior cingulate cortex after treatment should not automatically be read as treatment failure because that region may support cognitive control and reappraisal during exposure. Their main claim was that a successful exposure session should reduce coordinated threat and context processing, not merely make participants tired of the scanner environment.

Use this approach for most questions in this passage:

  • Start with the design. Identify which bars compare therapy itself (TB → TA) and which bars control for time and repeated scanning alone (WB → WA).
  • Separate regions by role. Use the passage to decide which regions are linked to fear/context processing versus which may reflect cognitive control.
  • Compare direction and magnitude. A strong inference usually depends on whether activity falls, stays elevated, or rises slightly after the 3-hour interval.
  • Eliminate one-source answers. If a choice could be answered from the passage without the graph, or from the graph without the passage, it is probably too weak.
Questions
Which finding most strongly supports the investigators’ claim that the post-treatment neural shift was produced by exposure therapy rather than by simply spending more time in the scanner?
Explanation
The best answer is C. The passage states that both groups experienced the same image order, scanner duration, and timing, so the waitlist group controls for habituation to the scanning procedure. In the figure, threat- and context-related regions drop sharply only in the treated group (TB → TA), while the same regions stay high or rise in the waitlist group (WB → WA). That pattern supports a treatment-specific effect rather than simple time-in-scanner fatigue.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • A: This uses the graph, but it does not address the key alternative explanation of repeated scanning.
  • B: Comparable baseline values support randomization, but they do not show that the later change was caused by therapy.
  • D: This describes activation during fear processing, not why TA changed differently from WA.
A treated participant reports that spider images no longer strongly evoke memories of places where spiders had previously been encountered. Based on the passage and Figure 1, which region should show the clearest therapy-related reduction?
Explanation
The best answer is A. The passage links lower distress most closely to reduced activity in the amygdala and parahippocampal gyrus, and the parahippocampal gyrus is the region most directly associated with contextual or scene-related memory. Figure 1 also shows one of the strongest treatment-related drops for this region from TB to TA.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • B: The passage specifically suggests that residual ACC activity may reflect cognitive control or reappraisal, not contextual recall.
  • C: The dmPFC changes with treatment, but the passage does not tie it as specifically to context-linked recall.
  • D: This region is not part of the measured network in Figure 1.
Why does the small positive ACC signal at TA not, by itself, contradict the conclusion that exposure therapy was beneficial?
Explanation
The best answer is D. The passage warns against treating a small residual ACC signal as treatment failure because the ACC can support deliberate regulation or reappraisal. Figure 1 shows that, despite a slight positive ACC value at TA, the amygdala and parahippocampal gyrus fall substantially, which is more consistent with improved fear regulation than with persistent unchecked threat responding.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • A: WA does not show reduced ACC activity; it remains high.
  • B: The passage gives no fixed rule that ACC must mirror amygdala activity.
  • C: The graph shows several regions change after therapy, not only the ACC.
If an untreated participant later showed a neural profile most similar to WA during another spider-viewing task, which behavioral outcome would be most consistent with the study design?
Explanation
The best answer is B. In the passage, the exposure group stayed with image sets until distress declined, whereas the waitlist group did not receive that training. Figure 1 shows the WA profile as continued elevation across the fear/context network rather than the reductions seen at TA. That pattern is most consistent with distress remaining high across repeated spider images.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • A: The passage associates successful regulation more with the TA pattern, not the WA pattern.
  • C: A later scan alone does not imply that fear disappeared; the graph shows the opposite.
  • D: Nothing in the passage or graph concerns motor coordination.
Suppose salivary cortisol were measured immediately before and after the 3-hour interval. Which result would best strengthen the interpretation that the TA pattern reflects a genuine reduction in fear responding rather than only a change in self-report strategy?
Explanation
The best answer is C. The investigators argue that successful exposure should reduce threat responding, not just alter what participants say. If cortisol also falls specifically in the treatment group while the waitlist group does not show the same change, that physiological result would converge with the fMRI pattern in Figure 1 and strengthen the interpretation of genuine fear reduction.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • A: Equal increases would not support a treatment-specific fear reduction.
  • B: Baseline differences complicate interpretation and do not show that the therapy caused the post-session change.
  • D: The passage does not justify treating endocrine and neural measures as unrelated.
Why was it especially important that the researchers matched image order and total scanner time between groups?
Explanation
The best answer is A. The passage explicitly warns against the common error of attributing post-session changes to treatment when they could instead reflect repeated scanning or repeated image viewing. Figure 1 is interpretable precisely because the waitlist group had the same scanner exposure but did not show the same decreases that appear at TA.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • B: Equal scanner time does not guarantee equal subjective distress by session end.
  • C: The purpose was not to cap amygdala responses; baseline activation is part of the measurement.
  • D: A waitlist control is still a control, not an active treatment.
If the investigators reanalyzed only treated participants whose distress ratings failed to decline during the exposure session, which TA feature would most likely become least pronounced?
Explanation
The best answer is D. The passage says lower post-session distress was most closely associated with reduced amygdala and parahippocampal gyrus activity. In Figure 1, the parahippocampal gyrus also shows one of the clearest treatment-related drops. If distress did not decline, that TA reduction should weaken first.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • A: WA belongs to the waitlist group and would not be altered by restricting the treated sample.
  • B: Baseline similarity is a between-group feature, not the post-treatment effect most tied to distress reduction.
  • C: The passage frames small ACC activity as possible cognitive control, not the strongest marker of reduced distress.
Which overall mechanism is most consistent with both the investigators’ interpretation and the pattern in Figure 1?
Explanation
The best answer is B. The passage says successful exposure should reduce coordinated threat and context processing, and it notes that some residual ACC activity may reflect reappraisal rather than failure. Figure 1 matches that interpretation: the amygdala and parahippocampal gyrus drop strongly at TA, while the ACC remains slightly positive rather than disappearing completely.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • A: The graph shows decreases after treatment, not global amplification.
  • C: The passage does not say therapy works only through the ACC, and the figure shows large changes in other regions.
  • D: The groups do not change in the same direction; TA differs clearly from WA.
