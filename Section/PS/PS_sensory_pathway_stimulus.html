Sensory Pathway Stimulus
Timer 13:00
Passage 1 (Questions 1–8)

Laboratory studies of pain must account for two competing effects that can unfold over time. Repeated exposure to the same painful stimulus can produce modest habituation, so later trials may last longer even when no special intervention is present. At the same time, a concurrent sensory input can alter performance either because it continues to engage a specific sensory pathway or because its novelty briefly captures attention and then fades.

To examine these possibilities, investigators recruited 90 healthy adults ages 18–40 for a repeated cold-pressor task. In each trial, participants submerged one hand in 2°C water and withdrew it when the pain became intolerable. Group 1 heard slow instrumental music through over-ear headphones. Group 2 heard irregular metallic construction noise through the same model headphones, and the average sound intensity was matched to that of Group 1. Group 3 wore identical headphones but heard no sound, allowing the investigators to estimate the effect of repeated cold exposure alone. Participants were instructed not to count, hum, or speak during the task.

The task was completed three times with 20-minute breaks between trials. Mean tolerance time for each group is shown in Figure 1. The investigators emphasized two cautions when interpreting the data. First, Group 3 should not be treated as a static “true baseline”; if that group drifts upward across trials, the change is more consistent with habituation to the cold pressor than with analgesia caused by silence. Second, because the two sound conditions were volume-matched, any divergence between Groups 1 and 2 cannot be attributed to sound intensity alone. The investigators therefore proposed that the pleasant auditory stimulus might produce a sustained benefit, whereas the aversive auditory stimulus might provide only a short-lived attentional capture before its unpleasantness or reduced novelty lowers tolerance.

Figure 1 Mean cold-pressor tolerance by group across three trials

Use a consistent 3-step method:

  • Identify the comparison the passage actually sets up: repeated exposure alone, pleasant auditory input, and aversive auditory input are not interchangeable conditions.
  • Read the line trends, not just single values: Group 3 estimates habituation to the cold pressor; Group 1 and Group 2 must be interpreted relative to that drift and relative to one another.
  • Use the investigators’ cautions to eliminate overclaims: the passage explicitly warns against treating Trial 1 as a pure baseline and against explaining Group 1 versus Group 2 differences by volume alone.
  • For follow-up or sensory-pathway questions, ask whether the new manipulation preserves the original auditory pathway, replaces it with another modality, or tests an alternative explanation such as novelty, distraction, or stress.

Question 1
Which interpretation is most strongly supported by Figure 1 together with the investigators’ warnings?
Correct answer: C
The graph shows that Group 2 starts highest on Trial 1 but then declines markedly, while Group 3 drifts upward slightly. The passage warns that Group 3’s upward drift should be interpreted as habituation to repeated cold exposure, not as a therapeutic effect of silence. It also states that Groups 1 and 2 were volume-matched, so the difference between them cannot be blamed on loudness alone. The best synthesis is that the harsh sound may have captured attention early but did not provide a sustained benefit, and by later trials its aversive quality or reduced novelty lowered tolerance.
  • A is too strong because Trial 1 does not isolate preexisting pain threshold from experimental effects.
  • B contradicts the passage: sound intensity was matched across the two auditory groups.
  • D ignores the investigators’ caution that Group 3 estimates repeated-exposure habituation, not analgesia caused by silence.
Question 2
If the study were repeated with Group 2 hearing a different unfamiliar aversive sound in each trial, which outcome would most directly support the investigators’ novelty explanation for Group 2’s original pattern?
Correct answer: A
The investigators propose that Group 2’s Trial 1 advantage may reflect novel attentional capture. If that explanation is correct, then restoring novelty on every trial by changing the aversive sound should preserve more of the early benefit. In other words, Group 2 should stay closer to its original Trial 1 value rather than dropping sharply toward Trial 3.
  • B could occur for many reasons, but it would not specifically test the novelty account.
  • C incorrectly assumes the passage supports “all sounds distract equally”; the volume-matched groups diverged strongly.
  • D concerns Group 3, which is the repeated-exposure control and should be unaffected by what Group 2 hears.
Question 3
The investigators want to repeat the study in participants with profound conductive hearing loss caused by otosclerosis. Which modification would preserve the most similar sensory-pathway stimulus to the original auditory conditions?
Correct answer: D
The original manipulation relied on an auditory sensory pathway stimulus. Otosclerosis causes a conductive hearing loss, so a method that bypasses normal middle-ear transmission while still delivering sound to the auditory system is most similar. Bone conduction does exactly that by vibrating the skull and stimulating the inner ear through an auditory route.
  • A uses the somatosensory system, not the auditory pathway.
  • B substitutes a visual pathway.
  • C involves internal imagery and cognition rather than an external sensory stimulus comparable to the original design.
Question 4
Which additional finding would most weaken the conclusion that Group 1’s Trial 3 advantage depends specifically on an auditory sensory pathway rather than on general cognitive distraction?
Correct answer: B
To weaken an auditory-pathway explanation, the best evidence would show that a benefit similar to Group 1 can be produced without actual auditory input. If silently imagining or rehearsing the melody reproduces Group 1’s later-trial advantage, then the benefit could come from general cognitive distraction or mental engagement rather than from stimulation of the auditory pathway itself.
  • A says nothing about whether the effect is auditory-specific.
  • C concerns novelty of the equipment, not a nonauditory mechanism that reproduces Group 1’s trajectory.
  • D may support a stress interpretation for Group 2, but it does not directly challenge auditory specificity for Group 1.
Question 5
Because Groups 1 and 2 received sound at matched average intensity, which conclusion is best justified by Figure 1?
Correct answer: A
The passage explicitly states that the auditory stimuli were volume-matched. Therefore, when Figure 1 shows Group 1 rising and Group 2 falling across trials, the divergence cannot be explained by loudness alone. The more defensible interpretation is that the groups differ because of stimulus valence, adaptation, novelty, or stress-related effects.
  • B is unsupported; the passage does not suggest different stimulus duration.
  • C is contradicted by Group 2, which received auditory input but declined substantially.
  • D is incorrect because Group 3’s silent-headphone condition was included precisely to estimate repeated-exposure drift under matched equipment conditions.
Question 6
Suppose a follow-up study records heart rate immediately after each trial. Which unshown result would most strongly support the claim that Group 2’s late decline reflects increasing stress rather than merely the loss of novelty?
Correct answer: D
Figure 1 shows that Group 2 declines across trials even though Group 3 drifts upward, suggesting something is counteracting ordinary habituation. If post-trial heart rate rose progressively only in Group 2, that would support the idea that the aversive sound produced a growing stress response, not just fading novelty.
  • A would not distinguish Group 2 from the other groups.
  • B points away from a Group 2-specific stress explanation.
  • C is internally inconsistent with the observed Trial 3 pattern and does not support escalating stress.
Question 7
Which statement best integrates the trends of all three groups in Figure 1?
Correct answer: B
Group 3 rises slightly, which the passage says is most consistent with habituation to repeated cold exposure. Group 1 rises more strongly, suggesting the pleasant auditory stimulus may enhance that improvement. Group 2 starts high but then falls below Group 1 and converges toward Group 3, indicating an early benefit that does not persist.
  • A is wrong because both Group 1 and Group 3 also increase across trials.
  • C overweights Trial 1 and ignores the full trajectory.
  • D is contradicted by the upward drift in Groups 1 and 3.
Question 8
A student argues that Figure 1 shows the music condition reduced pain from the very beginning of the experiment. Based on the passage and the figure, what is the best critique of that claim?
Correct answer: C
The passage explicitly warns against treating a single early value as a pure baseline. On Trial 1, between-group differences could reflect individual variability, immediate attentional effects, or other factors. The stronger inference comes from asking how each group changes relative to the repeated-exposure control (Group 3) across all trials. That is why the upward trajectory of Group 1 is more informative than Trial 1 by itself.
  • A discusses intensity matching, which does not by itself refute an immediate music effect.
  • B misstates the design: Group 3 still wore matched equipment and serves as the repeated-exposure control.
  • D is too strong; a helpful condition need not be highest on Trial 1 to show a later benefit.
