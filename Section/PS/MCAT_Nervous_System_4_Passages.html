Passage 1 (Questions 1–5)
A patient quickly describes the heat stimulus accurately and can later lift the arm on command, but no immediate withdrawal occurs when the forearm is touched with the painful probe. Which site of damage best matches the passage?
Explanation
The best answer is A. The passage states that spinal interneurons can relay sensory input into a rapid protective withdrawal while information is also sent to the brain. If conscious detection and delayed voluntary movement are preserved but the rapid withdrawal is lost, the missing component is the spinal interneuron portion of the reflex arc.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • B: Autonomic damage would not specifically abolish only the rapid somatic withdrawal while sparing sensation and later voluntary movement.
  • C: If the sensory receptors or afferent limb were lost, the patient would not describe the heat normally.
  • D: The patient does consciously detect the stimulus, so a thalamic relay failure is not the best fit.
Group P reported the heat normally and showed sweating and tachycardia, yet the stimulated arm neither withdrew reflexively nor moved voluntarily on request. Which pathway was most clearly preserved in that group?
Explanation
The best answer is C. Sweating and tachycardia are classic fight-or-flight responses, so the passage indicates that autonomic sympathetic output remained intact. The inability to withdraw or voluntarily move the arm points instead to damage in the somatic motor pathway to that limb.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • A: Somatic motor output was the impaired component, because neither reflexive nor commanded arm movement occurred.
  • B: If the spinal relay to skeletal muscle were preserved, at least the rapid withdrawal would still be expected.
  • D: The physiologic pattern given is sympathetic, not parasympathetic.
The investigators found that EEG and regional cerebral blood-flow changes associated with cortical processing increased only after withdrawal had already begun. Which interpretation is most consistent with that result?
Explanation
The best answer is D. The passage explicitly contrasts early withdrawal with later cortical EEG and blood-flow changes. That pattern means the protective movement can begin through spinal circuitry before the brain has completed conscious interpretation.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • A: This reverses the passage’s timing evidence.
  • B: The passage does not claim autonomic responses require full cortical analysis first.
  • C: The reflex still depends on sensory input reaching the spinal cord; intact cortex alone would not substitute for missing afferent input.
  • D: This is the only option that matches both the timing data and the reflex-arc explanation.
A student stimulates one autonomic branch and observes constricted pupils, increased gut motility, and slowed heart rate. Based on the passage, which branch was activated?
Explanation
The best answer is B. The passage reports that parasympathetic stimulation produces the opposite of sympathetic fight-or-flight effects: smaller pupils, greater digestive activity, and slower heart rate.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • A: Sympathetic stimulation enlarged pupils, decreased gut motility, and increased heart rate.
  • C: Somatic motor fibers control voluntary skeletal muscle, not these visceral patterns.
  • D: Afferent fibers carry sensory information toward the CNS rather than directly generating this coordinated visceral output.
Suppose a new patient has damage limited to the afferent sensory neuron carrying pain information from the forearm, while spinal interneurons, motor neurons, and the brain remain intact. Which outcome would most likely be seen during the heat-probe task?
Explanation
The best answer is C. Both the reflex and the conscious report depend on sensory information first entering the nervous system from the stimulated forearm. If the afferent limb is interrupted, the spinal cord and brain do not receive the relevant signal from that site.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • A: A reflex still needs sensory input to start the circuit.
  • B: Voluntary movement of the arm might be possible in general, but the patient would not know to move in response to an undetected forearm heat stimulus.
  • D: The cortex cannot compensate for a missing incoming signal that never reaches the CNS.
A patient has intact vision and hearing apparatus but is unreliable at detecting flashes and tones, while olfactory testing remains normal. Which forebrain structure is the most likely site of dysfunction?
Explanation
The best answer is B. The passage states that the thalamus relays sensory information to the cortex, with smell as the major exception. A patient who misses multiple non-olfactory sensory cues despite intact peripheral organs fits a thalamic relay problem.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • A: Hypothalamic damage would be expected to disturb homeostasis and endocrine integration rather than selectively spare smell while disrupting other sensory relay.
  • C: Hippocampal injury primarily affects memory consolidation, not broad non-olfactory cue detection.
  • D: An occipital lesion could explain visual problems but would not also account for impaired auditory detection with spared olfaction.
Which additional finding would most strongly support placement of a lesion in Group H rather than in Group T or Group L?
Explanation
The best answer is D. Group H in the passage had hypothalamic damage, producing disturbed homeostasis and impaired endocrine integration through the hypophyseal portal connection to the anterior pituitary.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • A: That points more toward hippocampal involvement in memory consolidation.
  • B: That pattern fits amygdalar injury within the limbic system.
  • C: Poor spoken-word detection suggests a sensory or temporal-cortical problem, not the homeostatic-endocrine profile of hypothalamic injury.
A patient can immediately repeat seven digits but forgets a newly introduced medication schedule by the next morning. Based on the teaching session, which lesion best explains this combination?
Explanation
The best answer is A. The passage specifically contrasts preserved immediate repetition with failure to retain new information over time in patients with hippocampal damage. That pattern indicates impaired consolidation rather than loss of attention or motor ability.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • B: Basal ganglia dysfunction mainly affects movement initiation, smoothness, and posture.
  • C: Parietal injury emphasizes somatic sensation and spatial processing deficits.

A patient understands the command “reach for the cup,” forms the plan, and has normal strength, yet movement begins hesitantly, looks poorly smoothed, and balance is unstable while standing. Which localization best matches the passage?
Explanation
The best answer is C. The passage assigns movement smoothing and postural stability to the basal ganglia. Preserved comprehension and motor planning make a cortical language problem less likely, and normal strength argues against a primary lower motor deficit.

A right-handed patient can hear spoken language and recognize objects visually, but speech becomes nonfluent and effortful, planning is poor, and impulsive decisions increase. Which lesion is the best single explanation?
Explanation
The best answer is B. The passage links the frontal lobe to executive function, impulse control, long-term planning, motor sequencing, and speech production. Because language is usually left-dominant, a left frontal lesion best fits nonfluent speech plus poor planning and impulsivity.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • A: Occipital lesions would primarily affect visual processing, which is preserved here.
  • C: Parietal damage would be more likely to disturb somatic sensation and spatial manipulation.
  • D: Septal nuclei are associated with pleasure-seeking and addiction, not the speech-production and planning pattern described.
Which statement best explains why the course treated the public-speaking response and the later cortisol rise as related but not identical phenomena?

The best answer is D. The passage distinguishes the rapid fight-or-flight pattern from the slower hormonal stress response. Immediate dilation of pupils and rising heart rate fit sympathetic catecholamine signaling, whereas cortisol comes later from the adrenal cortex.
Why the other choices are not as good

  • B: The passage emphasizes different timing and different signaling modes, not a single simultaneous peak.
  • C: The early physiologic pattern is sympathetic, not parasympathetic.
A study drug raises pain tolerance during a cold-pressor task but leaves light-touch detection unchanged. Which endogenous signaling system would this most closely resemble?

Why the other choices are not as good
  • B: Glutamate is described mainly as an excitatory brain transmitter, not a natural analgesic system.
  • C: Dopamine in this passage is linked to smooth movement and posture.
  • D: Acetylcholine is important at skeletal muscle, parasympathetic targets, and alertness, but it is not presented here as the main endogenous analgesic system.
A patient with recurrent seizures improves after treatment that strengthens inhibitory signaling in the brain. Which neurotransmitter was most likely enhanced?

A toxin causes skeletal-muscle weakness, reduced salivation, and impaired gastrointestinal “rest-and-digest” activity, while pain perception and mood remain unchanged. Which signaling molecule is most directly implicated by that pattern?
Explanation
The best answer is D. The passage presents acetylcholine as unusual because it participates at skeletal muscle in the somatic nervous system and also in the parasympathetic nervous system. A single toxin that weakens voluntary muscle contraction and blunts parasympathetic secretory/gut effects therefore most strongly suggests reduced cholinergic signaling.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • A: Serotonin is linked here to mood, sleep, eating, and dreaming rather than neuromuscular plus parasympathetic failure.
  • B: Endorphins mainly modulate pain.
  • C: Epinephrine is associated with sympathetic arousal, not the combined somatic-neuromuscular and parasympathetic deficits described.
