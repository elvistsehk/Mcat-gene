Passage 1 (Questions 1–7)

Emotion researchers have long debated whether conscious feeling follows directly from bodily arousal, arises simultaneously with physiological change, or depends on a person’s interpretation of why the body has become aroused. To compare these models, investigators designed a laboratory study in which a temporary autonomic response could be produced before participants viewed emotionally valenced stimuli.

Sixty participants first completed a neutral baseline survey and then watched six short film clips, three positive and three negative. Throughout testing, the researchers monitored heart rate, respiration, skin conductance, and EEG activity. Self-reported emotion was recorded on a 1-to-10 scale, with higher values indicating more positive feeling and lower values indicating more negative feeling.

Participants were randomly assigned to one of three groups. The dose informed group received epinephrine and was told that the injection would temporarily increase heart rate and shakiness. The dose misinformed group also received epinephrine but was told that the injection was an inert vitamin solution. The no dose group received no injection. Figure 1 shows the mean ratings for each group before the clips, during the positive clips, and during the negative clips.

Figure 1 Mean emotion ratings by group and viewing condition

Before the clips began, heart rate and respiration rose above baseline in the two injected groups but not in the no dose group. During positive clips, all groups showed greater left frontal EEG activation than at baseline; during negative clips, all groups showed greater right frontal EEG activation than at baseline. In a second phase, the same participants were instructed to suppress outward emotional expression while watching a new set of clips. Even when facial expression was reduced, skin conductance still rose above baseline in all three groups.

One interpretation of the first phase was that unexplained arousal magnified context-dependent labeling: participants who felt aroused but lacked an explanation relied more strongly on the emotional tone of the clips when deciding what they were feeling. A competing interpretation was that epinephrine simply intensified whatever emotion the clips produced, regardless of whether participants had an explanation for the arousal.

Use a three-step approach.

  • First identify the shared physiology: both injected groups had increased autonomic arousal before the clips, but only one group had an explicit explanation for it.
  • Then use Figure 1 to compare the size and direction of the rating shifts across conditions. The key question is not just who changed, but which comparison isolates unexplained arousal.
  • Finally, connect the graph to the theory being tested. If arousal is present but unexplained, Schachter-Singer predicts stronger use of environmental cues; if physiology alone determines emotion, the two epinephrine groups should look much more similar.
Question 1
Using the Schachter-Singer theory, which explanation best accounts for the larger positive-versus-negative rating gap in the dose misinformed group than in the dose informed group?
The best answer is C. The passage states that both injected groups showed increased heart rate and respiration before the clips, so both experienced arousal. Figure 1 shows, however, that the dose misinformed group gave the most extreme ratings in opposite directions: the highest positive rating during positive clips and the lowest rating during negative clips. Under Schachter-Singer, unexplained arousal is labeled using contextual cues, so the emotionally valenced clips should influence the misinformed group most strongly.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • Choice A is inconsistent with the passage, which says the two injected groups—not the no dose group—showed the pre-clip rise in autonomic activity.
  • Choice B reverses the study design: the misinformed group did receive epinephrine and therefore did have arousal.
  • Choice D invents an EEG pattern not reported in the passage. The passage reports left frontal increases during positive emotion and right frontal increases during negative emotion for all groups.
Question 2
Which comparison in Figure 1 most specifically isolates the effect of having physiological arousal without an explicit explanation?
The best answer is A. To isolate unexplained arousal, the two groups being compared should both have the same drug-induced physiological activation but differ only in whether that activation was explained. The dose informed and dose misinformed groups both received epinephrine, yet only the informed group had a correct explanation for the arousal. Figure 1 then shows how much more extreme the misinformed group’s labeling became under the same positive or negative cue.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • Choice B changes both arousal status and explanation status at the same time, so it does not isolate the role of explanation.
  • Choice C compares an injected group with a noninjected group before the clips, which does not isolate the effect of unexplained arousal during emotional labeling.
  • Choice D contains no manipulation of injected arousal at all.
Question 3
Suppose a fourth group received epinephrine and was falsely told that the injection would make them feel mildly sad before any clips appeared. Which pre-clip result would most strongly support the idea that cognitive labeling helps shape emotion ratings?
The best answer is D. Figure 1 shows that before the clips the dose misinformed group already rated itself more negatively than the other groups, even though the passage says both injected groups had increased heart rate and respiration. A fourth group that was given a specifically negative explanation for its arousal should, under a cognitive-labeling account, report a more negative pre-clip emotion than groups whose arousal was explained neutrally or absent. The key point is that similar physiology would now be paired with a different interpretation.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • Choice A would weaken the claim that labeling matters, because it would suggest explanation had little effect.
  • Choice B incorrectly assumes epinephrine inherently produces positive emotion; the study tests labeling, not a built-in positive valence.
  • Choice C ignores the existing figure pattern, in which the misinformed group already differs before the clips despite lacking clip-based cues.
Question 4
Which conclusion is least consistent with the combined evidence from Figure 1 and the passage’s report that both injected groups showed increased heart rate and respiration before the clips?
The best answer is B. The passage says both injected groups had elevated autonomic arousal before the clips, yet Figure 1 shows that their self-reported emotions differed substantially, especially during positive and negative clips. If physiology alone fully determined emotion, the two epinephrine groups should have looked much more similar. The observed divergence instead argues that interpretation and context matter.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • Choice A is supported by the figure because similar arousal did not produce identical ratings.
  • Choice C matches the study’s central interpretation.
  • Choice D also fits the pattern: the misinformed group, which lacked an explanation, showed the most context-dependent ratings.
Question 5
During the second phase, participants attempted to suppress outward emotional expression, yet skin conductance still increased above baseline in all groups. When this finding is combined with Figure 1, which inference is most justified?
The best answer is D. In Figure 1, the no dose and informed groups both changed with clip valence, but the misinformed group changed the most. The second-phase finding shows that physiology can remain elevated even when expression is deliberately suppressed. Together, these results support the idea that conscious report and labeling depend on how arousal is interpreted, not simply on whether the body is activated at all.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • Choice A contradicts the passage, which explicitly says skin conductance increased above baseline during suppression.
  • Choice B overstates the result; the no dose group did respond to clip content, but the injected groups were still critical for testing the role of arousal and explanation.
  • Choice C goes beyond the data: the suppression result was reported for all groups, not as an absence of physiology in the misinformed group.
Question 6
A critic argues that epinephrine simply intensifies any emotion produced by the clips, regardless of whether participants know why they are aroused. Which pattern in Figure 1 most directly challenges that claim?
The best answer is C. If epinephrine merely intensified emotion independent of interpretation, the two injected groups should have shown similar rating changes because both had the same drug-induced arousal. Instead, Figure 1 shows the misinformed group becoming much more extreme than the informed group in both positive and negative directions. That difference points to explanation and labeling, not simple pharmacologic amplification alone.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • Choice A shows that clips affected even the no dose group, but it does not directly test the critic’s claim about epinephrine.
  • Choice B is true but does not compare the two epinephrine groups, which is the critical test.
  • Choice D is too broad; it does not specifically address whether explanation of arousal matters.
Question 7
Based on Figure 1 and the EEG findings, which condition would most likely combine the strongest left frontal activation with the most positive mean self-report?
The best answer is A. The passage states that positive emotional periods were associated with increased left frontal activation. Figure 1 shows that the highest positive mean rating occurred in the dose misinformed group during positive clips. Combining those two pieces of information identifies that condition as the best match.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • Choice B involves negative clips, which the passage associates with right—not left—frontal activation.
  • Choice C is a baseline condition and does not show the strongest positive rating in the graph.
  • Choice D pairs the misinformed group with negative clips, which produced the lowest rating and the wrong EEG lateralization.
