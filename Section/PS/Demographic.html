Passage 1 (Questions 1–7)

Demographers often distinguish between the total burden of a disease in a population and the probability that a member of a specific subgroup has that disease. This distinction becomes especially important in aging populations, where large demographic groups may account for many cases simply because they contain more people, even if their within-group prevalence is not the highest.

To estimate the distribution of dementia in older adults, investigators recruited 1,200 participants age 65 and older from several regions of the United States. The sample included 540 males and 660 females and was stratified by age, sex, and race. Diagnoses were established using clinical records and standardized cognitive evaluations. Table 1 summarizes the sample and the number of dementia cases observed in each subgroup.

Table 1 Demographic and dementia data for the study sample.

The researchers emphasized that the last column shows each subgroup’s share of the entire pool of dementia cases, not the probability that a person in that subgroup has dementia. Because some subgroups are larger than others, the subgroup contributing the greatest number of cases is not automatically the subgroup with the highest risk.

The authors also cautioned that the table is descriptive rather than causal. Apparent differences by age, sex, or race could reflect multiple interacting factors such as access to care, survival bias, educational history, or diagnostic opportunity. Therefore, the table can support comparisons of burden and prevalence, but not simple conclusions that any one demographic category directly causes dementia.

Use a three-step approach.

  • First separate burden from risk. The last column shows share of all cases, whereas prevalence within a subgroup requires cases divided by total in that subgroup.
  • Then identify whether the question asks who contributes more total cases or who is more likely to have dementia. Those are different claims.
  • Finally keep the authors’ warning in mind: Table 1 supports descriptive comparisons, not simple causal claims.
Question 1

The best answer is B. White participants contribute 54% of all dementia cases, but they also make up 60% of the sample. Prevalence must be computed as cases divided by subgroup total, not by using only the final column. The passage explicitly warns against confusing total burden with within-group risk.

Question 2
Which subgroup listed in Table 1 has the highest within-group dementia prevalence?
The best answer is D. Compute within-group prevalence for each option: 65–74 = 15/520 ≈ 2.9%; males = 20/540 ≈ 3.7%; Black participants = 15/270 ≈ 5.6%; >85 = 18/270 ≈ 6.7%. The >85 subgroup is highest.
Why the other choices are not as good

  • Choice B is lower than both the Black subgroup and the >85 subgroup.
  • Choice C is elevated but still below the >85 subgroup.

Question 4
If a follow-up survey added 300 more dementia-free participants to the 65–74 group while all other counts stayed the same, which result would most likely follow?
The best answer is B. Adding dementia-free participants changes the denominator for the 65–74 prevalence from 520 to 820 while the numerator stays 15, so prevalence decreases. But the subgroup still contributes 15 of the total 54 dementia cases, so its share of all dementia cases remains the same.
Why the other choices are not as good
