Passage 1 (Questions 1–6)

A physiology laboratory compared ATP production in rapidly dividing mammalian cells grown under four conditions designed to perturb energy metabolism at different steps. The investigators reminded students that a fall in oxygen consumption does not automatically prove that glycolysis has stopped, and that lactate accumulation does not mean the mitochondria are absent. Instead, the location of each block must be considered: glycolysis occurs in the cytosol, pyruvate dehydrogenase converts pyruvate to acetyl-CoA in the mitochondrial matrix, the citric acid cycle occurs in the matrix, and the electron transport chain together with ATP synthase resides in the inner mitochondrial membrane.

The cells were supplied with glucose as the only exogenous fuel. In untreated control cells, pyruvate entered the mitochondria, pyruvate dehydrogenase generated acetyl-CoA and NADH, the citric acid cycle generated additional NADH and FADH2, and oxidative phosphorylation produced most of the ATP. In one experimental group, fluoropyruvate was used to inhibit pyruvate dehydrogenase. In a second group, malonate competitively inhibited succinate dehydrogenase. Because succinate dehydrogenase is both a citric acid cycle enzyme and Complex II of the ETC, the investigators warned that this perturbation can lower fumarate formation while also decreasing the delivery of electrons from FADH2 to ubiquinone. In a third group, oligomycin inhibited ATP synthase. In a fourth group, cells were placed under severe hypoxia.

The investigators highlighted several patterns. Blocking pyruvate dehydrogenase reduced entry of glycolytic carbon into the citric acid cycle, so ATP production shifted toward substrate-level phosphorylation in glycolysis and lactate release rose. Malonate caused succinate to accumulate upstream of succinate dehydrogenase and reduced, but did not abolish, oxidative phosphorylation because NADH generated elsewhere could still donate electrons through Complex I. Oligomycin prevented protons from flowing back through ATP synthase, which secondarily slowed the electron transport chain because the proton gradient became difficult to dissipate. Under severe hypoxia, the terminal electron acceptor became limiting, so the electron transport chain slowed markedly and cells regenerated cytosolic NAD+ primarily by reducing pyruvate to lactate.

Finally, the team cautioned students against a common shortcut: a high mitochondrial NADH/NAD+ ratio can arise either because NADH production is increased or because oxidation of NADH by the electron transport chain is impaired. Therefore, they argued, the redox ratio must be interpreted together with oxygen use, metabolite buildup, and ATP yield rather than in isolation.

Locate the block first. Ask whether the perturbation is acting in the cytosol, mitochondrial matrix, or inner mitochondrial membrane.

  • Match the data pattern to the step. Combine oxygen consumption, lactate release, redox ratio, and metabolite buildup instead of relying on only one column.
  • Track carbon and electrons separately. Pyruvate and acetyl-CoA describe carbon flow; NADH, FADH2, ubiquinone, and oxygen describe electron flow.
  • Remember substrate-level phosphorylation. Glycolysis can still make ATP even when oxidative phosphorylation is impaired.
  • Use dual-function enzymes carefully. Succinate dehydrogenase belongs to both the TCA cycle and Complex II, so its inhibition affects two linked systems.
