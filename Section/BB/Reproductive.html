Passage 1 (Questions 1–6)

A reproductive endocrinology service compared hormone responses in patients being evaluated for infertility. The attending warned students not to reduce the reproductive system to a list of organs. Internal and external male and female structures matter clinically because symptoms can arise from failed hormone signaling, failed gamete transport, or damage to accessory tissues rather than from the gonads alone. The hypothalamus releases gonadotropin-releasing hormone (GnRH), which stimulates the anterior pituitary to release follicle-stimulating hormone (FSH) and luteinizing hormone (LH). In males, LH acts on Leydig cells to support testosterone production, while FSH acts mainly on Sertoli cells to support spermatogenesis. In females, FSH promotes follicular development and granulosa-cell estrogen production, whereas the LH surge helps trigger ovulation and supports luteinization.

The team reminded trainees of another frequent mistake: infertility cannot be localized from sex steroid values alone. For example, intact testosterone in a male does not prove normal sperm production, because Sertoli-cell support and ductal patency may still be abnormal. Likewise, normal estrogen exposure in a female does not prove that ovulation occurred, because a mature follicle may fail to rupture if the periovulatory LH signal is inadequate. To force students to integrate anatomy with endocrine data, the clinic used a short protocol combining serum assays with focused bedside observations.

The attending then connected the data to reproductive anatomy. In the male, sperm are generated within seminiferous tubules of the testes, mature further in the epididymis, and normally travel through the vas deferens before mixing with seminal vesicle and prostatic secretions. Thus, azoospermia can reflect failure of sperm production or failure of sperm transport. In the female, the ovaries produce oocytes and sex steroids, the fallopian tubes receive the ovulated oocyte, and the uterus provides the site of implantation and fetal development. Therefore, evidence of estrogen exposure does not itself prove that the oocyte ever left the ovary.

Finally, the attending emphasized feedback logic. If the pituitary can respond vigorously to administered GnRH, then the pituitary is capable of releasing FSH and LH, shifting suspicion upstream toward the hypothalamus. If testosterone is relatively preserved in a male but spermatogenesis is poor, then Leydig-cell function cannot by itself explain the infertility. If estradiol rises but luteal progesterone never increases, then follicular development occurred without successful ovulation and corpus luteum formation.

Start with the hormone axis. Ask whether the problem is hypothalamic, pituitary, gonadal, or postgonadal transport.

  • Use GnRH testing logically. A strong FSH/LH response after administered GnRH means the pituitary can work and suspicion shifts upstream.
  • Separate steroid production from gamete production. Near-normal testosterone does not guarantee normal spermatogenesis, and estrogen exposure does not guarantee ovulation.
  • Map the anatomy. Testes and seminiferous tubules produce sperm; epididymis and vas deferens transport them. Ovaries produce follicles and hormones, but ovulation and tubal pickup are separate steps.
  • Use progesterone as a clue. Persistently low luteal progesterone after an estrogen rise suggests that ovulation and corpus luteum formation failed.
