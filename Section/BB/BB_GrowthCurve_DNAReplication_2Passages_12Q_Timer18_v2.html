Passage 1 (Questions 1–6)
1. The curve in Figure 1 plots log(viable cells) versus time. During the exponential phase, the graph segment appears approximately linear. Which inference is most justified?
Explanation

Correct (A): A straight line on a log-scale plot indicates exponential increase, consistent with a constant doubling time during exponential phase.

Why the other choices are not correct:

  • B: Constant absolute increase would appear linear on a linear y-axis, not necessarily on a log scale.
  • C: The plot does not measure nutrients; nutrients generally decline during growth.
  • D: Exponential growth implies many cells are viable and dividing.
2. In the follow-up run, a translation inhibitor is added during mid-exponential phase. Which outcome is most consistent with the passage’s causal description?
Explanation

Correct (B): Exponential division requires ongoing protein synthesis. Blocking translation should quickly slow or halt net division, flattening the curve toward a plateau.

Why the other choices are not correct:

  • A: Growth may stop without immediate lysis; abrupt death is more typical of lethal envelope damage.
  • C: Translation is essential for exponential growth.
  • D: Reducing waste cannot increase growth if biosynthesis is blocked.
3. An antibiotic that inhibits cell-wall crosslinking is added during mid-exponential phase. Based on the growth-curve logic, which pattern is most likely?
Explanation

Correct (C): Cell-wall synthesis is critical during division; blocking crosslinking preferentially kills rapidly dividing exponential-phase cells, producing a sharp viability drop.

Why the other choices are not correct:

  • A: Wall defects compromise survival; they do not accelerate growth.
  • B: Cell-wall remodeling occurs during growth and division.
  • D: Lethal wall defects do not preserve indefinite viability.
4. Two flasks reach stationary phase. Flask X has low nutrients but minimal toxic waste; Flask Y has abundant nutrients but high toxic byproducts. Both show no net growth. Which statement best explains why both can appear stationary?
Explanation

Correct (D): Stationary phase is ~zero net change; it can arise from different constraints (nutrients vs toxins) as long as net births ≈ net deaths.

Why the other choices are not correct:

  • A: Different stressors can yield different metabolic states.
  • B: Cells can remain metabolically active in stationary phase.
  • C: Rates need not be identical—only net change is near zero.
5. A student relabels the y-axis as “total biomass” instead of “log viable cell number.” Which observation would most strongly reveal the mislabeling?
Explanation

Correct (A): Viability can decline while dead cells/debris still contribute to biomass; biomass may not fall as sharply as viable counts during death phase.

Why the other choices are not correct:

  • B: Curve shape depends on scaling, not just labeling.
  • C: Biomass can still show lag depending on acclimation.
  • D: Biomass can plateau under nutrient limitation.
6. To estimate doubling time, the lab uses the slope of the exponential segment on a log scale. Which change would make the estimated doubling time appear longer without changing the true doubling time?
Explanation

Correct (B): If the calculation assumes ln but the plot uses log10, the slope is scaled by a constant, producing an incorrect doubling time unless converted properly.

Why the other choices are not correct:

  • A: Inoculum shifts the curve vertically; slope stays the same if doubling time is unchanged.
  • C: OD can decouple from viability, but log-base mismatch is a clearer pure analysis artifact.
  • D: More nutrients can extend exponential phase without changing doubling time.
7. Figure 2 shows a replication bubble with two forks moving away from an origin. If a prokaryotic cell had only one functional origin but forks were forced to move in only one direction, what is the most direct consequence?
Explanation

Correct (C): Bidirectional replication doubles effective replication speed from one origin. Unidirectional movement reduces active forks, increasing total replication time.

Why the other choices are not correct:

  • A: One fork cannot match two forks’ throughput.
  • B: Error rate is not inherently reduced by having one fork.
  • D: Lagging-strand synthesis still occurs at a fork due to antiparallel strands.
8. A drug inhibits topoisomerase activity ahead of replication forks (Figure 2). Which observation is most consistent with this inhibition?
Explanation

Correct (D): Topoisomerase relieves positive supercoils generated by unwinding. Without it, torsional stress accumulates and stalls fork progression.

Why the other choices are not correct:

  • A: Polymerase cannot synthesize 3′→5′.
  • B: Helicase unwinds DNA; primase makes primers.
  • C: Topoisomerase inhibition doesn’t directly increase ligase activity.
9. Using Figure 3, a student identifies one strand as lagging at a particular fork. Which evidence best supports that identification?
Explanation

Correct (A): Lagging-strand synthesis is discontinuous with repeated priming (Okazaki fragments) because polymerase must synthesize 5′→3′ while the fork opens.

Why the other choices are not correct:

  • B: Continuous synthesis describes the leading strand.
  • C: Bacteria do have primase; lagging synthesis occurs in both.
  • D: Polymerase cannot synthesize 3′→5′.
10. A eukaryotic chromosome is linear, while a prokaryotic chromosome is circular. Which replication-related problem is most specific to linear chromosomes?
Explanation

Correct (B): Linear chromosomes face the end-replication problem, requiring telomeres/telomerase to prevent progressive shortening.

Why the other choices are not correct:

  • A: Eukaryotes have many origins on linear chromosomes.
  • C: Okazaki fragments occur in both due to antiparallel strands.
  • D: Topoisomerase is needed in both because unwinding generates supercoils.
11. Compared with prokaryotes, eukaryotes use multiple origins on each chromosome. Which inference best follows from this difference?
Explanation

Correct (C): Multiple origins increase the number of active forks, shortening total replication time for long chromosomes.

Why the other choices are not correct:

  • A: Origins don’t replace replisome enzymes.
  • B: Leading/lagging still exist at each fork.
  • D: Primers are still required to start synthesis.
12. A mutant lacks DNA ligase activity during replication. Which pattern would most likely be detected after one round of replication?
Explanation

Correct (D): Ligase seals phosphodiester bonds between Okazaki fragments; without it, unsealed nicks persist, especially on the lagging strand.

Why the other choices are not correct:

  • A: Initiation depends on helicase/primase/origin recognition, not ligase.
  • B: Fork speed is not increased by leaving fragments unsealed.
  • C: Primer removal is done by nucleases/polymerase activities, not ligase.
