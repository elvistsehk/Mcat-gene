Metabolism: The pentose phosphate pathway and G6PD deficiency
Timer 12:00
Passage 1 (Questions 1–7)
Figure 1. Mean cloning efficiency for control and G6PD-null cells under 24% and 13% oxygen.

A cancer metabolism group investigates why loss of glucose 6-phosphate dehydrogenase (G6PD) impairs colony formation in some settings more than others. The trainees are reminded that G6PD catalyzes the rate-limiting step of the oxidative phase of the pentose phosphate pathway (PPP), where glucose 6-phosphate is oxidized to generate NADPH. NADPH is then used to regenerate reduced glutathione (GSH), which helps neutralize hydrogen peroxide and related reactive oxygen species. However, the group warns students not to assume that every defect in the PPP primarily reflects failure to make nucleotides. The nonoxidative phase, through enzymes such as transketolase and transaldolase, can rearrange glycolytic intermediates to generate ribose 5-phosphate even when oxidative PPP flux is limited.

To test whether the main consequence of G6PD loss is defective antioxidant defense or defective pentose synthesis, investigators compare control cells with G6PD-null cells in a cloning-efficiency assay. Cloning efficiency measures the percentage of plated single cells that successfully form colonies. The authors reason that if nucleotide precursor shortage is the dominant problem, G6PD-null cells should clone poorly regardless of oxygen tension because DNA replication would remain limited. By contrast, if oxidative defense is the dominant problem, lowering oxygen should selectively rescue the null cells by reducing oxidant burden while leaving the missing enzyme absent.

Figure 2. Mean cloning efficiency for control and G6PD-null cells after exposure to increasing diamide concentrations.

The team next exposes both genotypes to diamide, a thiol-oxidizing reagent that depletes cellular GSH. They emphasize another common mistake: the rescue seen at lower oxygen does not mean G6PD activity has returned. Rather, the key question is whether the cells can tolerate oxidant stress when GSH recycling becomes more difficult. If G6PD-null cells are mainly limited by loss of PPP-derived NADPH, increasing diamide should cause a much steeper fall in colony formation in the null cells than in controls, especially once the antioxidant buffer is overwhelmed.

After reviewing both figures, the trainees are asked to decide whether G6PD loss is better understood as a defect in ribose production, a defect in antioxidant defense, or some combination of the two. The instructors stress that the strongest answers must use both the experimental setup in the passage and the shape of the two datasets, rather than citing the PPP from memory.

Use a three-step approach for every question:

  • Step 1: Identify the comparison the graph is making. Decide whether the crucial contrast is 24% versus 13% oxygen or low versus high diamide.
  • Step 2: Link the graph to the passage mechanism. Ask whether the pattern fits loss of NADPH-dependent antioxidant defense or loss of ribose 5-phosphate production.
  • Step 3: Test the interpretation against both figures. A strong answer should explain why lower oxygen rescues G6PD-null cells and why diamide selectively collapses them as oxidant stress increases.

Targeted disruption of the housekeeping gene encoding glucose 6-phosphate dehydrogenase (G6PD): G6PD is dispensable for pentose synthesis but essential for defense against oxidative stress. EMBO J, 14(21), 5209-5215.

Question 1 of 7
Which interpretation best explains why G6PD-null cells clone very poorly at 24% oxygen but nearly as well as controls at 13% oxygen?
Explanation
Figure 1 shows a selective rescue of G6PD-null cells at 13% oxygen, not restoration of the missing enzyme. The passage states that G6PD generates NADPH for antioxidant defense and that the nonoxidative PPP can still generate ribose 5-phosphate. Together, those clues indicate that lowering oxygen decreases oxidant stress enough for null cells to proliferate, even though G6PD itself remains absent.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • A: The passage explicitly warns that rescue at lower oxygen does not mean G6PD activity has returned.
  • C: Without G6PD, the oxidative PPP cannot simply be sped up by oxygen lowering; the enzyme is missing.
  • D: Diamide increases oxidant burden by depleting GSH, whereas the low-oxygen condition does the opposite in terms of oxidative stress.
Question 2 of 7
Investigators add one intervention before plating G6PD-null cells at 24% oxygen. Which is most likely to improve cloning efficiency based on both the passage and the figures?
Explanation
Figure 1 shows that lowering oxidant burden rescues G6PD-null cells, and Figure 2 shows that raising oxidant burden with diamide selectively destroys their cloning efficiency. The passage links G6PD to NADPH-dependent regeneration of GSH. Therefore, the most rational rescue at 24% oxygen is an intervention that improves antioxidant defense, not one that only supplies pentose carbon.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • A: If nucleotide shortage were the main issue, the null cells should also have failed badly at 13% oxygen, but they did not.
  • B: More substrate cannot compensate for the absence of G6PD itself.
  • C: The figures argue that redox stress, not isolated ribose shortage, is the condition most tightly linked to loss of clonogenic survival.
Question 3 of 7
A student claims that the 13% oxygen result proves G6PD is unnecessary for NADPH-dependent antioxidant defense. Which observation from Figure 2 most directly refutes that claim when interpreted with the passage?
Explanation
The passage says diamide depletes GSH, so sensitivity to diamide specifically tests the cell’s ability to maintain NADPH-supported antioxidant buffering. Figure 2 shows that once diamide rises into the midrange, the G6PD-null curve drops precipitously while controls remain viable. That pattern directly supports the idea that G6PD becomes crucial when oxidant stress threatens the glutathione system.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • B: This supports rescue at lower oxygen but does not by itself show what happens when glutathione is challenged directly.
  • C: The control pattern is informative, but the strongest refutation comes from the divergence between the two genotypes under diamide.
  • D: Poor performance at 24% oxygen suggests stress sensitivity, but diamide is the more specific mechanistic probe because the passage identifies it as a GSH-depleting reagent.

In Figure 2, G6PD-null cells look relatively preserved at lower concentrations but then show a marked fall once diamide enters the ~20–30 μM range and above. That pattern is most consistent with exhaustion of NADPH-supported glutathione recycling, not with a sudden block in pentose carbon rearrangement.
Why the other choices are not as good

Question 6 of 7
Which follow-up result would most strongly contradict the investigators’ favored conclusion from the two figures?
Explanation
The two figures together support the idea that oxidative stress handling is the dominant vulnerability: lowering oxygen rescues the null cells, while diamide selectively wipes them out. A finding that ribose alone fully rescues both high-oxygen and diamide conditions would instead argue that pentose shortage, not antioxidant failure, is the main driver of the phenotype. That would directly oppose the authors’ interpretation.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • A: This would be consistent with oxidative-stress limitation because still-lower oxygen would further reduce ROS burden.
  • B: This would strengthen, not weaken, the antioxidant-defense interpretation.
  • C: This also supports the current model because antioxidant rescue should help more if redox stress is primary.
Question 7 of 7
A separate lab knocks down transketolase rather than G6PD. Compared with the patterns in Figures 1 and 2, which phenotype would be most consistent with that manipulation?
Explanation
The passage distinguishes the nonoxidative phase from the G6PD-dependent oxidative phase. If transketolase were reduced, the cell would have more trouble making ribose 5-phosphate from glycolytic intermediates, so colony formation could remain impaired even at 13% oxygen, unlike the rescued G6PD-null cells in Figure 1. Because transketolase is not the main source of PPP-derived NADPH, the phenotype would not need to show the same sharply oxidative-stress-selective diamide collapse seen in Figure 2.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • B: Loss of transketolase should not be metabolically silent if nucleotide precursor rearrangement becomes limiting.
  • C: The passage assigns NADPH production in the oxidative phase to G6PD, not to transketolase.
  • D: Antioxidant rescue addresses redox stress; it does not recreate the expected consequences of weakening nonoxidative carbon rearrangement.
