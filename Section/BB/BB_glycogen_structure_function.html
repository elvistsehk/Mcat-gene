Metabolism: The structure and function of glycogen
Timer 12:00
Passage 1 (Questions 1–7)
Figure 1. Schematic glycogen tetramer showing a branched arrangement of glucose residues.

A hepatology research group uses the simplified glycogen structure in Figure 1 to teach new trainees how glycogen architecture determines both rapid fuel mobilization and the pattern of disease seen in glycogen storage disorders. In the figure, one glucose residue serves as a branch point that connects three terminal glucose residues. The team reminds students that glycogen synthase extends chains by forming α-1,4 glycosidic bonds, whereas the branching enzyme introduces α-1,6 linkages that generate additional outer ends. In real glycogen particles, these outer chains are much longer than the tetramer shown, but the same bonding logic applies.

To model glycogen breakdown, investigators incubate purified glycogen with glycogen phosphorylase. They emphasize a common MCAT trap: phosphorylase does not hydrolyze glycogen with water and does not attack branch points directly. Instead, it uses inorganic phosphate to cleave α-1,4 linkages between the anomeric carbon of one glucose residue and the C4 carbon of the adjacent residue, releasing glucose 1-phosphate from nonreducing ends. When phosphorylase approaches an α-1,6 branch, it stalls several residues away from the branch point. Debranching enzyme must then rearrange the short branch and hydrolyze the remaining α-1,6 linkage, releasing one free glucose rather than glucose 1-phosphate.

The group next analyzes liver glycogen from three sources. Control glycogen is highly branched, remains soluble in the cytosol, and produces a rapid burst of glucose 1-phosphate when phosphorylase is added. Sample P shows a normal branching pattern on microscopy, but very little glucose 1-phosphate is released unless purified phosphorylase is added externally. Sample B contains fewer branch points, longer outer chains, and poorly soluble glycogen-like deposits. In short incubations, Sample B still releases some glucose 1-phosphate because its long outer α-1,4 chains remain accessible, but over months the deposits accumulate and the liver develops fibrosis.

Finally, the investigators compare hepatocytes forced to store equal amounts of carbohydrate either as free intracellular glucose or as glycogen. Cells storing free glucose swell more, whereas cells storing glycogen remain osmotically stable and can still mobilize carbohydrate quickly after glucagon or epinephrine stimulation. The trainees are told to connect those physiologic findings back to the structural features in Figure 1 rather than memorizing enzyme names in isolation.

Use a three-step approach for every question:

  • Step 1: Identify the bond or residue in the figure. Ask whether the question is pointing you to an α-1,4 linkage, an α-1,6 branch point, or the number of nonreducing ends.
  • Step 2: Match that structure to the enzyme evidence in the passage. Decide whether the result is most consistent with phosphorylase, debranching enzyme, or branching enzyme activity or deficiency.
  • Step 3: Predict the physiologic consequence. Translate the structural change into a functional outcome such as glucose 1-phosphate release, solubility, fasting response, or liver injury.
Questions
Question 1 of 7
Sample P shows normal branching on microscopy, but little glucose 1-phosphate appears unless purified phosphorylase is added. Which defect best explains the findings?
Explanation
The passage says that Sample P has a normal branching pattern, so the α-1,6 architecture seen in the figure is intact. It also says that glucose 1-phosphate production is restored when purified phosphorylase is added. The figure and passage together identify phosphorylase as the enzyme that removes glucose units from α-1,4 linkages, so a phosphorylase defect best fits the data.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • A: The experiment starts with preformed glycogen, so importing free glucose is not the step being tested.
  • C: A branching-enzyme defect would reduce α-1,6 branch points and change glycogen architecture, which the passage says is not seen in Sample P.
  • D: A pure debranching defect would still allow some early α-1,4 phosphorolysis from longer outer chains; the key clue here is rescue by added phosphorylase.
Question 2 of 7
Which explanation best accounts for the tendency of Sample B to progress to hepatic fibrosis?
Explanation
The figure shows that branching depends on an α-1,6 linkage. The passage states that Sample B has fewer branch points, longer outer chains, poor solubility, and fibrosis. Putting those together, the most likely mechanism is deficient branching, which generates abnormal long chains that precipitate and damage hepatocytes over time.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • A: Sample B has fewer branch points, not more, and the major problem described is insoluble deposition rather than osmotic lysis.
  • B: The passage distinguishes Sample P, not Sample B, as the one rescued by added phosphorylase.
  • C: Nothing in the passage suggests excessive debranching; the structural clue is reduced branching in the figure’s α-1,6 architecture.
Question 3 of 7
Suppose investigators place a 14C label on the anomeric carbon of the bottom glucose residue in Figure 1 before degradation begins. After phosphorylase stalls and debranching enzyme completes branch removal, where would that label first be expected to appear?
Explanation
In the figure, the bottom residue is part of the branch. The passage says that phosphorylase cannot cleave the α-1,6 branch point directly and that debranching enzyme ultimately hydrolyzes the remaining α-1,6 bond to release one free glucose. Therefore the labeled anomeric carbon would first appear in free glucose, not in glucose 1-phosphate.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • B: Glucose 1-phosphate is the product of phosphorylase acting on α-1,4 linkages, not the final hydrolysis of the branch linkage highlighted in the figure.
  • C: Branching enzyme is involved in synthesis, not in the degradative step described.
  • D: Lactate could form much later in metabolism, but it is not the first product released from the labeled branch residue.
Question 4 of 7
Equal masses of control glycogen and Sample B glycogen are exposed to phosphorylase for 20 seconds before any debranching enzyme is added. Which result is most likely?
Explanation
The passage links greater branching to more rapid mobilization, and the figure helps you see why: every added branch creates another outer terminal end. Because phosphorylase attacks nonreducing ends on α-1,4 chains, the more highly branched control glycogen supports more simultaneous attack sites and therefore a larger early burst of glucose 1-phosphate.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • A: Longer chains do not mean more α-1,6 bonds; Sample B actually has fewer branch points.
  • B: Initial rate depends on the number of accessible chain ends, not only total glucose content.
  • D: Branching increases overall access by creating more ends; it does not block every α-1,4 linkage.
Question 5 of 7
Why is the tetramer in Figure 1 a useful bonding schematic but a poor depiction of efficient real-world glycogenolysis by phosphorylase alone?
Explanation
This question requires combining the geometry of the figure with the enzyme rule in the passage. The passage says phosphorylase stalls several residues before an α-1,6 branch point. In the tetramer, each terminal glucose is already very close to the branch point, so the drawing is excellent for showing bonding logic but not for representing long physiologic chains that can undergo extended phosphorolysis.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • A: Liver glycogen definitely contains α-1,6 branches.
  • C: The passage explicitly says phosphorylase acts on α-1,4 linkages, not α-1,6 linkages.
  • D: The key limitation here is branch proximity in the schematic, not a requirement for reducing ends.
Question 6 of 7
During a brief glucagon challenge in isolated hepatocytes, which sample would be expected to show some early glucose 1-phosphate release yet still carry the greatest long-term risk of cirrhosis?
Explanation
The passage says Sample B has longer outer α-1,4 chains, so some early phosphorylase-mediated glucose 1-phosphate release is still possible. However, the figure reminds you that this sample has too few α-1,6 branches, and the passage links that reduced branching to poor solubility and fibrosis. Thus Sample B fits both parts of the question.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • A: Sample P lacks effective phosphorylase activity, so its acute release of glucose 1-phosphate is the problem.
  • B: Control glycogen is the normal, soluble reference and is not the fibrotic sample.
  • D: Debranching enzyme does not replace phosphorylase for bulk glycogen breakdown and does not generate most glucose 1-phosphate by itself.
Question 7 of 7
Researchers force hepatocytes to store equal carbon either as free intracellular glucose or as highly branched glycogen. The glycogen-storing cells swell less yet release carbohydrate faster during adrenergic stimulation. Which rationale best integrates those findings with Figure 1?
Explanation
The passage states that glycogen-storing cells are more osmotically stable and still mobilize carbohydrate rapidly. Converting many glucose monomers into a polymer reduces the number of dissolved particles, which lowers osmotic stress. The figure then explains the second part: branching creates multiple outer ends, allowing many α-1,4 chains to be degraded in parallel during stimulation.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • B: The osmotic effect is the opposite of what the passage reports, and branching accelerates rather than slows mobilization.
  • C: Phosphorylase does not directly cleave α-1,6 bonds.
  • D: Glycogen is a storage form of the same carbon load; the advantage is architectural, not that less carbohydrate is stored.
