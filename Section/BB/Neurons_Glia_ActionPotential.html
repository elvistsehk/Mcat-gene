Passage 1 (Questions 1–6)

A neurology research team investigated why one demyelinating lesion caused slowed but preserved conduction in a peripheral motor neuron, whereas a related lesion in central white matter produced a broader network deficit. The attending reminded students that the names of myelin-producing cells depend on location: Schwann cells myelinate axons in the peripheral nervous system, whereas oligodendrocytes myelinate axons in the central nervous system. The group also reviewed the major glial categories. Astrocytes help regulate the extracellular environment and support the blood-brain barrier. Microglia act as phagocytic immune cells of the central nervous system. Ependymal cells line ventricles and participate in cerebrospinal fluid handling. Oligodendrocytes and Schwann cells provide myelin, but only Schwann cells belong to the peripheral nervous system.

The experiment combined electrophysiology with synaptic pharmacology. A test neuron remained near a resting membrane potential because potassium leak conductance and the sodium-potassium ATPase maintained ionic gradients, even though the membrane was not equally permeable to all ions. The researchers then delivered repeated excitatory inputs. Closely spaced inputs from one presynaptic neuron gradually pushed the membrane toward threshold over time, whereas simultaneous inputs from several nearby presynaptic neurons did so by summing across space. Once threshold was reached at the axon hillock, voltage-gated sodium channels opened rapidly, producing the rising phase of the action potential. Shortly afterward, sodium channels inactivated and voltage-gated potassium channels dominated repolarization and after-hyperpolarization.

The attending further emphasized three classic ways to block neurotransmitter action at the synapse: prevent release from the presynaptic terminal, block receptor binding on the postsynaptic membrane, or remove the transmitter less efficiently by altering breakdown or reuptake pathways. Students often confused this last mechanism with direct receptor antagonism, but the two produce different pharmacologic signatures. In the release experiments, vesicle fusion depended critically on calcium entry at the nerve terminal. Without extracellular calcium influx, the arriving action potential did not efficiently trigger exocytosis despite intact depolarization of the axon.

Finally, the team compared the neuron’s major structural regions. Dendrites primarily receive synaptic input. The soma integrates signals and supports cell metabolism. The axon hillock is the trigger zone where threshold is typically reached. The axon propagates the action potential, and the nerve terminal converts the electrical signal into chemical transmission. Thus, in a single case students had to distinguish CNS from PNS myelin cells, glial support roles, resting potential maintenance, temporal versus spatial summation, channel changes during the action potential, and the calcium dependence of transmitter release.

Track where the problem is. Decide whether the stem points to myelin, synaptic input summation, membrane channels, or neurotransmitter release.

  • PNS versus CNS. Schwann cells belong to the peripheral nervous system; oligodendrocytes belong to the central nervous system.
  • Summation. Repeated input from one synapse suggests temporal summation; simultaneous input from several synapses suggests spatial summation.
  • Action potential phases. Rapid depolarization depends on voltage-gated sodium channels; repolarization and after-hyperpolarization depend mainly on potassium channel activity after sodium inactivation.
  • Vesicle fusion. Presynaptic calcium entry is the trigger for neurotransmitter-containing vesicle fusion.
