After the high-glucose pulse, investigators added a citrate lyase inhibitor while mitochondrial pyruvate dehydrogenase activity remained normal. Which change most directly explains why palmitate synthesis fell in that group?
Explanation
The best answer is C. The passage says mitochondrial acetyl-CoA must be exported indirectly as citrate and then cleaved by cytosolic citrate lyase (Equation 3) to regenerate cytosolic acetyl-CoA. When citrate lyase is inhibited, cytosolic citrate accumulates but malonyl-CoA and palmitate fall. That pattern means the block is the loss of usable cytosolic acetyl-CoA, which is needed both as the primer in Equation 2 and as the substrate for acetyl-CoA carboxylase in Equation 1.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • A: Low NADPH explains the pentose-phosphate-pathway inhibition group, not the citrate-lyase inhibition group.
  • B: Equation 2 still releases CO2; the problem is a shortage of cytosolic acetyl-derived substrate upstream.
  • D: The passage does not report defective mitochondrial citrate production; citrate actually accumulated in the cytosol.
Which finding would be most consistent with the group in which the pentose phosphate pathway was inhibited but citrate export and ATP remained near normal?
Explanation
The best answer is A. The passage states that pentose-phosphate-pathway inhibition lowered the NADPH/NADP+ ratio without lowering ATP or citrate export. Those cells still formed near-normal malonyl-CoA, yet palmitate output fell. Equation 2 shows why: fatty acid synthase requires 14 NADPH, so reductive chain elongation is limited even when upstream substrate supply remains relatively intact.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • B: Equation 2 cannot proceed normally without malonyl-CoA and NADPH; ATP alone is not enough.
  • C: Equation 3 uses ATP and CoA, not NADPH directly.
  • D: The passage says citrate export remained near normal in this group; the defining abnormality was low NADPH.
In the pulse-chase experiment, 14CO2 entered malonyl-CoA rapidly but was scarcely retained in purified palmitate after the chase. Which explanation best accounts for that result?
Explanation
The best answer is D. The passage explicitly warns students to compare Equation 1 with Equation 2. Equation 1 adds CO2 when acetyl-CoA is converted to malonyl-CoA, so label enters the intermediate quickly. Equation 2 then releases CO2 during chain elongation, so the transiently labeled carboxyl carbon is not stably retained in the final palmitate molecule after the chase.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • A: Citrate lyase generates cytosolic acetyl-CoA; it is not the step that explains transient CO2 labeling of malonyl-CoA.
  • B: The passage links label loss to pathway stoichiometry, not to ATP limitation.
  • C: Palmitate synthesis depends on cytosolic acetyl-derived intermediates, including labeled malonyl-CoA.
Which observation from the study most strongly supports the conclusion that citrate is acting as a feedforward activator of acetyl-CoA carboxylase rather than merely as a carbon shuttle?
Explanation
The best answer is B. The passage reports that exogenous citrate increased malonyl-CoA formation in otherwise untreated cells, showing a regulatory effect upstream of palmitate formation. But citrate could not restore malonyl-CoA when acetyl-CoA carboxylase was inhibited. That result localizes citrate’s feedforward effect to acetyl-CoA carboxylase rather than to a later synthetic step or to carbon delivery alone.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • A: This shows a transport-cleavage bottleneck but does not by itself prove regulatory activation of acetyl-CoA carboxylase.
  • C: Figure 1 helps with stoichiometry, not with pinpointing citrate’s regulatory target.
  • D: CO2 release in Equation 2 does not identify the site of citrate regulation.
A mutant hepatocyte line cannot export citrate from mitochondria, but its pentose phosphate pathway remains intact. Based on the passage and equations, which paired change is most likely in the fed state?
Explanation
The best answer is A. The passage explains that citrate is the exported carrier that links mitochondrial acetyl-CoA production to cytosolic fatty acid synthesis. If citrate export is blocked, citrate would tend to accumulate in mitochondria, while cytosolic acetyl-CoA supply would fall. Because acetyl-CoA carboxylase in Equation 1 needs cytosolic acetyl-CoA to make malonyl-CoA, cytosolic malonyl-CoA would decrease even if NADPH generation remained normal.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • B: Blocking export should not lower mitochondrial citrate while increasing palmitate; it should impair cytosolic synthesis.
  • C: Preserved NADPH does not overcome the shortage of exported carbon substrate.
  • D: Equation 2 explicitly requires one acetyl-CoA primer, and Equation 1 requires acetyl-CoA to make malonyl-CoA.
A student argues that every carbon in palmitate must come from malonyl-CoA because Equation 2 uses seven malonyl-CoA molecules. Which response best corrects the student using both Equation 2 and Figure 1?
Explanation
The best answer is C. Figure 1 shows a 16-carbon product, and Equation 2 shows that synthesis begins with one acetyl-CoA plus seven malonyl-CoA molecules. Each elongation cycle contributes a retained two-carbon unit after decarboxylation, while the primer provides the initial two carbons. Together that yields 16 carbons in palmitate.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • A: Citrate is a shuttle intermediate, not a separate one-carbon donor to the final fatty acid.
  • B: Equation 1 makes malonyl-CoA, not the palmitate backbone directly.
  • D: The released CO2 does not prevent inference of the retained 16 carbons when the primer and elongation units are counted correctly.
Investigators discover a new drug that leaves cytosolic citrate high and malonyl-CoA high, yet palmitate remains low. Which defect is most consistent with that pattern?
Explanation
The best answer is B. High cytosolic citrate means carbon has reached the cytosol, and high malonyl-CoA means acetyl-CoA carboxylase is still functioning. If palmitate is nevertheless low, the block must be downstream of malonyl-CoA production, most logically at fatty acid synthase itself or in the NADPH-dependent reductive steps of Equation 2.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • A: If citrate export failed, cytosolic citrate would not stay high.
  • C: Acetyl-CoA carboxylase inhibition should lower malonyl-CoA, not leave it high.
  • D: ATP shortage would mainly limit Equation 1 and therefore reduce malonyl-CoA formation.
