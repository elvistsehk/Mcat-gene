Passage 1 (Questions 1–6)

A developmental biology laboratory compared early embryonic cell populations after a transient mutation in a membrane-bound signaling protein. The supervising physician warned students not to confuse determination with differentiation. A cell can become committed to a developmental fate before it visibly acquires the specialized structures and proteins of that fate. The team also reminded students that potency decreases progressively during development: totipotent cells can generate all embryonic and extraembryonic tissues, pluripotent cells can generate derivatives of all three germ layers but not the placenta, and multipotent cells are restricted to a narrower family of related cell types.

To study the mutation, the researchers followed cells from the zygote through blastocyst formation and then into a neural-crest-derived lineage. They noted that some cells already failed to respond to a neighboring inducing tissue even before the cells expressed mature structural proteins. The attending emphasized that this is evidence of altered determination, not merely delayed differentiation. The project also used communication blockers to distinguish contact-dependent signaling from local signaling through secreted molecules. In one experimental arm, cells had to touch the signaling cell to respond. In another, cells responded only if they were nearby but not necessarily touching. A third arm produced a systemic hormonal-like effect in distant tissue, and a fourth required direct continuity through cytoplasmic channels.

In the signaling sub-study, cells exposed only when physically touching the signal source were classified as using juxtacrine communication. Cells that responded to a diffusible factor over a short distance were classified as paracrine. A long-distance endocrine-like response occurred only after the factor entered the circulation, whereas direct exchange through cytoplasmic channels was classified as communication through gap junctions. The researchers further observed that the mutant neural progenitors in Group C could still proliferate, but they no longer committed normally when the inducing neighbor was present. By contrast, Group D cells had already committed to a neuronal fate and later only showed delayed expression of mature neuronal features.

Finally, the team compared cell death patterns. Cells in Group E swelled and released contents into surrounding tissue, provoking inflammation after ischemic injury. Cells in Group F were quietly dismantled during normal sculpting of the embryo. The attending reminded students that apoptosis is an organized, ATP-dependent process important in development, whereas necrosis is more commonly associated with acute injury and disruption of membrane integrity. Thus, the same experiment required students to keep four distinctions separate at once: potency versus restriction, determination versus differentiation, physiologic apoptosis versus pathologic necrosis, and one mode of cell–cell communication versus another.

Separate commitment from appearance. If fate is chosen but mature markers are still absent, think determination before differentiation.

  • Potency declines with development. Totipotent → pluripotent → multipotent reflects progressive restriction in developmental options.
  • Communication depends on distance and contact. Juxtacrine requires touching; paracrine acts locally; endocrine acts at a distance through circulation; gap junctions require direct cytoplasmic continuity.
  • Apoptosis versus necrosis. Ordered shrinkage and fragmentation during remodeling suggests apoptosis; membrane rupture with inflammation suggests necrosis.
  • Use the table with the passage. The best answer often requires combining the stage, the response pattern, and the structural or inflammatory clue.
