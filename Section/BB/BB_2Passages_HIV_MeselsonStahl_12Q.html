Passage 1 (Questions 1–6)
1. A clinician notes that a patient’s HIV strain uses CCR5 as its co-receptor. A new drug occupies CCR5 without activating it. Which outcome is most directly predicted by the diagram and passage?
Explanation

Correct (A): Blocking CCR5 prevents the entry step that depends on co-receptor engagement. If the virus cannot enter, intracellular steps (reverse transcription → cDNA, integration, transcription/translation) cannot occur, so viral cDNA should not accumulate.

Why the other choices are not correct:

  • B: That describes integrase inhibition or a block after reverse transcription; CCR5 blockade acts earlier, before any cDNA is produced.
  • C: That describes a maturation/protease defect: budding can occur but virions are not infectious. CCR5 blockade prevents even the first infection cycle.
  • D: Ribosomal translation is downstream of entry and integration/transcription; CCR5 blockade does not directly disable host translation machinery.
2. In a cell culture, HIV particles attach to CD4 and a co-receptor, and fusion occurs. A separate compound then prevents conversion of viral RNA into cDNA. Which experimental observation would best support that the compound targets reverse transcriptase?
Explanation

Correct (B): If entry and uncoating occur, viral RNA can be present intracellularly. Reverse transcriptase inhibition prevents RNA → cDNA, so double-stranded viral DNA intermediates do not appear.

Why the other choices are not correct:

  • A: That suggests a late assembly/budding problem rather than reverse transcription failure.
  • C: If proviral DNA is integrated, reverse transcription must have already happened; this points to transcriptional regulation or polymerase-related issues, not RT.
  • D: This is a Meselson–Stahl-style density result and is unrelated to HIV reverse transcription.
3. A student argues that a drug yielding many budded but mostly noninfectious virions is most consistent with inhibiting a specific viral enzyme. Which enzyme is the best match?
Explanation

Correct (C): The passage notes that budding can produce immature particles that become infectious only after proteolytic processing (maturation). Protease inhibition allows budding but produces noninfectious virions.

Why the other choices are not correct:

  • A: Integrase inhibition prevents proviral integration; it would reduce production of viral proteins/particles rather than allow abundant budding of noninfectious virions.
  • B: RT inhibition prevents viral DNA formation; early block means far fewer particles are produced in the first place.
  • D: HIV is a retrovirus and does not use a nuclear RNA-dependent RNA polymerase for genome replication as described here; this choice is inconsistent with the diagram and HIV biology.
4. A patient is found to carry a homozygous loss-of-function mutation in CCR5. Based on the passage’s receptor logic, which clinical inference is most reasonable?
Explanation

Correct (D): CCR5 is a required co-receptor for CCR5-tropic HIV. If CCR5 is absent, those strains have difficulty entering. However, the passage notes that some strains can use CXCR4; those could still infect CD4+ cells via CD4 + CXCR4.

Why the other choices are not correct:

  • A: CD4 binding can still occur; the mutation is in CCR5, not CD4.
  • B: The diagram explicitly shows the need for a co-receptor in addition to CD4.
  • C: Absence of CCR5 doesn’t inherently increase susceptibility to CXCR4-tropic strains; it mainly removes one entry route. CXCR4-tropic infection depends on whether the strain can use CXCR4.
5. A researcher detects abundant circular, unintegrated double-stranded viral DNA in the nucleus but very little integrated proviral DNA. Which intervention best explains this pattern?
Explanation

Correct (A): If reverse transcription occurs, dsDNA can form and enter the nucleus. Integrase inhibition prevents insertion into the host genome, leading to accumulation of unintegrated (often circularized) viral DNA intermediates.

Why the other choices are not correct:

  • B: Blocking entry prevents intracellular DNA formation entirely, so you would not see abundant nuclear viral DNA.
  • C: Protease inhibition affects maturation of new virions, not integration of viral DNA in the initially infected cell.
  • D: Fusion is required for entry; without entry, reverse transcription cannot proceed. The premise contradicts the diagram.
6. In a longitudinal cohort, early isolates are predominantly CCR5-tropic, while later isolates from some patients are CXCR4-tropic. Which explanation best aligns with the passage’s logic and would most directly change the cell types the virus can efficiently enter?
Explanation

Correct (B): Tropism is driven by which co-receptor the envelope can engage after CD4 binding. Envelope variants that can use CXCR4 (instead of CCR5) expand the set of target cells and change entry efficiency.

Why the other choices are not correct:

  • A: HIV remains an RNA virus; tropism shifts are about receptor usage, not genomic material switches.
  • C: Protease affects maturation/infectivity of produced virions, not which co-receptor is used for entry.
  • D: Density-gradient logic in the passage set is from Meselson–Stahl, not HIV entry/tropism.
7. After shifting 15N-labeled E. coli to 14N medium, the class observes a single intermediate-density band after Gen 1. Which replication model is most directly ruled out by that single-band result?
Explanation

Correct (C): Conservative replication predicts that after one generation you would retain a heavy (15N/15N) duplex and produce a completely light (14N/14N) duplex, yielding two distinct bands (heavy + light). A single intermediate band is inconsistent with conservative replication.

Why the other choices are not correct:

  • A: Dispersive can produce a single band that shifts over time; Gen 1 alone does not rule it out.
  • B: Semiconservative predicts 100% hybrid after Gen 1, matching the single intermediate band.
  • D: If no replication occurred, you would still see the original heavy band, not an intermediate band.
8. Under semiconservative replication, what fraction of DNA molecules are expected to be hybrid (15N/14N) after Gen 3 following the shift to 14N?
Explanation

Correct (D): With semiconservative replication, all molecules are hybrid after Gen 1. Each additional generation halves the fraction of hybrids: Gen 2 = 50%, Gen 3 = 25%.

Why the other choices are not correct:

  • A: 75% is too high; hybrids decrease geometrically, not linearly.
  • B: 50% corresponds to Gen 2, not Gen 3.
  • C: 12.5% corresponds to Gen 4.
9. A student proposes that the observed appearance of a distinct light band by Gen 2 supports semiconservative replication over dispersive replication. What is the strongest reasoning?
Explanation

Correct (A): In dispersive replication, each molecule remains a mixture of old and new segments each round, producing one band that shifts progressively toward light. A distinct light band emerging alongside a stable hybrid band is characteristic of semiconservative replication.

Why the other choices are not correct:

  • B: Semiconservative gives 100% hybrid at Gen 1 (intermediate), not immediate all-light.
  • C: A persistent heavy band would fit conservative replication, not dispersive.
  • D: Hybrid density stays intermediate; it does not get heavier. What changes is the fraction of hybrid vs light molecules.
10. Suppose the culture was not fully equilibrated in 15N before the shift: 20% of DNA molecules were already 14N/14N at Gen 0. After one generation in 14N, which banding pattern is most consistent with semiconservative replication?
Explanation

Correct (B): Any 15N/15N molecules replicate to form hybrids (15N/14N) after Gen 1, eliminating pure heavy DNA. The preexisting 14N/14N molecules remain light and replicate to stay light. Thus you expect intermediate + light bands.

Why the other choices are not correct:

  • A: Light DNA replicates fine; replication does not require heavy labeling.
  • C: A pure heavy band would require unreplicated 15N/15N to persist, contradicting the ‘one generation’ replication step.
  • D: Not all molecules become hybrid if some were already light at Gen 0.
11. If DNA replication were conservative, what would be the expected banding pattern after Gen 2 (two generations in 14N), assuming complete labeling at Gen 0?
Explanation

Correct (C): Conservative replication keeps the original heavy duplex intact each generation while producing entirely light duplexes from new strands. After Gen 2 you still expect a heavy band (from the original duplexes) and a light band (from new duplexes), not an intermediate band.

Why the other choices are not correct:

  • A: A single intermediate band is not predicted by conservative replication.
  • B: Intermediate + light corresponds to semiconservative after Gen 2.
  • D: A gradually shifting single band corresponds most closely to dispersive replication.
12. A teaching assistant asks: “Which single observation would most strongly confirm that replication is semiconservative rather than dispersive?” Which choice best answers that question using only density-gradient logic?
Explanation

Correct (D): Both semiconservative and dispersive can produce an intermediate band at Gen 1. The key discriminator is Gen 2+: semiconservative yields two discrete populations (hybrid and light), whereas dispersive tends to maintain a single band that continuously shifts. Two clear bands with a persistent intermediate band strongly support semiconservative replication.

Why the other choices are not correct:

  • A: This is compatible with both semiconservative and dispersive models.
  • B: Growth rate differences do not directly test strand inheritance in DNA replication.
  • C: A single gradually shifting band is exactly what dispersive predicts, so it would not confirm semiconservative.
