Passage 1 (Questions 1–7)

Researchers often use apomyoglobin, the globin protein without its heme group, to study how amino-acid side chains influence protein folding. Previous work suggested that the A-helix helps nucleate early folding, whereas portions of the B- and E-helices contribute to a partially folded intermediate. Because hydrophobic packing can stabilize these early structures, replacing a bulky hydrophobic residue with a smaller residue may slow folding if that position is important for early core formation.

To test this idea, investigators generated site-directed mutants at selected positions and compared folding kinetics with wild-type apomyoglobin using stopped-flow fluorescence and circular dichroism. Table 1 summarizes the substitutions and the observed relative folding rate. Most substitutions were designed either to shrink a hydrophobic side chain or to introduce a charged residue expected to disturb local packing. The researchers emphasized, however, that a faster measured folding rate does not by itself prove that the final native state is more stable; a mutation may alter the path to folding without improving the final structure.

Figure 1. Amino-acid substitutions in different helices and their relative folding rates compared with wild-type apomyoglobin.

In a separate oxidation-protection assay, proteins were examined at pH 4, a condition that enriched a folding intermediate rather than the fully native state. Under that condition, the G-helix was the most protected from oxidation, whereas the F-helix remained comparatively exposed even at later stages of folding. The investigators therefore cautioned students not to assume that any helix carrying a slow mutant must necessarily be the first helix to fold; some mutations may instead disrupt late packing or local rearrangement.

How to reason

  • Start by separating mutation type from helix role. A substitution matters differently in an early nucleating helix than in a helix that becomes structured later.
  • Use the table to compare mutations within the same helix before concluding that an entire helix is always critical. In this set, not all B-helix substitutions behave the same way.
  • Do not equate a faster folding rate with greater final stability. The passage explicitly warns that kinetics and native-state stability are not identical.
  • When a question mentions the oxidation assay, combine that information with the table. Protection at pH 4 reflects the intermediate, while the table reports relative folding rate.
  • Be careful with charged substitutions. A slow mutant can reflect disrupted packing or local rearrangement, not simply the identity of the helix.
