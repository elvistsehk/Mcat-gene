Question 1
A researcher adds an inhibitor that specifically blocks isocitrate dehydrogenase. Which change would be expected to occur most immediately in one turn of the cycle?
Explanation
Isocitrate dehydrogenase is the first oxidative decarboxylation. Blocking it most directly causes isocitrate to build up while preventing formation of α-ketoglutarate, the first CO2, and the first NADH. Citrate formation occurs upstream, the FADH2 step is later at succinate dehydrogenase, and malate changes would be downstream rather than the most immediate effect.
  • A: Citrate synthase is upstream of the blocked step, so citrate can still form initially.
  • C: FADH2 is made later when succinate is oxidized to fumarate.
  • D: Malate is a later intermediate and would not be the earliest direct decrease.
Question 2
The figure shows that succinyl-CoA synthetase generates GTP when succinyl-CoA becomes succinate. Which feature of the substrate best explains why this step can support substrate-level phosphorylation?
Explanation
Succinyl-CoA contains a thioester bond, a relatively high-energy linkage. Hydrolysis of that bond provides enough free energy to support formation of GTP during conversion to succinate. The other choices do not explain direct energetic coupling at this step.
  • A: A carboxyl group alone does not explain GTP formation here.
  • B: The issue is bond energy and coupling, not whether succinate is more reduced.
  • C: Location does not make this a substrate-level phosphorylation reaction.
Question 3
A mutation eliminates the enzyme that oxidizes succinate. Which pair of outcomes would most directly follow during continued cycling?
Explanation
Succinate dehydrogenase catalyzes oxidation of succinate to fumarate and is the only citric acid cycle enzyme that reduces FAD to FADH2. If that enzyme is lost, succinate accumulates and FADH2 generation falls. The other options describe upstream or chemically incorrect events.
  • B: NADH production would not increase; a later block usually lowers overall flux.
  • C: The second CO2 is released earlier at α-ketoglutarate dehydrogenase.
  • D: Malate forms only after fumarate is hydrated by fumarase.
Question 4
A student claims that oxaloacetate is “used up” every time acetyl-CoA enters the cycle. Which observation from the pathway best refutes that claim?
Explanation
The best refutation is that oxaloacetate is regenerated from malate at the end of the cycle, allowing it to act catalytically in repeated turns. Although citrate’s carbon count shows acetyl entry, regeneration is the specific observation that disproves the claim that oxaloacetate is consumed overall.
  • A: This supports condensation with acetyl-CoA but does not by itself prove regeneration.
  • B: CO2 release occurs, but that does not directly identify which intermediate is regenerated.
  • D: FADH2 production is unrelated to whether oxaloacetate is consumed overall.
Question 5
If fumarase is inhibited while earlier enzymes continue to function briefly, which intermediate would be expected to accumulate first relative to downstream products?
Explanation
Fumarase hydrates fumarate to malate. Blocking it causes fumarate to accumulate first while malate and oxaloacetate fall downstream. Citrate is much earlier in the cycle and is not the immediate substrate of the inhibited step.
  • A: Malate is the product of fumarase, so it would decrease rather than accumulate first.
  • C: Oxaloacetate lies even farther downstream.
  • D: Citrate is upstream and not the immediate substrate at this block.
Question 6
During a high-energy state, mitochondrial ATP and NADH both rise. Based on the passage, which enzyme is most likely to be suppressed at a major regulatory point, thereby slowing carbon flux through the cycle?
Explanation
Isocitrate dehydrogenase is the rate-limiting step of the citric acid cycle and is inhibited by ATP and NADH. Elevated high-energy signals therefore suppress this enzyme and reduce cycle flux. The other listed enzymes are not presented as the principal regulatory point in the passage.
  • A: Fumarase is not described as the major regulated step.
  • B: Malate dehydrogenase generates NADH but is not the main control point emphasized here.
  • C: Succinyl-CoA synthetase performs substrate-level phosphorylation, not the main regulation.
Question 7
A labeled acetyl group enters the cycle. Before the labeled carbons can contribute directly to formation of oxaloacetate in a later turn, which sequence of events must occur in the current turn?
Explanation
The acetyl carbons first enter by condensing with oxaloacetate to form citrate. The cycle then proceeds through rearrangement, two oxidative decarboxylations, substrate-level phosphorylation, FADH2 formation, hydration, and final oxidation to regenerate oxaloacetate. That ordered progression best matches the pathway shown.
  • B: Acetyl-CoA is not oxidized independently before citrate forms.
  • C: NADH and FADH2 are generated after citrate formation, not before it.
  • D: Fumarate appears much later and is hydrated, not hydrolyzed, after succinate oxidation.
Question 8
A toxin prevents cells from entering S phase but does not affect growth signals during G1. Which pattern would most likely appear on a DNA-quantity-versus-time graph?
Explanation
If cells cannot enter S phase, they cannot replicate DNA, so the graph would remain at the lower G1-level DNA content rather than rise to the doubled G2 level. Mitosis separates duplicated chromosomes but does not create the doubling, and cytokinesis should not drop DNA below the starting per-cell amount.
  • A: Per-cell DNA should not drop below the normal G1 amount.
  • B: Replication occurs in S phase, not mitosis.
  • D: Without replication, the higher plateau would not be reached repeatedly.
Question 9
A student compares Diagram A with Diagram B and concludes that the increase in DNA quantity must correspond to S phase rather than G2. Which reasoning best supports that conclusion?
Explanation
Diagram B shows DNA quantity rising during S phase and then remaining high during G2. That means replication occurs during S phase, whereas G2 is a post-replication interval with elevated but stable DNA content. The other choices either are irrelevant or overgeneralized.
  • A: Phase duration is not the key evidence here.
  • C: Microscopic visibility does not determine when DNA synthesis occurs.
  • D: Gap phases are distinct intervals and need not imply identical cellular circumstances.
Question 10
A drug allows DNA replication to finish normally but prevents sister chromatids from separating during mitosis. Which statement best describes the likely consequence?
Explanation
Replication occurs before mitosis, so DNA content can still rise normally through S phase and remain high in G2. If chromatid separation fails during mitosis, the main defect is partitioning of already duplicated chromosomes into daughter cells, not the earlier replication itself.
  • A: The G2 plateau reflects completed replication, which the drug does not block.
  • B: The drug acts after replication, not at the G1-to-S transition.
  • C: Blocking chromatid separation does not erase S phase itself.
Question 11
If a population of rapidly dividing cells shows an unusually large fraction of cells with intermediate DNA content between the G1 and G2 levels, which interpretation is most consistent with Diagram B?
Explanation
Intermediate DNA content indicates that cells are between the low G1 plateau and the high G2 plateau, which is exactly the pattern expected during S phase as DNA is being synthesized. G1 and G2 correspond to stable plateaus, not intermediate values.
  • B: Post-cytokinesis cells should be back at the lower G1 DNA amount.
  • C: G2 cells have already reached the higher plateau.
  • D: Replication must precede mitosis in the normal cycle.
Question 12
A mutation shortens G1 but leaves the molecular events of S, G2, and mitosis unchanged. Which result would be most reasonable?
Explanation
If G1 is shortened, cells would reach S phase sooner, so the graph would begin its upward rise earlier. However, DNA synthesis would still be confined to S phase; the amount of DNA at the G2 plateau would remain the doubled value. The phase order would not be rearranged.
  • A: Shortening G1 does not relocate DNA synthesis into mitosis.
  • B: The doubled G2 plateau should remain doubled, not fall to the G1 amount.
  • D: Cytokinesis follows mitosis rather than preceding chromosome separation.
Question 13
A researcher measures DNA per cell immediately after cytokinesis. Based on Diagram B, which value is most likely for each daughter cell?
Explanation
After mitosis and cytokinesis, each daughter cell receives one full complement of DNA and returns to the lower per-cell DNA content characteristic of G1. The doubled value belongs to the pre-division cell after replication, not to each daughter after division.
  • A: The cell has already partitioned duplicated DNA into two daughter cells.
  • C: Intermediate values indicate active replication, not completed division.
  • D: Daughter cells retain DNA immediately after cytokinesis.
Question 14
Which comparison best distinguishes the roles of S phase and mitosis using both diagrams together?
Explanation
Taken together, the diagrams show that S phase is when DNA quantity rises because replication occurs, while mitosis is the later process that separates already duplicated chromosomes so they can be partitioned into daughter cells. That distinction is the central inference supported by both representations.
  • B: This reverses the actual pattern.
  • C: Growth can occur in gap phases, whereas DNA replication occurs in S phase.
  • D: DNA quantity is not constant through S phase; it increases there.
