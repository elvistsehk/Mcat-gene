Endocrine Integration: Adrenal Medulla, Pancreatic Islets, and Hepatic Glucose Output
Timer20:00
Passage 1 (Questions 1–6)

An endocrine physiology laboratory studied how acute stress and fasting interact to maintain blood glucose. The attending reminded students that the adrenal medulla is a modified neural tissue that releases the catecholamines epinephrine and norepinephrine directly into the bloodstream. During acute sympathetic stress, catecholamines rapidly increase hepatic glycogenolysis and help mobilize glucose. By contrast, cortisol from the adrenal cortex acts more slowly because it changes gene expression; its major contribution in this setting is to support gluconeogenesis and to exert a permissive effect that allows catecholamines and glucagon to produce a stronger hyperglycemic response.

The same session reviewed the endocrine pancreas. Pancreatic α cells secrete glucagon, which promotes hepatic glucose output, especially during fasting. β cells secrete insulin, which favors glucose uptake and storage and therefore opposes the rise in blood glucose. δ cells secrete somatostatin, which suppresses the release of both insulin and glucagon. The faculty emphasized a common testing trap: epinephrine can acutely raise plasma glucose even when insulin also rises modestly, because catecholamine-driven glycogenolysis may briefly outpace insulin-mediated glucose disposal. However, when liver glycogen becomes depleted during a longer fast, maintenance of glucose depends increasingly on gluconeogenesis rather than glycogenolysis.

To test these ideas, researchers studied six groups of overnight-fasted volunteers during a standardized acute stress task. Some groups received hormone infusions or receptor blockers shortly before the task. Hepatic glycogenolysis and gluconeogenesis were estimated from isotope-tracing studies during the first 20 minutes after stress onset. The team told students to pay attention not only to absolute glucose values but also to which hormone pattern best explains each metabolic shift.

The researchers pointed out several interpretive clues. First, the rise in norepinephrine during stress can reflect increased sympathetic nerve activity from peripheral nerve terminals, whereas a large circulating epinephrine signal more directly reflects adrenal medulla output. Thus, selective adrenal medulla suppression should reduce epinephrine much more than norepinephrine. Second, a high-glucose state caused primarily by glycogenolysis should appear early and is most convincing when epinephrine is elevated. Third, a shift toward gluconeogenesis is more compatible with cortisol pretreatment or a glucagon-dominant fasting state, especially when glycogenolysis is limited. Finally, because somatostatin suppresses both α- and β-cell secretion, a subject receiving somatostatin can show simultaneously low glucagon and low insulin; the net glucose result then depends on whether catecholamine-driven hepatic output can compensate for loss of pancreatic support.

At the end of the session, students were asked to distinguish short-latency catecholamine effects from slower cortisol effects, to identify which pancreatic islet cell type best explained a hormonal pattern, and to determine whether a glucose rise was more likely driven by glycogen breakdown or new glucose synthesis. The investigators cautioned that no single hormone should be interpreted in isolation; the strongest conclusions require combining the passage’s physiologic logic with the comparative patterns in the table.

Track time scale first. A rapid early glucose rise points more toward catecholamine-driven glycogenolysis, whereas a stronger gluconeogenic pattern fits slower cortisol or fasting-related gene regulation.

  • Match hormone source to pattern. A large circulating epinephrine signal points to the adrenal medulla; suppression of both glucagon and insulin points to δ-cell somatostatin effects.
  • Use the table comparatively. Do not read one row alone. Compare glycogenolysis, gluconeogenesis, and glucose change across groups before deciding which hormone is dominating.
  • Separate pancreatic cell types. α cells release glucagon, β cells release insulin, and δ cells release somatostatin.
  • Think mechanism, not vocabulary. The best answer usually comes from linking the hormonal pattern to the metabolic readout rather than recalling one fact in isolation.
