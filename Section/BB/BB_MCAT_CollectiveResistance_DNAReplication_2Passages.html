1. In Figure 1a, CmS counts drop markedly with chloramphenicol when CmS is alone, but not when CmS is co-colonized with CmR. Which inference most directly supports collective resistance?
Explanation

Correct (A): CmS survives antibiotic exposure only when CmR is present, consistent with CmR altering the local environment (e.g., lowering community-level drug stress).

Why the other choices are not correct:

  • B: Figure 1b still shows a mixed population; the key evidence is antibiotic-specific rescue in the presence of CmR, not complete conversion.
  • C: The manipulated variable is antibiotic dosing; nutrient changes are not required to explain the pattern.
  • D: Dormancy would reduce CFUs and would not explain rescue that depends on CmR.
2. Figure 1b shows that under antibiotic dosing, the fraction of CmR colonies increases in mixed populations. Which explanation best fits both Figure 1a and 1b?
Explanation

Correct (B): Antibiotics should favor resistant cells, raising CmR frequency. Figure 1a simultaneously indicates that CmS can persist in the mixed group, consistent with partial protection.

Why the other choices are not correct:

  • A: If CmR were selectively killed, its fraction would decrease.
  • C: Replication/competition still occur during colonization; antibiotics do not randomize frequencies.
  • D: Converting CmR into CmS would reduce the resistant fraction, contradicting Figure 1b.
3. A reviewer argues the rescue of CmS could be explained if CmS frequently evolved resistance during treatment. Which additional observation would most strongly refute that alternative?
Explanation

Correct (C): If recovered CmS remains susceptible in a new assay, it did not simply acquire stable genetic resistance during treatment; survival is better explained by community-level protection.

Why the other choices are not correct:

  • A: CmR presence does not exclude CmS evolution.
  • B: Variability does not test the evolutionary alternative.
  • D: Growth-rate differences do not rule out evolution of resistance.
4. In Figure 2’s model, antibiotic stress is high. Which outcome is most consistent if CmR growth efficiency is very low (large fitness cost) under that high stress?
Explanation

Correct (D): Under high stress, CmS is inhibited. If CmR also grows too poorly (high cost), the model predicts failure for both strains.

Why the other choices are not correct:

  • A: Equal coexistence requires parameters in a coexistence region, not extreme low CmR efficiency.
  • B: High antibiotic stress strongly suppresses CmS.
  • C: Resistance helps, but a severe cost can prevent persistence.
5. Suppose the resistant enzyme acts strictly inside CmR cells and does not diffuse. Under that assumption, which prediction best matches collective resistance?
Explanation

Correct (A): Many CmR cells can act as a sink for drug molecules; abundance matters for how much the environment’s effective stress is reduced.

Why the other choices are not correct:

  • B: If CmR is too rare, it cannot meaningfully alter effective stress.
  • C: Without CmR, CmS lacks protection and should be suppressed by antibiotics.
  • D: Baseline growth does not help if the drug strongly inhibits CmS.
6. You repeat the mixed experiment but start with a much lower initial fraction of CmR. Which outcome is most consistent with Figures 1 and 2?
Explanation

Correct (B): Collective protection depends on having enough resistant cells. If CmR is too rare, protection weakens and CmS is suppressed by antibiotics.

Why the other choices are not correct:

  • A: Reduced competition does not matter if CmS is inhibited by antibiotic stress.
  • C: Selection under antibiotics tends to favor CmR, not necessarily reduce it.
  • D: Both model logic and the concept of collective resistance imply frequency can matter.
7. A student concludes: “Because CmR survives antibiotics, it should always increase its growth efficiency (approach 1.0) to maximize fitness.” Using Figure 2’s logic, what is the best critique?
Explanation

Correct (C): The model suggests outcomes depend on both antibiotic stress and CmR efficiency. Increasing efficiency can move the system out of a coexistence region, changing community composition.

Why the other choices are not correct:

  • A: Bacteria can evolve growth traits; the key critique is ecological outcome, not mutational possibility.
  • B: Higher efficiency usually helps CmR; the point is that it can disrupt coexistence.
  • D: The figures imply coexistence can occur in some parameter regions.
8. A bacterial strain has normal proofreading by DNA polymerase but is deficient in mismatch repair. Which result is most likely after several generations?
Explanation

Correct (D): Proofreading is high fidelity but not perfect. Without mismatch repair, errors that slip through are more likely to become fixed mutations over generations.

Why the other choices are not correct:

  • A: Some mismatches escape proofreading.
  • B: UV dimers are mainly handled by nucleotide excision repair.
  • C: Mismatch repair targets mismatches, not chromosome ends specifically.
9. The passage notes bacteria can identify the newly synthesized strand by methylation state. Which prediction best follows if strand marking fails?
Explanation

Correct (A): Mismatch repair depends on distinguishing new from old strand; loss of the marker increases the chance repair is applied to the wrong strand, increasing mutation fixation.

Why the other choices are not correct:

  • B: Proofreading is intrinsic to polymerase and does not require methylation cues.
  • C: NER is triggered by bulky lesions, not methylation patterns.
  • D: BER glycosylases can remove bases without methylation being required.
10. Cells are exposed to UV light, generating bulky helix-distorting lesions. Which pathway most directly removes these lesions via cut-and-patch?
Explanation

Correct (B): Bulky helix-distorting UV lesions are classic substrates for nucleotide excision repair.

Why the other choices are not correct:

  • A: Mismatch repair targets replication mismatches.
  • C: BER targets small non-bulky base lesions.
  • D: NHEJ repairs double-strand breaks.
11. A cytosine deaminates to uracil, creating a small, non-helix-distorting lesion. Which sequence best matches the correct repair logic?
Explanation

Correct (C): Base excision repair begins with glycosylase-mediated base removal, then AP site processing, fill-in synthesis, and ligation.

Why the other choices are not correct:

  • A: That would be highly mutagenic and ignores templated synthesis.
  • B: Direct photoreversal is not the typical mammalian repair route.
  • D: Mismatch repair is not the primary pathway for deaminated bases; methylation cues are bacterial, not a universal human marker.
12. A tumor suppressor normally pauses the cell cycle when DNA damage is detected. If that suppressor is lost while repair pathways are weakened, which outcome is most plausible?
Explanation

Correct (D): Loss of checkpoint control lets cells replicate damaged templates; combined with weaker repair, mutations accumulate and transformation risk rises.

Why the other choices are not correct:

  • A: Faster division propagates damage; it does not reduce mutations.
  • B: Apoptosis is not guaranteed; loss of suppressors often removes safeguards.
  • C: Removing checkpoints does not reduce replication; it reduces control over damage.
