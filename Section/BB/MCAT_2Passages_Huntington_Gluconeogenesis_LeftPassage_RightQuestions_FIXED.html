Question 1. Based on Figure 1C, which conclusion about TP-10 is best supported?
Explanation
The best answer is C. At the same early-to-middle ages, the TP-10 curve is consistently below the vehicle curve, meaning treated mice show less clasping and therefore less severe neurologic impairment at those time points. But by the latest week, both groups approach similarly high values, so the graph supports a delay in progression, not complete prevention.

A is too strong because clasping still develops. B misreads the graph; lower clasping is better, not worse. D is unsupported because behavioral improvement does not imply genotype reversal.
Question 2. If clasping time is used as the dependent variable, the vehicle-treated R6/2 mice primarily serve which role in the experiment?
Explanation
The best answer is A. The vehicle-treated R6/2 mice have the same disease model but do not receive the active TP-10 effect, so they show how the disorder progresses under matched conditions without treatment. That makes them the key disease-matched control.

B is wrong because they do not demonstrate drug action. C would describe a wild-type group, which is not what is plotted in Figure 1C. D is irrelevant because this is a biological comparison, not a pipetting-control setup.
Question 3. Suppose a new drug produced a graph in which treated and vehicle R6/2 mice had identical clasping values from weeks 8 through 15. Which inference would be most reasonable?
Explanation
The best answer is D. If the two curves are identical across the entire study window, then the treatment has not changed the measured outcome, which here is clasping behavior.

A brings in a different variable not shown in the graph. B is false because the passage explicitly treats the disease as progressive. C reverses the logic; identical curves would indicate less efficacy, not more.
Question 4. At week 10, the vehicle group shows much more clasping than the TP-10 group. That difference most directly indicates that TP-10-treated mice at week 10 have:
Explanation
The best answer is B. The passage defines clasping as a sign of neurologic impairment, so lower clasping time at the same age means the treated animals are exhibiting less severe motor-system degeneration at that point in disease progression.

A is unsupported because the genotype is not changing. C compares to wild-type weight, which is not shown. D is too absolute; behavior improving does not prove fully normal histology.
Question 5. Which additional observation would most strengthen the claim that TP-10 alleviates neurologic deficits rather than merely sedating the mice?
Explanation
The best answer is B. An independent improvement in another motor or neurologic test would strengthen the interpretation that the drug is alleviating disease-related deficits rather than simply changing one behavior nonspecifically.

A, C, and D do not directly test whether the neurologic benefit is genuine.
Question 6. Which statement is most consistent with Figure 1C alone?
Explanation
The best answer is D. The TP-10 curve rises later, so the appearance of substantial clasping is delayed relative to vehicle-treated R6/2 mice.

A is false because controls remain necessary. B is false because the passage identifies clasping as a disease marker. C cannot be concluded because wild-type mice are not shown here.
Question 7. If a student concludes that TP-10 cures Huntington disease in the mouse model, the best critique is that the graph shows:
Explanation
The best answer is B. The graph supports a reduction or delay in one disease-related behavioral outcome, but the symptom still appears and eventually becomes severe. That supports partial amelioration, not cure.

A invents missing information. C is too narrow and unsupported. D is plainly contradicted by the high clasping values in vehicle-treated mice.
Question 8. During prolonged fasting, which combination would most strongly favor hepatic gluconeogenesis?
Explanation
The best answer is A. Glucagon promotes gluconeogenesis, β-oxidation generates acetyl-CoA that activates pyruvate carboxylase, and low fructose 2,6-bisphosphate relieves inhibition of the pathway at fructose-1,6-bisphosphatase.

B, C, and D all describe conditions that favor glycolysis or the fed state rather than gluconeogenesis.
Question 9. A researcher inhibits fructose-1,6-bisphosphatase in hepatocytes. Which immediate effect is most likely?
Explanation
The best answer is C. Fructose-1,6-bisphosphatase directly catalyzes the conversion of fructose 1,6-bisphosphate → fructose 6-phosphate. Inhibiting it would therefore block that exact step.

A concerns the terminal glucose-6-phosphatase step. B is unrelated to this enzyme. D confuses two separate bypass enzymes.
Question 10. Why does acetyl-CoA produced by fatty acid β-oxidation promote gluconeogenesis during fasting?
Explanation
The best answer is D. The passage states that acetyl-CoA activates pyruvate carboxylase, the enzyme that converts pyruvate to oxaloacetate. This is a key way that fasting fat oxidation drives hepatic gluconeogenesis.

A describes PEPCK, not acetyl-CoA. B is mechanistically wrong. C is not the role of acetyl-CoA here.
Question 11. A muscle biopsy contains abundant glucose 6-phosphate but cannot release much free glucose into the blood. Based on the passage, the best explanation is that muscle lacks sufficient:
Explanation
The best answer is A. The passage states that glucose-6-phosphatase is found in the endoplasmic reticulum of the liver and is required to convert glucose 6-phosphate into free glucose that can enter the bloodstream. Skeletal muscle does not efficiently perform this final release step.

B, C, and D are upstream enzymes and do not directly explain failure to release free glucose from existing glucose 6-phosphate.
Question 12. Which enzyme pair together bypasses the irreversible pyruvate kinase step of glycolysis?
Explanation
The best answer is C. Pyruvate is first carboxylated to oxaloacetate by pyruvate carboxylase and then converted to phosphoenolpyruvate by PEPCK. Together these steps bypass the irreversible pyruvate kinase reaction of glycolysis.

A concerns the glucose/glucose 6-phosphate step, B combines enzymes from different bypasses, and D lists TCA-cycle enzymes.
Question 13. Which experimental change would be expected to suppress the rate-limiting step of gluconeogenesis most directly?
Explanation
The best answer is D. The rate-limiting enzyme is fructose-1,6-bisphosphatase, and the passage states that AMP directly inhibits it. Increasing AMP is therefore the most direct way to suppress the rate-limiting step.

A and B would promote the pathway, while C activates pyruvate carboxylase upstream rather than directly inhibiting the rate-limiting step.
Question 14. A hepatocyte is exposed to insulin immediately after a high-carbohydrate meal. Which change is most likely to occur?
Explanation
The best answer is B. In the fed state, insulin promotes an increase in fructose 2,6-bisphosphate, which indirectly inhibits gluconeogenesis by opposing fructose-1,6-bisphosphatase and favoring glycolytic flux instead.

A would raise blood glucose, which is opposite to the fed-state goal. C and D are fasting-associated responses, not immediate insulin effects.
