Passage 1: Neurulation and Teratogenic Risk

A 27-year-old pregnant patient presents for prenatal counseling after learning a close relative had a child with a neural tube defect. The patient asks when the nervous system begins forming and what exposures might increase risk early in pregnancy.

The clinician explains that early nervous system development begins when ectoderm overlying the notochord forms a neural groove and neural folds. Cells at the leading edge of the folds migrate widely and later contribute to multiple tissues, while the closing tube itself becomes the central nervous system.

The clinician emphasizes that certain medications and maternal exposures can disrupt early development. The patient reports taking an antiepileptic medication before knowing she was pregnant and is worried because she read about spina bifida. They discuss that failure of neural tube closure can produce different malformations depending on location and severity.

How to reason:

  1. Time window: neurulation occurs around 3–4 weeks, so early exposures matter.
  2. Structure mapping: neural tube → CNS; neural crest → migratory derivatives (e.g., DRG, melanocytes).
  3. Mechanism for spina bifida: incomplete neural tube closure during neurulation.
  4. Choose the answer that links the exposure to the specific developmental failure described.

Passage 2: Primitive Reflexes and Neurologic Assessment

During a 9-month well-child visit, a physician checks for primitive reflexes as part of neurologic screening. The infant startles when the head position changes abruptly, extending the arms and then slowly withdrawing them with crying.

The parents report that this response has been present since birth and seems unchanged. On exam, the physician also tests foot sole stimulation and observes whether the big toe extends with fanning of the other toes.

The physician explains that many primitive reflexes are expected to disappear at predictable times, and persistence beyond typical ranges can indicate developmental concerns or neuromuscular problems.

How to reason:

  1. Identify the reflex pattern: arm extension then withdrawal/crying → Moro reflex.
  2. Use timing: Moro typically disappears by about 4 months; persistence near 1 year is concerning.
  3. Toe fanning with sole stimulation is Babinski reflex, which is primitive in infants.
  4. Select the option that uses both the reflex identity and the expected developmental timeline.

Passage 3: Motor and Social Milestones

A daycare program compares development among toddlers from varied home environments. Many children begin by mastering head control, then sitting and trunk stability, and only later show confident walking and running.

Staff observe that some children in more educationally enriched settings reach milestones slightly earlier, but the overall sequence—posture control before coordinated gait—appears consistent.

Socially, staff note that toddlers first play alone, then watch other children play, and later begin to play side-by-side without coordinating their actions. The director explains that these patterns reflect developmental themes rather than exact dates.

How to reason:

  1. Gross motor theme: development is head-to-toe and core before extremities.
  2. Environment effect: enrichment can change the rate, but usually not the order of milestones.
  3. Social play progression: solitary → onlooker → parallel play.
  4. Pick answers that match the described pattern rather than a memorized exact age when not required.
