Passage 1 (Questions 1–7)

In a quiet gallery, a book rests open beneath glass. Visitors lean in, expecting the “real” work to be the inked lines of a medieval scribe: scripture, law, a saint’s life—something solemn enough to justify a protective case. Yet the first thing many notice is not the central text at all, but the margins: a rabbit jousting on a snail, a monk dozing at his lectern, a face peering from a flourish that looks suspiciously like a doodle. These marginal images have become a minor celebrity of the museum world, reproduced on tote bags and shared online as proof that people in the past were “just like us.”

That familiarity is tempting, but it can also be lazy. The modern viewer tends to treat the margin as a private notebook—an accidental leak of personality that bypasses the official voice of the manuscript. In that story, the scribe is a tired worker who steals moments of mischief from the demands of authority. The problem is that this interpretation assumes what it wants to prove. It presumes that the central text represents the “true” medieval mind and that the margin is a rebellion against it; it treats solemnity as the default and play as the exception.

But medieval manuscripts were not neutral containers of words. They were made for particular communities—monasteries, universities, courts—and they were read in practices that mixed devotion, study, performance, and, occasionally, boredom. Marginalia could certainly be playful, but play itself can be disciplined. In a culture that taught through exemplars, inversion and parody were often pedagogical tools rather than escapes from pedagogy. A grotesque might ridicule vice; an absurd animal might dramatize disorder so that order could be felt more sharply. The margin, in other words, might not be “outside” the text at all; it could be one of the ways the text did its work.

This matters because the objects we place under glass are not only artifacts; they are arguments. When museums highlight the doodle-like margin as the most “authentic” part of the book, they offer a comforting moral: authenticity equals spontaneity, and spontaneity equals truth. Yet a museum cannot display the whole practice of reading a manuscript; it can only select details. Selection is interpretation, and interpretation has consequences for what we think the past can teach.

Restorers face a parallel dilemma. Consider a manuscript whose margins were trimmed in the nineteenth century to fit a fashionable binding. Modern conservation can sometimes reveal faint traces of the lost drawings at the cut edge, or even recover fragments from the binding itself. The temptation is to “restore” the margin, to return the book to a presumed original wholeness. But restoration is never a simple reversal. To rebuild the missing image is to decide what it was. Even a cautious reconstruction—say, a pale digital overlay—invites viewers to treat the overlay as fact and the uncertainty as a footnote.

The more honest choice may be to preserve the damage as evidence of history, not as a defect to be hidden. A trimmed margin tells a story about later tastes, markets, and the shifting value of books. At the same time, leaving the margin blank risks making the medieval craft invisible, as if the loss were the book’s natural state. Conservation, like curation, must balance competing duties: to stabilize, to explain, and to resist the seductive fiction that the past can be made present without interpretation.
