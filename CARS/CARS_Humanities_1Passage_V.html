Passage 1 (Questions 1–7)

When museums began to digitize their collections, early press releases often promised a simple salvation: if the public could only “access” art more easily, then art would become more democratic. The idea contained a quiet assumption—namely, that the primary barrier to appreciation is physical distance or scarcity. Yet anyone who has wandered through an online archive at 1 a.m., opening and closing images like browser tabs in a mild trance, suspects another barrier has been built in the act of removing the old one.

The museum gallery imposes a peculiar discipline. Paintings occupy walls at a fixed scale; sculptures keep their stubborn weight; and the visitor’s body is compelled to move, pause, approach, and retreat. In this choreography, looking is not merely a mental operation but a sequence of small negotiations: Can I stand close without blocking someone? Do I have the patience to read the label? Should I return later when the room is quieter? These constraints can be inconvenient, but they also serve as a kind of frame—an architecture of attention that tells the visitor, implicitly, that this object has earned time.

Online, the frame dissolves and reappears as interface. The artwork is flattened into a rectangle that sits beside other rectangles: advertising, messages, a half-finished document. The viewer’s body is no longer choreographed by rooms and corridors but by scroll bars, notifications, and battery life. It is tempting to describe this simply as distraction, as if the digital viewer merely lacks willpower. But the problem is subtler. In a gallery, scarcity of space forces curators to select; in an online archive, abundance invites the viewer to select—and to keep selecting. The result is that the viewer’s experience often becomes less like contemplation and more like curation of one’s own rapid sequence of impressions.

Advocates of digitization reply, correctly, that physical galleries are not neutral frames either. A museum’s architecture can intimidate, its wall texts can smuggle in a story of cultural superiority, and its admission price can function as a gate. Digitization can lower some of these gates. Yet lowering a gate does not guarantee that what lies beyond it will be inhabited in the same way. If a gallery teaches the visitor how to dwell with an object, a database teaches the visitor how to navigate. Each lesson carries values. Dwelling privileges slowness, tolerates ambiguity, and permits an artwork to “resist” immediate consumption. Navigating privileges breadth, comparison, and the satisfaction of coverage: the pleasurable feeling that one has seen enough to move on.

None of this means that digital access is an artistic disaster. It means only that “more access” is not a synonym for “more democracy,” because democracy is not merely the distribution of goods but the cultivation of capacities. A public that can click on ten thousand paintings but rarely learns to attend to any one of them may be well supplied yet impoverished in attention. If digitization is to fulfill its promise, it must be accompanied by tools and habits that teach a kind of looking suited to abundance—one that can resist the urge to treat art as a checklist and can restore, even on a screen, the sense that an object might deserve time.
