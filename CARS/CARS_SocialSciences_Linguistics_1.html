Passage 1 (Questions 1–8)

When people argue about language, they often treat words as if they were labels glued to reality. On this view, communication succeeds when two speakers attach the same label to the same thing, and it fails when the labels slip. Linguistics begins by suspecting the simplicity of that picture. Much of what we do with language is not naming objects but coordinating expectations: requesting, promising, joking, accusing, consoling. A sentence is less a tag than a move in a social game, and its meaning depends on what the move makes possible next.

This dependence on context is sometimes mistaken for vagueness, as if linguists were saying that words mean whatever anyone wants. In fact, context is structured. Consider pronouns like “she” or “that.” Their reference can shift from moment to moment, yet the shift is not random; it is guided by attention, shared knowledge, and the grammar that signals what kind of thing could be referred to. Even indexicals such as “here” and “now,” which seem maximally flexible, come with rules: “here” picks out a location relative to a speaker, and “now” selects a time relative to an utterance. Flexibility, in other words, is constrained by a system.

The same tension appears in debates about “correct” language. Prescriptive rules—do not split an infinitive, avoid double negatives—often present themselves as guardians of clarity. But many prescriptions are better understood as markers of status. They elevate a particular variety of speech as neutral while treating other varieties as sloppy or illogical. Descriptivists point out that speakers of so-called nonstandard dialects are not speaking without grammar; they are following a grammar that differs from the prestige one. Calling a form 'incorrect' can therefore be less a linguistic diagnosis than a social judgment about who counts as educated.

None of this implies that “anything goes.” Speech communities maintain norms, and those norms have practical value: they help strangers coordinate and institutions function. The question is which norms are being enforced and for what purpose. A courtroom, for example, may demand a standardized register to reduce ambiguity, but it may also punish a witness whose dialect is unfamiliar to jurors. In such cases, the norm is doing double duty—supporting comprehension while also distributing credibility unevenly.

Recent work on pragmatics makes the interplay between language and inference especially visible. Listeners do not usually decode utterances like encrypted messages; they infer intentions by assuming that speakers are broadly cooperative. If someone says, “Some of the reports were submitted,” listeners often infer “not all,” even though the sentence does not logically entail it. Such inferences are efficient, but they can also be exploited. A speaker can appear truthful while guiding an audience toward a conclusion the speaker never explicitly stated. The boundary between meaning and implication, then, is not merely academic; it affects how responsibility and honesty are assigned.

Seen this way, language is not a transparent conduit but a technology for managing social relations. It enables shared action precisely because it blends convention with improvisation: stable enough to be learnable, flexible enough to fit new circumstances. The most fruitful linguistic questions, accordingly, are not whether a sentence is 'right' in the abstract, but how a community uses forms to coordinate, to exclude, and to negotiate what counts as knowledge. Understanding meaning requires looking both at the rules that constrain speech and at the inferences that listeners bring to it.
