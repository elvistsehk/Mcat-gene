Passage 1 (Questions 1–6)

In public debates about “misinformation,” people often talk as if bad ideas spread the way viruses do: an infected post passes to an uninfected mind, and the remedy is to block transmission. The metaphor is convenient, but it hides something social scientists have long emphasized: belief is rarely a simple private possession. It is also a signal. When a person repeats a claim at a dinner table or reposts it to a feed, the act can be less about updating a fact-model and more about declaring membership in a community whose approval matters.

Consider the recurring puzzle that some individuals endorse statements they privately doubt. Survey researchers sometimes find that respondents will express confidence in a rumor but, when incentives reward accuracy, hedge or retreat. This gap is not necessarily hypocrisy; it can be a clue that the public response is performing a social task. If agreement with a group’s favored narrative functions like a badge, then the badge can be worn even when the wearer is uncertain about the narrative’s literal truth. In such settings, correcting the claim may not be experienced as help. It may be experienced as a demand to remove the badge in public.

Platform designers, faced with the urgency of harmful content, often reach for technical fixes: friction before sharing, warning labels, or downranking. These tools can reduce exposure, but they also change what participation feels like. A label can read like a neutral footnote to one user and like a public rebuke to another. A downranked post is not only less visible; it also deprives its author of the small rewards that make group performance enjoyable. As a result, the same intervention that improves information quality in the aggregate may intensify resentment in certain subgroups, especially if members interpret the intervention as imposed by outsiders.

This is why some scholars argue that the central problem is not information scarcity but trust. People rely on cues to decide which institutions deserve deference: a newspaper, a scientist, a neighbor, a local organizer. When trust is low, the cost of admitting error can be unusually high, because error is not merely a mistake; it is a gift to an opposing camp. In that context, a fact check may be evaluated less by its evidence than by its perceived allegiance. The question becomes not “Is this accurate?” but “Who is trying to make me say this?”

None of this implies that accuracy is irrelevant or that platforms should abandon moderation. It implies that informational interventions have social meanings, and those meanings shape compliance. If belief is partly a public signal, then successful strategies must address the incentives attached to signaling: they must make it safer to revise a claim without losing face, and they must offer alternative ways to belong that are not built on shared defiance. In short, improving the informational environment requires attending to the social environment in which information is displayed.
