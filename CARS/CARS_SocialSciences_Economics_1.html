Passage 1 (Questions 1–8)

During a recession, the same storefront can be read two ways. One passerby sees a shuttered café and concludes that demand has collapsed; another notices a new kiosk across the street and concludes that the city is adapting. Both stories can be true, and the tension between them reveals a persistent mistake: treating “the economy” as a single creature with one mood. What actually changes is an arrangement of relationships—wages, credit, expectations, and bargaining power—that can shift unevenly across neighborhoods and industries.

This is why arguments about stimulus and austerity often talk past each other. One side emphasizes aggregate spending: when households cut consumption, a government deficit can keep money circulating until confidence returns. The other side emphasizes credibility: when debt rises, investors may demand higher interest, and uncertainty can discourage private investment. Both positions invoke “confidence,” but they mean different things—confidence as the willingness to spend versus confidence as the belief that institutions will honor debts. Disagreement persists because the same word covers two mechanisms.

Even when people agree on the mechanism, they disagree about measurement. Official unemployment counts those actively seeking work, yet it can miss discouraged workers, underemployment, and precarious gig labor. Inflation statistics can track a representative basket, but households do not buy the same basket. Rent, health care, and childcare dominate some budgets while barely touching others. When a headline says “inflation is easing,” a family whose major expense is still rising may hear not reassurance but accusation, as if their anxiety were irrational. A number meant to summarize becomes a verdict on lived experience.

Economists sometimes respond by building better indices and by disaggregating data. Those improvements matter. But measurement debates also reflect a normative question: which experiences deserve to count as central? When a model treats lost leisure as minor, it quietly assumes that time is cheap for everyone. When it treats unpaid caregiving as invisible, it treats whole forms of labor as non-events. These choices are not mere technicalities; they distribute attention and, in the long run, policy priority.

Markets, too, are shaped by stories. A central banker’s announcement can move prices not because it changes today’s factories but because it changes expectations about tomorrow. A “housing boom” can be fueled by speculation that turns rising prices into evidence that prices will continue to rise. When narratives become self‑fulfilling, critics complain that the market is irrational. Yet narratives are not external to markets; they are part of how participants coordinate in the presence of uncertainty. The problem is not storytelling per se, but whose stories are treated as authoritative.

The healthiest public debates therefore resist two temptations: technocracy that pretends numbers are neutral, and populism that pretends numbers are lies. A statistic is neither an oracle nor a conspiracy. It is a tool that compresses a messy world, and every compression throws something away. The task is to ask, openly, what is being thrown away, who bears the cost, and which alternative measures might better match the goals a society claims to value.

How to reason (CARS)

  • Track the author’s thesis: economic debates hinge on uneven relationships, measurement choices, and narrative authority—not a single 'economy mood.'
  • Note key contrasts: stimulus vs austerity framed through different meanings of confidence; headline stats vs lived budgets.
  • For main idea, choose what spans all examples: tools/indices compress reality and must be examined for what they omit.
  • For within-text questions, tie answers to an example’s job (confidence ambiguity; inflation basket mismatch; norms in indices).
  • For beyond-text questions, apply the logic: ask who bears costs, what assumptions are embedded, and who controls authoritative stories.
  • Eliminate traps: extremes (“numbers are lies” / “numbers are neutral”), scope shifts, and reversals.
