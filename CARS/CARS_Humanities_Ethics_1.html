Passage 1 (Questions 1–8)

Ethics is often introduced as a set of answers: do this, avoid that, respect this rule. But the more unsettling ethical work begins not with answers but with a question about what kind of creature is making them. We act, after all, under descriptions. A promise can be framed as a sacred bond, a strategic move, or an inconvenient constraint; each frame makes different reasons feel compelling. When moral debate collapses into slogan and counter‑slogan, it is often because participants treat their own frame as obvious and their opponents’ frame as either malicious or irrational.

One familiar attempt to avoid framing disputes is to seek a single, universal moral algorithm—maximize happiness, follow the rule you can will as universal law, cultivate the virtues of a good person. These traditions are not interchangeable, yet they share a hope: that if we find the right principle, practical disagreement will shrink. In reality, principles do not apply themselves. They require judgment about what counts as harm, whose happiness is relevant, what a person could will, and which traits are virtues rather than vices. The algorithm fantasy treats moral life as a math problem; ethical experience is closer to interpretation.

Consider the difference between acting with clean hands and acting responsibly. Some agents refuse to participate in institutions they regard as compromised—a company with exploitative supply chains, a political system that tolerates injustice. Withdrawal can preserve personal purity, but it can also abandon the field to those with fewer scruples. Conversely, engagement can reduce harm by improving practices from within, yet it can also normalize wrongdoing by making it seem manageable. The point is not that one posture is always superior, but that both involve tradeoffs between complicity, effectiveness, and moral self‑understanding.

These tradeoffs become vivid in cases of 'remote responsibility,' where actions are mediated by systems. A consumer buys an inexpensive device and later learns that its components were produced under dangerous conditions in a distant factory. The consumer did not order harm; the harm was upstream. Still, the consumer’s demand helped make the system profitable. Some argue that responsibility tracks intention; others that it tracks foreseeable participation. But even foreseeability is graded: we cannot know everything, yet we can choose whether to remain curious or comfortably ignorant.

Because knowledge is uneven, ethical agency depends on practices of attention. Transparency reports, investigative journalism, and whistleblowing do not merely provide facts; they reshape what counts as a reasonable excuse. Once an industry’s harms are public, continued indifference looks less like innocence and more like a choice. At the same time, attention has limits, and moral life can be paralyzed by demands to track every causal thread. A mature ethics must therefore combine vigilance with a sense of proportionality—deciding which obligations are genuine and which are rhetorical traps designed to exhaust concern.

If this sounds less tidy than textbook theories, that is the point. Ethics is not only about deriving conclusions; it is about cultivating capacities—to notice hidden dependencies, to articulate the frame one is using, to revise that frame when evidence or argument warrants it. A society that wants ethical citizens should not train them merely to recite principles, but to practice this reflective skill: explaining why a reason counts here, and how it might count differently for someone situated elsewhere. Moral disagreement will not vanish, but it can become more honest.

How to reason (CARS)

  • Identify the author’s thesis: ethics is not a simple rulebook or moral algorithm, but an interpretive practice.
  • Track key contrasts: principles vs judgment, clean hands vs responsible engagement, intention vs foreseeable participation.
  • For main idea, pick what spans the whole passage (framing + attention + proportionality), not one example (supply chains).
  • For within-text questions, tie answers to an example’s function (device = mediated responsibility; transparency = excuses).
  • For beyond-text questions, extend the author’s logic about tradeoffs and graded responsibility—avoid extremes.
  • Eliminate traps: always/never claims, scope shifts, and reversals of the author’s balanced stance.
