Passage 1 (Questions 1–6)

Music criticism often begins with the temptation to translate sound into something more stable: a story, a message, an identity. We ask what a song is “about,” as if meaning were a parcel carried by melody from composer to listener. Yet music is unusually resistant to that model. A chord progression can suggest arrival without specifying where one has arrived; a rhythm can insist without telling us what is being insisted upon. If music communicates, it does so through patterns that invite attention and expectation rather than through propositions that can be neatly paraphrased.

This resistance has led some writers to declare musical meaning ineffable, a private sensation beyond argument. But ineffable is often a shortcut for unexamined. Listeners do not encounter music as raw sound; they hear it through habits shaped by training, genre, and social setting. A jazz standard performed in a small club asks for a different kind of listening than the same tune piped through a shopping mall. In one context, the solo’s deviations may be heard as risk and wit; in another, as mere background. The meaning is not locked inside the notes, yet it is not arbitrary either: it depends on shared conventions about what counts as foreground, surprise, and skill.

Consider the familiar dispute between formalists and contextualists. Formalists argue that music’s significance lies in its internal relations—motivic development, harmonic tension, thematic transformation. Contextualists respond that these relations become significant only within histories of patronage, technology, and politics. The dispute can harden into a false choice. A listener can be moved by a modulation because of its sonic logic and because the modulation carries the scent of a tradition that the listener has learned to value. Form and context are not rival explanations; they are layers that interact, each shaping what the other can be.

This interaction is easiest to see when music travels. A folk melody recorded for an archive may later be sampled in a dance track, or arranged for a concert hall. The notes may remain recognizable, but the roles they play shift. In the archive, the melody stands as evidence of a community’s continuity; in the dance track, it may function as an exotic texture; in the concert hall, as a token of authenticity. None of these uses is purely musical or purely social. They rely on both sound and the narratives listeners attach to sound, narratives that can confer dignity or reduce a tradition to decoration.

Digital platforms have intensified these transformations by sorting music into categories that feel both personal and impersonal. A recommendation system learns that a listener prefers “focus” playlists and supplies tracks engineered to be unobtrusive. The listener may experience this as customization, yet the very categories encourage certain functions of music: to regulate mood, to sustain productivity, to fill silence. When music is treated primarily as a utility, surprise can become a defect. But when surprise is always demanded, familiarity can be dismissed as laziness. The platform’s design thus quietly presses listeners toward a theory of what music is for.

A more careful criticism would take these pressures as part of the object under study. Instead of asking only what a piece expresses, it would ask what kinds of listening it invites and what institutions reward those kinds of listening. This does not mean reducing music to sociology; it means refusing to pretend that sound arrives without framing. The richest musical experiences may be those in which form and context check each other: patterns in sound generate expectations, and the world around the sound determines which expectations matter. Meaning, then, is not a message hidden in the score, but a relationship negotiated in time.

How to reason (CARS)

  • Find the author’s thesis: music’s meaning is not a paraphrasable message nor pure ineffability.
  • Track the key contrast: form (patterns/expectations) and context (institutions/genres/settings) as interacting layers.
  • For main idea, choose what spans the whole passage (meaning as a relationship), not one example (platforms, travel).

  • Eliminate traps: extremes, reversals, scope shifts, and “only” / “always” language.
