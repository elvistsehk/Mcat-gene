Passage 1 (Questions 1–6)

Literature is often praised as a mirror, a device that reflects society back to itself. The metaphor is comforting: it suggests that novels and poems merely display what is already there, and that the critic’s job is to identify the reflected object—class struggle, gender norms, colonial violence—as if pointing to a shape behind glass. But mirrors do not simply copy. They select an angle, flatten depth, and sometimes distort with intention. A novel does not reproduce a world; it organizes attention. The question, then, is less “What does this book represent?” than “What does this book train its reader to notice and ignore?”

Consider the phenomenon of the 'unreliable narrator,' a term that has migrated from specialist criticism into everyday conversation. Readers now diagnose unreliability in political speeches and social media posts, as if the concept were a portable filter for truth. Yet in literature, unreliability is not merely a trick for surprising an audience; it is a method for distributing responsibility. When a narrator misremembers, rationalizes, or omits, the reader is invited to become a co‑author of meaning, weighing evidence and resisting seduction. The story’s ethical pressure shifts from the narrator’s claims to the reader’s willingness to interpret.

This readerly labor becomes especially visible in novels that fictionalize historical trauma. Some writers choose a documentary tone, piling detail upon detail to honor what happened. Others use fragmentation, refusing smooth chronology on the grounds that trauma resists tidy narrative. Both strategies have defenders and critics. The documentary voice can feel authoritative, but it can also imply that suffering is fully knowable to an outsider. Fragmentation can resist voyeurism, but it can also frustrate readers into mistaking their confusion for profundity. The disagreement is not only aesthetic; it is about what forms of knowledge a text grants and what kinds of humility it demands.

Literary history contains episodes that expose these stakes outside the page. When a once‑ignored novelist is 'rediscovered'—through a new biography, a film adaptation, or the endorsement of a celebrity book club—the author’s work is often repackaged as if it were always destined for greatness. The market calls this correction. But rediscovery also rewrites the past: it selects which voices count as representative and which remain footnotes. The canon, in other words, is not a list of the best books; it is an argument about what a culture chooses to remember.

Translation sharpens the point. A translator does not carry a text intact across languages the way a courier carries a parcel. Choices about rhythm, register, and idiom determine what kinds of readers the work will find and what emotional tones will be available. When a publisher markets a translated novel as 'universal,' it may be inviting empathy. It may also be smoothing away local textures that would demand effort. Universality can therefore be a compliment or a conquest, depending on whether it expands a reader’s patience for difference or flatters the reader’s sense that nothing truly foreign exists.

Seen together, these phenomena suggest that literature is less a mirror than a workshop. It manufactures habits of interpretation: suspicion, tenderness, impatience, credulity. That is why debates about style and canon feel so heated. People are not only defending stories they like; they are defending the kind of reader the story makes them. Criticism at its best names these effects without pretending that there is one correct way to read. It asks what costs a reading imposes, what pleasures it authorizes, and what responsibilities it shifts onto those who turn the pages.
