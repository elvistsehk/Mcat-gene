Passage 1 (Questions 1–8)

A student once complained that argument is a way of avoiding life: you can keep talking about justice and never have to be just. The complaint carries a sting because it is partly right. Words can become a shelter from commitment. Yet the opposite mistake is equally common: to treat commitment as a substitute for thinking, as if conviction were evidence. Between these errors lies a harder practice—using reasons to test what we are already inclined to believe, and then letting the test change how we live.

This is why skepticism periodically returns as a moral discipline rather than a mere intellectual pose. The skeptic does not deny that anything is true; she doubts whether her certainty is earned. In ordinary life, we treat perception and memory as trustworthy until we have a reason not to. But in public life, the cost of mistaken confidence grows. A committee cites “common sense” to justify a policy, an editor labels a rumor “obvious,” a judge calls a decision “reasonable.” The language of certainty can sound modest, yet it often hides the fact that what counts as reasonable is shaped by background assumptions that rarely receive scrutiny.

Some traditions respond by seeking foundations: indubitable premises from which the rest of knowledge can be built. Others treat foundations as mirages and instead emphasize practices that keep inquiry honest—peer criticism, transparency about methods, and willingness to revise. The contrast is often taught as a technical dispute, but it also reflects different attitudes toward vulnerability. The foundationalist wants safety from error; the fallibilist accepts that exposure to correction is the price of learning. Neither attitude is costless. Safety can become dogma; perpetual revisability can become paralysis.

The tension appears vividly in the familiar scene of the thought experiment. Consider a device that perfectly simulates a cherished experience—a friendship, a triumph, a love—while keeping the user unaware of the simulation. Some people say the experience would be just as valuable; others insist that value depends on contact with reality. The debate is rarely settled by the imagined device. Its function is diagnostic: it reveals which commitments a person already holds about authenticity, pleasure, and the kind of life worth wanting.

Diagnosis, however, is only the beginning. If a thought experiment exposes a commitment, it also invites a question about why that commitment deserves allegiance. A person who rejects the simulated life might be defending truth, or might be defending status. A person who accepts it might be defending wellbeing, or might be dodging responsibility. The same verdict can be reached for different reasons, and those reasons matter because they determine what else one must accept. Reasoning is not a vote for a conclusion; it is a map of the obligations that come with it.

In this light, the best arguments are not those that force agreement, but those that clarify what disagreement costs. They show where a dispute is empirical (what happened?), where it is normative (what should count?), and where it is semantic (what do we mean by “harm” or “freedom”?). When arguments fail, it is often because these layers are collapsed into a single quarrel. The quarrel feels interminable because participants are not disagreeing about one thing; they are disagreeing about the rules of disagreement.
