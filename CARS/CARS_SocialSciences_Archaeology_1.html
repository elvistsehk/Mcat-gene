Passage 1 (Questions 1–7)

Archaeology is often imagined as a hunt for treasures: a tomb opened, a gold mask lifted into daylight, a headline announcing “the past revealed.” But for most archaeologists, the past is not revealed; it is inferred. The primary data are not stories but traces—soil stains, broken pottery, charred seeds, postholes arranged in a faint geometry. These traces do not speak in full sentences. They become meaningful only when the archaeologist places them into a disciplined conversation with context: where an object was found, what sits above and below it, and what patterns repeat across a site.

That emphasis on context explains why excavation is treated as a kind of controlled destruction. To dig is to remove layers that will never be replaced. A spectacular artifact stripped from its setting may be visually impressive yet scientifically impoverished, like a sentence torn from a paragraph. Stratigraphy—the reading of layered deposits—allows archaeologists to reconstruct sequences of activity, but it requires patience with ambiguity. A layer might record a single dramatic event, such as a fire, or it might accumulate gradually through ordinary disposal. The same material can signify accident, habit, or intention depending on its associations.

Because traces are incomplete, archaeologists lean on models. A cluster of animal bones could suggest feasting, everyday meals, or ritual offerings; a concentration of slag could indicate metalworking or the cleanup of a distant workshop. The responsible move is not to pick the most vivid narrative but to test competing explanations against multiple kinds of evidence. Cut marks on bone, the age profile of animals, the location of refuse pits, and the presence of specialized tools can each constrain interpretation. In archaeology, imagination is necessary, but it is bounded by what the record can plausibly support.

New technologies have intensified this tension between story and constraint. Remote sensing can reveal buried walls without turning a spade, while isotopic analysis can track diet or migration from teeth and bone. These methods expand what can be asked, but they do not eliminate interpretation. A chemical signature still must be connected to practices, and practices must be connected to social life. Even radiocarbon dates, seemingly the most objective of facts, are estimates with ranges and assumptions about contamination and calibration. Precision can narrow uncertainty, yet it cannot decide which human meaning a pattern should carry.

Public interest often presses archaeology toward simple conclusions: “This was a palace,” “That was a temple,” “These people vanished.” Yet the most difficult questions concern not labels but relationships—how households coordinated work, how authority was displayed, how communities responded to drought, trade, or conflict. Such questions require resisting the temptation to treat a single structure as a symbol that summarizes an entire society. A large building may index centralized power, but it may also reflect cooperative labor, seasonal gathering, or elite competition. What matters is how the structure fits into broader distributions of housing, storage, pathways, and everyday debris.

Ethical debates make the inferential character of the discipline especially visible. Who has the right to excavate, display, or rebury material remains? When archaeologists treat objects as data alone, they risk ignoring that descendants may treat the same objects as ancestors or as evidence of dispossession. Some critics worry that archaeology stabilizes national myths by selecting which pasts to highlight. Others argue that careful excavation can challenge myths by showing complexity where propaganda demands purity. Either way, the discipline is not merely about what happened; it is also about which inferences become authoritative, and for whom.

How to reason (CARS)

  • Start with the author’s contrast: “treasure” stories vs inference from traces.
  • Track how the passage builds constraints: context (stratigraphy/associations), competing explanations, and limits of technology.
  • For main idea, pick what covers the whole argument (inference + context + ethics), not a single method.
  • For within-text questions, anchor to a specific paragraph function (why excavation is destructive, why evidence lists matter).
  • For beyond-text questions, apply the author’s logic: avoid single-label conclusions; demand broader patterning and stated assumptions.
  • Eliminate traps: extremes, reversals, scope shifts, and claims that treat data as self-interpreting.
