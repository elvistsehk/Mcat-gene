Passage 1 (Questions 1–8)

Anthropology is sometimes caricatured as the study of distant customs, a discipline that collects the strange and displays it for the familiar. But the better anthropological question is not “Why do they do that?” It is “What makes that practice sensible here?” To ask this is to treat culture less as a decorative layer atop human nature and more as a set of learned expectations that coordinate daily life. A gesture, a meal, or a marriage rule is not merely behavior; it is also an interpretation of what counts as proper, risky, generous, or shameful.

Early ethnographers often assumed that if one could list a community’s rules, one would have captured its culture. Contemporary anthropologists are wary of that confidence. Rules are spoken most loudly when they are being contested, and many of the most powerful norms are not articulated at all. People learn them by participating—by noticing which stories receive laughter, which silences signal disapproval, and which requests are considered reasonable. For an outsider, the puzzle is not only translating words but learning what a situation is taken to be.

This is why anthropologists emphasize context and meaning rather than simply tallying traits. A visitor might record that a market opens at dawn, that bargaining is expected, and that elders occupy the central benches. Yet those observations remain thin until one understands what dawn signifies (urgency, purity, safety), what bargaining expresses (respect, rivalry, solidarity), and why elders sit where they do (authority, mediation, surveillance). The same outward pattern can carry different meanings in different places; conversely, similar meanings can be enacted through different practices.

The method that best fits this ambition is participant observation: the slow work of living alongside people long enough to be corrected by them. It is slow because it relies on relationships, and relationships reveal what surveys often miss—how people explain themselves differently depending on who is listening. Participant observation does not abolish bias; it relocates it. The anthropologist’s presence changes what is said and done, but so does every social presence. The task is to make those influences visible, not to pretend one has become a neutral recording device.

This methodological humility matters because culture is not a museum exhibit; it is an arena. Practices are tied to resources, and resources to power. When a development project redefines a fishing coast as “underutilized,” it imports a metric of value that may conflict with local ones. What looks like efficiency to an external planner may register as dispossession to residents who measure wellbeing through kin obligations, seasonal rhythms, or access to sacred sites. Anthropology is most useful when it shows that disputes are not only about interests but about incompatible assumptions regarding what counts as a good life.

The risk, of course, is romanticizing difference—treating every local practice as beyond criticism. Yet describing a practice as meaningful is not the same as endorsing it. Anthropology can illuminate how norms become persuasive and who benefits from them, while still asking whether some norms cause harm. In that sense, the discipline’s promise is double: it expands the range of human possibilities we can imagine, and it sharpens our scrutiny of the categories—progress, tradition, rationality—that are often used to judge those possibilities from a distance.

How to reason (CARS)

  • Identify the author’s core question: “What makes a practice sensible here?”
  • For each paragraph, label its function (redefine anthropology, warn about rule-lists, illustrate meaning, explain method, apply to power, qualify).
  • When answers mention “culture,” check whether they include meaning + context (not just traits).
  • For within-text questions, anchor your choice in a specific example (market, participant observation, development metric).
  • For beyond-text questions, extend the author’s logic about metrics of value, power, and assumptions about a “good life.”
  • Eliminate traps: romanticizing (“never criticize”), extremes, scope shifts, and reversals.
