Passage 1 (Questions 1–8)

Architectural debate often begins with a photograph: a celebrated façade, an aerial plan, a skyline at dusk. The image invites a comforting assumption—that a building’s meaning is visible, that what we see is what the structure is. Yet architecture is less an object than a sequence of negotiations. A wall can be a boundary, a display, a promise of safety, or a reminder of exclusion, depending on who is permitted to cross it and at what cost. The same column that tourists read as heritage may be experienced by residents as a sign that their neighborhood has become someone else’s museum.

Because buildings endure, they tempt us to imagine that their purposes endure as well. Preservation campaigns sometimes lean on this temptation, speaking as if a structure possesses an original, singular intention that later generations can recover. Developers, by contrast, often narrate the city as a blank page, where novelty justifies demolition. Both stories flatten time. They ignore that architecture is continually re-authored by use: the factory becomes a gallery, the mansion becomes apartments, the highway underpass becomes a market. What survives is not simply matter; it is an arrangement of expectations that must be maintained, disputed, or revised.

Consider the popular celebration of “adaptive reuse,” in which older buildings are converted to new functions. The phrase sounds practical and benign, as though the only question were how to fit a café into a former warehouse. But reuse can also launder conflict. When an industrial district becomes a district of exposed brick and curated “authenticity,” the past is treated as texture rather than labor. The building is allowed to remember only what flatters the present. In that sense, reuse is not the opposite of erasure; it can be a disciplined form of forgetting, selecting a few aesthetic fragments while discarding the social conditions that produced them.

Architects and critics have long argued about whether a building should express a coherent authorial vision or accommodate the messy improvisations of everyday life. Modernist rhetoric favored clarity: form follows function, ornament is crime, the plan is an argument in concrete. Yet even the most programmatic design is eventually edited by users who reroute pathways, block windows, or create informal thresholds—an extra chair in a corridor, a garden in a leftover lot. The “failures” of a design often reveal a different intelligence: how people actually value proximity, privacy, or community when given a space to inhabit rather than to admire.

This tension becomes sharpest when cities commission signature projects meant to broadcast identity. A dazzling museum can bring tourists and revenue, but it can also redirect public funds and attention away from less photogenic needs: housing repairs, accessible transit, shaded sidewalks. The question is not whether beauty matters; it is who gets to define what counts as beauty and who pays for its consequences. When critics praise a building for being “iconic,” they may be praising its capacity to stand apart from its context—precisely the trait that makes it difficult for ordinary residents to claim it as theirs.

A more generous way to read architecture is to treat it as a public hypothesis. A building proposes a relationship among bodies, activities, and resources, and then the city tests that proposal over time. On this view, preservation is not a quest for a pure origin, nor is development a license for perpetual amnesia. It is an ongoing argument about what should be carried forward and what must be changed. The most responsible architecture, then, may be the kind that makes its assumptions legible—inviting people to contest them—rather than insisting that its meaning is already settled in stone.

How to reason (CARS)

  • Identify the author’s central claim and the main contrast (what is the passage pushing back against?).
  • For each paragraph, ask: What job does it do? (define, exemplify, qualify, critique, or propose).
  • For main idea / purpose, choose the option that matches the passage’s overall aim, not a vivid detail.
  • For support / inference, match the choice to a specific claim or a tight inference the text licenses.
  • For strengthen / weaken, locate the author’s key assumption, then pick what directly changes it.
  • Use the scope test: eliminate answers that are too extreme, too narrow, or introduce new ideas.
