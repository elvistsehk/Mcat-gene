Passage 1 (Questions 1–8)

The Buddha is often imagined as a solitary sage who discovered timeless truths and then delivered them, intact, to the world. This picture is flattering to modern tastes: it makes enlightenment a private achievement and teaching a simple transmission. Yet the earliest layers of Buddhist literature, as scholars read them, suggest something less like a single revelation and more like a carefully calibrated pedagogy. The Buddha’s speech is frequently tailored to the listener, and the path is described in terms of practices—attention, restraint, inquiry—that reshape how experience is perceived. What matters is not only what is said, but what the saying does to a mind trained to cling.

That emphasis on pedagogy complicates the familiar question, “What did the Buddha really teach?” Historians of religion note that the Buddha is known to us almost entirely through communities that preserved his words, arranged them, and argued about their meaning. The figure of the Buddha therefore sits at the intersection of memory and authority: to attribute a teaching to him is to claim a warrant for a way of living. This does not make the tradition a mere invention. It does mean that the 'original' teaching is not a single sentence hidden behind later commentaries, but a set of recurring concerns—suffering, its causes, and the possibility of release—expressed through genres designed for instruction.

A central example is the Buddhist use of paradox and negation. Texts may insist that the self is not a permanent essence, and then warn against turning “no-self” into a new metaphysical doctrine. The point, many interpreters argue, is therapeutic: to loosen the grip of categories that generate anxiety and pride. In such passages, the Buddha is less a metaphysician than a strategist of attention. He employs concepts as tools, then cautions against worshiping the tool. To read these statements as straightforward theory is to miss their performative aim: they are meant to interrupt habitual identification.

This tool-like view of doctrine also helps explain why Buddhist traditions can appear to disagree while sharing a family resemblance. Some schools emphasize monastic discipline and renunciation; others highlight compassion in the marketplace; still others develop elaborate cosmologies and ritual technologies. To an outsider, these look like competing religions. But from within, they can be justified as different skillful means—adaptations to temperament, culture, and historical circumstance. The question becomes not which formulation is the one true summary, but which practices reliably reduce greed, hatred, and delusion in a given setting.

Modern readers sometimes translate the Buddha into a secular psychologist, praising mindfulness while discarding karma, ritual, and devotion as cultural clutter. Traditionalists sometimes answer by guarding orthodoxy, treating later accretions as essential. Both approaches risk simplifying the very dynamic the tradition records: the Buddha as a teacher whose authority is expressed through flexible instruction, and a community whose continuity is maintained through debate about what counts as faithful adaptation. The tension is not an embarrassment; it is part of how the tradition stays alive across languages and empires.

If we treat the Buddha as a historical figure, we are left with uncertainty: dates are debated, and texts are layered. If we treat him only as a symbol, we lose the concrete practices that shaped communities. A more careful approach treats “the Buddha” as a name for a teaching situation—an ongoing attempt to describe suffering in ways that make liberation imaginable, while warning that any description can become another object of clinging. In that sense, the tradition’s most distinctive claim may be methodological: salvation is not purchased by correct slogans, but cultivated by learning how not to be fooled by one’s own concepts.
