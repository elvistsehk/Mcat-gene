Passage 1 (Questions 1–7)

Walk into any bookstore and you will find a familiar promise: become your “authentic self.” The phrase is packaged as liberation. It suggests that beneath social roles there lies a truer person—steady, coherent, waiting to be uncovered. Many readers welcome this picture because it turns the messiness of life into a problem with a solution: remove the false layers and the real will appear. But the promise smuggles in a philosophical claim: that the self is a hidden object, and that living well is primarily a task of discovery.

A different tradition treats authenticity less like excavation and more like construction. The self is not a buried statue but an ongoing practice. What counts as “me” depends on commitments that take shape over time—relationships, projects, and the standards by which we judge ourselves. To call such a self authentic is not to say it has been found; it is to say it has been owned.

The construction view can sound dangerous: if the self is made, then anything can count as authentic. That worry fuels a defense of discovery: authenticity must be anchored in something prior to choice—temperament, desire, perhaps biology—otherwise the term becomes mere praise. Yet this defense confuses limits with essences. Constraints are real, but they do not entail a fixed inner blueprint that determines what is “really” us.

Consider how quickly the discovery story turns moral. When someone changes—leaves a career, adopts a new faith, ends a long relationship—observers ask whether the change reveals the person’s “true” nature or betrays it. The grammar of betrayal makes sense only if authenticity means loyalty to an original self. But many changes are answers to new reasons. They can be judged wise or foolish, but the verdict is not delivered by an inner court; it is delivered by standards for evaluating reasons: coherence, honesty, courage, and the willingness to take responsibility for consequences.

This is why authenticity cannot be reduced to spontaneity. The discovery story celebrates whatever feels immediate, as if the unfiltered impulse were the surest evidence of reality. But a life guided by impulse may be guided by what is loudest in the moment. Authenticity, if it is worth anything, must include reflection: the ability to step back, to ask what one’s desires mean, and to decide which deserve endorsement.

A final complication is that authenticity is often sold as a private achievement, while our identities are shaped in conversation. Language, norms, and even the categories by which we describe ourselves are learned. If we insist that the authentic self is what remains after society is stripped away, we risk imagining a person too thin to live. The construction view does not deny inwardness; it denies that inwardness is enough.

If authenticity is a moral ideal, its core demand is not “be what you already are,” but “be what you can justify becoming.” That demand preserves resistance to mere conformity without turning the self into a fossil. The authentic life is not uncovered in a single revelation; it is assembled, tested, and, sometimes, repaired.
