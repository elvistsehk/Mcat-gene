Standalone Questions (15)
1. Which cell type makes up the majority of cells in the epidermis and produces keratin?
A. Melanocytes
B. Langerhans cells
C. Keratinocytes
D. Fibroblasts
Correct
Incorrect
Show Explanation

Correct answer: C. Keratinocytes are the predominant epidermal cells; they proliferate from the stratum basale and produce keratin that strengthens the outer barrier.

Why the other choices are not the best:

  • A. Melanocytes synthesize melanin in the stratum basale, but they are far less numerous than keratinocytes.
  • B. Langerhans cells are specialized macrophage-like antigen-presenting cells in the epidermis, not the primary structural cell.
  • D. Fibroblasts live in the dermis and secrete collagen and other extracellular matrix components.

A. Stratum corneum → stratum lucidum → stratum granulosum → stratum spinosum → stratum basale

C. Stratum corneum → stratum granulosum → stratum spinosum → stratum basale → stratum lucidum

Correct
Incorrect
Show Explanation

Correct answer: A. From superficial to deep, the epidermis is corneum, lucidum (only in thick skin), granulosum, spinosum, basale.

Why the other choices are not the best:

  • B. This lists largely deep-to-superficial and incorrectly places lucidum at the surface; lucidum sits between corneum and granulosum.
  • C. Lucidum is not the deepest layer; it is superficial and present only in thick skin between corneum and granulosum.
  • D. Lucidum is not the most superficial layer; stratum corneum is.

12. The primary cooling benefit of sweating comes from which physical principle?

C. Evaporation absorbs heat from the skin because it is an endothermic phase change
D. Sweat lowers blood osmolarity, directly reducing metabolic heat production
Correct
Incorrect
Show Explanation

Correct answer: C. Heat is removed when liquid water on the skin evaporates; the phase change requires energy, which is drawn from body heat.

Why the other choices are not the best:

  • B. Sweat does not insulate; it promotes heat loss when it evaporates.
  • D. Sweating changes fluid balance but cooling is primarily due to heat absorbed during evaporation.

Passages (3)
Passage 1: Heat Illness and Autonomic Control of Sweating

A 23-year-old athlete begins a high-intensity interval workout on a hot, very humid day. After 20 minutes, he feels dizzy and nauseated. His skin is warm, and he complains of an unusually dry mouth despite drinking water.

Earlier that morning, he took an over-the-counter medication for motion sickness. The medication is known to reduce glandular secretions in some people. During evaluation, his heart rate is elevated, and his skin appears flushed. A clinician notes that his clothing is surprisingly dry given the heat.

The patient is moved to a cooler environment. As he rests, his symptoms slowly improve, but he continues to feel overheated compared with teammates who performed the same workout.

Step 1: Identify the key clues. Dry mouth + dry clothing/skin during heat stress points to reduced exocrine gland output, including sweat.

Step 2: Map clues to autonomic wiring. Thermoregulatory sweat glands are controlled by postganglionic sympathetic fibers that (unusually) release acetylcholine.

Step 3: Predict what fails. If sweating is reduced, evaporative heat loss drops; the body may still increase skin blood flow (flushing), but core temperature can rise.

Step 4: Use the environment. High humidity decreases the evaporation gradient, so sweating becomes less effective even if produced.

A. Acetylcholine
B. Norepinephrine
C. Dopamine
D. Serotonin
Correct
Incorrect
Show Explanation

Correct answer: A. Thermoregulatory sweating is mediated by postganglionic sympathetic fibers that unusually release acetylcholine onto sweat glands.

Why the other choices are not the best:

  • B. Most postganglionic sympathetic targets use norepinephrine, but sweat glands are the classic cholinergic exception.
  • C. Dopamine is not the primary neurotransmitter for sweat gland activation in thermoregulation.
  • D. Serotonin is mainly a CNS and GI signaling molecule; it is not the primary autonomic transmitter to sweat glands.

A. Cutaneous vasoconstriction to reduce sweat formation
B. Piloerection to trap a thick insulating air layer
C. Reduced skin blood flow to preserve blood volume
D. Cutaneous vasodilation to increase heat transfer to the skin surface
Correct
Incorrect

Correct answer: D. When core temperature is high, arterioles in the skin dilate to bring warm blood to the surface, enhancing heat loss (especially when combined with evaporation).

Why the other choices are not the best:

  • A. Vasoconstriction conserves heat and would worsen overheating; it is a cold response.
  • B. Piloerection is a cold response intended to conserve heat, not lose it.
  • C. Reducing skin blood flow would limit heat dissipation; it may occur in cold, not acute heat stress.

  • C. Keratinocyte turnover affects barrier maintenance, not acute heat exchange during exercise.

Passage 2: Pigmentation Changes and Epidermal Immune Cells

A 19-year-old presents with several well-demarcated patches of depigmented skin on the hands and around the eyes. The patches developed over months and are more noticeable after sun exposure because the surrounding skin darkens while the patches remain pale.

A skin biopsy from a depigmented patch shows normal stratified squamous epithelium with preserved keratinocyte layers. However, pigment granules are markedly reduced, and there are fewer pigment-producing cells at the epidermal–dermal junction compared with adjacent normal skin. Immune cells are also noted within the epidermis.

The clinician explains that the condition is not caused by reduced numbers of keratinocytes. Instead, a specific cell population is being targeted, altering the skin’s ability to protect itself from ultraviolet radiation.

Step 1: Classify the pattern. Patchy, acquired depigmentation suggests loss of a pigment cell population rather than a whole-body enzyme defect.

Step 2: Locate the pigment cell. Melanocytes sit in the stratum basale near the epidermal–dermal junction and transfer pigment to keratinocytes.

Step 3: Integrate the immune clue. Epidermal immune surveillance involves Langerhans cells (antigen presentation), supporting autoimmune targeting.

Step 4: Predict consequences. Less melanin means less UV shielding, so DNA damage risk rises in affected epidermis.

Correct answer: D. Patchy, acquired depigmentation with evidence of immune activity and reduced melanocytes is most consistent with vitiligo (autoimmune melanocyte loss).

Why the other choices are not the best:

  • A. Albinism is typically generalized and present from birth, not patchy acquired lesions.
  • B. Psoriasis produces scaly plaques from rapid keratinocyte turnover, not selective loss of pigmentation.
  • C. Contact dermatitis is usually pruritic and inflammatory after exposure and does not selectively eliminate melanocytes over months.

Passage 3: Partial-Thickness Burn and Sensory Testing

A patient sustains a partial-thickness burn on the forearm after contact with a hot surface. The area is painful and blistered, but some deeper sensation remains. On exam, the clinician notes that the patient has difficulty detecting very light touch over the burned region.

However, the patient can still perceive deep pressure applied to the area and can detect vibration from a tuning fork placed nearby. The clinician also notices that the burned skin is dry and that the patient reports reduced sweating in that region during exercise.

The patient is counseled about complications that can occur when the skin barrier is compromised and why careful wound care and hydration are important during healing.

Step 1: Match each modality to depth. Light touch is detected superficially; deep pressure/vibration are detected deeper.

Step 2: Choose receptors by depth. Meissner corpuscles are superficial (papillary dermis). Pacinian corpuscles are deeper (deep dermis/hypodermis).

Step 3: Use gland anatomy. Sweat glands originate in the dermis, so dermal injury can reduce local sweating.

Step 4: Translate “barrier loss.” Damaged skin increases water loss and infection risk—so hydration and protection matter.
