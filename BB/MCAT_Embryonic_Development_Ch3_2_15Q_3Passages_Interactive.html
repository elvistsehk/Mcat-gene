Standalone Questions (Q1–Q15)
1. A cell in culture has begun expressing early muscle-lineage markers, but if placed in a different microenvironment it can still become a neuron. This best describes a cell that is in:
A. specification
B. determination
C. terminal differentiation
D. necrosis
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A.

Why: Specification is a reversible early designation toward a fate; a specified cell can still change lineage if moved to a new context.

Other choices:

  • B: Determination is irreversible commitment; a determined cell would not switch to neuron simply due to relocation.
  • C: Terminal differentiation means the cell already adopted final structure/biochemistry of the lineage.
  • D: Necrosis is injury-related cell death, not fate designation.
2. A developmental defect prevents a committed progenitor from producing the proteins and morphology needed for its eventual role. The progenitor was committed, but did not complete the process of:
A. specification
B. cleavage
C. differentiation
D. autocrine signaling
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: C.

Why: Determination commits a cell to a lineage, but differentiation is when it actually acquires the structure, function, and biochemistry of that cell type.

Other choices:

  • A: Specification is reversible and earlier than commitment.
  • B: Cleavage is early embryonic mitosis and not the step that produces mature cell features.
  • D: Autocrine is one signaling mode; it doesn’t define acquiring lineage-specific machinery.
3. During cleavage, asymmetric distribution of maternal mRNA and proteins between daughter cells can cause those cells to follow different developmental pathways. This is an example of determination driven primarily by:
A. endocrine hormones traveling through blood
B. intrinsic cytoplasmic determinants
C. juxtacrine receptor–ligand contact
D. telomerase activation
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: B.

Why: If fate depends on which preexisting mRNA/protein a daughter cell inherits, determination is driven by intrinsic cytoplasmic determinants rather than signals from other tissues.

Other choices:

  • A: Endocrine signaling is long-range and not the typical mechanism for early cleavage asymmetry.
  • C: Juxtacrine signaling involves adjacent-cell contact, not inherited maternal factors.
  • D: Telomerase relates to senescence and proliferative capacity, not lineage commitment here.
4. A stem cell can generate neurons, astrocytes, and oligodendrocytes, but cannot generate trophoblast or hepatocytes. This stem cell is best described as:
A. totipotent
B. pluripotent
C. unipotent
D. multipotent
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: D.

Why: Multipotent stem cells can form multiple related cell types within a specific lineage (e.g., neural or hematopoietic), but not all tissues of the body.

Other choices:

  • A: Totipotent cells can form all cell types, including extraembryonic/placental structures.
  • B: Pluripotent cells can form any body cell (all three germ layers) but not placental structures; neurons and hepatocytes would both be possible if truly pluripotent.
  • C: Unipotent cells generate only one cell type.
5. Which statement most accurately captures how potency changes during development?
A. Potency increases as cells differentiate because specialized cells have more functions.
B. Totipotent cells appear only after formation of the three germ layers.
C. Potency generally decreases as development progresses from totipotent → pluripotent → multipotent.
D. Pluripotent cells can form placental tissues, but multipotent cells cannot.
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: C.

Why: As cells become more specialized, their developmental options narrow; potency decreases from totipotent (includes placental + fetal) to pluripotent (any fetal cell) to multipotent (multiple within a lineage).

Other choices:

  • A: Specialization is associated with loss of developmental potential, not gain.
  • B: Totipotency is earliest; later stages show reduced potency.
  • D: Totipotent (not pluripotent) includes placental structures.
6. A lab culture contains embryonic cells that can become any fetal tissue and can also contribute to chorion/placenta-like structures in a chimera assay. These cells are most consistent with being:
A. totipotent

Why: Totipotent cells can form all cell types, including extraembryonic (placental) tissues as well as the embryo proper.

  • B: Pluripotent cells cannot form placental structures.

7. A growth factor released by a developing tissue diffuses only a short distance and patterns neighboring cells in the same region. This is best classified as:

  • D: Necrosis is injury-related cell death, not a communication mode.

Why: Juxtacrine signaling typically requires direct cell–cell contact (membrane-bound ligands/receptors) and usually does not rely on diffusion.

Other choices:

  • A: Autocrine involves a secreted signal acting on the same cell.
  • B: Endocrine involves long-distance travel through blood.
  • D: Paracrine involves diffusion through local extracellular space.
9. A hormone produced by a developing endocrine organ travels through the bloodstream and influences differentiation in a distant target tissue. This is an example of:
A. paracrine signaling

D. endocrine signaling

Correct Answer: D.

Why: Endocrine signals are secreted hormones that travel through the bloodstream to distant targets.

Other choices:

  • A: Paracrine acts locally.
  • B: Juxtacrine requires direct contact.
  • C: Autocrine acts on the secreting cell itself.

  • C: Fertilization membrane is an early block to polyspermy, unrelated to later induction competence.

11. A patterning molecule is secreted from a point source and forms a concentration gradient. Cells closer to the source adopt fate X, while distant cells adopt fate Y. The signaling molecule is best described as a:
A. telomerase
B. morphogen
C. apoptotic bleb
D. gap junction
✅Correct
❌Incorrect

Correct Answer: B.

Why: Morphogens are diffusible molecules that can drive determination/differentiation in a concentration-dependent manner, often via gradients.

Other choices:

  • A: Telomerase is a reverse transcriptase that extends telomeres.
  • C: Apoptotic blebs are membrane-bound protrusions during apoptosis.
  • D: Gap junctions allow direct cytoplasmic exchange between adjacent cells; they are not defined by gradient patterning.
12. A developmental biologist describes a process in which the lens placode differentiates and then triggers the optic vesicle to further develop into an optic cup, which then further influences lens/cornea formation. This best exemplifies:
A. endocrine feedback inhibition
B. determinate cleavage
C. reciprocal development
D. necrosis

Correct Answer: C.

Why: Reciprocal development occurs when two tissues mutually induce each other’s differentiation in a back-and-forth sequence (classic example: eye development).

Other choices:

  • A: Endocrine feedback involves hormones and systemic regulation, not local tissue–tissue induction loops.
  • B: Determinate cleavage is early fate commitment of blastomeres, not tissue interactions.
  • D: Necrosis is injury-related death, not coordinated development.
13. During apoptosis, a key reason inflammation is limited compared with necrosis is that apoptosis typically involves:
A. loss of membrane integrity and leakage of intracellular contents

C. rapid swelling and lysis of the cell

Why: In apoptosis, the cell packages contents into membrane-bound blebs/bodies for cleanup, limiting leakage and thus reducing inflammation compared with necrosis.

15. A normal somatic cell line stops dividing after ~50 divisions in vitro. Which molecular change best explains this replicative limit?

C. telomere shortening leading to cellular senescence
D. failure of blastulation to form a blastocoel

Passage 1: Stem Cells, Potency, and Transplant Outcomes

A 29-year-old patient suffers a thoracic spinal cord injury. A clinical trial compares two strategies for cell-based repair. In Arm 1, researchers derive cells from a donated inner cell mass and expand them in culture before transplantation. In Arm 2, clinicians harvest the patient’s own adipose-derived stem cells and expose them to a defined cocktail of transcription factors to increase developmental potential prior to directing them toward a neural lineage.

Six months after transplantation, Arm 1 shows occasional masses near the injection site composed of mixed tissue types (including cartilage-like and gland-like structures). Arm 2 shows fewer immune complications and no masses, but the functional improvement is modest and varies widely between individuals. The investigators note that directing cells to the “correct” tissue is challenging because normal neural tissue depends on coordinated interactions among multiple specialized cell types.

Follow-up immunologic testing reveals that some Arm 1 participants developed robust T-cell responses to the transplanted cells. In contrast, Arm 2 participants show minimal evidence of graft rejection.

How to reason:

  1. Identify what each arm is starting with: inner cell mass → typically higher potency; patient adipose stem cells → lower potency unless reprogrammed.
  2. Use outcomes as clues: mixed tissue masses suggest pluripotent-like behavior (teratoma risk) and incomplete control of differentiation.
  3. Connect immune findings: genetically mismatched donor cells → higher rejection risk; autologous (self-derived) cells → lower risk.
  4. Answer by linking potency, control of differentiation, and immunology to the scenario evidence.
16. The mixed tissue masses observed in Arm 1 are most consistent with transplantation of cells that retained which potency level at the time of implantation?
A. multipotent
B. unipotent
C. fully differentiated
D. pluripotent
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: D.

Why: Masses containing multiple tissue types suggest uncontrolled differentiation typical of highly potent stem cells; pluripotent cells can generate diverse derivatives from all three germ layers and can form teratoma-like masses if not properly directed.

Other choices:

  • A: Multipotent cells are more restricted and are less likely to generate many unrelated tissue types.
  • B: Unipotent cells produce one lineage and would not generate mixed tissues.
  • C: Fully differentiated cells should not spontaneously generate multiple tissue types.

Correct Answer: A.

Why: Arm 2 is autologous (self-derived), so the cells share the patient’s genetic makeup and are less likely to trigger rejection.

Other choices:

  • B: Reprogramming can change potency, but does not inherently remove alloimmune mismatch if the cells are from a donor.
  • C: Tissue target does not determine compatibility; genetic mismatch does.
  • D: Culture duration alone does not explain reduced immune response.

Why: The passage notes that many organs (including neural tissue) require multiple cell types and different signals. Achieving correct organization and differentiation is difficult, producing variable outcomes.

Other choices:

  • A: Placental tissue formation is a totipotency feature, not a typical multipotent adult stem-cell issue.
  • C: Autocrine signaling can occur in many adult tissues; it is not “impossible.”
  • D: Some adult stem cells can proliferate and repair tissue; telomerase biology is not presented as the limiting factor here.
Passage 2: Eye Development, Competence, and Induction Timing

Researchers study early vertebrate eye development using microsurgery. In normal embryos, lateral outpocketings from the brain form optic vesicles that contact the overlying head ectoderm. Shortly after contact, the ectoderm thickens into a lens placode. The team then performs two transplantation experiments using fluorescently labeled ectoderm.

Experiment 1: After optic vesicles have already grown out and touched head ectoderm, the labeled ectoderm is transplanted to the trunk. Days later, a lens-like structure develops in the trunk at the graft site.

Experiment 2: Before optic vesicle outgrowth, the same region of head ectoderm is transplanted to the trunk. In this case, no lens-like structure develops in the trunk.

The researchers suspect that the optic vesicle provides a local signal required for lens formation and that the ectoderm’s ability to respond changes over developmental time. They also observe that once a lens placode forms, the optic vesicle reorganizes into an optic cup more reliably, suggesting mutual tissue–tissue influence.

How to reason:

  1. Compare the two experiments: same ectoderm, different timing → different outcome.
  2. Interpret timing as changes in competence (ability to respond) and/or prior exposure to an inducer.
  3. Lens formation at the trunk only after prior contact implies the ectoderm was already specified toward lens fate.
  4. Mutual influence between lens placode and optic vesicle points to reciprocal development.

C. After optic vesicle contact, the ectoderm becomes specified/biased toward lens fate and retains that bias after transplantation
D. Juxtacrine signaling requires blood flow to the trunk
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: C.

Why: Experiment 1 suggests the ectoderm had already received an inductive cue (or became specified) after optic vesicle contact, so it can proceed toward lens development even after relocation.

Other choices:

  • A: Totipotency is not the relevant concept for ectodermal lens formation.
  • B: The described interaction is local; endocrine signaling is not required to explain the effect.
  • D: Juxtacrine implies direct contact, but the key difference here is prior exposure/competence, not blood flow.

Passage 3: Injury Responses, Regeneration, and Cellular Aging

A 54-year-old patient experiences a myocardial infarction (heart attack) followed by reduced contractility in the affected region. Imaging months later shows a noncontractile scar. In a separate patient, a large portion of liver tissue is surgically removed; within weeks, liver mass and function largely recover. A third patient suffers renal tubular injury after exposure to a nephrotoxic drug; kidney function partially improves, but repeated exposure leads to progressive failure.

In a parallel animal study, investigators compare wound healing in salamanders versus humans. Salamanders regrow amputated limbs with restored bone, muscle, and nerves, while humans typically replace severe injuries with fibrotic scar tissue. Tissue samples from salamanders show clusters of undifferentiated cells that proliferate and migrate to the injury site.

At the cellular level, cultured fibroblasts from older donors stop dividing earlier than fibroblasts from younger donors. In contrast, a tumor cell line divides indefinitely and shows high activity of an enzyme that extends chromosome ends. When investigators induce traumatic membrane rupture in cells, the surrounding tissue shows marked inflammation; when they trigger a controlled internal “self-destruct” program, inflammation is minimal.

How to reason:

  1. Match tissue outcomes to regeneration capacity: heart (low) → scar; liver (high) → regrowth; kidney (moderate but overwhelmable).
  2. Use inflammation clues: membrane rupture/leakage → necrosis; controlled packaging into bodies → apoptosis.
  3. Connect indefinite division to telomerase maintaining telomeres, preventing senescence.
  4. For salamanders, infer complete regeneration supported by retained stem-cell clusters.
