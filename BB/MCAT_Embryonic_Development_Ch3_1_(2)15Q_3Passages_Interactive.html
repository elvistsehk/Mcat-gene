MCAT Biology — Embryonic Development (Ch. 3.1)
Standalone Questions (Q1–Q15)
1. A clinician explains that identical twins can occur because early embryonic cells can each still form an entire organism. This is most directly supported by:
A. indeterminate cleavage
B. determinate cleavage
C. gastrulation
D. neurulation
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A.

Why: In indeterminate cleavage, early blastomeres remain totipotent (their fate is not fixed), so separated cells can each develop into a complete embryo—one basis for monozygotic twinning.

Other choices:

  • B: Determinate cleavage commits cells early, so separated blastomeres typically cannot each form a full organism.
  • C: Gastrulation reorganizes the embryo into germ layers; it does not explain totipotency of early blastomeres.
  • D: Neurulation forms the neural tube later; it does not account for early embryonic totipotency.
2. Shortly after sperm–oocyte membrane fusion, Ca2+ triggers a rapid block to polyspermy and increases zygotic metabolism. The immediate membrane-level change that helps prevent additional sperm entry is:
A. formation of the archenteron
B. activation of the notochord
C. degradation of the zona pellucida by the acrosome
D. depolarization of the ovum membrane during the cortical reaction
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: D.

Why: The cortical reaction releases Ca2+, causing membrane depolarization (fast block) and leading to formation of a fertilization membrane that resists additional sperm entry.

Other choices:

  • A: The archenteron forms during gastrulation, much later than fertilization.
  • B: The notochord is mesodermal and appears during/after gastrulation before neurulation—again later.
  • C: Acrosomal enzymes help the first sperm penetrate; they do not constitute the oocyte’s block to additional sperm.
3. During early cleavage divisions, the embryo’s total size stays roughly constant while cell number increases. Which change best explains why diffusion/exchange becomes more efficient per unit embryo volume?
A. a decreased nuclear-to-cytoplasmic ratio
B. an increased surface area-to-volume ratio
C. formation of chorionic villi
D. elimination of the blastocoel
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: B.

Why: Cleavage produces many smaller cells without growth, which increases surface area-to-volume ratio, improving exchange relative to volume.

Other choices:

  • A: The N:C ratio increases during cleavage (nuclei per cytoplasm rise); it does not decrease.
  • C: Chorionic villi form from trophoblast after implantation to interface with maternal tissue.
  • D: Elimination of the blastocoel occurs during gastrulation, not early cleavage.
4. A biopsy of an early implanted embryo shows finger-like projections invading the endometrium and facilitating maternal–fetal exchange. These projections are produced by cells that will primarily give rise to:
A. the embryo proper
B. the yolk sac
C. the chorion and placenta
D. the neural tube
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: C.

Why: Trophoblast cells form chorionic villi, contribute to the chorion, and help generate the placenta for maternal–fetal exchange.

Other choices:

  • A: The inner cell mass forms the embryo proper, not the invasive villi.
  • B: The yolk sac supports early nutrition/hematopoiesis but is not the source of chorionic villi.
  • D: The neural tube derives from ectoderm during neurulation, later.
5. An ultrasound shows an umbilical cord with the normal vessel pattern. Which statement correctly describes fetal circulation through the umbilical cord?
A. The umbilical vein carries oxygenated, nutrient-rich blood from placenta to embryo; two umbilical arteries carry deoxygenated blood to the placenta.
B. Two umbilical veins carry deoxygenated blood to the placenta; one umbilical artery returns oxygenated blood to the fetus.
C. The umbilical arteries carry oxygenated blood to the fetus; the umbilical vein returns wastes to the placenta.
D. All umbilical vessels carry oxygenated blood because they are part of the fetal circulation.
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A.

Why: In fetal circulation, the umbilical vein brings oxygenated blood from the placenta to the fetus, while two umbilical arteries carry deoxygenated blood and wastes back to the placenta.

Other choices:

  • B: The vessel count and oxygenation directions are reversed here.
  • C: Arteries in the umbilical cord carry deoxygenated blood to the placenta (opposite of postnatal systemic pattern).
  • D: Only the umbilical vein is oxygen-rich; the arteries carry oxygen-poor blood.
6. A scientist labels a hollow, fluid-filled cavity in an early mammalian embryo. Which structure is being labeled, and at what stage is it first characteristic?
A. blastopore; gastrula
B. archenteron; neurula
C. blastocoel; blastula (blastocyst in mammals)
D. neural groove; morula
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: C.

Why: The blastocoel is the fluid-filled cavity of the blastula; in mammals the blastula is called a blastocyst.

Other choices:

  • A: The blastopore is an opening in the gastrula associated with the archenteron, not a cavity.
  • B: The archenteron is the primitive gut tube formed during gastrulation, not during neurulation.
  • D: A morula is a solid ball of cells with no cavity; the neural groove appears later during neurulation.
7. A clinician suspects an ectopic pregnancy. Based on typical epidemiology, the most likely implantation site is the:
A. ovary
B. cervix
C. abdominal cavity
D. fallopian tube (often the ampulla)
✅Correct
❌Incorrect

Why: Most ectopic pregnancies occur in the fallopian tube (commonly the ampulla), where the embryo cannot safely grow and may cause tubal rupture.

Other choices:

  • A: Ovarian implantation can occur but is much less common than tubal.

9. A developmental biologist describes a signaling center that releases diffusible factors to bias the fate of nearby responding cells (e.g., guiding axon growth and coordinating tissue proximity). This best describes:
A. induction by organizer cells
B. determinate cleavage
C. the cortical reaction
D. the acrosomal reaction
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A.

Why: Induction is when one group of cells (often an organizing region) influences the fate of nearby cells using diffusible inducers.

Other choices:

  • B: Determinate cleavage refers to early commitment of blastomere fate, not signaling between tissues.
  • C: The cortical reaction is a fertilization event blocking polyspermy.
  • D: The acrosomal reaction is a sperm event enabling zona penetration.

Passage 1: IVF Timing, Cleavage, and Twin Membranes

A fertility clinic transfers a single blastocyst into the uterus after in vitro fertilization. Ultrasound later reveals two fetuses with separate amniotic sacs but a shared outer membrane. The clinician notes that the embryo divided after leaving the fallopian tube but before one key extraembryonic membrane fully separated. In a separate case, another patient’s embryo implants late and the pregnancy fails despite normal fertilization and early cleavage in the tube. The clinic counsels patients that increasing the time between ovulation and embryo arrival in the uterus can reduce the likelihood of successful implantation.

How to reason:

  1. Identify the developmental stage clues: blastocyst, implantation timing, and extraembryonic membranes.
  2. For twin membranes, use the rule: earlier split → fewer shared structures; later split → more shared structures.
  3. Translate “separate amnions but shared outer membrane” into chorion vs. amnion sharing.
  4. For implantation failure, connect uterine readiness: embryos must reach the uterus while the endometrium can support implantation.
  5. Choose answers that require a step from evidence → classification/prediction, not direct quotes.
16. In Passage 1, “separate amniotic sacs but a shared outer membrane” most directly indicates which twinning classification?
A. dichorionic/diamniotic
B. monochorionic/diamniotic
C. monochorionic/monoamniotic
D. conjoined twins
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: B.

Why: Monochorionic/diamniotic twins share the chorion (outer membrane) but have separate amnions (separate amniotic sacs).

Other choices:

  • A: Dichorionic/diamniotic twins have two chorions and two amnions (least sharing).
  • C: Monochorionic/monoamniotic twins share both chorion and amnion (one amniotic sac).
  • D: Conjoined twinning is due to incomplete separation; membrane pattern alone does not imply conjoining.

B. The cortical reaction prevented completion of meiosis II
C. Determinate cleavage produced a morula too early to implant
D. Neural crest migration failed during implantation
✅Correct
❌Incorrect

Correct Answer: A.

Why: The passage emphasizes late arrival to the uterus; if implantation occurs too late, the endometrium may no longer support the embryo, leading to implantation failure.

Other choices:

  • B: The cortical reaction helps prevent polyspermy and increases metabolic activity; it does not prevent meiosis II completion.
  • C: Cleavage timing is not framed as “too early to implant,” and implantation occurs at the blastocyst stage, not at the morula.
  • D: Neural crest migration occurs much later (after neurulation), not during implantation.
18. Based on the membrane-sharing logic in Passage 1, which scenario would most increase fetal risk due to maximal sharing of gestational structures?
A. A split that occurs at the 2-cell stage
B. A split that occurs during early cleavage before the morula forms
C. A split that occurs after the amnion has already formed around the embryo
D. Two separate eggs fertilized by two separate sperm
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: C.

Why: A split after amnion formation can lead to monochorionic/monoamniotic twins (shared chorion + shared amnion), which generally carries higher risk because the fetuses share more structures and space.

Passage 2: Teratogen Windows, Folate, and Maternal Metabolism

In a cohort study, investigators compare two exposures during early pregnancy. Group A includes individuals with poorly controlled hyperglycemia in the first trimester; Group B includes individuals with low dietary folate intake before recognizing pregnancy. Ultrasound screening later identifies a subset of fetuses with spinal abnormalities in Group B and a subset of unusually large fetuses in Group A. Genetic testing suggests no single mutation explains the outcomes, and the investigators note wide variability in severity even among patients with similar exposures. They hypothesize that timing, dose, and placental transmission rate help explain why some embryos are affected while others are not.

In follow-up interviews, several participants report that they began vitamin supplementation only after a positive pregnancy test, whereas others took supplements preconception.

How to reason:

  1. Map exposure → likely developmental target: folate → neural tube closure; hyperglycemia → fetal overgrowth + neonatal hypoglycemia risk.
  2. Use the idea of critical windows: early organogenesis/germ layer patterning is highly sensitive.
  3. When outcomes vary despite similar exposure, consider gene–environment interactions and exposure parameters (route, dose, timing).
  4. Answer passage questions by using the scenario to make a prediction (what outcome, what stage, what mechanism).

C. blastopore becoming the anus

❌Incorrect

Correct Answer: D.

Why: Low folate is associated with neural tube defects (e.g., spina bifida), which arise when the neural tube fails to close during neurulation.

  • B: Blastocoel formation occurs during blastulation (blastula/blastocyst), earlier than neurulation.
  • C: Blastopore fate is a gastrulation concept and does not directly explain neural tube defects.

Passage 3: Induction, Neural Crest Migration, and Organ Origins

A lab studies a vertebrate embryo with a mutation that reduces secretion of an inducer molecule from a midline mesodermal rod. The mutant embryos show shallow neural folds that fail to fuse completely at the dorsal midline. In addition, pigment cells are markedly decreased in the skin, and autonomic ganglia are underdeveloped. When the researchers graft a normal midline rod into the mutant embryo early enough, neural fold formation improves, but pigment and autonomic defects persist unless a separate population of migratory cells is also restored.

The researchers conclude that one defect is due to failed induction of ectodermal tissue, while the other is due to impaired migration of a distinct ectoderm-derived cell population.

How to reason:

  1. Identify the “midline mesodermal rod” as the notochord; it induces overlying ectoderm to form neural folds → neural tube.
  2. Link missing pigment cells + autonomic ganglia to neural crest derivatives (melanocytes, PNS ganglia, Schwann cells, adrenal medulla).
  3. Use the graft result: fixing only the notochord signal rescues neural tube but not neural-crest outcomes → two mechanisms.
  4. Choose answers that integrate both lines of evidence (induction vs migration).
