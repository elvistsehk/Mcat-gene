MCAT Biology Chapter 10.1 — Excretory System (Kidney, Nephron, Urine Regulation)
Standalone Questions (Q1–Q15)
A physiologist describes the kidney as a portal system. Which feature best supports this description?
A Blood passes through the renal artery before reaching the kidney cortex.
B Urine passes through two sphincters before leaving the body.
C The nephron contains both horizontal and vertical segments.
D Blood passes through two capillary beds in series before returning to the heart.
Show Explanation
Correct Answer: D

A portal system has two capillary beds in series. In the kidney, blood goes through the glomerular capillaries first, then exits via the efferent arteriole and forms a second capillary bed (vasa recta/peritubular capillaries) before venous return.

Why the other choices are wrong:
  • A. Renal arterial inflow is true of many organs and does not define a portal system.
  • B. Sphincters are part of urinary outflow control, not a defining vascular feature.
  • C. Nephron geometry relates to function but does not define a portal system.
During the micturition reflex, which nervous system input directly causes contraction of the detrusor muscle?
A Somatic motor neurons to the external urethral sphincter
B Parasympathetic neurons to the bladder wall
C Sympathetic neurons causing contraction of the internal sphincter
D Sensory afferents from stretch receptors in the bladder
Show Explanation
Correct Answer: B

Parasympathetic activity contracts the detrusor muscle. Stretch afferents trigger the reflex, but the efferent limb that contracts the detrusor is parasympathetic.

Why the other choices are wrong:
  • A. Somatic input controls the external sphincter (voluntary), not detrusor contraction.
  • C. Sympathetic tone generally promotes storage (relax detrusor/contract internal sphincter), not voiding.
  • D. Afferents detect filling but do not directly contract smooth muscle.
A ureter is obstructed by a kidney stone, causing back pressure into the nephron. What is the most direct effect on glomerular filtration?
A Filtration decreases because hydrostatic pressure in Bowman’s space increases.
B Filtration increases because hydrostatic pressure in the glomerulus increases.
C Filtration increases because oncotic pressure in Bowman’s space increases.
D Filtration is unchanged because only oncotic pressure determines filtration.
Show Explanation
Correct Answer: A

Obstruction raises hydrostatic pressure downstream, including Bowman’s space. This pressure opposes filtration, reducing net filtration pressure and decreasing glomerular filtration.

Why the other choices are wrong:
  • B. A stone does not directly raise glomerular hydrostatic pressure; it raises pressure opposing filtration.
  • C. Oncotic pressure in Bowman’s space is normally low because proteins do not filter; obstruction doesn’t create oncotic pressure there.
  • D. Both hydrostatic and oncotic pressure gradients matter; hydrostatic is usually the dominant driver.
Which nephron segment is uniquely capable of making the filtrate more dilute than blood?
A Proximal convoluted tubule
B Descending limb of the loop of Henle
C Thick ascending limb (diluting segment) of the loop of Henle
D Collecting duct
Show Explanation
Correct Answer: C

The thick ascending limb actively reabsorbs NaCl while remaining impermeable to water, so solute leaves without water following. This can make tubular fluid hypotonic relative to plasma.

Why the other choices are wrong:
  • A. PCT reabsorbs solute and water together, keeping filtrate roughly isotonic.
  • B. Descending limb is permeable to water, so it concentrates (not dilutes) the filtrate.
  • D. Collecting duct water permeability is variable, but it does not create hypotonic fluid; with ADH it concentrates urine.
A patient has glucosuria (glucose in urine) despite a normal blood glucose level. Dysfunction in which nephron segment best explains this finding?
A Ascending limb of loop of Henle
B Proximal convoluted tubule
C Distal convoluted tubule
D Collecting duct
Show Explanation
Correct Answer: B

Glucose is filtered and then almost completely reabsorbed in the proximal convoluted tubule. A defect in PCT transporters can cause glucose to remain in the filtrate and appear in urine even when plasma glucose is normal.

Why the other choices are wrong:
  • A. This segment mainly handles salt reabsorption and is impermeable to water; it is not the primary site for glucose reabsorption.
  • C. The DCT fine-tunes ions and responds to aldosterone, but most glucose reabsorption has already occurred.
  • D. The collecting duct mainly adjusts water permeability (ADH) and certain ions; it is not where glucose is normally reclaimed.
As filtrate travels deeper into the medulla in the descending limb of the loop of Henle, what happens to the filtrate and why?
A It becomes more concentrated because water leaves into an increasingly hypertonic interstitium.
B It becomes more dilute because salts leave but water remains in the tubule.
C It remains isotonic because the descending limb is impermeable to water.
D It becomes more concentrated because NaCl is actively pumped into the tubule.
Show Explanation
Correct Answer: A

The descending limb is permeable to water but relatively impermeable to solutes. As medullary osmolarity rises with depth, water exits the tubule down its gradient, concentrating the filtrate.

Why the other choices are wrong:
  • B. Salt reabsorption without water characterizes the ascending limb, not the descending limb.
  • C. The descending limb is water-permeable; that’s why it concentrates the filtrate.
  • D. Active pumping of NaCl out of the tubule occurs in the thick ascending limb, not into the tubule.
Aldosterone levels rise after a drop in blood pressure. Which change in urine composition is most expected?
A Decreased potassium excretion
B Increased sodium excretion
C Increased potassium and hydrogen ion excretion with increased sodium reabsorption
D Decreased water reabsorption with unchanged sodium handling
Show Explanation
Correct Answer: C

Aldosterone acts mainly on the distal convoluted tubule and collecting duct to increase sodium reabsorption (water follows) while increasing secretion of potassium and hydrogen ions into the filtrate.

Why the other choices are wrong:
  • A. Aldosterone increases, not decreases, potassium secretion/excretion.
  • B. It promotes sodium retention (reabsorption), so sodium excretion generally falls.
  • D. It increases sodium reabsorption and tends to increase water reabsorption secondarily, not decrease it.
Which outcome is most consistent with high antidiuretic hormone (ADH) activity?
A Higher plasma osmolarity and more dilute urine
B Lower water reabsorption in the collecting duct
C Increased sodium reabsorption without changing collecting duct water permeability
D More concentrated urine and a decrease in plasma osmolarity
Show Explanation
Correct Answer: D

ADH increases collecting duct water permeability so more water is reabsorbed back into the bloodstream, concentrating urine and lowering plasma osmolarity.

Why the other choices are wrong:
  • A. ADH tends to lower plasma osmolarity by retaining water; urine becomes concentrated, not dilute.
  • B. ADH increases, not decreases, collecting duct water permeability and water reabsorption.
  • C. That describes aldosterone more than ADH; ADH primarily governs water handling.
Which change would most directly stimulate renin release from juxtaglomerular cells?
A Dilation of the efferent arteriole increasing glomerular hydrostatic pressure
B Increased blood osmolarity sensed by hypothalamic osmoreceptors
C Constriction of the afferent arteriole lowering pressure at the glomerulus
D Increased plasma oncotic pressure from elevated albumin levels
Show Explanation
Correct Answer: C

Lower perfusion pressure in the afferent arteriole/glomerulus is a key trigger for renin release, initiating the renin–angiotensin–aldosterone system to raise blood pressure.

Why the other choices are wrong:
  • A. Efferent dilation tends to reduce (not increase) glomerular pressure and is not the classic direct trigger described here.
  • B. High osmolarity primarily triggers ADH release, not renin.
  • D. Albumin affects oncotic pressure but is not the direct pressure-sensing trigger for renin release in this context.
Blood exiting a glomerulus next travels through which vessel?
A Afferent arteriole
B Efferent arteriole
C Renal vein
D Renal pelvis
Show Explanation
Correct Answer: B

Blood enters the glomerulus via the afferent arteriole and exits via the efferent arteriole, which then contributes to the second capillary network (vasa recta/peritubular capillaries).

Why the other choices are wrong:
  • A. The afferent arteriole is upstream of the glomerulus.
  • C. The renal vein is much later in the vascular pathway after capillary networks drain.
  • D. The renal pelvis collects urine, not blood.
Movement of urea from peritubular capillaries into the nephron tubule is best classified as which process?
A Secretion
B Filtration
C Reabsorption
D Endocytosis
Show Explanation
Correct Answer: A

Filtration occurs at Bowman’s capsule. Reabsorption moves substances from filtrate to blood. Secretion moves substances from blood into the tubule elsewhere along the nephron—such as secretion of urea into the PCT.

Why the other choices are wrong:
  • B. Filtration specifically refers to movement from glomerular blood into Bowman’s space.
  • C. Reabsorption is the opposite direction (tubule to blood).
  • D. Most solute transfer here occurs via transporters (active/passive), not bulk endocytosis.
Why does opposite-direction flow between the loop of Henle and the vasa recta enhance water conservation?
A It eliminates all osmolarity differences between cortex and medulla.
B It forces water to move from blood into the tubule throughout the medulla.
C It prevents rapid equilibrium and maintains a medullary osmotic gradient for continued water reabsorption.
D It allows proteins to enter Bowman’s space to increase oncotic pressure.
Show Explanation
Correct Answer: C

Countercurrent flow exposes filtrate to progressively different interstitial osmolarities while the vasa recta preserves the gradient. Opposite directions reduce washout and prevent quick equilibrium, supporting sustained water reabsorption.

Why the other choices are wrong:
  • A. The system creates and maintains (not eliminates) a cortex-to-medulla osmolarity gradient.
  • B. In conservation mode, water generally moves out of the tubule into the interstitium and back to blood.
  • D. Proteins normally do not filter into Bowman’s space; that would signal glomerular pathology.
A patient can no longer voluntarily hold urine despite intact bladder stretch sensation and an intact internal urethral sphincter. Which structure is most likely compromised?
A Detrusor smooth muscle
B Internal urethral sphincter (smooth muscle)
C Afferent sensory fibers from bladder stretch receptors
D External urethral sphincter (skeletal muscle)
Show Explanation
Correct Answer: D

Voluntary urinary control depends on the skeletal muscle of the external urethral sphincter (somatic control). If it cannot be voluntarily contracted, the patient loses the ability to ‘hold’ urine.

Why the other choices are wrong:
  • A. Detrusor dysfunction affects emptying force, not specifically voluntary retention with intact sensation.
  • B. The internal sphincter is involuntary; intact internal sphincter doesn’t restore voluntary control.
  • C. If stretch sensation were lost, the patient would not reliably feel bladder filling; the prompt says sensation is intact.
A patient develops metabolic acidosis (low blood pH). Which renal response would help restore blood pH toward normal?
A Decrease hydrogen ion secretion and decrease bicarbonate reabsorption
B Increase hydrogen ion secretion and increase bicarbonate reabsorption
C Increase bicarbonate secretion and increase hydrogen ion reabsorption
D Decrease filtration of all solutes at Bowman’s capsule
Show Explanation
Correct Answer: B

To correct low pH, the kidneys excrete more H+ (removing acid) and conserve/reabsorb bicarbonate (the major buffer), raising blood pH over time.

Why the other choices are wrong:
  • A. That would worsen acidosis by retaining acid and losing buffer.
  • C. That would also worsen acidosis by losing bicarbonate and retaining H+.
  • D. Global decreased filtration is not a targeted acid–base correction and would impair waste removal.
A protein hormone is synthesized and destined to be secreted from a cell. Which sequence best represents the secretory pathway?
A Rough ER → Golgi apparatus → secretory vesicles → plasma membrane
B Smooth ER → lysosome → nucleus → plasma membrane
C Cytosol → mitochondria → Golgi apparatus → plasma membrane
D Rough ER → nucleus → peroxisome → plasma membrane
Show Explanation
Correct Answer: A

Secreted proteins are synthesized into the rough ER, processed and sorted in the Golgi, packaged into secretory vesicles, and then released by exocytosis at the plasma membrane.

Why the other choices are wrong:
  • B. Smooth ER is for lipid synthesis/detox; lysosomes degrade, and proteins do not route through the nucleus for secretion.
  • C. Mitochondria are not part of the classical secretory route.
  • D. Nucleus and peroxisomes are not steps in the secretion pathway for proteins.
Passage-Based Questions (3 Passages × 3 Questions)
Passage 1

A 24-year-old endurance runner collapses after a long race on a hot day. On exam, she is tachycardic, her blood pressure is low, and she has dry mucous membranes.

Laboratory tests show elevated plasma osmolarity and elevated serum sodium. Her urine volume over the prior two hours is low, and the urine is very dark.

A urinalysis reports a high urine osmolarity. The clinician explains that the kidney can change the permeability of the collecting duct to conserve water when the body is dehydrated.

Over the next hour she drinks water and rests in a cool environment. Her heart rate decreases, her thirst improves, and her urine output gradually increases.

She mentions that during the race she drank several cups of coffee and intentionally avoided stopping to urinate.

• Step 1: Use plasma osmolarity + urine volume to decide whether the body is in water conservation mode.

• Step 2: Identify which hormone directly changes collecting duct water permeability (ADH).

• Step 3: Predict how lowering that hormone after drinking water changes urine volume and concentration.

Which hormonal change most directly explains the runner’s low urine volume and highly concentrated urine during the race?
A Decreased aldosterone leading to natriuresis
B Decreased ADH leading to reduced water reabsorption
C Increased gastrin leading to increased stomach acid
D Increased ADH leading to increased collecting duct water permeability
Show Explanation
Correct Answer: D

High plasma osmolarity stimulates ADH release. ADH increases collecting duct water permeability, so more water is reabsorbed, producing low-volume, concentrated urine.

Why the other choices are wrong:
  • A. Lower aldosterone would increase sodium loss and typically increase urine volume, not concentrate it.
  • B. Lower ADH would reduce water reabsorption and produce a larger volume of more dilute urine.
  • C. Gastrin affects the stomach, not renal water handling.
After she drinks water and plasma osmolarity falls, which immediate change in the collecting duct best supports the observed rise in urine output?
A Decreased water permeability of the collecting duct due to lower ADH signaling
B Increased active NaCl pumping into the collecting duct lumen
C Increased glucose reabsorption in the proximal tubule
D Increased hydrostatic pressure in Bowman’s space
Show Explanation
Correct Answer: A

As plasma osmolarity drops, ADH secretion decreases. With less ADH, the collecting duct becomes less permeable to water, reducing water reabsorption and increasing urine volume.

Why the other choices are wrong:
  • B. Active NaCl pumping is characteristic of the thick ascending limb, not the collecting duct as an immediate response here.
  • C. Glucose reabsorption occurs mainly in the PCT and is not the driver of rapid volume changes after water intake.
  • D. Bowman’s space pressure relates to filtration; the scenario describes hormone-driven changes in reabsorption.
The runner’s coffee intake likely contributed to her frequent production of dilute urine on another day. Based on the passage, which mechanism best explains this effect?
A Caffeine directly increases bile secretion, reducing fat absorption and water retention.
B Caffeine inhibits ADH release, reducing collecting duct water reabsorption.
C Caffeine increases aldosterone release, decreasing sodium reabsorption.
D Caffeine blocks glomerular pores, preventing filtration of water.
Show Explanation
Correct Answer: B

The passage states that caffeine inhibits ADH release. Less ADH means lower collecting duct water permeability and less water reabsorption, resulting in more dilute urine.

Why the other choices are wrong:
  • A. Bile secretion relates to digestion, not ADH-mediated water balance.
  • C. Aldosterone increases (not decreases) sodium reabsorption; caffeine’s key effect here is on ADH.
  • D. Glomerular pores are structural; caffeine does not ‘block’ filtration this way.
Passage 2

A 52-year-old patient presents with severe, colicky flank pain radiating toward the groin and nausea. Imaging reveals a stone lodged in the ureter near the kidney.

Over several hours, urine backs up behind the obstruction. The renal pelvis becomes distended and ultrasound suggests increasing pressure within the collecting system.

Clinicians note that urine output from the affected kidney is reduced. Blood tests show rising levels of nitrogenous wastes, consistent with impaired clearance.

The patient asks why a blockage in the ureter can affect filtration even though filtration occurs in the glomerulus. The clinician replies that filtration depends on pressure differences across the glomerular membrane.

The clinician also notes that, under normal conditions, the filtrate lacks cells and large proteins because the glomerular barrier selects by size.

• Step 1: Translate “backup” into increased downstream hydrostatic pressure in the nephron.

• Step 2: Apply Starling forces: net filtration decreases when Bowman’s space pressure rises.

• Step 3: Predict consequences of reduced GFR: lower urine output and waste accumulation in blood.

Which change in Starling forces most directly reduces filtration in the obstructed kidney?
A Decreased oncotic pressure in glomerular blood
B Increased hydrostatic pressure in the glomerulus
C Increased hydrostatic pressure in Bowman’s space
D Decreased hydrostatic pressure in Bowman’s space
Show Explanation
Correct Answer: C

Urine backup raises pressure in the nephron, including Bowman’s space. Increased Bowman’s space hydrostatic pressure opposes fluid movement into the nephron, lowering net filtration pressure and reducing GFR.

Why the other choices are wrong:
  • A. Lower oncotic pressure would reduce opposition to filtration and tend to increase filtration, not decrease it.
  • B. Higher glomerular hydrostatic pressure would favor more filtration, not less.
  • D. Lower Bowman’s space pressure would favor filtration, not inhibit it.
If the obstruction persists, which downstream consequence is most consistent with the reduced filtration described?
A Rapid appearance of large plasma proteins in the filtrate due to enlarged glomerular pores
B A sudden increase in urine volume due to enhanced collecting duct water reabsorption
C A decrease in blood volume because all filtered water must be excreted
D Accumulation of wastes in the blood because less filtrate is formed for excretion
Show Explanation
Correct Answer: D

With reduced filtration, fewer solutes enter the filtrate to be excreted. Nitrogenous wastes (such as urea and related metabolites) can accumulate in the blood when GFR falls.

Why the other choices are wrong:
  • A. Protein in urine suggests glomerular barrier damage, not a typical effect of ureteral obstruction alone.
  • B. Urine volume generally falls with reduced filtration; water reabsorption does not create urine.
  • C. Much filtered water is normally reabsorbed; reduced filtration would not force fluid loss this way.
Relief of the obstruction restores filtration. Which finding would most specifically suggest an additional primary problem at the level of the glomerular filter rather than the ureter?
A Persistent protein and blood cells in the urine
B Rapid normalization of plasma osmolarity after drinking water
C Decreased potassium excretion after aldosterone rises
D Increased bicarbonate reabsorption during acidosis
Show Explanation
Correct Answer: A

The glomerular filter normally prevents cells and large proteins from entering the filtrate. Persistent proteinuria and hematuria after obstruction relief points to glomerular barrier pathology rather than a purely downstream blockage.

Why the other choices are wrong:
  • B. That reflects systemic water balance and ADH, not specific glomerular barrier integrity.
  • C. Aldosterone typically increases potassium excretion; this option is inconsistent and not glomerulus-specific.
  • D. That is a kidney response but is not specific to the filtration barrier at the glomerulus.
Passage 3

A 60-year-old patient with long-standing hypertension is started on an ACE inhibitor. Over the next week, blood pressure decreases and mild swelling in the ankles improves.

The clinician explains that when renal perfusion pressure is low, the kidney releases renin. Renin leads to formation of angiotensin II, which normally promotes aldosterone release from the adrenal cortex.

With ACE inhibition, less angiotensin II is produced, so aldosterone levels fall. The patient’s labs show a modest rise in serum potassium after starting therapy.

The clinician reminds the patient that aldosterone primarily acts in the distal convoluted tubule and collecting duct to increase sodium reabsorption while promoting potassium and hydrogen ion secretion.

The patient asks how changing sodium handling can lower blood pressure without necessarily changing blood osmolarity.

• Step 1: Trace the pathway: renin → angiotensin II → aldosterone; ACE inhibitors reduce aldosterone.

• Step 2: Recall aldosterone effects: Na+ reabsorption and K+/H+ secretion in the distal nephron.

• Step 3: Separate volume (blood pressure) from osmolarity: aldosterone changes reabsorption of isotonic fluid.

The rise in serum potassium after starting an ACE inhibitor is best explained by which change?
A Increased ADH leading to dilutional hyperkalemia
B Decreased aldosterone leading to reduced potassium secretion in the distal nephron
C Increased enteropeptidase leading to increased potassium absorption
D Increased glomerular pore size allowing potassium to remain in blood
Show Explanation
Correct Answer: B

ACE inhibitors reduce angiotensin II formation, which lowers aldosterone release. With less aldosterone, potassium secretion in the distal nephron falls, so serum potassium can rise.

Why the other choices are wrong:
  • A. ADH mainly changes water handling; it does not directly reduce potassium secretion as described.
  • C. Enteropeptidase is a digestive enzyme activator, unrelated to renal potassium secretion.
  • D. Potassium is freely filterable; pore size changes are not the mechanism here.
Which nephron segments are the primary targets for aldosterone’s sodium-retaining action referenced in the passage?
A Bowman’s capsule and proximal convoluted tubule
B Descending limb and thick ascending limb of the loop of Henle
C Distal convoluted tubule and collecting duct
D Renal pelvis and ureter
Show Explanation
Correct Answer: C

Aldosterone acts mainly on the distal convoluted tubule and collecting duct to increase sodium reabsorption (and increase potassium/hydrogen secretion).

Why the other choices are wrong:
  • A. Most bulk reabsorption occurs in the PCT, but aldosterone’s classic regulated effect is distal.
  • B. Loop of Henle segments set gradients and reabsorb salt/water in characteristic ways, but aldosterone’s major regulated site is distal.
  • D. These are urine-collecting/transport structures, not hormone-regulated reabsorptive epithelium.
How can lowering aldosterone-mediated sodium reabsorption reduce blood pressure without necessarily changing blood osmolarity?
A It increases water reabsorption more than sodium reabsorption, making blood hypotonic.
B It decreases bicarbonate reabsorption, making blood hypertonic.
C It selectively removes proteins from plasma, lowering oncotic pressure.
D It reduces reabsorption of isotonic salt and water, lowering blood volume while keeping solute concentration roughly stable.
Show Explanation
Correct Answer: D

Aldosterone promotes reabsorption of sodium, and water follows, resulting in reabsorption of isotonic fluid. Blocking this lowers blood volume (and pressure) but does not inherently change osmolarity because salt and water move together.

Why the other choices are wrong:
  • A. ADH-driven water handling can change osmolarity, but aldosterone primarily changes volume via isotonic reabsorption.
  • B. Bicarbonate handling is tied to acid–base balance, not the core explanation here.
  • C. Aldosterone does not ‘selectively remove’ proteins; proteins do not filter under normal conditions.
