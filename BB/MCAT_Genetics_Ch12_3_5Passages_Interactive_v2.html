Passage 1
Passage 1: Field Trial of Two Traits in Pea Plants

A research greenhouse is tracking two traits in pea plants: flower color (purple vs. white) and stem height (tall vs. dwarf). Purple and tall are dominant phenotypes in this strain. A breeder begins with two true-breeding lines: purple-tall and white-dwarf. These are crossed to generate an F1 generation that is uniformly purple and tall.

The breeder then performs two different crosses. In Cross A, two F1 plants are crossed with each other. In Cross B, an F1 plant is crossed with a plant that is white and dwarf. For each cross, 200 offspring are scored after germination and growth under the same conditions.

Results: In Cross A, offspring phenotypes are 108 purple-tall, 34 purple-dwarf, 32 white-tall, and 26 white-dwarf. In Cross B, offspring phenotypes are 92 purple-tall, 8 purple-dwarf, 10 white-tall, and 90 white-dwarf.

The breeder suspects that one of the traits may reduce survival under greenhouse conditions but has not yet identified the exact cause. The breeder also keeps careful records of genotypes when they can be inferred from test crosses.

How to reason:

  1. Translate phenotypes into what they imply about genotype combinations under complete dominance.
  2. For multi-trait crosses, separate the problem into one gene at a time unless linkage is suggested.
  3. Compare observed ratios to ideal expectations (like 9:3:3:1 or 1:1) and ask what could distort them: sampling error, selection, or linkage.
  4. Use the cross design: an F1 × recessive parent is a classic setup to reveal gamete frequencies from the F1.
1. In Cross B, which inference best explains why the two most common phenotypes are purple-tall and white-dwarf?
A. The recessive parent contributes gametes that determine both traits in the offspring.
B. Independent assortment guarantees that two phenotypes will always dominate in an F1 × recessive cross.
C. The F1 parent is producing mainly parental gametes, suggesting the two loci are linked or that recombinants have reduced viability.
D. The white allele is dominant over purple in the test cross.
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Explanation
Cross B is an F1 crossed to a double-recessive parent, so each offspring phenotype directly reports which gamete the F1 produced. If loci assort independently with equal survival, the four phenotypes should be close to 1:1:1:1. Instead, the two parental combinations dominate (purple-tall and white-dwarf), while the recombinant combinations are rare. That pattern is classic for linkage (few crossovers) and can be amplified if recombinant phenotypes have reduced survival. Choice B is wrong because the recessive parent contributes the same recessive alleles every time; it cannot create the skew toward parental combinations. Choice C is wrong because independent assortment predicts equal frequencies, not two dominant classes. Choice A is wrong because dominance does not flip in a test cross; white cannot become dominant simply because the partner is recessive.
2. A student claims that a cross AaBB × AAbb will produce both dominant and recessive phenotypes for trait A. Which outcome is most consistent with complete dominance?
A. Half of the offspring show the recessive phenotype for trait A, and all show the dominant phenotype for trait B.
B. A 9:3:3:1 phenotypic ratio is expected because there are two genes.
C. All offspring show the dominant phenotype for both traits.
D. All offspring show the recessive phenotype for trait A, and half show the dominant phenotype for trait B.
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Explanation
Treat each gene separately. For the A locus: Aa × AA produces AA or Aa, but never aa, so all offspring show the dominant A phenotype. For the B locus: BB × bb produces only Bb, so all offspring show the dominant B phenotype. Therefore 100% of offspring are dominant for both traits. Choice A’s 1/2 recessive A outcome would require an a allele from the second parent, but that parent is AA. Choice C incorrectly mixes loci; the B locus is fixed by BB × bb, so it cannot produce a 1:2:1 or 1:1 outcome. Choice D is the dihybrid heterozygote × heterozygote ratio, which does not apply here.
3. If Cross A were governed purely by independent assortment and no selection, which observed feature would most strongly argue that the data are not just random sampling noise?
A. The fact that purple-tall is the most common phenotype.
B. The presence of any white-tall offspring at all.
C. A systematic deficit of one genotype class across repeated trials, consistent with selection against a specific allele combination.
D. Any deviation from exactly 9:3:3:1 in a sample of 200 offspring.
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Explanation
In a sample of 200, small deviations from 9:3:3:1 are expected. What argues against mere sampling noise is a consistent pattern—especially if one class is repeatedly depleted across repeats or conditions—because that suggests selection (differential survival) or a systematic inheritance effect. Choice B is wrong because white-tall can occur under independent assortment. Choice C is wrong because the most common phenotype being purple-tall is expected even under 9:3:3:1. Choice A is wrong because expecting an exact 9:3:3:1 in finite samples is unrealistic.
4. In Cross B (F1 × white-dwarf), which additional measurement would best distinguish between linkage and reduced survival of recombinant phenotypes?
A. Score genotypes among early embryos or seeds before differential survival can act, then compare to the later phenotype counts.
B. Increase the number of offspring to exactly 10,000 so the ratio must become 1:1:1:1.
C. Repeat Cross A instead of Cross B because dihybrid crosses are required to detect linkage.
D. Measure the height of the parental plants rather than the offspring.
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Explanation
If you measure genotype frequencies before survival differences accumulate (for example, at the seed or early embryo stage), you can see whether recombinants were produced at the expected frequency but then died off. If early-stage genotypes are already skewed, linkage is implicated; if early-stage genotypes are closer to 1:1:1:1 but later phenotypes skew, viability selection is implicated. Choice B is wrong because larger samples reduce sampling error but do not force a particular ratio if biology (linkage/selection) drives a skew. Choice C is wrong because Cross B is exactly the cross that reveals gamete frequencies from the heterozygote. Choice D is irrelevant to distinguishing the mechanisms.
5. Suppose the breeder performs a classic test cross on an unknown purple-tall plant and obtains 100% purple offspring (ignoring height). What is the most likely genotype at the flower-color locus?
A. Hemizygous.
B. Homozygous recessive.
C. Heterozygous dominant.
D. Homozygous dominant.
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Explanation
A test cross pairs the unknown with a homozygous recessive. If 100% of offspring show the dominant phenotype for flower color, the unknown parent must contribute only the dominant allele at that locus, meaning it is homozygous dominant. Choice B would produce a 1:1 mix of dominant and recessive phenotypes in the offspring. Choice C would produce 100% recessive phenotype offspring. Choice D (hemizygous) applies to sex chromosomes in males, not an autosomal flower-color locus in pea plants.
Passage 2
Passage 2: Counseling Session for an X-Linked Disorder

A genetic counselor is asked to estimate risks for an X-linked recessive bleeding disorder in a family. The disorder allele is carried on the X chromosome, and affected individuals have impaired clotting.

Individual I-1 is an unaffected male. Individual I-2 is an unaffected female whose brother is affected. Neither parent in generation I has a known diagnosis, but no genetic testing has been done. The couple (I-1 and I-2) has two children: an unaffected daughter (II-1) and an affected son (II-2).

Years later, the unaffected daughter (II-1) plans to have children with an unaffected male partner. She wants a risk estimate without genetic testing. In a separate branch of the family, an affected male cousin is considering having children with an unaffected female partner with no family history of the disorder.

Assume random X-chromosome segregation, no new mutations, and that carriers are clinically unaffected.

How to reason:

  1. For X-linked recessive traits, start by identifying who must carry the affected allele based on affected sons (sons get their X from their mother).
  2. Convert each individual’s status into possible genotypes, then compute offspring probabilities by tracking which parent contributes which sex chromosome.
  3. Keep sons and daughters separate: they have different genotype spaces because males are hemizygous.
6. Given that I-2 has an affected son (II-2) with an unaffected father, what must be true about I-2?
A. She cannot carry the allele because she is clinically unaffected.
B. She must have inherited the affected allele from her father.
C. She must carry the affected allele on one of her X chromosomes.
D. She must be affected because carriers cannot have affected sons.
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Explanation
For an X-linked recessive disorder, an affected son must have received the affected X from his mother because his father contributes a Y chromosome to sons. Therefore, I-2 must carry the affected allele on one X chromosome. Choice B is wrong because I-2 could have inherited the allele from either parent; the father does not uniquely determine this. Choice C is wrong because clinical normality is consistent with being a carrier. Choice A is wrong because carriers can absolutely have affected sons if they transmit the affected X.
7. Without genetic testing, what is the most reasonable probability that II-1 is a carrier?
A. 1/4
B. 1/2
C. 1
D. 0
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Explanation
I-2 is an obligate carrier because she has an affected son. A daughter receives a normal X from her unaffected father and then has a 50% chance of receiving the affected X from her carrier mother. Carriers are unaffected, so the daughter’s unaffected status does not eliminate the carrier possibility. Hence the probability is 1/2. Choice A (0) would only be true if the mother were not a carrier, which contradicts the affected son. Choice C (1/4) would apply in some situations when a mother has a carrier risk rather than being obligate. Choice D (1) would require certainty that the mother transmitted the affected X, which we do not have.
8. If II-1 (from the passage) has a child with an unaffected male partner, what is the probability that a randomly chosen son will be affected?
A. 1/8
B. 1/2
C. 0
D. 1/4
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Explanation
First, II-1 has a 1/2 chance of being a carrier. If she is a carrier, each son has a 1/2 chance of inheriting her affected X and being affected. Multiply: (1/2) × (1/2) = 1/4. Choice A (1/2) ignores the uncertainty that II-1 is a carrier. Choice C (1/8) would be appropriate if II-1 had only a 1/4 carrier probability, which is not the case here. Choice D (0) would require that II-1 cannot be a carrier, which is false.
9. In the separate branch, an affected male cousin has children with an unaffected female partner with no family history. Assuming the partner is not a carrier, which statement is most accurate?
A. All sons will be carriers, and all daughters will be affected.
B. All daughters will be carriers, and no sons will be affected.
C. Half of the daughters will be affected because they inherit an affected X from their father.
D. Half of the sons will be affected, and half of the daughters will be carriers.
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Explanation
An affected male has genotype XᵈY. If his partner is not a carrier (XᴺXᴺ), all daughters receive Xᵈ from their father and Xᴺ from their mother, making them carriers; all sons receive Y from their father and Xᴺ from their mother, so none are affected. Choice B incorrectly assigns a 50% affected-son risk, which would require a carrier mother. Choice C is wrong because sons cannot be carriers for an X-linked recessive trait; they either have the affected allele (affected) or they do not. Choice D is wrong because daughters would need two affected alleles to be affected; they receive only one affected X from the father in this scenario.
10. Which scenario would increase the prevalence of an X-linked recessive disorder over generations most directly through a non-Mendelian population effect?
A. Nonrandom mating that increases the chance carriers mate within a subgroup.
B. A single meiosis event that produces crossing over between X and Y chromosomes in a male.
C. A silent point mutation in an unrelated autosomal gene.
D. Complete dominance of the affected allele.
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Explanation
Nonrandom mating within a subgroup increases the probability that carriers mate with carriers, which raises the proportion of affected offspring over generations even without changing dominance relationships. This is a population-level deviation from random mating. Choice B describes an atypical event; crossing over between X and Y in males is limited to pseudoautosomal regions and does not generally drive prevalence. Choice C is unrelated to the disorder locus and would not increase its prevalence. Choice D is a misunderstanding: complete dominance would change phenotype expression patterns, not inherently increase allele prevalence for a recessive disorder.
Passage 3
Passage 3: Building a Genetic Map from Recombination Data

A lab studies three linked loci on the same autosome in a model insect. The loci affect easily scored traits: wing shape (W), eye color (E), and body banding (B). Two true-breeding lines are crossed to form F1 heterozygotes that carry one chromosome with W E B and the homolog with w e b.

To map the loci, researchers perform a test cross between an F1 heterozygote and a triple-recessive insect (w e b / w e b). Offspring phenotypes reflect the gametes produced by the F1 parent. A total of 1000 offspring are classified into eight phenotype categories corresponding to parental types, single crossovers, and double crossovers.

The counts are: 410 W E B, 400 w e b, 70 W e b, 60 w E B, 25 W E b, 20 w e B, 8 W e B, and 7 w E b.

Assume no chromatid interference and that recombination frequency is approximately additive across adjacent intervals.

How to reason:

  1. Identify the parental classes first: they are typically the most frequent.
  2. Identify the double crossover classes next: they are typically the least frequent; compare them to parental classes to deduce the middle gene.
  3. Compute each interval’s recombination frequency by adding the relevant single crossovers plus double crossovers, then divide by total offspring.
  4. Map units are approximately percent recombination: 1% ≈ 1 cM.
11. Which two phenotype classes are most likely to represent the parental (nonrecombinant) gametes produced by the F1 parent?
A. W E b and w e B.
B. W e B and w E b.
C. W e b and w E B.
D. W E B and w e b.
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Explanation
In a test cross, offspring phenotypes mirror the gametes produced by the heterozygous parent. The most frequent classes are typically the parental (nonrecombinant) gametes because recombination is less common than no recombination. Here, 410 W E B and 400 w e b are the largest counts, so they represent parental types. Choice B lists two rare classes that fit double crossovers rather than parental types. Choice C lists intermediate-frequency classes consistent with single crossovers. Choice D lists the clear recombinants between loci.
12. Based on the least frequent classes, which gene is most likely in the middle of the three-locus order?
A. E (eye color).
B. The middle gene cannot be determined without knowing the parental genotypes.
C. W (wing shape).
D. B (body banding).
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Explanation
The least frequent classes (8 W e B and 7 w E b) are most consistent with double crossovers. In double crossovers, the outside genes match the parental arrangement, while the middle gene flips. Comparing W E B (parental) to W e B (double crossover), only E changes, so E must be in the middle. Choice A and Choice B place the wrong gene in the middle and would not explain why only one locus differs between parental and double-crossover classes. Choice D is incorrect because the parental genotypes are explicitly described.
13. Using the data, what is the approximate recombination frequency between W and E?
A. About 30%.
B. About 6%.
C. About 1.5%.
D. About 15%.
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Explanation
With order W–E–B, recombinants for the W–E interval are those with W e _ or w E _. Add the single crossovers in that interval (70 W e b and 60 w E B) plus the double crossovers (8 W e B and 7 w E b): 70 + 60 + 8 + 7 = 145. Divide by 1000 total offspring to get 14.5%, about 15%. Choice B (6%) is closer to the E–B interval in this dataset. Choice C (1.5%) is too small and would correspond to only ~15 recombinants. Choice A (30%) overestimates and would require ~300 recombinants.
14. Using the data, what is the approximate map distance between E and B?
A. 20%
B. 14%
C. 6%
D. 2.5%
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Explanation
For the E–B interval, recombinants are W E b (25) and w e B (20) plus double crossovers (8 and 7). Total = 25 + 20 + 8 + 7 = 60, so recombination frequency is 60/1000 = 6%. Choice A (2.5%) would correspond to ~25 recombinants and ignores the double crossovers. Choice C (14%) is closer to the W–E interval. Choice D (20%) is inconsistent with the small number of recombinant classes observed.
15. If a fourth gene X were found to be 2 cM to the right of B (same chromosome), what would be the best estimate of recombination frequency between E and X?
A. Approximately 8%.
B. Approximately 50%.
C. Approximately 18%.
D. Approximately 4%.
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Explanation
If X is 2 cM to the right of B, and E–B is about 6 cM, then E–X is approximately 6 + 2 = 8 cM under additivity. That corresponds to roughly 8% recombination for modest distances. Choice A (4%) would be too small unless B–X or E–B were about 2 cM each. Choice B (50%) is the upper limit for unlinked loci and does not fit a mapped distance of only 8 cM. Choice C (18%) would require a much larger distance.
Passage 4
Passage 4: Testing Hardy–Weinberg Assumptions in a Coastal Town

A coastal town is surveyed for an autosomal recessive condition. Individuals with the condition have genotype aa and show a clear phenotype from birth. Over several years, clinicians record that about 1 in 2500 newborns is affected.

A public health team wants to estimate how many individuals are carriers (Aa) to decide whether to offer voluntary screening. They also interview residents and learn that the town’s population has recently increased due to immigration from a nearby region, and that a small group within the town tends to marry within the group.

In addition, a new treatment introduced 15 years ago allows many affected individuals to survive to reproductive age and have children.

Assume the condition is rare, the gene has only two alleles (A and a), and that the newborn prevalence is representative of the town’s current allele frequencies.

How to reason:

  1. For a recessive condition, the affected frequency is q². Take the square root to get q, then compute p = 1 − q.
  2. Carrier frequency is 2pq; when q is small, 2pq is close to 2q but don’t assume—calculate.
  3. After computing expected genotype frequencies, evaluate which Hardy–Weinberg criteria might be violated: random mating, large population, no migration, no selection, no mutation.
16. If 1 in 2500 newborns is affected (aa), what is the best estimate of q (frequency of allele a)?
A. 0.0004
B. 0.98
C. 0.04
D. 0.02
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Explanation
For an autosomal recessive condition, affected frequency equals q². If q² = 1/2500 = 0.0004, then q = √0.0004 = 0.02. Choice B is q², not q. Choice C (0.04) would correspond to q² = 0.0016 (1/625). Choice D is p, not q.
17. Using the estimate above, what is the approximate carrier frequency (Aa) under Hardy–Weinberg assumptions?
A. About 4%.
B. About 96%.
C. About 2%.
D. About 0.04%.
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Explanation
Compute p = 1 − q = 0.98. Carrier frequency is 2pq = 2(0.98)(0.02) ≈ 0.0392, about 4%. Choice A (0.04%) confuses carriers with affected frequency. Choice C (2%) would be close to 2q only if p ≈ 0.5, which is not true here. Choice D (96%) is far too high and roughly corresponds to p².
18. Which observation from the interviews most directly violates the Hardy–Weinberg requirement of random mating?
A. The condition is rare.
B. Immigration from a nearby region.
C. A treatment allows affected individuals to survive longer.
D. A subgroup tends to marry within the subgroup.
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Explanation
Random mating is violated when individuals preferentially mate within a subgroup, which increases homozygosity relative to Hardy–Weinberg expectations. Choice B (immigration) violates the no-migration criterion, not random mating. Choice C (improved survival) relates to selection, not random mating. Choice D is irrelevant; rarity does not violate Hardy–Weinberg.
19. How would improved survival of affected individuals most likely affect allele frequencies over time, all else equal?
A. It has no possible effect on q because allele frequencies never change after birth.
B. It reduces negative selection against a, tending to increase q compared with the pre-treatment era.
C. It forces q to decrease because affected individuals must now be removed from the population.
D. It increases mutation rate at the locus, directly changing q.
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Explanation
If affected individuals (aa) previously had reduced reproductive success, the treatment reduces that disadvantage. That weakens selection against allele a, so q can increase (or at least decline less) over time compared with the era without treatment. Choice B reverses the logic of selection. Choice C is wrong because allele frequencies can change across generations. Choice D confuses selection with mutation.
20. Suppose screening finds fewer carriers than predicted by Hardy–Weinberg. Which explanation best fits that direction of discrepancy?
A. The allele must be dominant, not recessive.
B. Selection against heterozygotes (Aa) or nonrandom sampling that undercounts carriers.
C. Migration always increases the number of carriers relative to Hardy–Weinberg.
D. q² must be larger than q, so the math must have been wrong.
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Explanation
Fewer carriers than predicted could occur if carriers are undercounted (sampling bias) or if there is selection against heterozygotes, which would reduce 2pq relative to Hardy–Weinberg. Choice B is wrong because migration can increase or decrease carrier frequency depending on incoming allele frequencies; it is not directionally fixed. Choice C is wrong because dominance changes phenotype mapping, not the algebra already based on a recessive phenotype. Choice D reflects a misunderstanding of the relationship between q and q².
Passage 5
Passage 5: Combining Mapping and Population Data in Conservation Genetics

A conservation team monitors an endangered mammal that lives in two valleys separated by a mountain ridge. Valley East has 800 animals; Valley West has 120 animals. A recessive coat phenotype is observed and is suspected to be caused by allele r at a single locus (R is dominant).

In Valley West, 3 of 120 animals show the recessive phenotype. In Valley East, 2 of 800 animals show the recessive phenotype. The team considers relocating 20 animals from East to West to increase genetic diversity.

Separately, geneticists identify a DNA marker locus (M/m) near the coat locus by doing a controlled breeding study. In 500 informative offspring from a test-cross-like design, marker and coat phenotype show 45 recombinant individuals.

Assume random mating within each valley before relocation, the recessive phenotype is fully penetrant, and the marker and coat loci are on the same chromosome.

How to reason:

  1. Within each valley, estimate q from recessive phenotype frequency (q²) and then compute carriers (2pq).
  2. Relocation changes allele frequencies via migration (gene flow); combine allele counts from each valley to predict the new q in West.
  3. Marker recombination frequency estimates genetic distance: recombinant fraction ≈ percent recombination, capped near 50% for unlinked loci.
21. Before relocation, which valley has the higher estimated frequency of allele r?
A. Both valleys must have the same r frequency because they are the same species.
B. Valley West.
C. Cannot be determined without knowing carrier counts directly.
D. Valley East.
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Explanation
Recessive phenotype frequency equals q². Valley West has 3/120 = 0.025, giving q ≈ √0.025 ≈ 0.158. Valley East has 2/800 = 0.0025, giving q = √0.0025 = 0.05. Therefore Valley West has the higher allele r frequency. Choice B is wrong because East’s recessive phenotype frequency is much lower. Choice C ignores population structure; the same species can have different allele frequencies in different subpopulations. Choice D is wrong because recessive phenotype frequency provides q² directly.
22. Under Hardy–Weinberg assumptions, what is the best estimate of carrier frequency (Rr) in Valley East?
A. About 50%.
B. About 9.5%.
C. About 2.5%.
D. About 0.25%.
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Explanation
In Valley East, q² = 0.0025, so q = 0.05 and p = 0.95. Carrier frequency is 2pq = 2(0.95)(0.05) = 0.095, or 9.5%. Choice B (0.25%) confuses q with q². Choice C (2.5%) is q² expressed as a percent. Choice D (50%) would require q near 0.5, which is inconsistent with the rare recessive phenotype.
23. If 20 animals are moved from East to West and mating becomes random in West, what is the most direct population-genetic effect this intervention is intended to reduce in West?
A. Genetic drift and inbreeding due to small population size.
B. Complete dominance of the R allele.
C. Mutation rate at the coat locus.
D. Crossing over frequency between the coat locus and the marker.
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Explanation
Valley West is small, so chance fluctuations and mating among relatives can strongly change allele frequencies and increase homozygosity. Bringing in individuals from a larger population increases genetic diversity and counters genetic drift/inbreeding. Choice B is wrong because relocation does not directly change mutation rate. Choice C is wrong because recombination frequency is a property of meiosis and physical distance, not population size. Choice D is wrong because dominance is a trait of allele expression, not something migration reduces.
24. The marker–coat data show 45 recombinants out of 500 informative offspring. What is the best estimate of the distance between the marker and coat loci?
A. About 4.5 cM.
B. About 45 cM.
C. About 90 cM.
D. About 9 cM.
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Explanation
Recombinant fraction = 45/500 = 0.09, or 9%. For modest distances, percent recombination is approximately map distance in centimorgans, so the loci are about 9 cM apart. Choice A (4.5 cM) would match 22–23 recombinants. Choice C (45 cM) misreads 45 recombinants as 45%. Choice D (90 cM) is not meaningful because recombination frequency does not exceed ~50% for unlinked loci.
25. If the marker and coat loci were actually unlinked, which outcome would be most expected in a very large test-cross-like dataset?
A. All offspring would match one of the two parental classes.
B. Recombinant frequency would equal q² for the recessive coat allele.
C. Approximately half of offspring would be recombinant with respect to marker and coat phenotype.
D. Recombinant frequency would approach 0% as sample size grows.
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Explanation
If loci are unlinked (different chromosomes or far apart), recombination approaches 50% in large datasets because marker and trait assort independently. Choice B would describe very tight linkage, not unlinked loci. Choice C confuses linkage with allele frequency of the recessive trait. Choice D describes zero recombination, again implying tight linkage.
