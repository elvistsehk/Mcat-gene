MCAT Biology — Lymphatic System (Ch. 8.4)
Standalone Questions (Q1–Q15)
1. A patient has a long-standing low plasma albumin concentration. Which change best explains why the lymphatic system becomes especially important for preventing swelling in this patient?
A. Increased hydrostatic pressure at the venule end of capillaries drives more fluid into blood
B. Higher oncotic pressure at the venule end pulls more fluid into tissues
C. Reduced oncotic pressure decreases reabsorption at the venule end, leaving more fluid in tissues that must be drained by lymphatics
D. Lymphatic vessels convert interstitial fluid into plasma proteins to restore oncotic pressure
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: C.

Why: Albumin is a major contributor to plasma oncotic pressure. When oncotic pressure falls, less water is pulled back into capillaries at the venule end, so more fluid remains in tissues. Lymphatics must drain this excess to limit edema.

Other choices:

  • A: Reabsorption happens at the venule end when hydrostatic pressure has dropped; “driving fluid into blood” is not a direct effect of higher hydrostatic pressure.
  • B: Oncotic pressure pulls fluid into vessels, not tissues.
  • D: Lymphatics return fluid to blood but do not synthesize plasma proteins.
2. Most lymph from the body ultimately enters the bloodstream at which location?
A. Left subclavian vein via a large duct in the posterior chest
B. Right atrium through direct openings in lymph nodes
C. Hepatic portal vein after filtration by the spleen
D. Pulmonary veins after oxygenation in the lungs
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A.

Why: Lymphatic vessels merge into larger trunks and commonly form a major duct in the posterior chest that empties into the left subclavian vein near the heart.

Other choices:

  • B: Lymph returns through venous circulation, not direct atrial openings from lymph nodes.
  • C: The portal vein carries blood from the GI tract to the liver; lymph is separate from portal blood flow.
  • D: Pulmonary veins carry oxygenated blood from lungs, not lymphatic fluid.
3. Compared with arteries and veins, lymphatic vessels are best characterized by which property?
A. They are high-pressure tubes driven by a central muscular pump
B. They are bidirectional conduits that rapidly equalize pressure between tissues
C. They are closed-loop circuits that return fluid to the arterial end of capillaries
D. They are one-way vessels that enlarge toward the body’s center and rely on valves and external forces for flow
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: D.

Why: Lymphatics form a one-way drainage network that becomes larger toward the center of the body. Because pressure is low, flow depends on valves plus tissue/skeletal muscle movement.

Other choices:

  • A: There is no central pump like the heart for lymph.
  • B: Lymph flow is not bidirectional.
  • C: Lymph returns to venous circulation near the heart, not to arterial capillary ends.
4. A lymph node is removed during surgery. Which feature is most directly lost at that site?
A. The ability of blood to exchange gases with tissues
B. A regional meeting point where antigen-presenting cells and lymphocytes interact to mount immune responses
C. The primary location of red blood cell synthesis in adults
D. The main organ that secretes antibodies into the bloodstream
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: B.

Why: Lymph nodes filter lymph and provide a space for immune communication, including antigen presentation and B-cell activation/proliferation (germinal centers).

Other choices:

  • A: Gas exchange occurs at capillary beds in tissues and lungs, not in lymph nodes.
  • C: Red blood cells are produced in bone marrow, not lymph nodes.
  • D: Antibodies are produced by plasma cells (activated B-cells), which can be generated in lymph nodes, but lymph nodes are not “the main organ that secretes antibodies.”
5. A patient develops swelling of an arm after removal of several axillary lymph nodes. Which immediate physiologic change most directly causes the swelling?
A. Reduced drainage of interstitial fluid from tissues into lymphatic vessels
B. Increased plasma oncotic pressure that pulls water out of capillaries
C. Increased venous return that raises capillary hydrostatic pressure at the venule end
D. Enhanced filtration of lymph into tissues at the lymph node
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A.

Why: Removing lymph nodes and associated vessels can obstruct lymph flow, so excess interstitial fluid cannot be returned efficiently to the bloodstream. The result is lymphedema.

Other choices:

  • B: Higher oncotic pressure would favor reabsorption into blood, opposing edema.
  • C: This does not describe the typical post-lymph node removal mechanism; the core issue is impaired lymph drainage.
  • D: Lymph nodes do not “filter lymph into tissues” as a primary function; lymph flows through them.
6. A newborn has milky, lipid-rich fluid in intestinal lymphatic channels after a meal. Which structure is directly responsible for initial entry of dietary fats into the lymphatic system?
A. Splenic cords of the red pulp
B. Pancreatic ducts draining digestive enzymes
C. Hepatic sinusoids that filter blood from the GI tract
D. Lacteals located in the centers of intestinal villi
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: D.

Why: Lacteals are specialized lymphatic capillaries within intestinal villi that take up chylomicrons (fat packages) to form lipid-rich lymph.

Other choices:

  • A: The spleen filters blood; it does not collect dietary chylomicrons from the intestine.
  • B: Pancreatic ducts deliver enzymes to the duodenum; they are not lymphatic entry points.
  • C: Hepatic sinusoids handle blood from the portal vein; most large fat packages enter lymph first.
7. A clinician describes that lymph nodes contain a lymphatic channel plus their own artery and vein. Which implication follows most directly from this anatomy?
A. Lymph nodes pump lymph forward using smooth muscle contractions like the heart
B. Immune cells can enter and exit lymph nodes via both lymphatic flow and blood circulation
C. Lymph nodes produce albumin to restore plasma oncotic pressure
D. Lymph nodes oxygenate lymph to match arterial oxygen content
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: B.

Why: With both lymphatic channels and blood vessels, lymph nodes serve as hubs where leukocytes can traffic between the circulation and lymph to coordinate immune surveillance.

Other choices:

  • A: Lymph nodes do not act as a central pump.
  • C: Albumin is synthesized mainly by the liver, not lymph nodes.
  • D: Oxygenation is the function of lungs and blood; lymph nodes are for filtration and immune interaction.
8. A patient has obstruction of lymph flow from a lower-limb lymph node. Which downstream effect is most likely?
A. Decreased arterial pressure due to loss of red blood cells into lymph
B. Immediate inability of neutrophils to perform chemotaxis in infected tissue
C. Accumulation of interstitial fluid and proteins upstream with swelling of tissues drained by that node
D. Increased formation of chylomicrons in intestinal mucosal cells
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: C.

Why: Lymphatic obstruction prevents return of fluid that normally remains in tissues after capillary exchange. Fluid and macromolecules accumulate upstream, causing lymphedema.

Other choices:

  • A: Lymph obstruction primarily affects interstitial fluid balance, not direct red blood cell loss.
  • B: Chemotaxis is guided by local gradients; lymph blockage does not instantly abolish neutrophil movement.
  • D: Chylomicron formation depends on intestinal absorption and packaging, not a limb lymph node’s patency.

Correct Answer: D.

Why: Lymphatic vessels can carry cells as well as fluid; many cancers spread through lymphatic drainage pathways, making regional nodes important for staging and management.

Other choices:

10. A medical student explains that lymph provides a secondary circulation for fluid that leaves the blood at capillaries. Which comparison best captures how lymphatic return differs from venous return?
A. Lymphatic return uses high intraluminal pressure generated by a muscular ventricle
B. Lymphatic return depends more on one-way valves and external compression because pressure is low
C. Lymphatic return is fastest in arteries because arterial walls are thick and elastic
D. Lymphatic return primarily delivers oxygen to tissues that are poorly perfused by blood
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: B.

Why: Lymphatics are low-pressure, one-way vessels. Valves prevent backflow, and movement relies on muscle contraction, respiration, and tissue compression rather than a central pump.

Other choices:

  • A: The heart pumps blood, not lymph.
  • C: Arteries carry blood away from the heart; lymph is a separate drainage system.
  • D: Oxygen delivery is primarily via blood and hemoglobin.

15. A patient has recurrent skin infections in an area with chronic lymphedema. Which explanation best links lymphatic dysfunction to impaired local immune defense?

Passages (3 passages × 3 questions)
Passage 1: Post-Surgical Swelling and Fluid Balance

A 55-year-old patient undergoes surgery for breast cancer that includes removal of several regional lymph nodes. Two months later, she reports progressive heaviness and swelling of the ipsilateral arm. On exam, the skin is taut and pitting is minimal. She notes that small scratches on that arm often become red and take longer than usual to resolve.

Laboratory tests show normal kidney function and normal cardiac function. Plasma albumin is within the reference range. Ultrasound shows no evidence of deep vein thrombosis. The clinician explains that, during normal capillary exchange, fluid leaves the arterial end of capillaries due to hydrostatic forces and much of it returns at the venule end due to oncotic forces, but a small remainder stays in tissues.

The clinician further explains that one-way lymphatic vessels normally drain this residual interstitial fluid and return it toward central venous circulation. She adds that lymph nodes serve as regional sites where immune cells and antigen-presenting cells can communicate, and that altering lymph flow can affect local immune surveillance.

The patient asks whether the swelling is related to her surgery, why it persists even when she elevates the arm, and why she seems to get more frequent mild skin infections in that region.

How to reason:

  1. Rule out common causes: normal heart/kidney function and no DVT makes “back-up” less likely; think impaired lymph drainage.
  2. Normal albumin means oncotic forces are intact; persistent swelling suggests a mechanical obstruction to fluid return.
  3. Post-node removal can interrupt pathways, leading to lymphedema upstream of the disruption.
  4. Minimal pitting supports protein-rich fluid accumulation typical of chronic lymph issues.
  5. More local infections can follow reduced antigen delivery and reduced immune cell interaction in regional lymph nodes.
16. Which mechanism best explains why this patient’s arm swelling persists despite normal albumin levels and the absence of venous thrombosis?
A. Lymphatic outflow disruption prevents return of residual interstitial fluid to central venous circulation
B. Excessive oncotic pressure drives fluid out of capillaries into tissues at the venule end
C. Increased arterial oxygen delivery causes expansion of interstitial volume
D. Autoimmune destruction of red blood cells reduces plasma volume and concentrates tissue fluid
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A.

Why: With lymphatic pathways disrupted by node removal, the small amount of fluid that normally remains in tissues cannot be drained effectively. This causes chronic fluid and protein accumulation (lymphedema) even when albumin and venous flow are normal.

Other choices:

  • B: Oncotic pressure pulls fluid into vessels, not out into tissues.
  • C: Oxygen delivery is unrelated to persistent interstitial fluid accumulation in this context.
  • D: The scenario points to mechanical disruption of lymph flow, not hemolysis-driven volume changes.
17. The patient’s reduced tendency for pitting and the chronic nature of swelling are most consistent with accumulation of which component in the interstitial space?
A. Pure water without solutes, rapidly equilibrating across capillaries
B. Carbon dioxide stored in tissues due to reduced perfusion
C. Oxygen bound to hemoglobin that leaks through capillary walls
D. Protein-rich interstitial fluid that is normally cleared through lymphatic drainage
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: D.

Why: Lymphatics clear excess interstitial fluid and macromolecules. When lymph drainage is blocked, proteins accumulate in tissues and can increase interstitial oncotic effects and fibrosis, contributing to chronic, less-pitting swelling.

Other choices:

  • A: Pure water shifts easily and typically produces more pitting edema than chronic, protein-rich lymphedema.
  • B/C: These are not central drivers of lymph-obstruction swelling patterns.
18. Which explanation best accounts for the patient’s increased frequency of mild skin infections on the affected arm?
A. Lymphatic obstruction increases complement activity, which damages local tissues
B. Impaired lymph flow reduces efficient antigen and immune cell trafficking through regional nodes, weakening coordinated immune responses
C. Removal of lymph nodes eliminates all macrophages from the body

❌Incorrect

Correct Answer: B.

Why: Lymph nodes are communication hubs for adaptive responses. When lymph flow is impaired, antigens and immune cells may reach nodes less effectively, diminishing local immune surveillance and coordination.

  • C: Macrophages reside throughout tissues and are not eliminated by removing a few nodes.
  • D: Antibodies are produced by plasma cells; lymphatics transport fluid and cells but are not “antibody-secreting organs.”
Passage 2: Fat Absorption, Chyle, and Lymphatic Flow

A research team studies two patients with chronic diarrhea and unintended weight loss. Patient A reports symptoms that worsen after fatty meals. A stool sample shows increased fat content. Endoscopy reveals normal intestinal mucosa, but imaging suggests dilation of small lymphatic vessels within the intestinal wall.

Patient B has similar stool findings but also reports recurrent pancreatitis and poor digestion of multiple macronutrients. Imaging shows pancreatic duct abnormalities. Both patients show low levels of lipid-rich particles in blood shortly after standardized fatty meals.

The researchers note that, under normal conditions, intestinal mucosal cells package absorbed fats into large particles that enter specialized lymphatic vessels in villi. The lymphatic fluid carrying abundant fat particles appears milky and eventually reaches central venous circulation. The team also notes that nonfat nutrients can enter the portal blood more directly.

Based on these observations, trainees are asked to infer which patient most likely has a primary lymphatic transport problem, which immediate laboratory change would best distinguish a lymphatic blockage from poor enzymatic digestion, and which step in the normal pathway explains the milky appearance of intestinal lymph after a fatty meal.

How to reason:

  1. Fat-specific worsening plus dilated intestinal lymphatic vessels suggests impaired lacteal/lymphatic transport.
  2. Pancreatic disease suggests impaired digestion (enzyme delivery), which affects many nutrients.
  3. Milky lymph after a fatty meal is chyle, caused by lots of chylomicrons in lymph.
  4. If fats are absorbed but not delivered to blood, expect low post-meal blood chylomicrons despite normal mucosa.
  5. Differentiate “can’t break down fats” vs “can’t transport fats” by checking other digestion markers and pancreatic history.

Passage 3: Trauma, a Milky Effusion, and Central Lymph Return

A patient experiences blunt chest trauma in a motor vehicle collision. Over the next day, the patient develops shortness of breath and a pleural effusion. Thoracentesis yields a cloudy, milky fluid with high lipid content. The treating team suspects that a major lymphatic structure was damaged.

The team reviews that lymphatic vessels are one-way channels that become larger toward the center of the body and that many of these channels converge into a large duct in the posterior chest. This duct normally delivers lymph to the venous circulation near the heart. They also review that fat absorbed in the intestine travels first through lymphatic channels, giving intestinal lymph a milky appearance after a fatty meal.

The team considers how damage to this duct could lead to accumulation of lipid-rich lymph in the chest, and they discuss what changes might appear in venous return and peripheral tissue fluid balance if lymph return is disrupted.

Finally, they ask trainees to predict the most likely site where this lymph would normally enter the bloodstream, which physiologic function of the lymphatic system is most directly affected by the injury, and which clinical sign would be expected downstream if lymph return remains compromised.

How to reason:

  1. Milky pleural fluid with high lipids suggests leakage of chyle from a central lymph pathway.
  2. Central convergence in the posterior chest points to a major duct that empties into the venous system.
  3. Identify where lymph normally re-enters blood: near the heart at the left subclavian vein.
  4. Loss of lymph return reduces the body’s ability to return interstitial fluid to the bloodstream, predisposing to edema.
  5. Connect fat transport: if chyle can’t reach blood, expect reduced delivery of dietary fat packages to circulation after meals.
