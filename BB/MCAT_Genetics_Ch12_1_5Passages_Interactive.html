Passage 1
Passage 1: Dominance patterns in a breeding program

Researchers are studying coat texture in a small population of laboratory rodents. The trait has two alleles: R produces rough fur and r produces smooth fur. In this line, R is completely dominant to r. Two rodents with rough fur are crossed, and 25% of the offspring have smooth fur.

The same lab also keeps a flower strain in which red (CR) and white (CW) alleles show incomplete dominance: CRCR flowers are red, CWCW flowers are white, and CRCW flowers are pink. A student accidentally labels a tray of pink flowers as “heterozygous for a dominant allele” and uses them for a cross.

In a separate colony, a surface antigen gene has two dominant alleles that are both expressed in heterozygotes. Individuals can display antigen A, antigen B, both antigens, or neither depending on genotype. Finally, a recessive vision trait is found on the X chromosome; males show the trait whenever their single X carries the recessive allele.

To confirm the rodent locus behaves as expected, the lab performs a test cross between one rough-fur offspring and a smooth-fur rodent. Some rough-fur offspring produce both rough and smooth progeny in this test cross, suggesting their genotype cannot be inferred from phenotype alone.

The student is reminded that a phenotype label like “rough” or “pink” does not automatically imply a specific genotype across all traits; the dominance pattern determines whether heterozygotes are distinguishable from homozygotes.

How to reason:

  1. Translate phenotype to genotype: decide whether dominance is complete, incomplete, or codominant before writing any cross.
  2. Use proportions as constraints: a 25% recessive phenotype from two dominant-phenotype parents strongly implies both parents were heterozygous.
  3. For X-linked traits: remember males are hemizygous, so genotype-to-phenotype mapping is different from autosomes.
  4. Check your assumptions: intermediate phenotypes usually indicate incomplete dominance, not “weak dominance.”
1. Which parental genotypes are most consistent with the rodent cross that produced 25% smooth-fur offspring?
A. RR × RR
B. RR × Rr
C. Rr × Rr
D. Rr × rr
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Explanation

Correct answer: C

A 3:1 dominant:recessive phenotype ratio is the signature of a heterozygote × heterozygote cross under complete dominance.

Why the other options are less appropriate:

  • A: Two homozygous dominant parents cannot produce any recessive (smooth) offspring under complete dominance.
  • B: Heterozygote × heterozygote yields a 3:1 phenotype ratio, matching 25% recessive offspring.
  • D: This cross would yield ~50% smooth offspring, not 25%.
2. The student crosses two pink flowers from the incomplete-dominance strain. Which offspring phenotype distribution is most likely?
A. All red
B. 3 red : 1 white
C. 1 red : 2 pink : 1 white
D. 9 red : 3 pink : 3 white : 1 mixed
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Explanation

Correct answer: C

In incomplete dominance, the heterozygote has its own phenotype, so CRCW × CRCW produces 1/4 CRCR (red), 1/2 CRCW (pink), and 1/4 CWCW (white).

Why the other options are less appropriate:

  • A: Pink flowers are heterozygotes, so they can produce both red and white alleles in gametes.
  • B: A 3:1 ratio is expected for complete dominance, not incomplete dominance with an intermediate heterozygote.
  • D: A 9:3:3:1 ratio is for a dihybrid cross with independent assortment, not a single-locus incomplete-dominance cross.
3. Two parents each express only antigen A, yet they have a child who expresses both antigens A and B. Which is the best genetic explanation?
A. The child must be homozygous for the B allele, which can occur even if both parents show antigen A.
B. One parent is likely mis-typed or incompletely phenotyped, allowing a hidden B allele to be present despite an A-only label.
C. At least one parent must carry the B allele without showing it, which is possible under complete dominance.
D. Both parents must carry a recessive “no antigen” allele and the child inherited two copies of it.
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Explanation

Correct answer: B

Codominant alleles are both expressed in heterozygotes, so an AB child requires a B allele from a parent. If both parents appear to be A-only, the cleanest explanation is that at least one parent actually carried B (or was mis-typed).

Why the other options are less appropriate:

  • A: If the child were BB, both parents would need to provide a B allele, which would usually affect their own phenotype in a codominant system.
  • C: Complete dominance would hide an allele, but codominance means B should be expressed if present.
  • D: A data/phenotyping issue (or an unrecognized B allele in a parent) is the most parsimonious explanation among the choices.
4. For the X-linked recessive vision trait, which statement most directly explains why affected males are common even when the allele is rare?
A. The Y chromosome usually contains a compensatory dominant allele that masks the X-linked allele.
B. Males undergo crossing over between their X chromosomes, increasing the probability of recessive expression.
C. The allele is dominant in males but recessive in females.
D. Males have only one X chromosome, so a single recessive allele can determine phenotype.
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Explanation

Correct answer: D

Because males are hemizygous for most X-linked genes, they express whichever allele is on their single X chromosome.

Why the other options are less appropriate:

  • A: Hemizygosity means there is no second allele to mask a recessive allele.
  • B: Males do not have two X chromosomes; crossing over is not the core reason for expression.
  • C: Dominance does not typically switch based on sex for classic X-linked recessive traits.
5. Under complete dominance at the rodent fur locus, which pair of different genotypes is guaranteed to produce the same phenotype?
A. RR and rr
B. Rr and rr
C. RR and Rr
D. RR and RR
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Explanation

Correct answer: C

Complete dominance means one dominant allele is sufficient for the dominant phenotype, so RR and Rr look the same.

Why the other options are less appropriate:

  • A: RR is dominant phenotype; rr is recessive phenotype, so they differ.
  • B: Rr shows dominant phenotype while rr shows recessive phenotype, so they differ.
  • D: These are not different genotypes.
Passage 2
Passage 2: Penetrance vs. expressivity in a tumor predisposition syndrome

A clinician follows a large pedigree for an autosomal dominant allele, D, associated with a tumor predisposition syndrome. Genetic testing identifies 250 individuals who carry the allele. Of these, 175 eventually develop at least one tumor by age 50, while the remaining carriers have normal clinical exams through that age.

Among the 175 affected carriers, symptom severity varies widely. Some individuals develop only a single benign skin lesion. Others develop bilateral vestibular tumors with progressive hearing loss, and a few develop cataracts early in life. Environmental exposures and modifier genes are suspected to influence outcomes.

In a counseling session, two siblings who both carry allele D ask whether their children will inevitably show the same disease features. The clinician explains that population-level probability and individual-level presentation are different ideas, even when the underlying genotype is the same.

When the same carriers are examined at age 30, only 120 of the 250 carriers have any detectable abnormality. By age 50, more carriers have developed symptoms, and the clinicians suspect penetrance increases with age in this condition.

In addition, a small subset of carriers is found to have an identical mutation but no clinical findings even after extensive imaging, while their relatives with the same mutation have multiple lesions. The team discusses how to describe these patterns precisely for research papers and counseling.

How to reason:

  1. Separate two concepts: penetrance asks “who shows anything at all,” while expressivity asks “how severe or what form.”
  2. Use the denominator correctly: penetrance is calculated among genotype carriers, not among the whole clinic population.
  3. Look for variability: multiple clinical presentations among carriers points to variable expressivity.
  4. Age matters: penetrance can be age-dependent; a normal exam today may not mean lifelong nonexpression.
6. Based on the follow-up data, what is the penetrance of allele D by age 50?
A. 0.30
B. 0.70
C. 0.80
D. 0.90
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Explanation

Correct answer: B

Penetrance is the proportion of individuals with the genotype who show the phenotype. Here, 175 of 250 carriers are affected, giving 0.70.

Why the other options are less appropriate:

  • A: 30% would correspond to 75 of 250 carriers expressing the phenotype, not 175.
  • C: 80% would correspond to 200 of 250 carriers, which is higher than observed.
  • D: 90% would correspond to 225 of 250 carriers, which is higher than observed.
7. Which observation best supports variable expressivity rather than incomplete penetrance?
A. Different carriers show different tumor types and severities.

C. The allele is found on an autosome rather than a sex chromosome.
D. The allele frequency changes over time in the population.
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Explanation

Correct answer: A

Expressivity concerns differences in phenotype among individuals who do express the trait; the wide range of presentations among affected carriers indicates variable expressivity.

Why the other options are less appropriate:

  • B: Different clinical manifestations among affected carriers is the definition of variable expressivity.
  • C: Chromosome location does not define expressivity.
  • D: Allele frequency is a population genetics concept, not an individual phenotype-variation concept.
8. If additional follow-up showed that many previously unaffected carriers develop tumors after age 50, which interpretation is most consistent?
A. The allele has variable expressivity but constant penetrance.
B. The allele has reduced penetrance that is age-dependent.
C. The allele is actually recessive and was misclassified.
D. The allele must be located on mitochondrial DNA.

❌Incorrect
Explanation

Correct answer: B

Age-dependent penetrance means the probability of expressing the phenotype increases as carriers get older, which fits late-onset disease.

Why the other options are less appropriate:

  • A: Late onset changes whether carriers count as expressing at a given age, so penetrance is not constant.
  • C: Recessiveness is not implied by late onset in carriers.
  • D: Nothing about the pattern implies mitochondrial inheritance.
9. Two siblings both carry allele D. One has mild disease and the other has severe disease. What is the best conclusion about their children’s outcomes?
A. Their children will have identical disease features because genotype determines phenotype completely.
B. Their children’s risk depends only on environmental exposure, not genotype.
C. Their children can inherit the allele with similar probability, but clinical severity may vary even among carriers.
D. Only the more severely affected sibling can transmit the allele.
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Explanation

Correct answer: C

Autosomal dominant alleles can be transmitted regardless of severity, and variable expressivity means carrier children may present differently.

Why the other options are less appropriate:

  • A: Variable expressivity means identical genotypes can yield different phenotypes.
  • B: Genotype still influences risk; environment/modifiers affect expression, not the basic inheritance probability.
  • D: Transmission depends on gametes, not symptom severity.
10. A student calculates penetrance by dividing the number of affected carriers by the total number of people seen in clinic, including noncarriers. What is the main problem with this approach?
A. Penetrance is the same as expressivity, so severity must be scored first.
B. Penetrance can only be measured in animal models, not humans.
C. Penetrance equals allele frequency, so clinic counts are irrelevant.
D. Penetrance should be computed using genotype carriers as the denominator.
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Explanation

Correct answer: D

Penetrance asks: among individuals who carry the allele, what fraction express the phenotype?

Why the other options are less appropriate:

  • A: Penetrance is conditional on genotype; you must restrict the denominator to individuals with the allele.
  • B: Penetrance is routinely estimated in humans when genotypes and phenotypes are known.
  • C: Allele frequency is a population measure; penetrance is genotype-to-phenotype probability.
Passage 3
Passage 3: Mendel’s laws and their meiotic correlates

In a genetics teaching lab, students collect gametes from a heterozygous fruit fly (Aa) and notice that, over many meioses, A and a appear in roughly equal proportions among the gametes. The instructor emphasizes that this is not a coincidence; it reflects how homologous chromosomes behave during meiosis.

In a second exercise, the class tracks two different genes, one on chromosome 2 and one on chromosome 3. When the fly is heterozygous at both loci, the combination of alleles in gametes appears to be independent: knowing which allele was inherited at the first locus does not help predict the allele at the second locus.

Finally, the instructor introduces a twist: two loci are placed on the same chromosome close to one another. Students still observe new allele combinations in a small fraction of gametes. They are told that this arises from a specific prophase I event that physically exchanges segments between homologous chromatids.

During prophase I, homologous chromosomes pair and form tetrads. When chromatids exchange segments, the original allele combinations along a chromosome can be broken up, generating gametes that would not occur from independent assortment alone.

The instructor cautions that observing some recombinants does not automatically mean genes are on different chromosomes; a low recombinant fraction can reflect linkage plus occasional crossing over.

How to reason:

  1. Map laws to meiosis: segregation aligns with separation of homologs; independent assortment aligns with random orientation of homolog pairs.
  2. Distinguish homologs vs sister chromatids: meiosis I separates homologs; meiosis II separates sister chromatids.
  3. Use “independence” carefully: unlinked genes behave independently; linked genes can be reshuffled by crossing over but not at 50% unless far apart.
  4. Identify the mechanism: recombination requires physical exchange during prophase I.
11. Which meiotic event most directly explains Mendel’s law of segregation?
A. Alignment of chromosomes at the metaphase plate during mitosis
B. Separation of homologous chromosomes during anaphase I
C. Replication of DNA during S phase
D. Separation of sister chromatids during anaphase I
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Explanation

Correct answer: B

Segregation refers to the separation of the two alleles carried on homologous chromosomes into different gametes, which occurs when homologs separate in anaphase I.

Why the other options are less appropriate:

  • A: Homologs separate in anaphase I, so each gamete receives only one allele per locus.
  • C: Replication duplicates alleles; it does not segregate them into different gametes.
  • D: Sister chromatids separate mainly in anaphase II; anaphase I separates homologs.
12. Random orientation of homologous chromosome pairs that produces independent assortment is established primarily during which stage?
A. Prophase II
B. Anaphase II
C. Metaphase I
D. Telophase I
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Explanation

Correct answer: C

Independent assortment comes from the random way each homolog pair aligns in metaphase I, making allele inheritance at different chromosomes independent.

Why the other options are less appropriate:

  • A: Prophase II occurs after homologs have already separated; it does not set pair orientation.
  • B: In metaphase I, homolog pairs line up randomly at the metaphase plate, determining segregation patterns.
  • D: By telophase I, the orientation decision has already been made.
13. Two loci on the same chromosome produce 8% recombinant gametes. Which statement best accounts for the existence of these recombinants?
A. The loci are linked, but crossing over between homologous chromatids occasionally separates them.
B. The loci assort independently because they are on different chromosomes.
C. The loci are linked, and linkage guarantees 0% recombination.
D. Recombinants are created only by mutation, not meiosis
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Explanation

Correct answer: A

Linked genes can still be separated by crossing over during prophase I, producing a minority of recombinant gametes; the small percentage suggests the loci are close together.

Why the other options are less appropriate:

  • B: Crossing over in prophase I can exchange segments and create recombinant chromatids, even for linked loci.
  • C: Linkage reduces recombination frequency but does not eliminate it in general.
  • D: Meiotic recombination creates new allele combinations without mutation.
14. If crossing over were experimentally prevented during prophase I, what would be the most immediate effect on gametes from a heterozygous individual?
A. An increase in the number of Barr bodies in female offspring
B. A complete loss of independent assortment between genes on different chromosomes
C. A decrease in the variety of allele combinations for linked loci on the same chromosome
D. A switch from diploid to haploid gamete production
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Explanation

Correct answer: C

Crossing over specifically increases diversity by creating recombinant chromatids, most noticeably affecting linked loci.

Why the other options are less appropriate:

  • A: Without crossing over, linked alleles tend to stay together, reducing recombinant haplotypes.
  • B: Independent assortment depends on metaphase I orientation, not crossing over.
  • D: Meiosis produces haploid gametes regardless of crossing over.
15. The observation that A and a appear in approximately equal proportions among gametes from an Aa individual is best explained by:
A. Selection favoring A gametes over a gametes
B. Cytoplasmic inheritance of the A allele
C. Preferential replication of the A allele during S phase
D. Random segregation of homologous chromosomes into different gametes
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Explanation

Correct answer: D

Over many meioses, random segregation of homologous chromosomes yields equal allele frequencies in gametes from a heterozygote.

Why the other options are less appropriate:

  • A: Selection would create unequal proportions, not ~50:50.
  • B: Each gamete receives one of the two homologs after meiosis I, yielding ~50% A and ~50% a over many events.
  • C: Replication copies both alleles equally; it does not bias gamete allele frequencies.
Passage 4
Passage 4: Classic evidence for DNA as the heritable material

A microbiology lab investigates why some strains of Streptococcus pneumoniae cause lethal pneumonia in mice. A virulent strain produces a smooth polysaccharide capsule, while a nonvirulent strain lacks this capsule and appears rough. Heat-killing the virulent strain destroys its ability to cause disease on its own.

In a classic set of trials, mice injected with live rough bacteria survive. Mice injected with live smooth bacteria die. Mice injected with heat-killed smooth bacteria survive. However, mice injected with a mixture of live rough bacteria and heat-killed smooth bacteria die, and live smooth bacteria can be recovered from the dead mice.

To identify the molecule responsible for this change, investigators purify material from heat-killed smooth bacteria and treat it with specific enzymes before mixing it with live rough bacteria. One enzyme degrades proteins, another degrades RNA, and another degrades DNA. In a separate experiment, bacteriophages are built with either radioactive sulfur in their proteins or radioactive phosphorus in their DNA. After the phages infect bacteria, a blender shears off the external phage coats, and centrifugation separates the heavier bacterial cells (pellet) from lighter material (supernatant). The location of each radioactive label is then measured.

In the enzyme experiments, protease-treated extracts still transform rough cells into smooth cells, whereas DNase-treated extracts do not. The lab records these outcomes across multiple replicates to rule out contamination and confirms the change is heritable across many bacterial generations.

In the phage experiment, the blender step is used specifically to separate what remains outside the cell from what has entered. The centrifugation step then allows investigators to ask whether the label follows the bacterial cells or stays with the detached coats.

How to reason:

  1. Track what must be inherited: if rough cells become smooth and the change is stable, the transferred material must carry information.
  2. Use enzyme logic: if destroying DNA removes the effect but destroying protein does not, the genetic material is DNA.
  3. Interpret the fractionation: pellet vs supernatant tells you what entered cells after phage infection.
  4. Prefer the minimal explanation: choose the conclusion that matches all observations without adding new assumptions.
16. The appearance of live smooth bacteria after mixing live rough with heat-killed smooth most strongly supports which conclusion?
A. Rough bacteria secrete a toxin that induces capsule formation in any nearby bacterium.
B. Heat-killing converts smooth bacteria into rough bacteria, which then revert back in mice.
C. A heritable factor from the smooth strain entered rough cells and altered their phenotype.
D. Mouse immune cells mutate bacterial genes during infection.
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Explanation

Correct answer: C

Recovering live smooth bacteria indicates that information for capsule production was transferred to rough cells and then replicated—consistent with genetic transformation.

Why the other options are less appropriate:

  • A: A transient toxin would not explain stable, replicating smooth colonies recovered later.
  • B: Stable conversion implies transfer of genetic information (the transforming principle).
  • D: Host-directed mutation is not supported by the experimental setup.

Passage 5
Passage 5: Epigenetic regulation, X-inactivation, and imprinting

A developmental genetics team studies why genetically similar individuals can show different gene expression patterns without changes to DNA sequence. They compare two cell lines with identical nucleotide sequences at a tumor suppressor locus. In cell line 1, the promoter region is heavily methylated and the gene is largely silent. In cell line 2, the promoter is unmethylated and histones near the promoter are highly acetylated, and the gene is actively transcribed.

The team also examines X-chromosome regulation. In typical XX somatic cells, one X chromosome is transcriptionally silenced early in development, forming a condensed Barr body. This creates dosage compensation: most X-linked genes are expressed at similar overall levels in XX and XY individuals. Some patients with sex-chromosome aneuploidy show predictable changes in the number of Barr bodies.

Finally, clinicians discuss disorders linked to parent-of-origin effects. A deletion in the same region of chromosome 15 can cause two different syndromes depending on whether the deletion was inherited from the mother or the father. The group uses this as a model for imprinting, where epigenetic marks differ based on the contributing parent and influence which allele is expressed.

In a separate project, epidemiologists examine whether environmental stressors can leave epigenetic marks that persist into subsequent generations. They collect health and mortality records across multiple generations and focus on outcomes that correlate with ancestral nutrition during sensitive developmental windows.

For chromosome 15 disorders, genetic testing confirms the same deletion boundaries in two patients, yet one shows hyperphagia and developmental delay while the other shows severe intellectual disability and distinct neurological features. Clinicians use these cases to explain parent-of-origin effects to students.

How to reason:

  1. Keep sequence vs regulation separate: epigenetics changes expression without altering nucleotide order.
  2. Promoter methylation usually represses: think “methyl = silence” for promoter regions in many MCAT contexts.
  3. Histone acetylation usually activates: acetylation loosens chromatin, increasing transcriptional access.
  4. Barr body rule: number of Barr bodies equals number of X chromosomes minus one.
  5. Imprinting logic: same deletion, different phenotype implies parent-of-origin expression differences.

❌Incorrect
Explanation

Correct answer: D

Epigenetic inheritance can transmit altered gene regulation patterns without changing the underlying DNA sequence, consistent with exposure-associated effects in descendants.
