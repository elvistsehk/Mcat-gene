1. In fetal circulation, which statement correctly describes oxygenation in the umbilical vessels?
A. Umbilical arteries carry oxygenated blood from fetus to placenta; umbilical vein carries deoxygenated blood to fetus
B. Umbilical arteries carry deoxygenated blood from fetus to placenta; umbilical vein carries oxygenated blood to fetus
C. Both umbilical arteries and umbilical vein carry deoxygenated blood because fetal lungs are inactive
D. Umbilical arteries carry oxygenated blood to fetal heart; umbilical vein carries deoxygenated blood to placenta
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: B.

Why: Oxygenation occurs at the placenta, not fetal lungs. Umbilical arteries carry blood away from the fetal heart toward the placenta and are therefore relatively deoxygenated; the umbilical vein returns oxygenated, nutrient-rich blood from the placenta to the fetus.

Other choices:

  • A: This reverses the oxygenation status.
  • C: One vessel (umbilical vein) is oxygenated because it returns blood from the placenta.
  • D: This reverses both direction and oxygenation status.

Passage 1: Placental Exchange and Maternal–Fetal Interface

A 30-year-old pregnant patient has a routine prenatal visit at 28 weeks. Laboratory testing shows that the patient’s arterial blood has a higher partial pressure of oxygen than the fetus’s circulating blood, and ultrasound demonstrates normal placental structure with numerous villous projections increasing surface area. The clinician explains that nutrient transfer of water, glucose, amino acids, and inorganic salts relies mainly on movement down concentration gradients across a thin barrier.

Later, the patient develops a febrile illness after returning from travel. Serologic screening is ordered because a subset of pathogens can cross the placental barrier and cause congenital disease. The patient asks whether the fetus will “share the same blood” with the mother. The clinician clarifies that under normal conditions the two bloodstreams remain separate, yet exchange still occurs efficiently at the interface.

In a teaching session, a resident adds that the placenta also produces hormones needed to maintain pregnancy and that late in gestation the fetus receives increasing immune protection through transfer of maternal antibodies. The resident notes that fetal red blood cells use a hemoglobin variant that binds oxygen more tightly than adult hemoglobin, which helps fetal blood load oxygen even when the oxygen gradient is modest.

How to reason:

  1. Identify the exchange mechanism: diffusion needs gradients and a large surface area; villi increase surface area.
  2. Use the key constraint: maternal and fetal blood do not mix, so exchange must occur across a barrier.
  3. Link oxygen transfer enhancements: higher oxygen affinity hemoglobin in the fetus helps loading.
  4. Recognize placenta’s additional roles: endocrine hormone production and selective antibody transfer.
  5. For pathogen questions, connect “can cross placenta” to the classic congenital infection group.

✅Correct

Passage 2: Fetal Shunts and Postnatal Circulatory Transition

A neonate is evaluated shortly after delivery for a continuous cardiac murmur. Echocardiography demonstrates persistent flow between the pulmonary artery and the aorta. The clinician notes that before birth, the fetus relies on placental gas exchange rather than pulmonary gas exchange, and blood flow is routed to minimize exposure of the developing lungs and liver to high pressures.

Reviewing the fetal circulation diagram, the clinician explains that one shunt routes blood from the right atrium to the left atrium, allowing incoming venous blood to reach systemic circulation without passing through the right ventricle and pulmonary circuit. A second shunt provides an alternate pathway for blood leaving the right ventricle to reach the aorta without traversing the pulmonary capillaries. A third shunt directs blood returning from the placenta toward the inferior vena cava, bypassing most hepatic circulation, although the liver still receives some flow via smaller systemic vessels.

The clinician adds that after birth, pressure relationships between the left and right sides of the heart change, contributing to closure of fetal shunts. If closure is incomplete, abnormal mixing patterns can persist and may require intervention.

How to reason:

  1. Translate “pulmonary artery to aorta connection” into the named fetal shunt responsible.
  2. Map each shunt to what it bypasses: right atrium to left atrium bypasses lungs; pulmonary artery to aorta bypasses lungs; umbilical vein to IVC bypasses liver.
  3. Use birth transition logic: pressure reversal helps close shunts after lungs expand and resistance drops.
  4. Pick answers that use the scenario evidence (murmur + echo findings) to identify the structure and consequence.

Passage 3: Trimester Milestones, Prematurity, and Parturition

  2. For prematurity at 24 weeks, predict which systems are most affected based on “development not complete.”
  3. For birth stages, match the described order to the standard three-phase sequence.
  4. For contraction mediators, connect lipid-derived mediators and a peptide hormone to the named labor signals.
