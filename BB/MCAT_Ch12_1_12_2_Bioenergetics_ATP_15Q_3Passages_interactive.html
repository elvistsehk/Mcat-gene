MCAT Biochemistry — Chapter 12.1–12.2: Bioenergetics & ATP
Standalone Questions (Q1–Q15)
1. Which statement best describes the relationship between ΔG° and ΔG°′ in biochemistry?
A. ΔG°′ is defined at 37°C; ΔG° is defined at 25°C.
B. ΔG°′ applies only to ATP hydrolysis; ΔG° applies to all other reactions.
C. ΔG°′ fixes [H+] at 10⁻⁷ M (pH 7), whereas ΔG° assumes 1 M protons (pH 0).
D. ΔG°′ is always equal to ΔG at equilibrium.
Show explanation

Correct Answer: C. ΔG°′ fixes [H+] at 10⁻⁷ M (pH 7), whereas ΔG° assumes 1 M protons (pH 0).

Why: Standard biochemical free energy (ΔG°′) modifies the standard state by setting proton concentration to pH 7, since pH 0 is not physiological.

Why the other choices are not as good:

  • A. ATP involvement is not what defines ΔG°′; the key modification is [H+].
  • B. Temperature is not changed by the notation; standard state is still 25°C unless specified.
  • D. ΔG depends on Q; ΔG°′ is a reference state.
2. Which equation correctly relates ΔG to ΔG° and the reaction quotient Q (at a given temperature)?
A. ΔG = ΔG° + RT ln(Q)
B. ΔG = ΔH − TΔS
C. ΔG = −RT ln(K)
D. ΔU = Q − W
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A. ΔG = ΔG° + RT ln(Q)

Why: This relationship accounts for how current concentrations (Q) shift free energy away from the standard-state value.

Why the other choices are not as good:

  • B. This relates ΔG° to the equilibrium constant K, not Q at arbitrary conditions.
  • C. ΔU = Q − W is the First Law for internal energy, not Gibbs free energy.
  • D. Use ΔG = ΔG° + RT ln(Q) to include concentration effects.
3. A reaction has ΔH < 0 and ΔS > 0. Which statement is most accurate about spontaneity?
A. It is spontaneous only at high temperature.
B. It is spontaneous only at low temperature.
C. It is spontaneous at all temperatures.
D. It is never spontaneous.
Show explanation

Correct Answer: C. It is spontaneous at all temperatures.

Why: ΔG = ΔH − TΔS. With ΔH negative and ΔS positive, both terms drive ΔG negative for any T.

Why the other choices are not as good:

  • A. Low-temperature dependence applies when ΔH<0 and ΔS<0.
  • B. High-temperature dependence applies when ΔH>0 and ΔS>0.
  • D. Here, both signs favor ΔG < 0.
4. Why can heat exchange often be treated as a proxy for changes in internal energy (ΔU) in many biological contexts?
A. Because entropy changes are negligible in living systems.
B. Because pressure–volume work is usually small when pressure and volume are roughly constant.
C. Because biological systems are always isolated from the environment.
D. Because ΔU and ΔG are the same state function in cells.
Show explanation

Correct Answer: B. Because pressure–volume work is usually small when pressure and volume are roughly constant.

Why: For a closed system, ΔU = Q − W. If PV work is minimal, Q becomes the main contributor to ΔU.

Why the other choices are not as good:

  • A. Entropy changes can be important; they are not generally negligible.
  • C. ΔU and ΔG are different state functions with different meanings.
  • D. The key reason is small PV work, not identical definitions.
5. A reaction has ΔH > 0 and ΔS > 0. Under what condition is it most likely to be spontaneous?
A. At low temperature.
B. At high temperature.
C. Only at equilibrium.
D. Never; ΔH > 0 prevents spontaneity.
Show explanation

Correct Answer: B. At high temperature.

Why: When ΔS > 0, increasing T increases TΔS, making ΔG = ΔH − TΔS more likely to become negative.

Why the other choices are not as good:

  • A. Low temperature reduces TΔS, so ΔG stays more positive.
  • C. ΔH > 0 can be overcome by sufficiently large TΔS when ΔS > 0.
  • D. High temperature favors spontaneity for this sign combination.
6. If Q is much smaller than K for a reaction, then ΔG is most likely:
A. Unrelated to direction unless ΔH is known.
B. Positive, favoring the reverse reaction.
C. Zero, because Q indicates equilibrium.
D. Negative, favoring the forward reaction.
Show explanation

Correct Answer: D. Negative, favoring the forward reaction.

Why: When Q < K, the mixture has ‘too many reactants’ relative to equilibrium, so the reaction proceeds forward and ΔG < 0.

Why the other choices are not as good:

  • A. Positive ΔG corresponds to Q > K.
  • B. ΔG = 0 occurs when Q = K.
  • C. Direction can be inferred from Q vs K without separately knowing ΔH.
7. Why is ATP considered a ‘mid-level’ energy carrier rather than an extremely high-energy carrier?
A. It releases a moderate amount of free energy, reducing waste when coupling to many cellular reactions.
B. It has the most negative ΔG of hydrolysis of all phosphate compounds.
C. It cannot be regenerated from ADP in cells.
D. It is used only in oxidative phosphorylation.
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A. It releases a moderate amount of free energy, reducing waste when coupling to many cellular reactions.

Why: A mid-level carrier like ATP can power many processes without wasting large ‘leftover’ energy each time.

Why the other choices are not as good:

  • B. ATP is continually regenerated (ATP synthase, substrate-level phosphorylation).
  • C. ATP is used widely across metabolism and signaling, not just oxidative phosphorylation.
  • D. The key idea is minimizing wasted free energy while remaining useful.
8. Which feature most directly contributes to ATP’s large negative ΔG of hydrolysis?
A. Hydrogen bonding between adenine and ribose.
B. Electrostatic repulsion among phosphate groups and resonance stabilization of ADP and Pi.
C. Aromaticity of the adenine ring.
D. Presence of a nitrogenous base with three rings.
Show explanation

Correct Answer: B. Electrostatic repulsion among phosphate groups and resonance stabilization of ADP and Pi.

Why: Hydrolysis relieves phosphate charge repulsion and yields products stabilized by resonance/solvation, making the reaction strongly exergonic.

Why the other choices are not as good:

  • A. Base–sugar bonding isn’t what drives phosphoanhydride hydrolysis energetics.
  • C. Adenine has two fused rings, not three; ring count isn’t the key driver anyway.
  • D. The dominant contributors are charge repulsion relief and product stabilization.
9. A pathway couples an endergonic step (ΔG = +12 kJ/mol) to ATP hydrolysis (ΔG = −30 kJ/mol). The net ΔG is:
A. −42 kJ/mol
B. −18 kJ/mol
C. +18 kJ/mol
D. +42 kJ/mol
Show explanation

Correct Answer: B. −18 kJ/mol

Why: Coupled free energy changes add: (+12) + (−30) = −18 kJ/mol.

Why the other choices are not as good:

  • A. −42 would require both steps to be strongly exergonic with those magnitudes.
  • C. +42 is inconsistent with adding +12 and −30.
  • D. Add ΔG values to get the net.
10. A reaction is nonspontaneous under standard conditions (ΔG° > 0). Which change could make ΔG negative without changing ΔG°?
A. Add an enzyme catalyst to lower the activation energy.
B. Shift concentrations so RT ln(Q) becomes sufficiently negative.
C. Move the reaction from cytosol to nucleus.
D. Redefine the reaction to use ΔG°′ instead of ΔG°.
Show explanation

Correct Answer: B. Shift concentrations so RT ln(Q) becomes sufficiently negative.

Why: ΔG = ΔG° + RT ln(Q). Concentrations (Q) can drive ΔG negative even when ΔG° is positive.

Why the other choices are not as good:

  • A. Enzymes change rate, not the thermodynamic state function values.
  • C. ΔG°′ is a reference for pH 7; it doesn’t automatically change ΔG for a given Q.
  • D. The direct lever is Q via the ln(Q) term.
11. Which statement best distinguishes thermodynamics from kinetics in the context of ΔG?
A. ΔG predicts reaction rate, while activation energy predicts spontaneity.
B. ΔG depends only on enzymes, while activation energy depends only on temperature.
C. ΔG predicts favorability/direction, while activation energy and mechanism determine rate.
D. If ΔG is negative, the reaction must proceed instantly.
Show explanation

Correct Answer: C. ΔG predicts favorability/direction, while activation energy and mechanism determine rate.

Why: ΔG is a thermodynamic measure of driving force; kinetics depends on activation energy and pathway (often enzyme-catalyzed).

Why the other choices are not as good:

  • A. This reverses the concepts: ΔG is thermodynamics; activation energy relates to rate.
  • B. ΔG does not depend only on enzymes; enzymes do not change ΔG.
  • D. The key correction is thermodynamics vs kinetics.
12. As a reaction mixture approaches equilibrium at constant temperature, what happens to ΔG?
A. ΔG° changes continuously as concentrations change.
B. ΔG becomes more negative as products accumulate.
C. ΔG approaches 0 because Q approaches K.
D. ΔG becomes independent of Q.
Show explanation

Correct Answer: C. ΔG approaches 0 because Q approaches K.

Why: Driving force decreases as Q → K; at equilibrium, ΔG = 0.

Why the other choices are not as good:

  • A. Product accumulation increases Q and tends to raise ΔG (less negative), not make it more negative indefinitely.
  • B. ΔG° is a standard-state constant for a given reaction at a given T; it does not vary with concentrations.
  • D. The hallmark is ΔG → 0 as Q → K.
13. Which statement correctly applies the First Law of Thermodynamics to a closed system?
A. ΔG = ΔG° + RT ln(Q)
B. ΔG = ΔH − TΔS
C. ΔS = Q/T
D. ΔU = Q − W
Show explanation

Correct Answer: D. ΔU = Q − W

Why: The First Law relates changes in internal energy to heat added and work done by the system.

Why the other choices are not as good:

  • A. This is Gibbs free energy, not the First Law.
  • B. ΔS = Q/T applies to reversible heat transfer, not the general First Law statement.
  • C. This relates ΔG to concentrations, not internal energy.
14. Which best describes ‘ATP cleavage’ as used in many coupled reactions?
A. Breaking the adenine ring to release energy.
B. Transfer of a phosphoryl group from ATP to another molecule, often altering the target’s activity.
C. Removing two phosphates to form AMP is always required.
D. Spontaneous decomposition of ATP without enzymes is the main cellular mechanism.
Show explanation

Correct Answer: B. Transfer of a phosphoryl group from ATP to another molecule, often altering the target’s activity.

Why: ATP frequently drives reactions via phosphoryl group transfer (phosphorylation), forming activated intermediates or regulatory modifications.

Why the other choices are not as good:

  • A. Energy is not released by destroying adenine; the key chemistry is phosphoanhydride/phosphoryl transfer.
  • C. Cells use enzymes to control ATP hydrolysis/transfer; ‘main mechanism’ is not uncontrolled decomposition.
  • D. Phosphoryl transfer is central to ATP coupling.
15. Why is ΔG°′ used so often in biochemistry problems, even when other concentrations are not 1 M?
A. Because it replaces the need for the RT ln(Q) term.
B. Because it converts all reactant and product concentrations to their physiological values automatically.
C. Because it guarantees ΔG is negative for all metabolic reactions.
D. Because it corrects only the standard state for protons to pH 7; other species may still be nonstandard and handled by Q.
Show explanation

Correct Answer: D. Because it corrects only the standard state for protons to pH 7; other species may still be nonstandard and handled by Q.

Why: ΔG°′ is a modified standard state reflecting pH 7. Actual cellular ΔG still depends on concentrations through Q.

Why the other choices are not as good:

  • A. ΔG°′ does not automatically account for non-1 M concentrations of other species.
  • B. ΔG can be positive or negative depending on Q; ΔG°′ does not guarantee spontaneity.
  • C. You still need RT ln(Q) to adjust from standard to actual conditions.
Passage-Based Questions (3 Passages × 3 Questions)
Passage 1: Thermodynamics Under Physiological Conditions

A researcher studies an enzyme-catalyzed reaction at 37°C. Under standard conditions (1 M reactants/products, 1 atm, 25°C), the reaction has ΔG° = +4 kJ/mol.

Inside cells, the substrate is maintained near 5 mM while the product is rapidly consumed, keeping product concentration near 50 μM. The buffer is near pH 7, and the system shows heat release during turnover with increased dispersion of energy into the solvent.

The investigator uses ΔG = ΔG° + RT ln(Q) (and the concept of ΔG°′ at pH 7) to reconcile ‘unfavorable’ standard data with observed forward flux in vivo.

How to reason:

  1. Write the governing relationship: ΔG = ΔG° + RT ln(Q).
  2. Infer Q from the narrative: product kept very low while reactant is higher → Q ≪ 1 → ln(Q) < 0.
  3. Translate qualitative thermodynamics: heat release → ΔH < 0; increased dispersion/disorder → ΔS > 0.
  4. Pick the option consistent with forward flux: forward progress implies ΔG < 0 under cellular conditions.
16. Which statement best explains why the reaction can proceed forward in the cell despite ΔG° being positive?
A. Heat release forces ΔG to equal ΔH.
B. Enzymes change ΔG° by lowering the activation energy.
C. Using ΔG°′ instead of ΔG° automatically makes the reaction favorable.
D. Cellular Q is very small, so RT ln(Q) is negative enough to offset +ΔG°.
Show explanation

Correct Answer: D. Cellular Q is very small, so RT ln(Q) is negative enough to offset +ΔG°.

Why: Because ΔG depends on concentration via Q, maintaining low product and higher reactant drives ΔG negative even if ΔG° > 0.

Why the other choices are not as good:

  • A. Enzymes change rate, not ΔG°.
  • B. ΔG°′ adjusts the proton standard state but does not automatically flip reaction favorability.
  • C. ΔG includes entropy; it is not forced to equal ΔH.
17. Based on the passage’s qualitative observations, which sign combination is most consistent?
A. ΔH < 0 and ΔS < 0
B. ΔH > 0 and ΔS < 0
C. ΔH < 0 and ΔS > 0
D. ΔH > 0 and ΔS > 0
Show explanation

Correct Answer: C. ΔH < 0 and ΔS > 0

Why: Heat release indicates ΔH < 0. Increased energy dispersion/disorder indicates ΔS > 0.

Why the other choices are not as good:

  • A. ΔH > 0 contradicts heat release and ΔS < 0 contradicts increased dispersion.
  • B. ΔS < 0 contradicts increased dispersion; would make temperature dependence critical.
  • D. The best match is ΔH < 0 and ΔS > 0.
18. If the product were allowed to accumulate substantially (increasing Q) while temperature stays constant, what happens to ΔG?
A. ΔG increases (becomes less negative or more positive), moving toward equilibrium.
B. ΔG becomes more negative, driving the reaction faster.
C. ΔG is unchanged because ΔG° is constant.
D. ΔG instantly becomes 0 because enzymes enforce equilibrium.
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A. ΔG increases (becomes less negative or more positive), moving toward equilibrium.

Why: As Q increases, ln(Q) increases, raising ΔG and reducing the driving force toward products.

Why the other choices are not as good:

  • B. ΔG depends on Q via RT ln(Q).
  • C. Enzymes speed approach to equilibrium but do not force ΔG = 0 unless Q = K.
  • D. The correct trend is ΔG increases as Q increases.
Passage 2: ATP Coupling and Net Free Energy

A ligase reaction in vitro has ΔG = +18 kJ/mol under physiological conditions, so it does not proceed measurably on its own.

In cells, the ligase couples the process to ATP by first forming a phosphorylated intermediate (Step 1: ΔG = +13 kJ/mol), then completing bond formation (Step 2: ΔG = +2 kJ/mol). The enzyme also catalyzes ATP → ADP + Pi (Step 0: ΔG = −30 kJ/mol) in the same active site during the overall cycle.

A student argues that because some individual steps are still endergonic, the overall process cannot be favorable. The instructor points out that for coupled processes, free energies add across the linked steps.

How to reason:

  1. Identify what is actually coupled: Step 0 (ATP hydrolysis) + Steps 1–2 (mechanism).
  2. Add ΔG values: net ΔG = ΣΔG (state functions add).
  3. Interpret the sign: net negative → thermodynamically favorable overall, even if some steps are positive.
  4. Don’t confuse rate with sign: enzymes tune kinetics, but the net ΔG comes from the sum.
19. What is the net ΔG for the coupled cycle described (Step 0 + Step 1 + Step 2)?
A. −15 kJ/mol
B. −33 kJ/mol
C. +15 kJ/mol
D. +33 kJ/mol
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A. −15 kJ/mol

Why: Add the coupled steps: (−30) + (+13) + (+2) = −15 kJ/mol.

Why the other choices are not as good:

  • B. +15 would be missing the ATP term or adding incorrectly.
  • C. +33 is inconsistent with a large negative ATP term.
  • D. Sum the given ΔG values for the coupled steps.
20. Which statement best addresses the student’s objection that ‘a positive step means the pathway cannot run’?
A. ATP hydrolysis must be endothermic to drive coupling.
B. Any endergonic step is impossible in biology.
C. Enzymes change ΔG for each step so signs do not matter.
D. A pathway can proceed if the overall ΔG across coupled steps is negative, even if some individual steps are endergonic.
Show explanation

Correct Answer: D. A pathway can proceed if the overall ΔG across coupled steps is negative, even if some individual steps are endergonic.

Why: Coupling works because state functions add. Local uphill steps can occur if compensated by larger downhill steps in the same coupled process.

Why the other choices are not as good:

  • A. Biology frequently uses uphill steps (e.g., transport) powered by coupling.
  • B. Enzymes alter rates and pathways, not the thermodynamic state function values.
  • C. ATP hydrolysis is exergonic (not endothermic) and provides negative ΔG.
21. Which change would most directly make this coupled ligase cycle less favorable (net ΔG less negative), assuming ΔG° values are unchanged?
A. Moving the enzyme to a different organelle without changing concentrations.
B. Adding more enzyme to the cell.
C. Lowering activation energy of Step 1.
D. Allowing ADP and Pi to accumulate while ATP becomes relatively scarce.
Show explanation

Correct Answer: D. Allowing ADP and Pi to accumulate while ATP becomes relatively scarce.

Why: Changing concentrations alters Q for ATP hydrolysis, making its ΔG less negative, which reduces the net driving force for the coupled cycle.

Why the other choices are not as good:

  • A. More enzyme changes rate, not ΔG at given concentrations.
  • B. Activation energy affects kinetics, not thermodynamic favorability.
  • C. Location alone doesn’t change ΔG unless it changes conditions/concentrations.
Passage 3: ATP as a Cellular ‘Energy Currency’

A physiologist compares fuels and energy carriers. Complete combustion yields ~9 kcal/g for fats but ~4 kcal/g for carbohydrates or proteins, so fats are preferred for long-term storage.

In contrast, cells rely on ATP as an immediately usable currency. Under physiological conditions, ATP hydrolysis releases about 30 kJ/mol. A high-energy carrier like creatine phosphate releases more (≈43 kJ/mol), while lower-energy phosphates like glucose-6-phosphate release much less.

In a muscle biopsy, a subject performs repeated short sprints. The lab observes rapid ATP turnover and notes that total ATP present at any moment is small compared to daily ATP use, implying extensive recycling of ATP ↔ ADP + Pi.

How to reason:

  1. Separate storage vs currency: fats store lots of energy per gram; ATP is about rapid, controllable energy transfer.
  2. Use the ‘change-making’ analogy: mid-level ΔG avoids wasting large excess free energy for small tasks.
  3. Connect turnover to pool size: small ATP pool + large daily usage implies rapid regeneration cycles.
  4. Pick choices consistent with physiology: sprinting favors rapid ATP regeneration, not ‘one-and-done’ ATP use.

A. They are more water-soluble, making storage easier.
B. They can be used directly by every tissue, including red blood cells.
C. They are more energy-dense per gram, storing more chemical energy in the same mass.
D. They contain phosphate bonds with the highest ΔG of hydrolysis.
Show explanation

Correct Answer: C. They are more energy-dense per gram, storing more chemical energy in the same mass.

Why: Fats yield roughly 9 kcal/g versus ~4 kcal/g for carbohydrates/proteins, making them efficient long-term stores.

Why the other choices are not as good:

  • A. RBCs lack mitochondria and rely on glucose anaerobically; they do not use fats directly for ATP production.
  • B. Fats are largely hydrophobic and stored as triacylglycerols; they are not water-soluble.
  • D. The key distinction is energy density per gram.
23. The passage argues ATP is a ‘mid-level’ carrier. Which scenario best illustrates the advantage of that?
A. If a process needs ~10 kJ/mol, coupling to ATP (~30 kJ/mol) wastes less energy than coupling to creatine phosphate (~43 kJ/mol).
B. Using the highest-energy carrier always minimizes waste.
C. Mid-level carriers are used because they cannot be regenerated.
D. ATP is mid-level because it is the only phosphate compound in cells.
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A. If a process needs ~10 kJ/mol, coupling to ATP (~30 kJ/mol) wastes less energy than coupling to creatine phosphate (~43 kJ/mol).

Why: A moderate ΔG of hydrolysis allows energy payments closer to the ‘price’ of many reactions, reducing leftover free energy that can’t be recovered.

Why the other choices are not as good:

  • B. ATP is regenerated constantly; mid-level status is not due to irreversibility.
  • C. Cells contain multiple phosphate compounds (e.g., creatine phosphate, G6P).
  • D. The advantage is minimizing waste while remaining broadly useful.

A. ATP is recycled rapidly: ADP + Pi are continually rephosphorylated to ATP many times per day.
B. ATP is stored long-term in adipose tissue.
C. ATP is not actually consumed; it only binds enzymes.
D. Cells obtain ATP only by dietary absorption of ATP molecules.
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A. ATP is recycled rapidly: ADP + Pi are continually rephosphorylated to ATP many times per day.

Why: A small pool can support huge daily flux if turnover is high; ATP is repeatedly regenerated via oxidative and substrate-level phosphorylation.

Why the other choices are not as good:

  • B. ATP hydrolysis/transfer is real consumption in reactions; it’s not merely binding.
  • C. Dietary ATP is not the main source; cells synthesize ATP internally.
  • D. High turnover explains small standing pool with large daily use.
