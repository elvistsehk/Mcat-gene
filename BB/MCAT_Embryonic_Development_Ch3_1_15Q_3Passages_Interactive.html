1. An embryologist separates one blastomere from a 4‑cell human embryo, and the isolated cell later forms a complete, normal fetus. Which cleavage pattern is most consistent with this observation?
A. Neurulation
B. Indeterminate cleavage
C. Gastrulation
D. Determinate cleavage
Correct ✅
Incorrect ❌
Show explanation

Correct Answer: B.

Why: Indeterminate cleavage produces early cells whose fates are not fixed; each blastomere retains the potential to form an entire organism (which is also why early splitting can yield identical twins).

Other choices:

  • A: Neurulation is neural tube formation later in development, not a description of early cleavage cell potency.
  • B: Indeterminate cleavage produces early cells whose fates are not fixed; each blastomere retains the potential to form an entire organism (which is also why early splitting can yield identical twins).
  • C: Gastrulation establishes germ layers later; it does not describe whether early blastomeres are totipotent.
  • D: Determinate cleavage commits early cells to specific fates; an isolated blastomere would not be expected to generate a whole organism.

Passage 1: IVF Embryo Splitting and Early Cleavage

A fertility clinic cultures a human zygote in vitro and tracks development over the first several days. The embryo undergoes rapid mitotic divisions (cleavage) while its overall diameter remains essentially unchanged. By day 3, the embryo reaches the 8‑cell stage and each blastomere is noticeably smaller than at the 2‑cell stage.

To test developmental potential, the embryologist gently separates one blastomere from the 8‑cell embryo and places it into a separate culture droplet with identical media. Both the original embryo and the isolated blastomere continue dividing and each later forms a blastocyst with a distinct inner cell mass and surrounding trophoblast.

In a separate case, an early split of a single embryo results in twins that share one chorion but have two separate amniotic sacs on ultrasound. The clinician uses this placental configuration to infer when the splitting occurred.

How to reason:

  1. Underline what the passage shows: an isolated blastomere forms a full blastocyst; twins share a chorion but not an amnion.
  2. Translate evidence into concepts: blastomere potential → determinate vs indeterminate cleavage; constant embryo size → SA:V and N:C shifts; shared chorion vs separate amnion → timing of splitting.
  3. Pick the answer that fits all observations; eliminate choices that require a different stage or different membrane arrangement.

Passage 2: Neurulation, Induction, and Neural Tube Defects

A pregnant patient presents for prenatal screening. Laboratory testing shows elevated alpha‑fetoprotein (AFP), and ultrasound reveals a defect in the posterior vertebral arches with protrusion of neural tissue, consistent with spina bifida. The patient reports limited prenatal vitamin use early in pregnancy and has poorly controlled diabetes with intermittent hyperglycemia.

During early development, a midline rod of mesodermal cells forms along the embryo’s long axis. This structure releases signals that cause the overlying ectoderm to thicken into a neural plate, which folds inward and fuses to create a neural tube. Cells at the crest of these folds detach and migrate broadly to form additional tissues.

The care team discusses how timing, route of exposure, and embryo genetics can influence teratogen severity, and why prevention strategies target very early developmental windows.

How to reason:

  1. Identify the failed process (neural tube closure) and map it to the tissue that forms the neural tube.
  2. Find the inducer in the passage (a mesodermal midline rod) and connect it to induction of overlying ectoderm.
  3. For neural crest questions, list key derivatives (PNS ganglia, Schwann cells, adrenal medulla, melanocytes) and match to outcomes.

Passage 3: Implantation Site and Placental Exchange

A 28‑year‑old with a positive pregnancy test presents with sharp unilateral pelvic pain and light vaginal spotting. Transvaginal ultrasound shows no intrauterine gestational sac but identifies a small complex mass near the ampulla of the right fallopian tube. Serum hCG is positive but rises more slowly than expected over 48 hours.

The clinician explains that normal implantation requires trophoblast invasion of the endometrium and formation of chorionic villi to establish maternal–fetal exchange. Until robust placental exchange is established, early support comes from extraembryonic structures, and later the umbilical cord carries blood between embryo and placenta via two arteries and one vein.

Because the fallopian tube cannot expand like the uterus, continued growth at the implantation site can cause rupture and life‑threatening hemorrhage.

How to reason:

  1. Use location clues: no uterine sac + tubal mass + pain/spotting → implantation outside uterus.
  2. Recall what makes villi and placenta (trophoblast/chorion) and which umbilical vessel carries oxygenated blood (vein).
  3. Choose the most direct match to the clinical pattern and basic anatomy; avoid overinterpreting lab trends not needed.
