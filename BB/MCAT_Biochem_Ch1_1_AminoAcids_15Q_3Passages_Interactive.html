Biochemistry — Amino Acids (Ch. 1.1)
Standalone Questions (Q1–Q15)
1. Which statement best defines an α-amino acid?
A. The amino group and the carboxyl group are attached to the same carbon (the α-carbon)
B. The amino group is always attached to the γ-carbon
C. The molecule must be incorporated into proteins to be considered an amino acid
D. The side chain must contain a carboxylate group at physiologic pH
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A

α-amino acids have both the amino and carboxyl functional groups on the α-carbon (the carbon adjacent to the carboxyl carbon).

Why the other choices are not best:

  • γ-carbon attachment: describes molecules like GABA, not α-amino acids.
  • Must be incorporated into proteins: many amino acids and related molecules exist without being proteinogenic.
  • Side chain carboxylate: applies only to Asp/Glu, not all amino acids.
2. Which amino acid is achiral because its side chain is a hydrogen atom?
A. Glycine
B. Alanine
C. Valine
D. Cysteine
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A

Glycine’s R group is H, so the α-carbon does not have four different substituents and is not chiral.

Why the other choices are not best:

  • Alanine/Valine: have alkyl side chains, making the α-carbon chiral.
  • Cysteine: is chiral and is the notable exception for absolute configuration.
3. All proteinogenic amino acids in eukaryotic proteins are typically:
A. D-amino acids
B. L-amino acids
C. A 50/50 mixture of L and D amino acids
D. Racemized at physiologic pH
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: B

Proteins are synthesized using L-amino acids. D-amino acids can exist but are not typically incorporated into eukaryotic proteins.

Why the other choices are not best:

  • D-amino acids: occur in some bacterial products but are not standard for eukaryotic proteins.
  • 50/50 mixture: not true for ribosomally synthesized proteins.
  • Racemized at pH 7.4: chirality does not spontaneously racemize under normal cellular conditions.
4. Most chiral amino acids have (S) absolute configuration, except:
A. Glycine, which is (R)
B. Histidine, which is (R)
C. Proline, which is (R)
D. Cysteine, which is (R)
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: D

Because sulfur in cysteine’s side chain changes priority order under CIP rules, cysteine is L but has (R) configuration.

Why the other choices are not best:

  • Glycine: is achiral (no R/S).
  • Histidine: is chiral and typically (S).
  • Proline: is chiral and typically (S); its uniqueness is ring formation, not configuration.
5. Which amino acid is unique because its amino nitrogen is part of a five-membered ring, restricting backbone flexibility?
A. Serine
B. Methionine
C. Leucine
D. Proline
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: D

Proline’s side chain covalently links back to the amino nitrogen, making it cyclic and conformationally restrictive.

Why the other choices are not best:

  • Serine: is polar due to –OH but not cyclic.
  • Methionine: contains sulfur but is not cyclic.
  • Leucine: is a hydrophobic branched-chain amino acid (BCAA) but not cyclic.
6. Which amino acid is classified as aromatic and also has a phenolic –OH group that increases polarity?
A. Phenylalanine
B. Tryptophan
C. Tyrosine
D. Valine
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: C

Tyrosine is aromatic like phenylalanine but has an –OH on the ring, making it more polar and capable of hydrogen bonding.

Why the other choices are not best:

  • Phenylalanine: aromatic but lacks the –OH.
  • Tryptophan: aromatic with an indole nitrogen, but not a phenolic –OH.
  • Valine: nonaromatic, aliphatic.
7. Which pair of amino acids has amide side chains and is polar but uncharged at physiologic pH?
A. Aspartate and glutamate
B. Lysine and arginine
C. Asparagine and glutamine
D. Serine and threonine
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: C

Asn and Gln contain amide side chains that do not ionize significantly with pH changes, remaining uncharged but polar.

Why the other choices are not best:

  • Asp/Glu: have carboxylate side chains and are negatively charged at physiologic pH.
  • Lys/Arg: are positively charged (basic) at physiologic pH.
  • Ser/Thr: are polar due to –OH but are not amides.
8. At physiologic pH (~7.4), which amino acid side chain is most likely to be partially protonated because its pKa is near 6?
A. Histidine
B. Lysine
C. Arginine
D. Aspartate
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A

Histidine’s imidazole side chain has a pKa around 6, so it can exist in both protonated and unprotonated forms near physiologic pH.

Why the other choices are not best:

  • Lysine: pKa ~10.5; largely protonated at pH 7.4.
  • Arginine: pKa ~12.5; almost always protonated at pH 7.4.
  • Aspartate: pKa ~4; largely deprotonated at pH 7.4.
9. Which amino acid side chain is most prone to oxidation due to its thiol group?
A. Serine
B. Cysteine
C. Threonine
D. Glutamine
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: B

Cysteine’s thiol (–SH) can be oxidized to form disulfide bonds, affecting protein structure and stability.

Why the other choices are not best:

  • Serine/Threonine: have –OH groups; they do not form disulfides.
  • Glutamine: has an amide side chain, not a thiol.
10. Which amino acid is strongly hydrophobic and most likely to be buried in the interior of a soluble globular protein?

Leucine has a long aliphatic side chain and is strongly hydrophobic, favoring the protein core away from water.

Why the other choices are not best:

  • Aspartate: negatively charged and hydrophilic.

  • Asparagine: polar amide and hydrophilic.

Passages (3 passages × 3 questions)
Passage 1: Enzyme Active Site Substitutions

A research group is studying a serine protease that uses a catalytic triad to accelerate peptide bond hydrolysis. In the wild-type enzyme, a histidine residue helps activate a serine side chain by accepting a proton, increasing the nucleophilicity of the serine oxygen. The team engineers a variant in which that histidine is replaced by leucine.

In a separate variant, the histidine is replaced by lysine. Both mutants fold into structures that appear grossly similar to wild type by a low-resolution assay, but their catalytic rates differ dramatically. The investigators suspect that the substitutions altered local side-chain chemistry rather than overall folding.

The researchers also note that the enzyme’s surface is enriched in charged residues, while its core is enriched in strongly hydrophobic residues. They hypothesize that moving a charged residue into the core could destabilize the protein even if the backbone remains intact.

How to reason:

  1. Identify what the passage implies about catalysis: histidine participates in proton transfer; therefore, substitutions should be evaluated by whether they can exist in both protonated and unprotonated forms near physiologic pH.
  2. Recall the key pKa clue: histidine has a side-chain pKa near 6, so it can readily accept or donate a proton around pH 7.4.
  3. Compare substitutions chemically: leucine is hydrophobic and cannot participate in acid–base catalysis; lysine is positively charged (high pKa) and may be less effective as a reversible proton shuttle.
  4. Use structure–function logic: charged residues are favored on the surface; strongly hydrophobic residues are favored in the protein interior.
  5. Choose answers that connect side-chain properties to enzyme mechanism and protein stability, not just to amino-acid names.
16. In Passage 1, replacing histidine with leucine at the active site most directly reduces catalysis because leucine:
A. Has a negatively charged side chain that repels the substrate
B. Forms disulfide bonds that lock the active site closed
C. Has a pKa near physiologic pH and is therefore partially protonated
D. Cannot participate in acid–base chemistry or proton shuttling
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: D

Histidine’s imidazole can accept/donate protons near physiologic pH. Leucine is hydrophobic and lacks a proton-accepting functional group for this catalytic role.

Why the other choices are not best:

  • Negatively charged: leucine is nonpolar, not acidic.
  • Disulfide bonds: are formed by cysteine thiols, not leucine.
  • pKa near physiologic: describes histidine, not leucine.

A. Lysine is hydrophobic and cannot interact with water
B. Lysine is negatively charged at physiologic pH
C. Lysine is achiral and therefore cannot fit into proteins
D. Lysine is usually protonated at physiologic pH and is less effective as a reversible proton shuttle
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: D

Lysine has a high side-chain pKa and tends to remain protonated, making it less suited for switching protonation states the way histidine can during catalysis.

Why the other choices are not best:

  • Hydrophobic: lysine is basic and hydrophilic.
  • Negatively charged: lysine is positively charged at pH 7.4.
  • Achiral: lysine is chiral; glycine is the classic achiral amino acid.

Passage 2: A Disulfide-Rich Secreted Protein

A secreted signaling protein isolated from plasma contains multiple disulfide bonds that stabilize its tertiary structure. When the protein is treated with a reducing agent, its disulfide bonds are broken and the protein becomes less stable, unfolding more readily at body temperature.

A student suggests replacing several cysteine residues with serine residues to prevent disulfide formation. Another student suggests replacing cysteine with methionine instead, arguing that both contain sulfur. The instructor notes that the chemical nature of the sulfur-containing functional group matters for reactivity.

The class also discusses why disulfide-rich proteins are common in extracellular environments but are less common in the cytosol, where the redox environment can differ.

How to reason:

  1. Start with the functional group: disulfide bonds form by oxidation of two cysteine thiols; therefore, identify which amino acid provides a thiol.
  2. Differentiate sulfur chemistry: cysteine has a reactive thiol; methionine has a thioether and is relatively nonpolar and less prone to forming disulfides.
  3. Predict the consequence of substitutions: serine (–OH) cannot form disulfides; replacing cysteines reduces covalent stabilization and can lower thermal stability.
  4. Use environment logic: extracellular proteins often use disulfides for stability; intracellular conditions can limit disulfide formation.
  5. Pick answers that infer effects on stability/reactivity rather than only memorizing sulfur-containing amino acids.
19. In Passage 2, disulfide bonds form by oxidation of which functional group?
A. Two serine hydroxyl (–OH) groups
B. Two cysteine thiol (–SH) groups
C. Two methionine thioether groups
D. Two lysine amino (–NH2) groups
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: B

Disulfides are covalent bonds formed between oxidized cysteine thiols, stabilizing tertiary structure especially in extracellular proteins.

Why the other choices are not best:

  • Serine hydroxyl: does not form disulfide bonds.
  • Methionine thioether: is not a thiol and is much less prone to forming disulfides.
  • Lysine amino: participates in acid–base chemistry but not disulfide formation.
20. Why is methionine not a good substitute for cysteine if the goal is to preserve disulfide bonding (Passage 2)?
A. Methionine is negatively charged and would repel other cysteines
B. Methionine contains a thioether rather than a thiol, so it does not readily form disulfide bonds
C. Methionine is aromatic and cannot fit in folded proteins
D. Methionine is an imino acid and disrupts peptide bonds
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: B

Methionine’s sulfur is in a thioether (–S–), which is relatively nonreactive for disulfide formation compared to cysteine’s thiol (–SH).

Why the other choices are not best:

  • Negatively charged: methionine is neutral and relatively nonpolar.
  • Aromatic: methionine is not aromatic.
  • Imino acid: proline is the cyclic amino acid; methionine is not an imino acid.
21. In Passage 2, replacing cysteine with serine would most directly be expected to:
A. Create new disulfide bonds through serine hydroxyl groups
B. Increase the protein’s net negative charge at physiologic pH
C. Reduce covalent stabilization and decrease protein stability under oxidizing conditions
D. Make the protein aromatic-rich and more hydrophobic overall
✅Correct
❌Incorrect

Correct Answer: C

Serine cannot form disulfide bonds, so the stabilizing covalent crosslinks are lost, often reducing stability for disulfide-rich proteins.

Why the other choices are not best:

  • Serine disulfides: –OH does not form disulfides.
  • Net negative charge: serine is uncharged at physiologic pH.
  • Aromatic-rich: serine is nonaromatic.
Passage 3: Interpreting Protein Sequence Annotations

A clinician reviews a genetic report describing a single amino acid substitution in a membrane-associated protein: "E120K." The report notes that the mutated residue is located in a region that is normally exposed to the aqueous cytosol. The patient’s symptoms suggest altered protein interactions rather than complete loss of the protein.

In a lab follow-up, researchers compare the wild-type and mutant proteins in a model membrane system. They observe that the mutant protein has a higher tendency to form abnormal electrostatic interactions with nearby residues and with cytosolic binding partners. They also note that the protein’s hydrophobic segments remain embedded in the membrane.

A trainee proposes that if the same substitution occurred within a deeply hydrophobic segment, the effect might differ substantially. The supervising scientist asks the trainee to justify that prediction using amino acid side-chain properties and hydrophobic/hydrophilic reasoning.

How to reason:

  1. Decode the mutation notation: the first letter is the original residue, the number is the position, and the last letter is the new residue.
  2. Translate the letters to chemistry: E is glutamate (negatively charged at pH 7.4); K is lysine (positively charged), so the substitution flips the sign of charge.
  3. Use location reasoning: a charged residue on a protein surface can participate in electrostatic interactions; changing its charge can alter binding and signaling.
  4. Contrast with a hydrophobic segment: strongly hydrophobic regions favor residues like Leu, Ile, Val, Phe; introducing a charged residue can disrupt membrane embedding or local structure.
  5. Choose the option that integrates notation + charge + environment (aqueous vs hydrophobic), not just the correct letter mapping.
