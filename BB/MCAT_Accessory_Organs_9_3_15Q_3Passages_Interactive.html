MCAT Biology — Accessory Organs of Digestion (Ch. 9.3)
Standalone Questions (Q1–Q15)
1. Which statement best distinguishes exocrine from endocrine function in the pancreas?
A. Exocrine pancreas releases insulin into blood; endocrine pancreas releases enzymes into ducts
B. Exocrine pancreas secretes bile; endocrine pancreas stores bile
C. Exocrine pancreas releases bicarbonate-rich digestive secretions into ducts; endocrine pancreas releases peptide hormones into blood
D. Exocrine pancreas performs gluconeogenesis; endocrine pancreas emulsifies fats
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: C.

Why: Exocrine acinar cells secrete digestive enzymes (plus bicarbonate) into a duct system, while endocrine islets secrete hormones (insulin, glucagon, somatostatin) into the bloodstream.

Other choices:

  • A: This reverses the definitions: insulin is endocrine; enzymes into ducts are exocrine.
  • B: Bile is produced by the liver and stored in the gallbladder, not the pancreas.
  • D: Gluconeogenesis is primarily a liver function; emulsification is a bile salt role.
2. A student labels enteropeptidase as a “master switch” in intestinal protein digestion. What is the most direct reason for this label?
A. It activates trypsinogen to trypsin, which then activates multiple pancreatic zymogens
B. It conjugates bilirubin for excretion
C. It hydrolyzes disaccharides into monosaccharides
D. It emulsifies dietary fats into micelles
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A.

Why: Enteropeptidase activates trypsinogen. Trypsin then activates other protease zymogens (e.g., chymotrypsinogen) and supports the protease cascade.

Other choices:

  • B: Bilirubin conjugation occurs in the liver.
  • C: Disaccharidases (maltase, lactase, sucrase) do carbohydrate cleavage at the brush border.
  • D: Emulsification is performed by bile salts, not enteropeptidase.
3. Which pancreatic secretion is most directly responsible for hydrolyzing dietary lipids into absorbable components?
A. Bile salts
B. Trypsin
C. Pancreatic amylase
D. Pancreatic lipase
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: D.

Why: Pancreatic lipase hydrolyzes ester bonds in triglycerides to produce free fatty acids and glycerol (or monoglycerides/free fatty acids).

Other choices:

  • A: Bile salts are not enzymes; they emulsify fats to increase surface area for lipase.
  • B: Trypsin is a protease that cleaves peptide bonds.
  • C: Amylase digests polysaccharides into smaller sugars.

Correct Answer: B.

Why: Zymogens are inactive precursors that reduce tissue damage risk; premature activation can cause pancreatitis via autodigestion.

Other choices:

  • C: Intact proteins are generally not absorbed directly; proteins require digestion into smaller units.
  • D: Pancreatic enzymes work best in the alkaline duodenum, not the acidic stomach.

Passage 1: Duct Obstruction and Pancreatic Injury

A 39-year-old patient arrives at the emergency department with severe epigastric pain radiating to the back. The pain began abruptly after a large, fatty dinner. The patient is nauseated and cannot tolerate oral intake. On exam, the abdomen is tender, and the patient prefers to sit leaning forward.

Laboratory testing reveals elevated serum lipase and amylase. Ultrasound shows echogenic material within the gallbladder and a small stone near the region where ducts empty into the duodenum. The clinician explains that pancreatic acinar cells normally secrete a bicarbonate-rich, enzyme-containing fluid into a duct system that drains into the duodenum, and that many pancreatic proteases are released as inactive precursors.

The clinician adds that if drainage is blocked, pancreatic enzymes may become activated before reaching the intestinal lumen. This can damage pancreatic tissue and trigger inflammation. The patient asks why a stone originating in the gallbladder could be related to pancreatic injury and why fatty meals seem to precipitate symptoms.

How to reason:

  1. Start with anatomy: the bile duct system and pancreatic duct can converge near the duodenum; a stone there can block **outflow** from the pancreas.
  2. Apply enzyme safety logic: pancreatic proteases are secreted as **zymogens** to prevent autodigestion; obstruction can promote premature activation.
  3. Use meal physiology: fatty meals increase **CCK**, which stimulates gallbladder contraction and pancreatic enzyme secretion—raising flow demands at the shared outlet.
  4. Predict downstream effects: impaired bile delivery → fewer **micelles** and poorer fat digestion; impaired pancreatic enzyme delivery → poorer protein/fat digestion.
  5. Match labs to mechanism: high **lipase/amylase** supports pancreatic injury rather than isolated carbohydrate malabsorption.

  • C: Edema is associated with low albumin, not high albumin.
  • D: Obstructive jaundice typically raises bilirubin and can produce pale stools and dark urine.

Passage 2: Liver Failure, Portal Hypertension, and Systemic Effects

A 57-year-old patient with a long history of heavy alcohol use presents with increasing abdominal distension, ankle swelling, and confusion. Physical exam shows jaundiced skin and sclera, a distended abdomen with shifting dullness, and multiple dilated superficial abdominal veins.

Blood tests reveal low albumin and prolonged clotting times. Imaging suggests a nodular liver architecture consistent with cirrhosis. The clinician explains that scarring can increase resistance to blood flow through the liver, raising pressure in vessels that drain the gastrointestinal tract.

The patient later has an episode of vomiting blood. The clinician explains that elevated pressure can divert blood through fragile collateral veins, particularly in the esophagus. The clinician also notes that impaired liver processing can allow toxic nitrogen waste to accumulate and that impaired bile flow can worsen absorption of fat-soluble vitamins involved in coagulation.

How to reason:

  1. Recognize the core path: cirrhosis → increased resistance → **portal hypertension**.
  2. Connect portal HTN to bleeding: portal HTN causes **esophageal varices**, which can rupture → hematemesis.
  3. Explain edema/ascites: low **albumin** → lower oncotic pressure → fluid leaks into tissues/peritoneum.
  4. Explain confusion: impaired ammonia-to-**urea** conversion → ammonia accumulates → altered mentation.
  5. Link coagulation: liver makes **clotting factors** and bile helps absorb vitamin K; both can worsen bleeding tendency.

Passage 3: Bile Pigments, Drug Handling, and Digestion Integration

A pharmacology team evaluates two experimental medications. Drug X is inactive when administered but becomes active after processing by liver enzymes. Drug Y is active as administered but loses potency when taken orally; it is much more effective when injected.

In a separate clinic, an infant develops yellowing of the skin during the first week of life. The pediatrician explains that the pigment responsible is generated from red blood cell turnover and must be handled by the liver before being eliminated in bile. The yellowing improves over time without other signs of infection.

During a teaching session, the team reviews that blood from the digestive tract first passes through the liver via the hepatic portal vein. The team also reviews that bile contains amphipathic salts, cholesterol, and pigments, and that bile can be stored and concentrated in the gallbladder before release into the duodenum when dietary fat arrives.

How to reason:

  1. Separate the drug patterns: Drug X needs hepatic **activation** → portal delivery to liver supports activation; Drug Y is harmed by **first-pass metabolism**.
  2. Use circulation logic: absorbed oral compounds go to the liver via the **hepatic portal vein** before systemic circulation.
  3. Identify the jaundice pigment: hemoglobin breakdown → **bilirubin** → liver processing → bile excretion.
  4. Keep bile composition straight: **bile salts** (emulsify), **cholesterol**, and **pigments** (bilirubin derivatives).
  5. Predict digestion impact: reduced bile delivery → fewer **micelles** → impaired fat digestion/absorption.
