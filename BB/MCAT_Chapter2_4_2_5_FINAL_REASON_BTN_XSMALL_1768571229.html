üìò Passage 1 ‚Äî Olfaction, taste, and the 'flavor' illusion

How to reason from this passage

  • Separate chemical access routes: ask whether the stimulus reaches olfactory epithelium (volatile/aerosolized) or taste buds (dissolved in saliva).
  • Use pathway exceptions: olfaction is the sensory pathway classically noted for bypassing a mandatory thalamic relay and projecting strongly to limbic regions (emotion/memory).
  • Turn observations into constraints: if basic tastes are intact but 'flavor' is gone, infer loss of smell rather than loss of taste.
  • Prefer mechanistic answers: choose options that explain why the data occur (binding/solubility/pathway routing), not options that merely restate the observation.

In a sensory lab, participants sample a warm soup under two conditions. In Condition 1, they eat normally. In Condition 2, they wear a nose clip that prevents airflow through the nasal cavity while tasting, but the soup contacts the tongue normally.

A subset of participants also performs an odor-only task: they inhale faint vapor-phase compounds released from the soup while keeping the mouth closed and dry. During this task, many participants report vivid autobiographical memories even though they cannot describe the exact chemical identity of the odorant.

Neuroimaging in the odor-only task shows strong activation in limbic-associated regions during odor presentation, whereas thalamic activation is less prominent than in comparable vision tasks.

üìò Passage 3 ‚Äî Depth cues and perceptual organization in an ambiguous display

How to reason from this passage

  • Identify cue type: binocular cues (retinal disparity, convergence) vs monocular cues (relative size, interposition, linear perspective, motion parallax).
  • Separate bottom-up vs top-down: bottom-up = data-driven features; top-down = expectations/priming/memory templates.
  • Use Gestalt as the 'fill-in' engine: when elements are incomplete/ambiguous, choose grouping rules (proximity, similarity, continuation, closure) that would simplify the scene.

Researchers design a virtual scene containing sparse dots that can be interpreted as either two overlapping shapes or a single continuous contour, depending on how the dots are grouped. The display is shown under two conditions: binocular viewing (both eyes) and monocular viewing (one eye patched).

Before the task, half of participants are primed with the instruction: 'You will see an animal-related object.' The other half are told: 'You will see a tool-related object.' Despite identical sensory input, primed participants tend to report the primed category more quickly.

When the scene is animated so that the observer‚Äôs viewpoint shifts sideways, closer elements appear to move faster across the visual field than distant elements, improving depth judgments even under monocular viewing.
