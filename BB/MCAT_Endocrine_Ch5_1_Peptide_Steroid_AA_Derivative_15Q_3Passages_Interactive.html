Chapter 5.1 — Hormone Structure, Signaling, and Classification
Standalone Questions
1. A hormone causes a rapid increase in intracellular cAMP within seconds of binding its target cell, and the effect disappears minutes after the hormone is removed. Which description best fits this hormone’s mechanism?
A. Intracellular receptor that directly binds DNA response elements
B. Membrane receptor that triggers a second-messenger cascade
C. Membrane receptor that is itself a transcription factor
D. Carrier-protein–bound hormone that slowly enters cells to alter gene expression
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: B.

Why: A fast, transient response mediated by cAMP strongly suggests a peptide (or catecholamine) signaling through a membrane receptor and second messengers.

Other choices:

  • A: Steroid and thyroid hormones bind intracellular receptors and primarily change transcription over hours to days.
  • C: Most membrane receptors are not transcription factors; gene regulation is classically via intracellular receptors.
  • D: Carrier-bound, lipid-soluble hormones typically produce slower, longer-lived effects.
2. Compared with peptide hormones, steroid hormones typically have which combination of properties?
A. Fast onset and short duration; freely soluble in blood
B. Fast onset and long duration; stored in secretory vesicles
C. Slow onset and short duration; act only via second messengers
D. Slow onset and long duration; require carrier proteins in blood
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: D.

Why: Steroid hormones are lipid-soluble, travel bound to carrier proteins, cross membranes, and alter transcription—so effects are slower but longer-lasting.

Other choices:

  • A: Peptides are water-soluble and tend to act quickly via second messengers, with shorter duration.
  • B: Steroids are not stored in vesicles for exocytosis; they are synthesized and diffuse out.
  • C: Steroids do not act only via second messengers; they commonly regulate gene transcription.
3. A researcher increases the concentration of a thyroid hormone–binding protein in plasma. All else equal, which immediate change is most expected?
A. Decreased free (active) thyroid hormone concentration
B. Increased hormone degradation in the liver due to faster delivery
C. Decreased total thyroid hormone concentration
D. Immediate increase in intracellular second messengers in target tissues
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A.

Why: When more binding protein is available, a larger fraction of hormone becomes protein-bound, transiently lowering the free (bioactive) fraction until compensatory secretion restores the set point.

Other choices:

  • B: Binding proteins mainly change distribution between free and bound; they do not directly accelerate hepatic degradation.
  • C: Total hormone (free + bound) often rises when binding capacity rises; total does not fall immediately.
  • D: Thyroid hormones act intracellularly to regulate transcription, not by rapidly producing second messengers.

Passage 1: Hepatocyte Signaling: Rapid vs Delayed Responses

In a study of hormone regulation of liver metabolism, cultured hepatocytes were exposed to two unknown hormones, Hormone A and Hormone B, at equal molar concentrations.

Within 15 seconds of adding Hormone A, intracellular cAMP rose sharply and protein kinase A activity increased. When the culture medium was replaced with hormone-free medium, cAMP returned to baseline within 3 minutes.

Hormone B produced no measurable change in cAMP, but after 4 hours the cells showed increased mRNA levels for a gluconeogenic enzyme and increased enzyme activity. The mRNA increase was blocked by actinomycin D, while the enzyme activity increase was reduced when a translation inhibitor was present.

Plasma samples from the animal model used in the study showed that Hormone A was mostly free in solution, whereas Hormone B was largely bound to a specific carrier protein. The investigators hypothesized that the two hormones act through different receptor locations and time scales.

How to reason:

  1. Separate fast second-messenger effects from slower gene-expression effects: seconds–minutes suggests membrane receptor + second messengers; hours suggests transcriptional regulation.
  2. Use molecular inhibitors as clues: blocking transcription implicates DNA-level control; blocking translation tests whether new proteins are needed.
  3. Use plasma transport clues: mostly bound in blood points to lipid-soluble hormones that often require carrier proteins.
  4. Pick the answer that connects multiple clues (time course + binding + inhibitors), not a single detail.

Passage 2: Pregnancy, Binding Proteins, and Thyroid Hormone Measurements

A 28-year-old pregnant patient in her second trimester has routine thyroid testing. She feels well, has no heat intolerance, and reports normal energy levels.

Her laboratory results show elevated total T4 but normal free T4 and a thyroid-stimulating hormone (TSH) level within the expected range for pregnancy. Additional testing reveals increased thyroxine-binding globulin (TBG) compared with nonpregnant reference values.

Her physician explains that during pregnancy, estrogen can increase hepatic production of certain plasma proteins, including hormone-binding globulins. As binding capacity rises, the distribution of hormone between free and bound pools changes until endocrine feedback restores the physiologically active fraction.

The patient asks why pregnancy is associated with high levels of multiple hormones, including progesterone, and why reference ranges for some endocrine tests differ during pregnancy.

How to reason:

  1. Decide whether the key lab number is total or free hormone; only the free fraction is immediately bioactive.
  2. Ask what happens when binding capacity increases: free hormone often drops transiently until feedback increases secretion.
  3. Link hormone chemistry to transport: steroids are lipid-soluble and tend to be carrier-bound; many peptides circulate free.
  4. Choose answers that explain the pattern (normal physiology despite altered totals), not answers that equate “high total” with disease.

Passage 3: Direct vs Tropic Hormones in the HPG Axis

To compare direct and tropic hormone signaling, a physiology class reviews the hypothalamus–pituitary–gonad (HPG) axis. Neurons in the hypothalamus secrete gonadotropin-releasing hormone (GnRH) into the hypophyseal portal system.

GnRH stimulates the anterior pituitary to release luteinizing hormone (LH) and follicle-stimulating hormone (FSH) into systemic circulation. LH then acts on the gonads to promote production of sex steroid hormones.

The class contrasts this with hormones such as insulin, which is released from the pancreas and acts directly on peripheral tissues to alter glucose uptake and metabolism without first stimulating another endocrine gland.

In a case study, a patient has damage confined to the anterior pituitary. Hypothalamic neurons are intact, but pituitary hormone output is markedly reduced. The clinician predicts downstream endocrine consequences based on the organization of the axis.

How to reason:

  1. First label each hormone as direct (acts on target tissue) or tropic (stimulates another endocrine gland).
  2. Trace the pathway step-by-step: hypothalamus → pituitary → gonads; a lesion upstream causes downstream decreases in dependent hormones.
  3. Use the definition, not the organ name: LH is tropic because it drives another gland’s hormone secretion.
  4. Eliminate choices that confuse where a hormone is produced vs where it acts.
