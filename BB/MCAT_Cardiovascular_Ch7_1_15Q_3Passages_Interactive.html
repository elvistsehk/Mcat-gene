MCAT Biology — Cardiovascular System (Ch. 7.1)
Standalone Questions (Q1–Q15)
1. Which chamber directly pumps blood into the pulmonary arteries during systole?
A. The right atrium
B. The right ventricle
C. The left atrium
D. The left ventricle
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: B

Blood flows from the right ventricle through the pulmonary valve into the pulmonary arteries, which carry deoxygenated blood to the lungs.

Why the other choices are not best:

  • Right atrium: receives systemic venous return; it does not pump into the pulmonary artery.
  • Left atrium: receives oxygenated blood from pulmonary veins.
  • Left ventricle: pumps into the aorta for systemic circulation.
2. Which valve prevents backflow from the left ventricle into the left atrium?
A. The mitral (bicuspid) valve
B. The tricuspid valve
C. The aortic (semilunar) valve
D. The pulmonary (semilunar) valve
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A

The atrioventricular valve on the left side is the mitral valve; it closes during ventricular systole to prevent regurgitation into the left atrium.

Why the other choices are not best:

  • Tricuspid: right atrium → right ventricle.
  • Aortic valve: left ventricle → aorta (prevents backflow from aorta).
  • Pulmonary valve: right ventricle → pulmonary artery.
3. Place the cardiac conduction pathway in the correct order of excitation.
A. AV node → SA node → Purkinje fibers → bundle of His
B. SA node → bundle of His → AV node → Purkinje fibers
C. SA node → AV node → bundle of His → Purkinje fibers
D. Purkinje fibers → bundle of His → AV node → SA node
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: C

Normal excitation begins at the SA node, spreads across the atria, pauses at the AV node, then travels through the bundle of His and Purkinje fibers to coordinate ventricular contraction.

Why the other choices are not best:

  • Choice B: swaps the initiating pacemaker (SA) and the AV node.
  • Choice C: incorrectly places the AV node after the His bundle.
  • Choice D: reverses the entire sequence.
4. Which statement best explains why the AV node delays the electrical signal?
A. It initiates ventricular depolarization when the SA node fails
B. It prevents atrial contraction so ventricles fill passively
C. It increases the strength of ventricular contraction by releasing catecholamines
D. It allows the ventricles more time to fill before they contract
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: D

The AV nodal delay ensures atrial contraction (including the atrial kick) precedes ventricular systole, optimizing end-diastolic volume.

Why the other choices are not best:

  • SA node failure backup: AV node can serve as a pacemaker, but the delay is primarily for filling.
  • Prevents atrial contraction: the atria still contract; the delay coordinates timing.
  • Catecholamines: contractility changes are autonomic/hormonal, not the AV node’s job.
5. A patient is given a β-blocker after a myocardial infarction. Which immediate cardiovascular effect is most consistent with this therapy?
A. Increased heart rate and increased contractility to maintain cardiac output
B. Decreased systemic vascular resistance by opening venous valves
C. Decreased heart rate and decreased contractility, reducing myocardial oxygen demand
D. Increased stroke volume by strengthening the atrial kick
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: C

β-blockers blunt sympathetic stimulation of the heart, lowering heart rate and contractility so the injured myocardium consumes less oxygen.

Why the other choices are not best:

  • Increase HR/contractility: that is sympathetic activation (opposite of β-blockade).
  • Venous valves: are mechanical structures; drugs do not “open” them to lower resistance.
  • Atrial kick: is a filling contribution; β-blockers do not specifically increase it.
6. On an ECG, which electrical event most closely precedes ventricular contraction?
A. The QRS complex (ventricular depolarization)
B. The P wave (atrial repolarization)
C. The T wave (ventricular repolarization)
D. The PR segment (atrial mechanical systole)
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A

Depolarization comes just before contraction. The QRS complex represents ventricular depolarization, which is immediately followed by ventricular systole.

Why the other choices are not best:

  • P wave: represents atrial depolarization, not atrial repolarization.
  • T wave: is ventricular repolarization, which occurs after ventricular contraction.
  • PR segment: reflects conduction delay; it is not a primary depolarization spike.
7. Which vessel type has a single endothelial cell layer and is the primary site of exchange of gases and nutrients?
A. Arteries
B. Veins
C. Arterioles
D. Capillaries
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: D

Capillaries are one cell thick, allowing diffusion of O2/CO2, nutrients, and wastes between blood and tissues.

Why the other choices are not best:

  • Arteries/arterioles: have thicker walls with more smooth muscle for pressure control.
  • Veins: are thin-walled but not specialized for exchange like capillaries.
8. Which feature best distinguishes veins from arteries in the systemic circulation?
A. Veins have thicker smooth muscle to maintain high pressure
B. Veins have lower pressure and often contain valves to prevent backflow
C. Veins carry oxygenated blood, while arteries carry deoxygenated blood
D. Veins have the largest elastic recoil to sustain diastolic pressure
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: B

Systemic veins are low-pressure, compliant vessels; many contain valves that help return blood against gravity and prevent pooling.

Why the other choices are not best:

  • Thicker smooth muscle: describes arteries/arterioles.
  • Oxygenation rule: is reversed for systemic vessels; exceptions are pulmonary/umbilical.
  • Elastic recoil sustaining pressure: is a key arterial property, not venous.
9. Why is the right side of the heart less muscular than the left side?
A. It receives oxygenated blood and therefore requires less force
B. It has fewer valves and therefore needs less muscle
C. It contains the SA node, which reduces mechanical workload
D. It pumps blood to the nearby lungs at lower resistance, so high pressure is unnecessary and could damage pulmonary vessels
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: D

Pulmonary circulation is short and low resistance; excessive right-sided pressure would transmit to delicate pulmonary vasculature and cause injury.

Why the other choices are not best:

  • Oxygenated blood: right side receives deoxygenated blood from the body.
  • Valves: both sides have AV and semilunar valves.
  • SA node location: does not explain wall thickness; it sets rhythm.
10. Which sequence correctly traces blood flow through the heart starting at the right atrium and ending at the aorta?
A. Right atrium → tricuspid valve → right ventricle → pulmonary valve → pulmonary arteries → lungs → pulmonary veins → left atrium → mitral valve → left ventricle → aortic valve → aorta
B. Right atrium → mitral valve → right ventricle → aortic valve → aorta
C. Right atrium → tricuspid valve → right ventricle → aorta → lungs → left atrium
D. Right atrium → pulmonary veins → left atrium → tricuspid valve → right ventricle → aorta
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A

This route reflects the two pumps in series: right heart to lungs (pulmonary circulation), then left heart to body (systemic circulation).

Why the other choices are not best:

  • B: assigns left-sided valve (mitral) to the right side and skips lungs.
  • C: incorrectly sends right ventricle directly to aorta and misorders lung step.
  • D: confuses pulmonary veins (return from lungs) and misroutes valves/chambers.
11. Which statement about cardiac muscle explains coordinated ventricular contraction?
A. Cardiac myocytes are independent so each cell contracts at its own pace
B. Semilunar valves propagate the electrical signal to the ventricles
C. Gap junctions in intercalated discs electrically couple adjacent myocytes, allowing rapid spread of depolarization
D. Ventricles contract first, then atria contract to top off filling
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: C

Intercalated discs contain gap junctions that permit ions to flow between cells, synchronizing depolarization and contraction across the myocardium.

Why the other choices are not best:

  • Independent contraction: would produce ineffective pumping.
  • Valves: are mechanical structures, not electrical conductors.
  • Ventricle-first: would reduce filling; normal order is atria then ventricles.
12. During ventricular systole, which valve state is expected in a normal heartbeat?
A. Atrioventricular valves closed; semilunar valves open (after ventricular pressure exceeds arterial pressure)
B. Atrioventricular valves open; semilunar valves closed
C. Both atrioventricular and semilunar valves open
D. Both atrioventricular and semilunar valves closed throughout systole
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A

As ventricles contract, AV valves close to prevent backflow into atria; once ventricular pressure surpasses aortic/pulmonary pressure, semilunar valves open to eject blood.

Why the other choices are not best:

  • AV open / semilunar closed: describes ventricular filling (diastole).
  • Both open: would cause backflow and loss of pressure.
  • Both closed throughout: would prevent ejection; semilunar valves must open to pump blood out.
13. Which heart sound is produced by closure of the atrioventricular valves?
A. S1 (“lub”)
B. S2 (“dub”)
C. S3 (associated with rapid filling)
D. S4 (associated with stiff ventricle)
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A

S1 occurs at the start of systole when the mitral and tricuspid valves close to prevent backflow into the atria.

Why the other choices are not best:

  • S2: semilunar valve closure at the end of systole.
  • S3/S4: extra sounds related to filling dynamics or stiffness, not AV closure.
14. A person hyperactivates the sympathetic nervous system during a sprint. Which paired effects on the heart are most expected?
A. Increased heart rate and increased contractility
B. Decreased heart rate and decreased contractility
C. Increased heart rate and decreased contractility
D. Decreased heart rate and increased contractility
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A

Sympathetic stimulation (fight-or-flight) increases SA node firing and enhances myocardial contractility to boost cardiac output during exertion.

Why the other choices are not best:

  • Decreased contractility: is not a typical sympathetic effect on the heart.
15. If heart rate increases substantially while stroke volume stays constant, what happens to cardiac output?
A. It decreases because diastolic filling time shortens
B. It increases proportionally (CO = HR × SV)
C. It stays constant because cardiac output is fixed near 5 L/min
D. It becomes zero because valves cannot open fast enough
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: B

Cardiac output is defined as HR multiplied by SV. If SV is unchanged, raising HR raises CO, at least over physiologic ranges.

Why the other choices are not best:

  • Shortened filling time: can limit SV at extreme rates, but the question holds SV constant.
  • Fixed CO: CO varies with exercise/rest.
  • CO becomes zero: would require severe rhythm pathology, not simply higher HR with constant SV.
Passages (3 passages × 3 questions)
Passage 1: Palpitations and a Delayed Signal

A 34-year-old patient reports intermittent palpitations and lightheadedness during brisk walking. An ECG obtained during symptoms shows a normal P wave before each QRS complex, but the time between the onset of the P wave and the onset of the QRS complex is noticeably prolonged compared with a prior tracing. The clinician explains that a key junction in the cardiac conduction system intentionally slows impulse propagation.

On physical exam, the patient’s blood pressure is normal at rest. The clinician notes that most ventricular filling occurs passively when the ventricles relax, but atrial contraction contributes an additional volume of blood that can meaningfully support output in some patients. The clinician calls this contribution the atrial kick.

The clinician also explains that coordinated contraction relies on specialized cell-to-cell connections within cardiac muscle. These connections allow the electrical wave to spread efficiently so that the ventricles contract as a unit rather than as isolated patches.

How to reason:

  1. Identify what is abnormal on the ECG : a prolonged time between atrial depolarization and ventricular depolarization points to slowed conduction between atria and ventricles.
  2. Map the ECG interval to anatomy: the structure that produces a purposeful delay is the AV node .
  3. Use the function of the delay: it supports ventricular filling before systole; link this to the idea of the atrial kick .
  4. For coordination, look for the structural feature that enables rapid electrical spread: gap junctions in intercalated discs .
  5. Choose the option that requires an inference from physiology (timing and mechanics), not a direct quote.
16. In Passage 1, the prolonged time from the start of the P wave to the start of the QRS complex most directly implicates slowed conduction at which structure?
A. The SA node
B. The bundle of His
C. The AV node
D. The Purkinje fibers
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: C

The PR interval reflects conduction from atria to ventricles; the AV node is the physiologic site of delay, so slowed AV nodal conduction prolongs this interval.

Why the other choices are not best:

  • SA node: sets the rhythm and begins atrial depolarization; it does not produce the atrioventricular delay.
  • Bundle of His/Purkinje fibers: distribute signals through ventricles; issues here more strongly affect QRS morphology/ventricular activation timing.
17. Based on Passage 1, which change would most directly reduce the contribution of atrial contraction to ventricular filling?
A. Increased elasticity of the aorta
B. Closure of semilunar valves earlier in systole
C. Increased venous valves in the legs
D. Loss of coordinated atrial contraction prior to ventricular systole
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: D

The atrial kick is the added blood delivered by atrial systole; if atria fail to contract effectively in coordination, that extra contribution falls.

Why the other choices are not best:

  • Arterial elasticity: affects pressure maintenance, not the atrial kick.
  • Semilunar timing: affects ejection, not atrial filling contribution.
  • Leg vein valves: influence venous return mechanics, not atrial contraction.
18. Passage 1 mentions specialized connections that allow the ventricles to contract as a unit. Which structure provides direct electrical coupling between adjacent cardiac myocytes?
A. Tight junctions within endothelial cells
B. Gap junctions within intercalated discs
C. Desmosomes between red blood cells
D. Collagen fibers in valve leaflets
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: B

Gap junctions permit ion flow between neighboring myocytes, propagating depolarization rapidly to synchronize contraction.

Passage 2: Long Flight, Leg Pain, and Sudden Dyspnea

A 47-year-old patient returns from a long transoceanic flight and notices swelling and tenderness in one calf over the next day. Two days later, the patient develops sudden shortness of breath and sharp chest pain that worsens with deep inspiration. In the emergency department, the clinician suspects a clot that formed in the deep veins of the leg and traveled to the lungs.

The clinician explains that venous blood from the lower body must move upward against gravity to return to the heart, and that many large veins contain valves that reduce backward flow. The clinician also points out that veins are highly compliant and can hold a large fraction of total blood volume at any given time.

During the exam, the clinician asks the patient to flex the calf repeatedly and explains that contraction of surrounding skeletal muscle can squeeze veins and help propel blood toward the heart. The clinician warns that prolonged immobility can promote blood pooling and stasis, increasing the chance of clot formation.

How to reason:

  1. First, recognize the pattern: calf swelling after immobility suggests deep vein thrombosis ; sudden dyspnea/chest pain suggests pulmonary embolism .
  2. Use the anatomy route: a venous clot from the leg reaches the right heart, then enters the pulmonary arteries and lodges in pulmonary vessels.
  3. Connect structure to function: venous valves limit backflow; skeletal muscle pump helps overcome gravity.
  4. Differentiate vessels: veins are low-pressure and compliant; arteries have more smooth muscle and elastic recoil.
  5. Pick answers that explain mechanism (stasis, route, pressure) rather than simply naming the diagnosis.
19. In Passage 2, a clot that forms in a deep leg vein and embolizes to the lungs would most likely travel next through which cardiac chamber immediately before entering the pulmonary artery?
A. The left atrium
B. The left ventricle
C. The right ventricle
D. The aorta
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: C

Venous thrombi return via venae cavae to the right atrium, pass through the tricuspid valve to the right ventricle, then are ejected through the pulmonary valve into the pulmonary arteries.

Why the other choices are not best:

  • Left atrium/left ventricle/aorta: are part of systemic outflow after oxygenation; they are reached after blood passes through the lungs.

Passage 3: Hypertension, Arterial Elasticity, and Heart Sounds

A 62-year-old patient with long-standing hypertension is evaluated for exertional fatigue. The clinician explains that arteries are not just passive tubes: their elastic walls stretch during ventricular ejection and recoil during ventricular relaxation, helping to maintain blood flow between heartbeats.

On auscultation, the clinician hears the typical “lub dub” sequence and explains that each sound corresponds to valve closure. The clinician also notes that some extra sounds can occur when the ventricle is stiff or when pressures are abnormally high.

During counseling, the clinician emphasizes why the left ventricle must generate higher pressures than the right ventricle: systemic arteries create substantial resistance and the left ventricle must push blood to distant tissues. The clinician cautions that if the right ventricle generated systemic-level pressures, delicate pulmonary vessels would be exposed to damaging forces.

How to reason:

  1. Translate the physiology: arterial elastic recoil helps sustain pressure during diastole when the ventricle is relaxed.
  2. Map sounds to valves: S1 is AV closure at the start of systole; S2 is semilunar closure at the end of systole.
  3. Link chronic hypertension to mechanics: increased resistance means the left ventricle must generate higher pressure (greater afterload ).
  4. Use side-to-side comparison: the right ventricle serves low-resistance pulmonary circulation; high pressures would injure pulmonary vessels.
  5. Choose the answer that follows from the chain of cause → effect rather than restating a definition.
22. Passage 3 emphasizes that arterial elasticity helps maintain pressure during ventricular relaxation. If large arteries suddenly became much less elastic, what would be the most direct change in blood pressure profile?
A. Systolic pressure would become zero because the ventricle cannot contract
B. Mean arterial pressure would increase because arteries cannot stretch
C. Diastolic pressure would fall more than normal because recoil would be reduced
D. Pulse pressure would disappear because systolic and diastolic would become identical
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: C

Elastic arteries store energy during systole and release it during diastole. Losing elasticity reduces this buffering, allowing pressure to drop more during diastole.

Why the other choices are not best:

  • Systolic becomes zero: systolic pressure comes from ventricular contraction; it would not become zero solely from reduced elasticity.
  • MAP increases: less compliance often increases systolic pressure, but the key immediate change highlighted is loss of diastolic support.
  • Pulse pressure disappears: it would not vanish; differences may actually widen.

A. Closure of the mitral and tricuspid (AV) valves at the start of systole
B. Closure of the aortic and pulmonary (semilunar) valves at the end of systole
C. Opening of the aortic valve during ventricular ejection
D. Opening of the mitral valve during ventricular filling
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: B

S2 corresponds to semilunar valve closure when ventricular pressure falls below arterial pressure at the end of systole, preventing backflow into ventricles.

Why the other choices are not best:

  • AV closure: produces S1.
  • Valve opening: is not what generates the classic heart sounds described.
  • Mitral opening: occurs in diastole and is not S2.
24. Passage 3 compares the left and right ventricles. Which inference best explains why the left ventricle must generate higher pressures?
A. Pulmonary arteries contain more smooth muscle than systemic arteries
B. The right atrium receives more blood than the left atrium
C. Oxygenated blood is more viscous and therefore requires less pressure to move
D. Systemic arteries create greater resistance and longer distribution distance, increasing afterload on the left ventricle
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: D

Systemic circulation has higher resistance and longer path length; the left ventricle pumps against this higher afterload to deliver blood to distant tissues.

Why the other choices are not best:

  • Pulmonary arteries: are lower resistance relative to systemic circulation.
  • Atria receive volume: in a closed circuit, flows match over time; the key difference is resistance/afterload.
