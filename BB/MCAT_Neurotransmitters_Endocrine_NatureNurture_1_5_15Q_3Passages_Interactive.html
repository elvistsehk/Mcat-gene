Standalone Questions (Q1–Q15)
1. A drug that mimics a neurotransmitter by binding the same receptor and producing the same response is best described as a(n):
A. Agonist
B. Antagonist
C. Second messenger
D. Reuptake inhibitor
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A.

Why: An agonist binds to a receptor and activates it, producing a biological response similar to the endogenous ligand/neurotransmitter.

Other choices:

  • B: An antagonist blocks receptor activation or prevents the neurotransmitter from exerting its effect.
  • C: Second messengers are intracellular signaling molecules downstream of receptor activation.
  • D: A reuptake inhibitor blocks transporter-mediated clearance but does not necessarily mimic binding at the receptor.
2. Which pairing most accurately matches neurotransmitter with a common function?
A. Endorphins — circadian rhythm regulation
B. Glutamate — inhibitory stabilization
C. Acetylcholine — voluntary muscle control and parasympathetic signaling
D. Dopamine — primary sensory relay
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: C.

Why: Acetylcholine is used at neuromuscular junctions for voluntary skeletal muscle control and is the primary neurotransmitter for the parasympathetic nervous system.

Other choices:

  • A: Circadian rhythms are classically linked to melatonin (pineal), not endorphins.
  • B: Glutamate is typically excitatory; inhibitory stabilization is more associated with GABA/glycine.
  • D: Dopamine is important for movement/posture and reward-related processing; sensory relay is thalamic, not dopamine.

Why: Most postganglionic sympathetic neurons use norepinephrine at target organs; acetylcholine is used by the somatic efferent pathway, parasympathetic postganglionic neurons, and the unusual sympathetic fibers to sweat glands.

  • B: Parasympathetic postganglionic neurons commonly release acetylcholine at targets.

Passage 1: Neurochemistry and Drug Mechanisms

A neuroscience team tests two experimental drugs in a rodent model of anxiety. Drug A binds to the same receptor as a naturally occurring neurotransmitter and produces a similar downstream effect. Drug B binds at the same receptor site but prevents the neurotransmitter from producing its usual effect. Both drugs cross the blood–brain barrier.

During a stress-induction task, rodents also show reduced pain behaviors despite exposure to a mild noxious stimulus. The investigators suspect that endogenous neuromodulators are contributing to analgesia.

Separately, the team compares neurotransmitter profiles across conditions and observes that some molecules act rapidly at synapses, while others have slower, longer-lasting effects consistent with peptide signaling.

How to reason:

  1. Translate definitions: mimics transmitter at the same receptor → agonist; blocks transmitter action → antagonist.
  2. Stress-related analgesia points to endorphins (opioid-like natural painkillers).
  3. Peptide neuromodulators tend to be slower and longer-lasting than classic fast neurotransmitters.
  4. Pick the answer that matches the mechanism (receptor activation vs blockade), not just the drug’s label.

A. Prevent a neurotransmitter from activating its receptor
B. Bind a receptor and mimic the neurotransmitter’s effect
C. Convert a neurotransmitter into a hormone in the adrenal cortex
D. Increase neurotransmitter synthesis by the presynaptic neuron
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A.

Why: An antagonist blocks receptor activation (or blocks binding), thereby reducing or preventing the neurotransmitter’s effect at the postsynaptic cell.

Passage 2: Endocrine Links to the Brain Under Stress and Dehydration

A medical student observes that an anxious patient has a rapid heart rate and reports feeling ‘keyed up.’ In discussion, a resident explains that the sympathetic response can be mediated both by local neurotransmission and by systemic hormone release.

Later the same day, the team evaluates a dehydrated athlete with elevated plasma osmolarity. Laboratory testing supports increased water conservation by the kidneys. The attending reminds the group that a hormone released from the posterior pituitary increases water reabsorption.

In a teaching diagram, the attending highlights that the hypothalamus sits at the interface of the nervous system and endocrine control and regulates pituitary output through a specialized portal circulation.

How to reason:

  1. Separate local vs systemic: sympathetic neurotransmission often uses norepinephrine, while systemic hormone release uses epinephrine from the adrenal medulla.
  2. Dehydration (high osmolarity) triggers posterior pituitary release of ADH to increase water reabsorption.
  3. The brain–endocrine bridge is the hypothalamus, which regulates the pituitary via the hypophyseal portal system.
  4. Use the stimulus → hormone → effect chain to select the best answer.

Passage 3: Nature vs Nurture Study Designs

A behavioral genetics group investigates whether a trait is more influenced by heredity or environment. In one dataset, rates of a psychiatric diagnosis are higher among biological relatives of affected individuals than in the general population. However, the researchers note that relatives often share the same household environment.

In another dataset, concordance rates for the same trait are compared between monozygotic (identical) and dizygotic (fraternal) twins. The researchers also identify a subgroup of twins who were raised apart in different families.

Finally, the researchers analyze adopted individuals and compare trait similarity to biological parents versus adoptive parents. They aim to choose the design that most cleanly separates shared genes from shared environments.

How to reason:

  1. Family studies are limited because family members share genes and environment (confound).
  2. Twin studies use genetic relatedness: MZ (~100% shared genes) vs DZ (~50%); higher MZ concordance supports genetics.
  3. Strongest separation comes from twins raised apart and from adoption studies comparing biological vs adoptive relatives.
  4. Pick the choice that best isolates genetic similarity while holding environment constant (or vice versa).
