1. Which forebrain structure acts as a relay station for most incoming sensory information (except smell)?
A. Hypothalamus
B. Thalamus
C. Cerebellum
D. Amygdala
✅Correct
❌Incorrect

Correct Answer: B.

Why: The thalamus sorts and transmits incoming sensory signals to appropriate regions of the cerebral cortex; olfaction largely bypasses it.

Passage 1: Drives, Homeostasis, and Endocrine Output

A research team studies how forebrain structures regulate homeostasis and motivated behavior. In one experiment, rats receive targeted lesions in different hypothalamic regions and are monitored for feeding behavior, body mass changes, and water intake over two weeks.

Group X shows profound reduction in eating and drinking immediately after surgery, requiring tube feeding for survival. Group Y shows dramatic overeating despite normal access to food, and gains substantial weight. A third group displays disrupted sleep patterns and abnormal thermoregulation when placed in cool or warm environments.

Separately, investigators test dehydration responses. They observe that rising plasma osmolarity triggers increased release of a hormone from the posterior pituitary. Tracing studies show the releasing neurons originate in the hypothalamus and project axons to the posterior pituitary.

How to reason:

  1. Match lesion → behavior: lack of eating/drinking suggests lateral hypothalamus damage; overeating/obesity suggests VMH damage.
  2. Sleep/temperature disruption points toward anterior hypothalamus roles.
  3. Dehydration response: posterior pituitary releases ADH made in the hypothalamus.
  4. Choose the option whose mechanism best fits the evidence pattern.

Passage 2: Emotion, Fear, and Memory After Temporal Lobe Surgery

A 29-year-old patient undergoes removal of portions of the medial temporal lobe to treat severe epilepsy. After surgery, the patient’s general intelligence is largely intact, but they cannot reliably remember new names or events from day to day.

Family members report the patient seems unusually fearless. The patient approaches unfamiliar animals without caution and shows diminished reaction to threatening stimuli. Clinicians review classic work linking particular limbic structures to defensive behavior and to consolidation of new memories.

Because the resection included multiple nearby structures, the team emphasizes that some symptoms may arise from more than one damaged region, and careful matching of symptoms to mechanisms is required.

How to reason:

  1. New long-term memory formation failure points to hippocampus → anterograde amnesia.
  2. Reduced fear/aggression aligns with amygdala involvement.
  3. When symptoms overlap, identify which structure is the most direct causal driver for each symptom.
  4. Pick the choice that matches the specific deficit described in the stem.

Passage 3: Language, Laterality, and the Corpus Callosum

A patient with intractable epilepsy undergoes surgery that severs the corpus callosum to limit seizure spread. After recovery, the patient speaks fluently and follows spoken instructions.

In a lab task, an object is placed in the patient’s left hand while eyes are closed. The patient can manipulate it correctly but cannot name it aloud. When the same object is placed in the right hand, the patient names it immediately.

The experimenters explain that most language functions are lateralized to a dominant hemisphere in most people, and that severing the corpus callosum prevents information from crossing between hemispheres.

How to reason:

  1. Left-hand sensory input projects mainly to the right hemisphere (contralateral).
  2. Speech/language is usually in the left (dominant) hemisphere. Without the corpus callosum, info can’t cross.
  3. So the object can be recognized but not named when only the left hand is used.
  4. Select the answer that explicitly ties severed interhemispheric transfer to the observed naming deficit.
