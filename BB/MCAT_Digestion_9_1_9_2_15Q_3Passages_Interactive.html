Standalone Questions (Q1–Q15)
1. A student says, “Chewing a cracker breaks it into smaller pieces but does not change the molecules.” Which term best describes the chewing process?
A. Absorption
B. Mechanical digestion
C. Intracellular digestion
D. Chemical digestion
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: B.

Why: Mechanical digestion is physical breakdown that increases surface area without cleaving chemical bonds.

Other choices:

  • A: Absorption is transport of digested products into circulation, not breaking food apart.
  • C: Intracellular digestion refers to metabolic oxidation inside cells, not processing food in the lumen.
  • D: Chemical digestion requires enzymatic bond cleavage (e.g., glycosidic or peptide bonds).

Why: Digestive gland secretion is primarily stimulated by parasympathetic activity (rest-and-digest).

  • A: The enteric nervous system can operate locally, but major regulation comes from the autonomic system.

  • C: Somatic motor controls skeletal muscle, not exocrine gland secretion.

Passages (3 passages × 3 questions)
Passage 1: Autonomic Control and Early Digestion

A graduate student volunteers for a physiology study examining digestion under different stress conditions. On Day 1, the student arrives relaxed and is given a standardized starch-rich snack and a measured volume of water. Within minutes, the student produces abundant watery saliva, forms a cohesive bolus, and swallows without difficulty.

Investigators record robust peristaltic waves that move the bolus down the esophagus. They also observe normal, steady gastric mixing after the bolus reaches the stomach, and they note that the participant subjectively feels “sleepy” after finishing the snack.

On Day 2, the same snack is given immediately after the student completes a stressful public-speaking task. The student reports dry mouth and describes saliva as “thick and tacky.” The investigators record reduced gut motility and reduced blood flow to the digestive tract, and the student reports the first phase of swallowing feels more effortful even though food still reaches the stomach.

The investigators remind trainees that chewing increases the surface area of food without breaking covalent bonds, whereas enzymatic activity cleaves chemical bonds. They add that digestive activity is coordinated by local neural circuits in the gut wall, but is strongly modulated by autonomic input, and that the upper and lower esophagus differ in muscle type and control.

How to reason:

  1. First, connect stress to physiology: stress → **higher sympathetic** tone and **lower parasympathetic** tone.
  2. Then map to GI effects: sympathetic tone ↓ **blood flow** and ↓ **peristalsis/motility**; parasympathetic tone normally ↑ secretions and motility.
  3. For “tacky saliva,” recall that sympathetic input can ↑ **viscosity** and create a dry-mouth sensation.
  4. For swallowing differences, use anatomy: upper esophagus has **skeletal** muscle (more somatic influence), lower esophagus is **smooth** muscle (autonomic/enteric).
  5. For digestion type, classify actions: chewing = **mechanical**; enzymes breaking bonds = **chemical**.

Passage 2: Acid, Protection, and Protein Digestion

A 48-year-old patient takes high doses of an over-the-counter pain medication for chronic back pain. Over several weeks, the patient develops epigastric discomfort that worsens shortly after meals. Endoscopy reveals inflamed gastric mucosa with a small ulcer.

The clinician explains that the stomach must maintain a harsh environment to help digest proteins and kill many microbes, yet it must also protect itself from autodigestion. The clinician notes that post-meal neural signals can increase gastric secretions and mixing, and that the gastric lining normally produces a barrier that resists both acidity and proteolysis.

In the gastric lumen, protein digestion is initiated by an enzyme that functions best at low pH. This enzyme is produced as an inactive precursor and becomes active only after exposure to the acidic environment. The clinician also explains that one cell type releases a protective secretion rich in bicarbonate, while another cell type releases a glycoprotein required for normal absorption of a specific vitamin later in digestion.

The patient asks why the pain medication increases ulcer risk and why symptoms often flare after meals rather than between meals, even when diet composition seems similar from day to day.

How to reason:

  1. Identify what the drug disrupts: many NSAIDs reduce the stomach’s **mucus/bicarbonate** barrier → mucosa becomes vulnerable.
  2. Link symptom timing: meal cues ↑ **parasympathetic (vagal)** input and hormones → ↑ acid secretion and gastric mixing.
  3. Connect enzyme activation: low pH converts **pepsinogen** to **pepsin**, increasing proteolysis in the lumen.
  4. Keep cell products straight: **mucous** cells protect; **parietal** cells make acid + **intrinsic factor**; **chief** cells make pepsinogen.
  5. Then predict consequences: less protection + more acid/protease → worse post-meal irritation.

Passage 3: Duodenal Digestion and Malabsorption Patterns

Two patients present with post-meal gastrointestinal symptoms. Patient A reports bloating and flatulence after ice cream and soft cheeses, and sometimes develops watery diarrhea within hours. Patient B reports greasy, floating stools after fatty meals and notes that symptoms improve when eating very low-fat foods.

Neither patient has fever, and both have normal appetite. A clinician suspects that both cases involve incomplete breakdown of nutrients within the small intestine lumen, but the underlying missing factor differs between patients.

In a teaching session, the instructor reviews that the duodenum supplies brush-border enzymes that complete carbohydrate and peptide digestion. If a disaccharide cannot be cleaved, it can retain water osmotically and can be fermented by gut bacteria, producing gas as a byproduct.

The instructor also reviews that bile salts are amphipathic molecules that disperse dietary lipids into droplets and micelles, increasing surface area and keeping fats accessible in an aqueous environment. Water-soluble pancreatic lipase then cleaves ester bonds within lipids. The instructor adds that pancreatic bicarbonate creates a more alkaline pH that supports many digestive enzymes.

Trainees are asked to predict which missing factor best explains each patient’s symptoms and which hormones are expected to rise when acidic, fatty chyme enters the duodenum.

How to reason:

  1. Use symptom patterns: gas + watery diarrhea after dairy → likely missing **lactase** (a brush-border disaccharidase).
  2. For greasy, floating stools after fat, think impaired lipid handling: missing **bile** (micelles) and/or missing **lipase** (ester cleavage).
  3. Map hormone triggers: acidic chyme → **secretin** (↑ bicarbonate, ↓ gastric acid, slows motility).
  4. Map fat/amino acids triggers: **CCK** (↑ bile release and pancreatic enzymes; promotes satiety).
  5. Separate steps: bile does **mechanical** dispersion (micelles); lipase does **chemical** digestion (bond hydrolysis).
