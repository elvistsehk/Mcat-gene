Cardiovascular System (Ch. 7.3)
Standalone Questions (Q1–Q15)
1. Blood pressure measured with a sphygmomanometer in the systemic circulation is best described as:
A. Absolute pressure including atmospheric pressure
B. Pressure exclusively within the pulmonary arteries
C. Pressure generated only during ventricular relaxation
D. Gauge pressure above atmospheric pressure
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: D

A sphygmomanometer reports gauge pressure: the pressure above atmospheric pressure, expressed as systolic/diastolic values.

Why the other choices are not best:

  • Absolute pressure: would include atmospheric pressure (~760 mmHg) and is not what is reported clinically.
  • Pulmonary arteries: systemic blood pressure refers to the systemic circuit, not pulmonary pressures.
  • Only during relaxation: blood pressure has both systolic and diastolic components.
2. Where does the largest drop in blood pressure normally occur in the systemic circulation?
A. Across the aorta
B. Across the capillaries
C. Across the arterioles
D. Across the large veins
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: C

Arterioles are the primary resistance vessels, producing the greatest pressure drop and protecting fragile capillaries from arterial pressures.

Why the other choices are not best:

  • Aorta: pressure remains high in large elastic arteries.
  • Capillaries: pressure is lower already; the biggest drop happens earlier.
  • Large veins: venous pressures are low and change less dramatically.
3. Using the analogy to Ohm’s law, the best cardiovascular analog of V = IR is:
A. CO = ΔP × TPR
B. TPR = CO + ΔP
C. ΔP = TPR / CO
D. ΔP = CO × TPR
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: D

Pressure gradient (ΔP) drives flow (cardiac output) against resistance (TPR), mirroring voltage, current, and resistance.

Why the other choices are not best:

  • CO = ΔP × TPR: incorrectly multiplies flow by resistance again.
  • TPR = CO + ΔP: adds unlike quantities.
  • ΔP = TPR / CO: inverts the relationship; higher flow would not reduce ΔP if resistance is fixed.
4. Opening additional systemic capillary beds (more parallel pathways) would be expected to:
A. Increase total peripheral resistance
B. Leave resistance unchanged because capillaries are not resistors
C. Stop blood flow to existing tissues by “stealing” pressure
D. Decrease total peripheral resistance
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: D

Adding parallel pathways lowers total resistance, analogous to adding resistors in parallel in a circuit.

Why the other choices are not best:

  • Increase resistance: parallel networks reduce resistance, not increase it.
  • Capillaries not resistors: microvasculature contributes to resistance, especially arterioles and small arteries.
  • Stops blood flow: redistribution can occur, but the primary effect on TPR is a decrease.
5. A key mechanism by which baroreceptors help restore low blood pressure is by triggering:
A. Increased parasympathetic tone causing vasoconstriction
B. Immediate conversion of fibrinogen to fibrin
C. Increased sympathetic tone causing vasoconstriction
D. Decreased heart contractility and heart rate
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: C

Low BP reduces baroreceptor firing, increasing sympathetic output to constrict vessels (raising TPR) and support pressure.

Why the other choices are not best:

  • Parasympathetic vasoconstriction: parasympathetic activity generally slows the heart and does not drive widespread vasoconstriction.
  • Fibrinogen to fibrin: is coagulation, not acute BP regulation.
  • Decreased contractility/HR: would worsen hypotension.
6. In response to high blood pressure and atrial stretch, atrial natriuretic peptide (ANP) most directly tends to:
A. Increase aldosterone release to retain sodium
B. Increase ADH release to retain water without changing osmolarity
C. Increase total peripheral resistance by constricting arterioles
D. Promote salt and water excretion to lower blood volume and pressure
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: D

ANP is released with atrial stretch and acts as a (weak) natural diuretic/natriuretic, lowering volume and pressure.

Why the other choices are not best:

  • Increase aldosterone: aldosterone retains sodium and water—opposite of ANP’s role.
  • Increase ADH: ADH retains water; ANP promotes excretion.
  • Increase TPR: ANP’s primary effect is volume/salt loss rather than generalized vasoconstriction.
7. The bicarbonate buffer system in red blood cells is catalyzed by which enzyme?
A. Carbonic anhydrase
B. Carbonic reductase
C. Pepsin
D. Thromboplastin
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A

Carbonic anhydrase catalyzes CO2 + H2O ⇌ H2CO3, facilitating rapid buffering and CO2 transport as bicarbonate.

Why the other choices are not best:

  • Carbonic reductase: is not the physiologic enzyme for this reaction.
  • Pepsin: is a gastric protease unrelated to buffering.
  • Thromboplastin: participates in coagulation, not acid–base chemistry.
8. In blood, the majority of carbon dioxide is transported primarily as:
A. Dissolved CO2 gas in plasma
B. Carbonic acid (H2CO3) that remains undissociated
C. CO2 bound to hemoglobin as the dominant form
D. Bicarbonate ion (HCO3−)
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: D

Most CO2 is converted to bicarbonate (and H+) in RBCs, which is highly soluble and an efficient transport form.

Why the other choices are not best:

  • Dissolved CO2: only a small fraction is carried dissolved due to low solubility.
  • Undissociated H2CO3: carbonic acid largely dissociates; bicarbonate predominates.
  • CO2 bound to hemoglobin: some binds, but it is not the dominant transport form compared with bicarbonate.
9. A right shift of the oxyhemoglobin dissociation curve generally indicates:
A. Increased affinity and decreased unloading at tissues
B. Increased oxygen saturation in the lungs at all PaO2 values
C. Decreased hemoglobin affinity for oxygen and increased unloading at tissues
D. Reduced CO2 transport as bicarbonate
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: C

A right shift means hemoglobin holds O2 less tightly, promoting release to tissues—useful during high metabolic demand.

Why the other choices are not best:

  • Increased affinity: is a left shift, not a right shift.
  • Higher saturation at all PaO2: saturation at a given PaO2 is typically lower with a right shift.
  • Reduced bicarbonate transport: CO2 transport depends on buffering/enzymes, not directly on curve shifting.
10. Which set of changes is most consistent with a right shift during vigorous exercise?
A. Decreased PaCO2, decreased temperature, decreased 2,3-BPG
B. Increased PaCO2, increased temperature, increased [H+]
C. Decreased [H+], increased pH, decreased temperature
D. Increased fetal hemoglobin concentration in adult erythrocytes
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: B

Exercise increases CO2 production, acidity (H+), and temperature; these lower hemoglobin’s O2 affinity (Bohr effect and related factors).

Why the other choices are not best:

  • Decreased PaCO2/temperature/2,3-BPG: favors a left shift.
  • Decreased H+/higher pH: favors a left shift.
  • Fetal hemoglobin: left-shifts due to higher O2 affinity; adults do not acutely increase HbF.
11. Which factor causes a left shift of the oxyhemoglobin dissociation curve?
A. Increased PaCO2
B. Increased [H+] (lower pH)
C. Decreased 2,3-BPG
D. Increased temperature
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: C

Lower 2,3-BPG increases hemoglobin’s O2 affinity, shifting the curve left (similar direction as higher pH or lower CO2).

Why the other choices are not best:

  • Increased PaCO2: drives a right shift (Bohr effect).
  • Higher H+ / lower pH: drives a right shift.
  • Increased temperature: drives a right shift.
12. Fetal hemoglobin (HbF) is left-shifted relative to adult hemoglobin primarily because HbF:
A. Has lower affinity for oxygen to promote rapid fetal tissue unloading
B. Has higher affinity for oxygen, allowing fetal blood to extract O2 from maternal blood
C. Binds carbon dioxide more strongly than adult hemoglobin
D. Cannot bind oxygen cooperatively
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: B

HbF’s higher affinity facilitates transfer of oxygen from maternal to fetal circulation across the placenta.

Why the other choices are not best:

  • Lower affinity: would hinder oxygen extraction from maternal blood.
  • CO2 binding: is not the key reason for HbF’s left shift.
  • No cooperative binding: hemoglobin remains cooperative; the shift is about affinity differences.
13. Hyperventilation most directly tends to cause which acid–base change via the bicarbonate buffer system?
A. Respiratory alkalosis (increased pH) due to decreased CO2
B. Respiratory acidosis (decreased pH) due to increased CO2
C. Metabolic alkalosis due to increased bicarbonate production by lungs
D. Metabolic acidosis due to increased lactic acid from hemoglobin
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A

Blowing off CO2 shifts the buffer left, lowering H+ and raising pH (respiratory alkalosis).

Why the other choices are not best:

  • Respiratory acidosis: occurs with CO2 retention (hypoventilation).
  • Metabolic alkalosis by lungs: lungs change CO2, not primary metabolic bicarbonate generation.
  • Metabolic acidosis from hemoglobin: hemoglobin does not generate lactic acid; tissues do.
14. In Starling forces at capillary beds, which statement is most accurate?
A. Oncotic pressure pushes fluid out while hydrostatic pulls it in
B. Hydrostatic pressure favors fluid leaving the capillary; oncotic pressure favors fluid entering the capillary
C. Both pressures always favor net filtration at both ends of the capillary
D. Oncotic pressure is primarily generated by dissolved oxygen in plasma
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: B

Hydrostatic pressure drives filtration outward; oncotic (osmotic) pressure from plasma proteins draws water back into the vasculature.

Why the other choices are not best:

  • Reversed roles: swaps the directionality of the two forces.
  • Always net filtration: filtration dominates at arteriolar end; reabsorption dominates at venular end.
  • Dissolved oxygen: oncotic pressure is largely from proteins (e.g., albumin), not gases.
15. The key endpoint of the coagulation cascade emphasized for the MCAT is that thrombin:
A. Converts fibrinogen into fibrin
B. Converts plasminogen into plasmin
C. Converts collagen into tissue factor
D. Directly activates platelets to adhere to exposed endothelium
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A

Thrombin generation leads to fibrin formation from fibrinogen, creating a mesh that stabilizes the platelet plug into a clot.

Why the other choices are not best:

  • Plasminogen to plasmin: is part of clot breakdown (fibrinolysis).
  • Collagen to tissue factor: is not a thrombin reaction.
  • Platelet adhesion: occurs with exposed collagen and platelet activation; thrombin is not the primary adhesion trigger.
Passages (3 passages × 3 questions)
Passage 1: Vasodilation During Heat Exposure

During a summer marathon, a runner is monitored at a medical tent. The runner’s skin is flushed and warm, and clinicians note that many superficial capillary beds in the skin appear dilated. Despite drinking water, the runner’s blood pressure reading is lower than earlier in the race.

A clinician explains that systemic capillary beds are generally arranged in parallel. When more beds are open, blood can be distributed to additional pathways, which changes overall vascular resistance. The clinician also notes that arterioles act like adjustable resistors because their smooth muscle can constrict or relax to alter bloodflow into local capillary networks.

To stabilize blood pressure, the runner’s body engages rapid neural feedback. Sensors in vessel walls detect stretch and adjust autonomic output. The clinician emphasizes that the largest pressure drop in the circulation normally occurs at a specific vascular segment, which protects delicate downstream structures.

How to reason:

  1. Identify the physiologic change: widespread skin vasodilation means more parallel pathways are open, which tends to lower total resistance.
  2. Translate to the circuit analogy: lower resistance at constant driving pressure would increase flow; if pressure drops, compensation must raise either cardiac output or resistance.
  3. Use the key location clue: the largest pressure drop is across arterioles, so they are the main control knobs for TPR.
  4. Apply baroreceptor logic: low pressure reduces stretch signaling, increasing sympathetic tone to raise heart rate/contractility and cause vasoconstriction.
  5. Pick answers that integrate multiple cues (parallel beds + arterioles + baroreceptors), not ones that simply restate that BP is low.
16. In Passage 1, widespread opening of skin capillary beds during heat exposure would most directly tend to change total peripheral resistance (TPR) in which direction?
A. Increase TPR because blood must travel farther
B. Decrease TPR because more vascular pathways are open in parallel
C. Leave TPR unchanged because only arteries determine resistance
D. Increase TPR because capillaries are always arranged in series
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: B

More parallel pathways lowers total resistance, analogous to adding resistors in parallel.

Why the other choices are not best:

  • Travel farther: vessel length can affect resistance, but opening parallel beds lowers overall resistance.
  • Only arteries determine resistance: arterioles and microvasculature are key resistance sites.
  • Capillaries in series: systemic capillary beds are primarily arranged in parallel (except portal systems).
17. Passage 1 highlights the segment where pressure drops most steeply, protecting downstream thin-walled vessels. Which segment is this?
A. Arterioles
B. Pulmonary veins
C. Large elastic arteries
D. Venules
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A

Arterioles are the major resistance vessels; the largest pressure drop occurs there, preventing capillaries from seeing arterial pressures.

Why the other choices are not best:

  • Pulmonary veins: are part of pulmonary circulation and are not the primary systemic pressure-drop site.
  • Large elastic arteries: maintain high pressure; the big drop comes later.
  • Venules: are low-pressure vessels after capillaries.
18. If the runner in Passage 1 has low BP, which autonomic adjustment is most consistent with baroreceptor-mediated compensation?
A. Increased sympathetic output to raise heart rate/contractility and promote vasoconstriction
B. Increased parasympathetic output to raise heart rate and constrict arterioles
C. Decreased sympathetic output to dilate arterioles and lower BP further
D. Immediate increase in ANP release to promote natriuresis and diuresis
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A

Low BP reduces baroreceptor firing, increasing sympathetic tone to elevate CO and TPR, helping restore pressure.

Why the other choices are not best:

  • Parasympathetic raise HR/constrict: parasympathetic generally lowers HR.
  • Decrease sympathetic/dilate: would worsen hypotension.
  • Increase ANP: ANP lowers BP/volume and is more relevant to high pressure states.
Passage 2: Altitude, Hyperventilation, and Oxygen Delivery

A hiker rapidly ascends to a high-altitude environment and begins to breathe faster. A pulse oximeter shows a modest decrease in oxygen saturation compared with sea level. The hiker also feels lightheaded and tingling in the fingers after several minutes of sustained rapid breathing.

A physiologist explains that changing carbon dioxide levels can shift the bicarbonate buffer system and alter blood pH. These pH changes can allosterically affect hemoglobin’s affinity for oxygen, which is captured by shifts in the oxyhemoglobin dissociation curve. Temperature and a glycolysis-derived metabolite in red blood cells can also shift the curve.

The physiologist asks the hiker to predict how prolonged exertion at altitude might alter oxygen unloading to tissues compared with resting at sea level, and which shift would best support delivery to working muscle.

How to reason:

  1. Start with ventilation: hyperventilation lowers CO2, shifting the buffer left and lowering H+, which raises pH (alkalosis).
  2. Connect pH to hemoglobin: higher pH (lower H+) tends to left-shift the curve, increasing affinity and reducing unloading at tissues.
  3. Add the exercise context: working muscle increases CO2, H+, and temperature and can raise 2,3-BPG, producing a right shift that improves unloading.
  4. Use the curve regions: lungs sit near high PaO2 (plateau), while tissues (especially exercising) sit on the steep part where shifts meaningfully change unloading.
  5. Choose the option that reconciles competing influences (altitude hyperventilation vs exercise metabolites) using the direction of curve shifts.
19. In Passage 2, sustained hyperventilation at altitude would be expected to shift blood pH in which direction initially?
A. Decrease pH (respiratory acidosis) due to retained CO2
B. Decrease pH (metabolic acidosis) due to bicarbonate loss in urine within minutes
C. No meaningful pH change because hemoglobin buffers all protons instantly
D. Increase pH (respiratory alkalosis) due to decreased CO2
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: D

Hyperventilation blows off CO2, shifting the bicarbonate system left, lowering H+ and raising pH.

Why the other choices are not best:

  • Respiratory acidosis: occurs with hypoventilation/CO2 retention.
  • Metabolic acidosis in minutes: renal compensation is slower and not immediate.
  • No meaningful change: buffering exists but ventilation changes CO2 rapidly and does change pH.
20. Which shift in the oxyhemoglobin dissociation curve would best support oxygen unloading to exercising muscle during prolonged exertion, as discussed in Passage 2?
A. A left shift
B. No shift because lungs always fully saturate hemoglobin
C. A right shift
D. A vertical shift upward caused by increased hemoglobin concentration
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: C

A right shift decreases hemoglobin affinity for oxygen, increasing unloading at tissue PaO2 levels typical of active muscle.

Why the other choices are not best:

  • Left shift: increases affinity and reduces unloading.
  • No shift: shifts matter most at tissue PaO2 even if saturation in lungs remains near-maximal.
  • Vertical shift upward: hemoglobin concentration affects oxygen content, not the affinity shift depicted by the curve.
21. Passage 2 notes that curve shifts matter differently in lungs versus tissues. Why does a right shift particularly enhance unloading at tissues?
A. Tissues lie on the steep region where small PaO2 changes cause large saturation changes
B. Lungs lie on the steep region so unloading there increases
C. Tissues have higher PaO2 than lungs so saturation is always maximal
D. The right shift increases PaO2 in tissues directly
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A

At tissue PaO2, the curve is steep; shifting right reduces saturation at the same PaO2, releasing more O2.

Why the other choices are not best:

  • Lungs steep region: lungs are near the plateau (high PaO2), where saturation stays high.
  • Tissues higher PaO2: tissues have lower PaO2 than lungs.
  • Increases tissue PaO2 directly: the curve reflects binding affinity, not direct PaO2 generation.
Passage 3: A Deep Cut and Abnormal Bleeding

After a minor kitchen injury, a patient continues bleeding longer than expected from a deep fingertip cut. The wound is cleaned, and clinicians observe that platelets adhere at the damaged site and begin to clump together. They explain that endothelial injury exposes underlying connective tissue, including collagen, which signals platelets that the vessel wall has been breached.

At the same time, plasma proteins made largely by the liver respond to tissue factor exposure and initiate a sequence of activation steps. The team emphasizes that the cascade converges on a key enzyme that transforms an inactive precursor into an active protease, which then produces a fibrous mesh that stabilizes the platelet plug.

Later, the clinicians note that any formed clot must eventually be removed and that an enzyme derived from an inactive precursor carries out most fibrin breakdown. The patient’s family history includes relatives with a hereditary disorder that impairs normal clot formation.

How to reason:

  1. Separate phases: primary hemostasis = platelet adhesion/aggregation; secondary hemostasis = coagulation factors generating a fibrin mesh.
  2. Track the cascade endpoint: tissue factor triggers reactions that lead to thrombin, which converts fibrinogen to fibrin to stabilize the clot.
  3. Look for clues about breakdown: plasmin is made from plasminogen and dissolves fibrin during fibrinolysis—this is after the clot forms.
  4. Use inference: prolonged bleeding despite platelets suggests a factor deficiency (e.g., hemophilia) affecting the fibrin-stabilizing step.
  5. Eliminate answers that confuse plasmin (breakdown) with thrombin (formation) or that treat collagen/tissue factor as products of thrombin.

A. Conversion of plasminogen to plasmin
B. Formation of a fibrin mesh from fibrinogen via thrombin
C. Release of carbon dioxide from bicarbonate in red blood cells
D. Activation of baroreceptors to increase sympathetic tone
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: B

Secondary hemostasis generates fibrin, which stabilizes and cross-links the platelet plug into a durable clot.

Why the other choices are not best:

  • Plasmin: dissolves fibrin during clot breakdown.
  • CO2 release: is acid–base/respiratory physiology, unrelated to clot stabilization.
  • Baroreceptors: regulate blood pressure, not clot structure.

Hereditary disorders like hemophilia impair steps of the coagulation cascade, reducing thrombin/fibrin formation and prolonging bleeding.

24. Passage 3 mentions eventual clot removal. Which enzyme is primarily responsible for breaking down fibrin?
A. Thrombin
B. Thromboplastin
C. Plasmin
D. Erythropoietin
✅Correct
❌Incorrect

Plasmin, generated from plasminogen, degrades fibrin during fibrinolysis to remove the clot once repair is underway.
