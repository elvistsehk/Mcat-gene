❌Incorrect

Correct Answer: C.

Why: Resting Vm is ~−70 mV and is closer to Ek (~−90 mV) because the membrane has higher permeability to K+ than to Na+. Leak channels plus Na+/K+ ATPase maintain gradients.

Passage 1: Demyelination, Conduction Speed, and Signal Integrity

A 29-year-old patient reports episodes of blurry vision and unsteady gait that come and go over several months. Neurologic examination shows weakness in one leg and impaired coordination. MRI reveals scattered lesions in the brain and spinal cord that are consistent with loss of insulating material around axons.

Electrophysiology testing demonstrates that action potentials still reach distal axon segments in some pathways, but conduction velocity is reduced and signal timing becomes less reliable. The clinician explains that in healthy myelinated axons, ion flow across the membrane is largely restricted to periodic gaps where the axonal membrane is exposed. Because depolarization can only be regenerated at those gaps, the signal effectively travels by jumping between them.

In this patient, disruption of insulation increases current leakage along the axon. The clinician predicts that the effect will be especially noticeable in long CNS pathways that normally require rapid, precisely timed transmission to coordinate motor output and sensory feedback.

How to reason:

  1. Match “brain and spinal cord myelin loss” to a CNS demyelinating disorder and its functional consequences.
  2. Use the conduction mechanism: myelin restricts ion flow to nodes; loss of myelin increases leakage and slows propagation.
  3. Identify the feature enabling jumping conduction and predict how signal timing changes when insulation is disrupted.
  4. Connect slower conduction and less reliable timing to sensory and motor symptoms described.

Passage 2: Synaptic Inputs, Summation, and Threshold

A postsynaptic neuron receives input from several presynaptic neurons. Some presynaptic terminals release neurotransmitters that open ligand-gated cation channels on the postsynaptic membrane, producing small depolarizations. Other terminals release neurotransmitters that increase permeability to ions that drive the membrane potential more negative, producing hyperpolarizations.

During an experiment, a researcher delivers three small excitatory inputs to the same dendritic region in rapid succession and observes that the postsynaptic neuron fires an action potential. In a second condition, the researcher delivers the same three excitatory inputs but spaces them farther apart in time; now the neuron does not fire. In a third condition, the researcher delivers multiple inhibitory inputs clustered near the soma while simultaneously delivering the rapid excitatory inputs at dendrites; firing fails again.

The researcher concludes that both the timing and the location of incoming signals determine whether the membrane at the decision point reaches threshold. They note that a typical threshold may be around −55 to −40 mV from a resting potential near −70 mV.

How to reason:

  1. Depolarizations increase firing likelihood; hyperpolarizations decrease it.
  2. Rapid repeated inputs over time → temporal summation; many inputs at different sites/locations → spatial summation.
  3. The decision point for threshold is the axon hillock, not the dendrites.
  4. Use which condition fires vs fails to infer which summation principle is limiting.

Passage 3: Synaptic Transmission and Drug Mechanisms

A lab studies synaptic transmission at a neuromuscular junction-like preparation. When an action potential arrives at the presynaptic terminal, a rapid rise in a particular divalent cation inside the terminal is recorded. Immediately afterward, vesicles fuse with the presynaptic membrane and a neurotransmitter is released into the synaptic cleft. The postsynaptic cell depolarizes and contracts.

In one trial, the preparation is exposed to a compound that blocks the enzyme that normally breaks down the neurotransmitter in the cleft. The postsynaptic response becomes prolonged and repeated contractions occur. In a second trial, the preparation is exposed to a different drug that blocks a presynaptic reuptake carrier used by monoamine neurotransmitters. Neurotransmitter signaling is also prolonged, but the mechanism does not require inhibiting an enzyme in the cleft.

The investigators remind trainees that neurotransmitter action can be terminated by enzymatic breakdown, transporter-mediated reuptake, or simple diffusion away from the cleft. They also emphasize that electrical signaling occurs within a neuron along the axon, whereas chemical signaling occurs between neurons (or to effectors) across the synapse.

How to reason:

  1. The divalent cation that triggers vesicle fusion at terminals is Ca2+.
  2. Prolonged response from blocking breakdown points to inhibiting an enzyme like acetylcholinesterase.
  3. Blocking reuptake carriers prolongs signaling without enzyme inhibition (think monoamine transporters).
  4. Keep electrical (within neuron) vs chemical (between cells) transmission straight.
