MCAT Biology — Nervous System Organization
Standalone Questions (Q1–Q15)
1. Which structure is part of the central nervous system (CNS)?
A. Spinal cord
B. Sciatic nerve
C. Dorsal root ganglion
D. Sympathetic chain ganglion
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A.

Why: The CNS consists of the brain and spinal cord. All peripheral nerves and ganglia lie outside the CNS.

Other choices:

  • B: Sciatic nerve is a PNS nerve bundle.
  • C: Dorsal root ganglia contain sensory neuron cell bodies in the PNS.
  • D: Autonomic ganglia are in the PNS.
2. A clinician lightly touches a patient’s cornea, and sensory information travels toward the spinal cord/brain. Which term best describes the neuron carrying this information?
A. Efferent neuron
B. Motor neuron
C. Afferent neuron
D. Preganglionic neuron
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: C.

Why: Afferent (sensory) neurons carry information toward the CNS. “Afferent ascends.”

Other choices:

  • A/B: Efferent/motor neurons carry commands away from the CNS to effectors.
  • D: Preganglionic neurons are autonomic efferents, not general sensory input.
3. A lesion selectively damages the dorsal roots entering a segment of the spinal cord. Which deficit is most directly expected at that segment?
A. Loss of motor output to skeletal muscle (ventral horn)
B. Increased sympathetic output to the heart
C. Loss of descending cortical input only
D. Loss of sensory input from the periphery
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: D.

Why: Sensory (afferent) fibers enter the spinal cord via the dorsal roots. Damaging them blocks incoming sensory signals.

Other choices:

  • A: Motor output exits through ventral roots, not dorsal roots.
  • B: Dorsal roots are sensory; sympathetic output is efferent.
  • C: Descending pathways are in cord tracts; dorsal root damage is not selective for “descending only.”
4. Which statement correctly compares white and gray matter distribution in the brain versus the spinal cord?
A. In both brain and spinal cord, white matter lies superficial to gray matter
B. Brain: white matter lies deeper than gray; spinal cord: white matter lies outside gray
C. Brain: gray matter lies deeper than white; spinal cord: gray matter lies outside white
D. White matter is composed primarily of dendrites; gray matter is composed of myelinated axons
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: B.

Why: In the brain, cortical gray matter is more superficial with white matter deeper. In the spinal cord, white matter surrounds deeper gray matter.

Other choices:

  • A/C: These reverse or overgeneralize the distributions.
  • D: White matter is mostly myelinated axons; gray matter is mainly cell bodies and dendrites.
5. Most cranial nerves are part of the peripheral nervous system, but two are considered outgrowths of the CNS. Which pair is it?
A. Olfactory (I) and optic (II)
B. Oculomotor (III) and trochlear (IV)
C. Trigeminal (V) and facial (VII)
D. Vagus (X) and hypoglossal (XII)
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A.

Why: The olfactory and optic nerves are considered CNS outgrowths (developmentally distinct from typical PNS nerves).

Other choices: The remaining cranial nerves listed are classified as PNS components.

6. Which statement best captures a key organizational difference between somatic and autonomic motor pathways?
A. Somatic pathways use two neurons in series; autonomic pathways use one neuron
B. Somatic pathways control glands only; autonomic pathways control skeletal muscle only
C. Autonomic pathways bypass the spinal cord entirely
D. Autonomic pathways typically use a preganglionic and a postganglionic neuron; somatic motor uses a single neuron to skeletal muscle
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: D.

Why: The ANS uses a two-neuron chain (preganglionic → ganglion → postganglionic). The somatic motor system uses one motor neuron from CNS to skeletal muscle.

Other choices:

  • A: Reversed.
  • B: Reversed and incorrect (somatic controls skeletal muscle; autonomic controls smooth/cardiac muscle and glands).
  • C: Autonomic output originates from the CNS and travels via peripheral ganglia.
7. A student is startled by a sudden loud noise. Which physiological change is most consistent with sympathetic nervous system activation?
A. Increased gastric peristalsis and salivation
B. Decreased heart rate and bronchoconstriction
C. Bronchodilation with increased heart rate and increased blood glucose availability
D. Pupillary constriction to reduce light intake
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: C.

Why: Sympathetic “fight-or-flight” responses include increased heart rate, bronchodilation, and mobilizing fuel (raising blood glucose).

Other choices: A, B, and D reflect parasympathetic-leaning or opposite effects.

8. Which neurotransmitter is classically associated with parasympathetic activity at both the preganglionic-to-ganglion synapse and the postganglionic-to-organ synapse?
A. Norepinephrine only
B. Acetylcholine
C. Dopamine
D. Serotonin
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: B.

Why: Parasympathetic preganglionic neurons release ACh, and parasympathetic postganglionic neurons also release ACh onto target organs.

Other choices: Norepinephrine is most associated with sympathetic postganglionic signaling; dopamine/serotonin are not classic parasympathetic efferent transmitters.

9. Which pairing is most accurate for typical sympathetic neurotransmission?
A. Preganglionic: norepinephrine; postganglionic: acetylcholine
B. Preganglionic: dopamine; postganglionic: serotonin
C. Preganglionic: GABA; postganglionic: glutamate
D. Preganglionic: acetylcholine; postganglionic: norepinephrine (most targets)
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: D.

Why: Sympathetic preganglionic neurons release ACh onto ganglia, while most sympathetic postganglionic neurons release norepinephrine onto organs.

Other choices: The other pairings do not match classic autonomic physiology tested on the MCAT.

10. Which structure provides much of the parasympathetic innervation to thoracic and abdominal organs?
A. Vagus nerve (cranial nerve X)
B. Phrenic nerve
C. Sciatic nerve
D. Sympathetic chain ganglia
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A.

Why: The vagus nerve carries major parasympathetic outflow to heart, lungs, and GI organs.

Other choices: Phrenic nerve innervates the diaphragm; sciatic is somatic to the leg; sympathetic chain ganglia are sympathetic.

11. Which description best defines a preganglionic neuron in the autonomic nervous system?
A. Its soma lies in a peripheral ganglion and its axon innervates the organ directly
B. Its soma lies in the CNS and its axon synapses in a peripheral ganglion
C. It is a sensory neuron whose soma lies in the dorsal root ganglion
D. It is an interneuron located entirely within the spinal cord
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: B.

Why: Preganglionic neurons originate in the CNS and synapse in an autonomic ganglion on a postganglionic neuron.

Other choices: A describes postganglionic; C describes afferent sensory; D is not the defining ANS efferent role.

12. Which reflex arc necessarily includes an interneuron between the sensory and motor neurons?
A. Knee-jerk (patellar) stretch reflex
B. Any reflex that uses a single synapse
C. Withdrawal reflex after stepping on a sharp object
D. A monosynaptic reflex by definition
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: C.

Why: Withdrawal reflexes are polysynaptic and use interneurons to coordinate flexion of the injured limb and often extension of the opposite limb.

Other choices: The patellar reflex is monosynaptic; monosynaptic reflexes do not require interneurons.

13. A person intentionally holds their hand still while a clinician taps the patellar tendon, preventing a noticeable knee kick. Which concept best explains how this is possible?
A. Supraspinal circuits can modulate spinal reflex output
B. Reflexes require the cerebral cortex to begin
C. Motor output enters the spinal cord through dorsal roots
D. Afferent neurons exit the spinal cord ventrally
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A.

Why: Reflex arcs can occur at the spinal level, but the brain can modulate spinal output via supraspinal inputs (inhibitory or excitatory control).

Other choices: B is false (reflexes do not require cortex); C/D reverse dorsal vs ventral orientation.

14. After stepping on a nail with the right foot, a person rapidly flexes the right leg and simultaneously extends the left leg to maintain balance. Which spinal pathway feature best accounts for the left-leg extension?
A. A single synapse between sensory and motor neurons on the contralateral side
B. Interneurons that connect sensory input to contralateral motor neurons (crossed-extensor component)
C. Parasympathetic postganglionic fibers innervating quadriceps
D. Somatic motor pathways that must pass through an autonomic ganglion first
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: B.

Why: The balance-preserving response is produced by polysynaptic spinal circuits with interneurons that activate contralateral extensors while ipsilateral flexors withdraw the injured limb.

Other choices: A is too simple; C is autonomic; D is incorrect because somatic motor neurons go directly to skeletal muscle.

15. A drug blocks nicotinic acetylcholine receptors in autonomic ganglia. Which outcome is most likely?
A. Only parasympathetic signaling is blocked, while sympathetic signaling is unaffected
B. Only somatic motor signaling to skeletal muscle is blocked
C. Only postganglionic sympathetic neurotransmission to organs is blocked
D. Both sympathetic and parasympathetic outflow are reduced because both use acetylcholine at the ganglion synapse
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: D.

Why: Both sympathetic and parasympathetic preganglionic neurons release acetylcholine onto nicotinic receptors in autonomic ganglia. Blocking these receptors disrupts both branches.

Other choices: A is false because sympathetic ganglia also use ACh; B targets somatic NMJ rather than autonomic ganglia; C ignores the upstream ganglionic block.

Passages (3 passages × 3 questions)
Passage 1: A Spinal-Level Problem

A 32-year-old patient presents after a cycling accident with a complaint: “My right leg feels numb, and I don’t notice light touch on that side unless I look.” On exam, the clinician notes that vibration and pain sensation from the right foot are reduced. However, the patient can still voluntarily extend the knee and plantarflex the ankle with near-normal strength. When the clinician taps the patellar tendon, the patient’s right knee shows a much smaller reflex response than the left.

MRI suggests damage to structures entering the spinal cord from the posterior (back) side at a specific lumbar level. The clinician explains that some circuits in the spinal cord can generate a response without waiting for a command from the brain, but those circuits still depend on incoming sensory information to trigger them.

How to reason:

  1. Separate sensation vs motor: numbness implies afferent loss; preserved strength implies efferent somatic motor pathways are mostly intact.
  2. Use anatomy rules: sensory fibers enter dorsally; motor fibers exit ventrally; sensory cell bodies sit in dorsal root ganglia.
  3. Reflexes require an afferent trigger and an efferent output; a weak reflex with intact voluntary movement suggests the trigger is impaired.
  4. Pick the answer that explains all findings with one lesion location.
16. Which damaged structure best explains the sensory loss with relatively preserved voluntary motor strength described in Passage 1?
A. Ventral root fibers at the affected level
B. Motor neuron cell bodies in the ventral horn
C. Dorsal root fibers entering the spinal cord
D. Postganglionic sympathetic fibers to the leg
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: C.

Why: Dorsal roots carry afferent sensory information into the spinal cord. Damaging them produces sensory deficits while leaving ventral (motor) outflow relatively intact.

Other choices: A/B would cause prominent motor weakness; D does not explain loss of pain/vibration or a reduced patellar reflex trigger.

17. If the clinician wanted to localize the cell bodies of the affected sensory neurons, which location would be most consistent with Passage 1?
A. Dorsal root ganglion at the affected lumbar level
B. Ventral horn of the spinal cord at the affected level
C. Sympathetic chain ganglion near the vertebral column
D. Cerebellar nuclei
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A.

Why: Sensory neuron cell bodies are located in the dorsal root ganglia, just outside the spinal cord.

Other choices: Ventral horn is motor; sympathetic chain is autonomic; cerebellar nuclei are not sensory neuron cell bodies.

18. Passage 1 reports a reduced patellar tendon reflex on the injured side. Which inference best explains this finding?
A. Reflexes require the cerebral cortex to initiate movement
B. The sensory (afferent) limb needed to trigger the spinal reflex arc is impaired
C. Parasympathetic tone to the quadriceps is increased
D. Only the postganglionic autonomic neuron is damaged
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: B.

Why: A reflex needs an afferent trigger (stretch receptor → sensory neuron). If dorsal input is reduced, the reflex is blunted even if motor output can still work voluntarily.

Other choices: A is false; C/D involve autonomic concepts not responsible for skeletal muscle reflex arcs.

Passage 2: Two Branches, One Body

During an overnight call shift, a medical trainee is paged to a rapid response. While running down the hallway, the trainee notices a pounding heartbeat, rapid breathing, and a dry mouth sensation. After the event resolves, the trainee sits in a quiet room, eats a meal, and gradually feels the heart rate slow while abdominal sounds become noticeable. The trainee jokes that the body must have a built-in “gas pedal and brake.”

A pharmacology instructor adds a twist: the instructor administers a drug that blocks neurotransmitter release from most postganglionic sympathetic neurons. The trainee’s heart rate still increases modestly with exertion, but the magnitude of the response is reduced compared with baseline. The instructor asks the trainee to connect these observations to the organization of autonomic pathways.

How to reason:

  1. Map symptoms to branch: fight-or-flight (sympathetic) vs rest-and-digest (parasympathetic).
  2. Remember the two-neuron chain: preganglionic (CNS → ganglion) then postganglionic (ganglion → organ).
  3. If a drug blocks postganglionic sympathetic transmission, predict which sympathetic organ effects should drop the most.
  4. Choose the option that fits both the before/after drug pattern and basic physiology.
19. Which post-event change described in Passage 2 is most directly mediated by the parasympathetic nervous system?
A. Increased blood glucose availability during the sprint
B. Bronchodilation to increase airflow
C. Redistribution of blood toward skeletal muscle
D. Increased digestive activity after the trainee sits and eats
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: D.

Why: Parasympathetic activity increases peristalsis and exocrine secretions, supporting digestion after stress resolves.

Other choices: A–C are sympathetic responses.

20. The drug in Passage 2 blocks neurotransmitter release from most postganglionic sympathetic neurons. Which immediate physiological effect would most directly decrease because of this blockade?
A. Acetylcholine release from preganglionic neurons in autonomic ganglia
B. Sensory (afferent) transmission from muscle spindles to the spinal cord
C. Norepinephrine signaling at many sympathetic target organs
D. Action potential conduction along somatic motor neurons
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: C.

Why: Most sympathetic postganglionic neurons release norepinephrine onto organs. Blocking their transmitter release reduces NE-driven effects.

Other choices: A is upstream; B and D are somatic processes, not postganglionic sympathetic transmission.

21. The trainee’s heart rate still increases modestly with exertion despite the postganglionic sympathetic blockade. Which explanation best supports this observation?
A. Additional regulatory inputs (including reduced parasympathetic tone) can still raise heart rate even if one pathway is weakened
B. Heart rate is controlled exclusively by somatic motor neurons
C. The vagus nerve is the primary sympathetic nerve to the heart
D. Postganglionic sympathetic neurons normally release acetylcholine to the heart, so blocking norepinephrine has no effect
✅Correct

Why: Heart rate is influenced by multiple mechanisms. If sympathetic postganglionic output is reduced, HR can still rise via reduced parasympathetic braking and other controls, but the response is smaller.

Other choices: B is false; C mislabels vagus (parasympathetic); D misstates typical sympathetic transmitter (mostly norepinephrine).

Passage 3: Reflexes and Real-World Movement

A physical therapist evaluates two patients during rehab. Patient A receives a quick tap to the patellar tendon and shows a rapid knee extension with minimal delay. Patient B steps on a sharp tack during a balance drill; within a moment, Patient B flexes the injured ankle and knee while the opposite leg stiffens to prevent a fall. The therapist notes that both reactions can occur before the patients could consciously decide to move.

Later, the therapist asks Patient A to try to suppress the knee movement by focusing attention and tensing certain muscles. Patient A can reduce the size of the response but cannot completely eliminate it. The therapist explains that spinal processing is sufficient to produce some behaviors, but higher brain centers can still influence how strongly those behaviors appear.

How to reason:

  1. Classify each response: knee-jerk is typically monosynaptic; withdrawal/balance is polysynaptic.
  2. Ask what extra features require interneurons (contralateral extension, coordination across joints).
  3. Separate initiation (spinal) from modulation (supraspinal influence).
  4. Choose the answer that fits timing, circuit complexity, and anatomy.
22. Which statement best distinguishes the knee-jerk response from the tack-stepping response described in Passage 3?
A. The knee-jerk reflex requires multiple interneurons; the tack-stepping response is monosynaptic
B. Both responses require cortical processing before any muscle activation can occur
C. The tack-stepping response cannot involve the spinal cord because it uses both legs
D. The knee-jerk reflex is classically monosynaptic; the tack-stepping withdrawal/balance response is polysynaptic
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: D.

Why: The patellar reflex is the classic monosynaptic reflex, while withdrawal plus contralateral support requires interneurons and is polysynaptic.

Other choices: A–C contradict basic reflex organization.

23. Which feature in the tack-stepping response most strongly implies that interneurons must be involved?
A. The injured limb flexes at the ankle
B. The opposite limb stiffens/extends to support balance while the injured limb withdraws
C. Sensory receptors detect pain in the foot
D. The response occurs quickly
✅Correct
❌Incorrect

Correct Answer: B.

Why: Coordinating the contralateral limb alongside ipsilateral withdrawal requires additional spinal connections—classically via interneurons in a polysynaptic circuit.

Other choices: A/C can occur without proving interneurons; D speed alone doesn’t distinguish circuit complexity.

A. The reflex is produced only by the brainstem and cannot be influenced by the cortex
B. The response is autonomic rather than somatic, so it bypasses the spinal cord
C. The spinal reflex arc can initiate the response, but supraspinal inputs can modulate its magnitude
D. All reflexes are voluntary behaviors if the person concentrates hard enough

❌Incorrect

Correct Answer: C.

Why: The patellar reflex can be generated at the spinal level, but descending pathways from the brain can increase or decrease how strongly it appears—so partial suppression is possible without total elimination.
