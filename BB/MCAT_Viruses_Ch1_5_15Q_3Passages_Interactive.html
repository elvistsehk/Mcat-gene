Standalone Questions (Q1–Q15)
1. A researcher isolates an infectious agent that cannot synthesize proteins unless it is inside a host cell. Which missing component most directly explains why this agent is an obligate intracellular parasite?
A. A cell wall made of peptidoglycan
B. Ribosomes for translation
C. A mitochondrion for oxidative phosphorylation
D. A plasma membrane
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: B.

Why: Viruses lack ribosomes and therefore must hijack host ribosomes, tRNA, amino acids, and enzymes to translate viral proteins—making them obligate intracellular parasites.

Why the other choices are not the best fit:

  • B: Some viruses have envelopes; lack of a plasma membrane is not the key reason they cannot synthesize proteins.
  • C: Cell walls are bacterial structures; viruses are acellular and do not require peptidoglycan.
  • D: Viruses do not need their own mitochondria because they use host ATP and metabolic machinery.
2. A disinfectant protocol is redesigned for an outbreak on hospital surfaces. Which virus type is most likely to persist longest on dry surfaces and resist detergents?
A. Enveloped virus
B. Bacteriophage (always killed by heat)
C. Nonenveloped virus
D. Retrovirus (always fragile)
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: C.

Why: Nonenveloped viruses lack a lipid envelope and are generally more resistant to heat, detergents, and desiccation, allowing longer environmental persistence.

Why the other choices are not the best fit:

  • A: Enveloped viruses have lipid envelopes that are sensitive to detergents and drying.
  • C: Retroviruses are enveloped, but the key distinction is envelope vs none.
  • D: Bacteriophages vary; persistence is driven mainly by envelope presence/absence here.

Correct Answer: C.

Why: Positive-sense RNA functions like mRNA and can be directly translated by host ribosomes.

Why the other choices are not the best fit:

  • C: dsDNA viruses generally require transcription to mRNA, often involving the nucleus.
  • D: Viroids are small circular ssRNA pathogens (classically plant) and do not encode proteins like typical viruses.

Why: Negative-sense RNA cannot be directly translated; an RNA-dependent RNA polymerase (replicase) is needed to produce a complementary (+) strand that can be translated.

Why the other choices are not the best fit:

  • B: Ribosomes translate RNA (mRNA), not DNA directly.
  • C: Reverse transcription is a retrovirus feature, not required for all negative-sense RNA viruses.
  • D: Integration into host DNA is not required for negative-sense RNA viruses in general.

Passage 1: Two Outbreaks, Two Disinfection Failures

A university clinic experiences two outbreaks of respiratory illness in separate dormitories. In Dorm A, cases rise quickly but decline after custodial staff begin routine cleaning with detergent-based wipes. In Dorm B, however, new cases continue for days despite the same cleaning protocol, and environmental swabs from doorknobs remain positive even after surfaces dry.

Electron microscopy of virions from Dorm A reveals a lipid-containing envelope with surface proteins. Virions from Dorm B lack an envelope and appear to have only a protein coat surrounding the genome. Both viruses bind specific receptors on epithelial cells, but only Dorm A’s virus is readily inactivated by heat and desiccation.

To reduce transmission, the infection-control team considers adding an alcohol-based sanitizer, increasing drying time, and emphasizing hand hygiene. They also note that in Dorm A, infected cells release virus over multiple days without widespread cell lysis, while in Dorm B, infected cells frequently burst after accumulating large numbers of virions.

How to reason:

  1. Classify by structure: lipid envelope vs no envelope predicts sensitivity to detergents/desiccation.
  2. Link persistence: if swabs stay positive after drying + detergent cleaning, suspect a nonenveloped virus that resists desiccation.
  3. For release: budding/extrusion keeps cells alive (productive infection); lysis spills virions but kills the cell.
  4. Use multiple clues together (cleaning response + EM structure + release behavior) rather than one sentence.

  • C: Envelopes are sensitive to desiccation; they do not protect strongly against drying.

Passage 2: A Newly Discovered RNA Virus

A team isolates an RNA virus from patients with acute gastroenteritis. After entry into cultured human cells, viral proteins appear within minutes. Early translation occurs even when nuclear import is chemically blocked, and viral replication factories are visualized in the cytoplasm.

When purified virions are analyzed, researchers detect an enzyme that can synthesize RNA using an RNA template. However, no DNA intermediate is detected during infection. In a separate assay, the viral genome is added to a cell-free translation system containing ribosomes, tRNA, and amino acids. Protein is produced without adding any other viral components.

A related virus from the same family is found to have a genome that cannot be translated in the cell-free system unless an RNA-dependent RNA polymerase is added. This related virus also shows a longer ‘eclipse period’ before protein production is detected in infected cells.

How to reason:

  1. Use where/when translation happens: rapid cytoplasmic protein production + nuclear blockade suggests an RNA virus translated in the cytoplasm.
  2. Cell-free translation is decisive: if the naked genome produces protein, it behaves like positive-sense RNA (mRNA-like).
  3. If translation requires added polymerase, the genome likely starts as negative-sense RNA and must be copied to (+) RNA first.
  4. Exclude retrovirus: the passage finds no DNA intermediate and emphasizes RNA→RNA copying.

Passage 3: Persistent Phage Infection and Gene Transfer

A microbiology lab studies a bacteriophage that infects a strain of Escherichia coli. Under nutrient-poor conditions, infected bacteria continue to divide, and phage DNA is detected in the bacterial chromosome across many generations. During this period, the culture shows reduced susceptibility to infection by a second, unrelated phage strain.

After exposure to ultraviolet radiation, many bacteria in the culture lyse and release large numbers of phage particles. Sequencing of some released phage particles reveals that they carry a small segment of bacterial DNA adjacent to the original phage integration site.

When these phage particles infect a new bacterial strain, some recipients acquire the bacterial gene segment and display a new phenotype. The investigators conclude that environmental stress can switch the phage’s strategy and can also contribute to genetic exchange between bacteria.

How to reason:

  1. Quiet heritable phase: phage DNA in chromosome across generations = lysogenic cycle (prophage).
  2. Superinfection clue: lysogeny can reduce susceptibility to other phages (superinfection exclusion).
  3. After UV, widespread lysis + many particles indicates induction to the lytic cycle.
  4. Phage carrying adjacent bacterial DNA supports transduction (phage-mediated gene transfer).
