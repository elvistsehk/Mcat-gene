üìò Passage 1 ‚Äî Middle-ear pressure, ossicles, and cochlear fluid mechanics

How to reason from this passage

  • Tag each structure ‚Üí function: Eustachian tube (pressure equalization), ossicles (mechanical transmission), oval window (entry), round window (pressure release for incompressible fluid).
  • Identify the failure point: a retracted tympanic membrane during descent points to pressure mismatch ‚Üí Eustachian tube.
  • Use the ‚Äúincompressible fluid‚Äù rule: if perilymph cannot displace, basilar membrane vibration falls ‚Üí hair-cell stimulation drops.
  • Convert to question logic: when asked ‚Äúwhy hearing decreases,‚Äù look for which step reduces basilar membrane motion (oval/round window mechanics).

A 21-year-old develops severe ear discomfort during airplane descent. Otoscopic examination shows an intact tympanic membrane that appears retracted. Audiometry shows transient conductive hearing loss. The clinician explains that the middle ear is connected to the nasopharynx by the Eustachian tube, which normally equalizes pressure.

To explore sound conduction, researchers model the middle ear as a mechanical linkage: malleus ‚Üí incus ‚Üí stapes. Vibrations from the tympanic membrane are transmitted to the stapes footplate at the oval window of the cochlea.

Inside the cochlea, perilymph transmits the vibration through fluid spaces surrounding the hearing apparatus. Because fluid is essentially incompressible, a membrane-covered opening called the round window allows perilymph to move back and forth, enabling vibration of the basilar membrane under the organ of Corti.

The researchers predict that dysfunction at any step that reduces movement at the oval window or prevents perilymph displacement will diminish basilar membrane vibration and reduce sensory transduction by hair cells.

üìò Passage 2 ‚Äî Place theory, basilar membrane mapping, and selective pitch loss

How to reason from this passage

  • Translate the mapping: cochlea is tonotopic ‚Üí frequency is encoded by place along the basilar membrane.
  • Anchor points: high frequency peaks near the base/oval window; low frequency peaks near the apex.
  • Lesion logic: damage at one location predicts a selective pitch loss (not a global deficit).
  • Pathway check: CN VIII ‚Üí brainstem ‚Üí MGN ‚Üí temporal auditory cortex; use this to eliminate vision (LGN/occipital) distractors.

Researchers deliver pure tones of different frequencies while recording auditory-nerve responses. They emphasize that the cochlea is tonotopically organized: different sound frequencies activate different locations along the basilar membrane.

High-frequency tones peak near the base close to the oval window, whereas low-frequency tones peak toward the apex farther from the oval window. Hair cells in the organ of Corti sit on the basilar membrane and generate receptor potentials when their stereocilia bend.

Signals travel via the vestibulocochlear nerve to the brainstem and typically relay through the medial geniculate nucleus (MGN) of the thalamus before reaching auditory cortex in the temporal lobe.

Based on this mapping, the team predicts that localized damage in specific basilar membrane regions will produce frequency-specific hearing deficits.

üìò Passage 3 ‚Äî Vestibular hair cells: linear vs rotational acceleration

How to reason from this passage

  • Classify the motion: linear (elevator/acceleration) vs rotational (spinning/turning head).
  • Match structure: linear ‚Üí utricle/saccule with otoliths; rotational ‚Üí semicircular canals with ampulla.
  • Inertia principle: otoliths or endolymph lag behind during motion ‚Üí hair-cell bending ‚Üí receptor potential.
  • Differential deficit: normal rotation but poor linear sensing ‚Üí points to utricle/saccule (not canals).

A student studies balance perception using two motion paradigms: (1) elevator-like linear acceleration and (2) rapid head rotation in a swivel chair. The student notes that vestibular receptors use hair cells, but they are located in different inner-ear structures than the organ of Corti.

For linear acceleration, the utricle and saccule (vestibule) contain hair cells topped with otoliths. During acceleration, otoliths resist motion due to inertia, bending stereocilia and changing receptor potentials.

For rotational acceleration, the three semicircular canals end in ampullae containing hair cells. When the head rotates, endolymph lags behind due to inertia, bending hair cells in the ampulla.

The student predicts that selective damage to vestibular substructures will disrupt only one class of acceleration sensing.
