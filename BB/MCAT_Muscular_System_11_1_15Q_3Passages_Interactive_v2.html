Chapter 11.1 — Muscular System
Standalone Questions (15)
1. A muscle sample shows striations under the microscope, is multinucleated, and is under voluntary control. Which innervation pattern best matches this tissue?
A. Autonomic (sympathetic) innervation
B. Autonomic (parasympathetic) innervation
C. Somatic motor innervation
D. No innervation required because contraction is purely myogenic
Correct ✅
Incorrect ❌
Explanation

Correct answer: C

Voluntary, striated, multinucleated muscle is skeletal muscle, which is controlled by somatic motor neurons.

Why the other choices are not ideal:

  • A. Autonomic sympathetic control primarily regulates involuntary tissues like smooth muscle and cardiac muscle.
  • B. Parasympathetic output modulates involuntary functions; it does not directly control voluntary skeletal contraction.
  • D. Skeletal muscle requires motor neuron input at the neuromuscular junction to contract.
2. A sprinter wants to improve short, powerful bursts of activity. Compared with slow-twitch fibers, the fibers most useful for this goal typically have:
A. Higher myoglobin content and a greater reliance on aerobic metabolism
B. Greater capillary density and higher oxidative phosphorylation capacity
C. Lower myoglobin content and faster fatigue onset
D. More mitochondria and slower fatigue onset
Correct ✅
Incorrect ❌
Explanation

Correct answer: C

Fast-twitch (white) fibers have less myoglobin, rely more on anaerobic pathways, generate force quickly, and fatigue sooner.

Why the other choices are not ideal:

  • A. That describes slow-twitch (red) fibers better.
  • B. Greater capillary density and oxidative capacity are endurance (slow-twitch) features.
  • D. Slow-twitch fibers have more mitochondria and resist fatigue.
3. Which property best distinguishes smooth muscle from skeletal muscle in terms of contraction control?
A. Smooth muscle cells are multinucleated because of cell fusion during development
B. Smooth muscle can contract in response to stretch without requiring nervous system input
C. Smooth muscle is striated because it contains sarcomeres
D. Smooth muscle requires Ca2+ for contraction whereas skeletal muscle does not
Correct ✅
Incorrect ❌
Explanation

Correct answer: B

Smooth muscle can show myogenic activity, contracting directly in response to stretch or other local stimuli.

Why the other choices are not ideal:

  • A. Multinucleation from fusion is characteristic of skeletal muscle, not smooth muscle.
  • C. Smooth muscle is not organized into visible sarcomeres, so it is nonstriated.
  • D. Both skeletal and smooth muscle require Ca2+ for contraction.
4. A cardiac tissue preparation continues rhythmic contractions even after its autonomic nerves are cut, and its cells depolarize nearly simultaneously. Which structure most directly supports this coordinated behavior?
A. Gap junctions within intercalated discs
B. Neuromuscular junctions with acetylcholinesterase
C. Tight junctions preventing ion flow between adjacent cells
D. Desmosomes binding keratin intermediate filaments in the epidermis
Correct ✅
Incorrect ❌
Explanation

Correct answer: A

Gap junctions in intercalated discs allow ions to pass directly between cardiac myocytes, enabling rapid, coordinated depolarization.

Why the other choices are not ideal:

  • B. Neuromuscular junctions describe skeletal muscle innervation, not cardiac cell-to-cell coupling.
  • C. Tight junctions would reduce ion movement and hinder coordination.
  • D. That refers to epithelial cell adhesion, not cardiac electrical coupling.
5. During sarcomere contraction, which region must decrease in length while the A-band remains unchanged?
A. A-band
B. H-zone
C. M-line
D. Myosin filament length
Correct ✅
Incorrect ❌
Explanation

Correct answer: B

The H-zone contains only thick filaments; as thin filaments slide inward, overlap increases and the H-zone shrinks, while the A-band (thick filament length) stays constant.

Why the other choices are not ideal:

  • A. The A-band represents the full length of thick filaments and does not change.
  • C. The M-line is a landmark, not a band whose length is tracked like the H-zone or I-band.
  • D. Myosin filament length does not change; sliding changes overlap, not filament length.
6. In skeletal muscle regulation, troponin and tropomyosin are associated with which filament?
A. Titin (elastic filament)
B. Myosin (thick filament)
C. Intermediate filaments linking adjacent myofibrils
D. Actin (thin filament)
Correct ✅
Incorrect ❌
Explanation

Correct answer: D

Troponin and tropomyosin sit on actin; Ca2+ binding to troponin shifts tropomyosin to expose myosin-binding sites on actin.

Why the other choices are not ideal:

  • A. Titin anchors and provides elasticity but is not the Ca2+-regulated blocker of binding sites.
  • B. Myosin provides the motor heads but does not carry troponin/tropomyosin.
  • C. Intermediate filaments are structural, not the main contraction regulatory proteins.
7. A toxin prevents action potentials from traveling down T-tubules. Which immediate downstream event in skeletal muscle is most directly impaired?
A. Actin synthesis by ribosomes
B. ATP hydrolysis by myosin heads
C. Acetylcholine release into the synaptic cleft
D. Ca2+ release from the sarcoplasmic reticulum into the sarcoplasm
Correct ✅
Incorrect ❌
Explanation

Correct answer: D

T-tubules carry depolarization into the cell interior to trigger Ca2+ release from the sarcoplasmic reticulum, which is required to uncover actin binding sites.

Why the other choices are not ideal:

  • A. Actin synthesis is not an immediate step in contraction signaling.
  • B. Myosin can hydrolyze ATP, but without Ca2+ exposure of binding sites, cross-bridge cycling cannot proceed normally.
  • C. ACh release is presynaptic at the neuromuscular junction and occurs before T-tubule conduction in the muscle fiber.
8. At the neuromuscular junction, acetylcholine binding to receptors on the sarcolemma most directly causes:
A. Immediate degradation of acetylcholine by acetylcholinesterase
B. Passive diffusion of myosin heads off actin
C. Depolarization of the sarcolemma leading to an action potential
D. Direct exposure of actin binding sites without Ca2+ release
Correct ✅
Incorrect ❌
Explanation

Correct answer: C

ACh binding depolarizes the muscle membrane, initiating an action potential that propagates along the sarcolemma and down T-tubules.

Why the other choices are not ideal:

  • A. AChE terminates the signal after ACh has acted.
  • B. Myosin detachment requires ATP binding, not passive diffusion.
  • D. Actin sites are exposed when Ca2+ binds troponin, not directly by ACh.
9. Which step in the cross-bridge cycle requires ATP binding (rather than ATP hydrolysis) to proceed?
A. Detachment of myosin from actin after the powerstroke
B. The powerstroke that pulls actin toward the M-line
C. Release of Pi from the myosin head to trigger force generation
D. Binding of Ca2+ to troponin
Correct ✅
Incorrect ❌
Explanation

Correct answer: A

ATP binding to myosin decreases myosin’s affinity for actin, allowing detachment. Hydrolysis then re-cocks the head for the next cycle.

Why the other choices are not ideal:

  • B. The powerstroke is driven by release of Pi and ADP, not ATP binding.
  • C. Pi release is part of force generation after myosin is bound to actin.
  • D. Ca2+ binding regulates access to binding sites but is not an ATP-binding step.
10. Rigor mortis occurs after death primarily because:
A. Acetylcholinesterase is overactive, causing constant depolarization
B. ATP production stops and myosin heads cannot detach from actin
C. Troponin permanently blocks actin binding sites even in the presence of Ca2+
D. Excess ATP forces continuous detachment and prevents contraction
Correct ✅
Incorrect ❌
Explanation

Correct answer: B

Without ATP, myosin cannot release from actin, so muscles become stiff in a locked, contracted state.

Why the other choices are not ideal:

  • A. AChE breaks down ACh; overactivity would reduce stimulation, not cause sustained contraction.
  • C. The problem is cross-bridge detachment failure; troponin does not explain postmortem stiffness by itself.
  • D. The issue is ATP depletion, not excess ATP.
11. A person lifts a heavier object and produces greater overall muscle force without changing the all-or-nothing response of individual muscle fibers. Which mechanism most directly explains the increased force?
A. Each sarcomere produces a larger powerstroke because it hydrolyzes more ATP per cycle
B. Myosin filaments lengthen to generate more overlap
C. Individual muscle fibers partially contract because their action potentials become graded
D. Additional motor units are recruited, increasing the number of contracting fibers
Correct ✅
Incorrect ❌
Explanation

Correct answer: D

Force is graded at the whole-muscle level by recruiting more motor units (more fibers contracting), since single fibers are all-or-nothing.

Why the other choices are not ideal:

  • A. Powerstroke size is not increased simply by more ATP hydrolysis per cycle.
  • B. Filament length does not change; overlap changes with sliding.
  • C. Muscle fiber action potentials are all-or-nothing, not graded.
12. In a simple twitch, the latent period most directly reflects the time required for:
A. Blood to deliver oxygen to the muscle fiber
B. The action potential to propagate and Ca2+ to be released from the sarcoplasmic reticulum
C. Myosin to synthesize new actin filaments
D. ATP to be regenerated in mitochondria after contraction
Correct ✅
Incorrect ❌
Explanation

Correct answer: B

The latent period is the delay between stimulus and contraction while the AP travels and triggers Ca2+ release needed for cross-bridge formation.

Why the other choices are not ideal:

  • A. Oxygen delivery is not the limiting step for the initial twitch timing.
  • C. Actin filaments are not synthesized during a twitch.
  • D. ATP regeneration is important for sustained activity but is not the main cause of the immediate latent delay.
13. A muscle fiber receives repeated stimuli before it can fully relax; the resulting contractions add together to produce a stronger contraction. This phenomenon is called:
A. Negative selection
B. Myogenic pacing
C. Frequency summation
D. Rigor mortis
Correct ✅
Incorrect ❌
Explanation

Correct answer: C

When stimuli arrive close together, the fiber has insufficient time to relax and the contractions summate, increasing force and duration.

Why the other choices are not ideal:

  • A. Negative selection is a thymic process for T-cells, unrelated to muscle.
  • B. Myogenic pacing refers to intrinsic rhythmicity (e.g., heart), not added twitches.
  • D. Rigor mortis is postmortem stiffness due to lack of ATP.
14. A patient develops sustained, painful muscle spasms after a puncture wound. A toxin is suspected to block release of an inhibitory neurotransmitter onto motor neurons. Which transmitter is most consistent with this mechanism?
A. Dopamine
B. Acetylcholine
C. Serotonin
D. GABA
Correct ✅
Incorrect ❌
Explanation

Correct answer: D

Tetanospasmin from Clostridium tetani blocks inhibitory input (classically GABA) to motor neurons, making them overexcitable and causing sustained contraction.

Why the other choices are not ideal:

  • A. Dopamine is primarily a CNS neuromodulator, not the classic inhibitory brake on spinal motor neurons in tetanus.
  • B. ACh is the excitatory transmitter at the neuromuscular junction.
  • C. Serotonin has CNS roles but is not the key inhibitory transmitter blocked in tetanus.
15. Which pair of tissues both commonly exhibit myogenic activity (ability to contract without nervous system input)?
A. Smooth muscle and cardiac muscle
B. Cardiac muscle and bone
C. Skeletal muscle and epidermis
D. Skeletal muscle and cardiac muscle
Correct ✅
Incorrect ❌
Explanation

Correct answer: A

Both smooth and cardiac muscle can initiate contraction in response to local stimuli or intrinsic pacemaking, although each can be modulated by the autonomic nervous system.

Why the other choices are not ideal:

  • B. Bone is not contractile and does not show myogenic contraction.
  • C. Epidermis is not contractile muscle tissue.
  • D. Skeletal muscle generally requires somatic motor neuron input at the neuromuscular junction.
Passages (3)
Passage 1

A sports physiology lab compared two training programs over 12 weeks. Group E performed long-distance runs at a steady pace four days per week. Group S performed repeated 10-second maximal sprints with long rest intervals.

Muscle biopsies from the quadriceps showed that Group E had increased myoglobin content and higher mitochondrial density in many fibers. Group S showed less change in myoglobin, but improved peak force output during short efforts. During a treadmill test, participants in Group E maintained performance for a long time with only gradual fatigue, whereas Group S participants reached high power quickly but fatigued after repeated bouts.

The investigators reminded participants that both groups rely on actin–myosin cross-bridge cycling for contraction, but differences in fiber properties shift how ATP is supplied during activity and how quickly fatigue sets in.

  • Map the evidence to fiber properties: myoglobin and mitochondria point to oxidative endurance fibers.
  • Use performance patterns: fatigue resistance suggests slow-twitch; short burst power suggests fast-twitch.
  • When asked about metabolism, connect time scale to ATP supply: seconds favors rapid anaerobic output; minutes favors aerobic output.

A. Smooth muscle cells with tonus

C. Slow-twitch oxidative fibers (red fibers)
D. Fast-twitch glycolytic fibers (white fibers)
Correct ✅
Incorrect ❌
Explanation

Correct answer: C

High myoglobin and many mitochondria indicate slow-twitch oxidative (red) fibers optimized for endurance and fatigue resistance.

Why the other choices are not ideal:

  • A. Smooth muscle is not categorized into red/white fibers and is nonstriated.
  • B. Cardiac muscle is striated and oxidative, but a limb muscle biopsy for running performance points to skeletal fiber types.
  • D. Fast-twitch glycolytic fibers have less myoglobin and fatigue faster.

A. Greater reliance on anaerobic ATP production with quicker fatigue

Incorrect ❌
Explanation

Correct answer: A

Short, high-power efforts depend heavily on rapid ATP generation via anaerobic pathways and fast-twitch fiber recruitment, which also leads to quicker fatigue.

Why the other choices are not ideal:

  • B. Sprint training aims to increase force and rapid cycling, not reduce it.
  • C. That pattern fits endurance activity and slow-twitch fibers.
  • D. Low glycolysis and high myoglobin describe oxidative endurance muscle.

Postural endurance muscles are enriched in slow-twitch red fibers: high myoglobin, many mitochondria, and primarily aerobic metabolism.

Passage 2

A 28-year-old patient reports that their eyelids droop more as the day goes on and that repeated chewing quickly becomes tiring. On exam, strength decreases with repeated muscle use but improves after rest. Nerve conduction studies show that the nerve can carry action potentials normally.

The clinician administers a medication that inhibits acetylcholinesterase. Within minutes, the patient’s strength improves. The clinician explains that this intervention increases how long acetylcholine remains available in the synaptic cleft at the neuromuscular junction.

In a separate experiment, a different compound is applied to an isolated motor neuron terminal and is found to block Ca2+ entry into the presynaptic terminal during depolarization.

  • Localize the problem: normal nerve conduction shifts focus to the neuromuscular junction rather than the axon.
  • Interpret the drug response: acetylcholinesterase inhibition helps when effective signaling is too weak per impulse.
  • Remember the sequence: presynaptic depolarization → Ca2+ influx → vesicle fusion → ACh release → muscle depolarization.
  • For cross-bridge questions: identify which step needs ATP binding versus ATP hydrolysis.
19. A patient has fluctuating weakness that worsens with repeated use. After giving a drug that inhibits acetylcholinesterase, strength improves temporarily. Which underlying defect best explains both the symptom pattern and the response to treatment?
A. Autoantibodies targeting postsynaptic nicotinic acetylcholine receptors
B. Loss of gap junctions between skeletal muscle fibers
C. Failure of the sarcoplasmic reticulum to store Ca2+
D. Mutation that increases myoglobin breakdown in muscle
Correct ✅
Incorrect ❌
Explanation

Correct answer: A

Postsynaptic receptor loss (as in myasthenia gravis) makes each ACh release less effective; inhibiting AChE increases ACh availability to improve end-plate depolarization.

Why the other choices are not ideal:

  • B. Skeletal muscle fibers are not electrically coupled by gap junctions for contraction like cardiac muscle.
  • C. SR dysfunction would impair contraction broadly but would not be specifically improved by increasing synaptic ACh.
  • D. Myoglobin changes would not specifically cause fatigable weakness that improves with AChE inhibition.
20. A second toxin is found to prevent Ca2+ entry into the presynaptic terminal of a motor neuron. Which immediate effect on the neuromuscular junction would be expected?
A. Decreased acetylcholine release from the motor neuron terminal
B. Direct opening of voltage-gated Na+ channels on the muscle without neurotransmitter
C. Increased exposure of myosin-binding sites on actin
D. Increased breakdown of acetylcholine in the synapse
Correct ✅
Incorrect ❌
Explanation

Correct answer: A

Presynaptic Ca2+ influx is required for synaptic vesicle fusion; blocking Ca2+ entry decreases ACh release.

Why the other choices are not ideal:

  • B. Skeletal muscle depolarization at the NMJ depends on ACh receptor activation; it is not directly triggered without neurotransmitter in this scenario.
  • C. Exposure of binding sites depends on Ca2+ inside the muscle fiber binding troponin, not presynaptic Ca2+ entry.
  • D. AChE activity is not directly increased by blocking presynaptic Ca2+ channels.
21. During the cross-bridge cycle, why does blocking ATP binding to myosin lead to impaired muscle relaxation?
A. ATP binding increases the length of myosin filaments
B. ATP binding is required for myosin to detach from actin after the powerstroke
C. ATP binding directly increases acetylcholine release from motor neurons
D. ATP binding is required for Ca2+ to bind troponin
Correct ✅
Incorrect ❌
Explanation

Correct answer: B

ATP binding lowers myosin’s affinity for actin; without ATP binding, cross-bridges remain attached and relaxation is impaired.

Why the other choices are not ideal:

  • A. Filament lengths do not change during contraction; sliding changes overlap.
  • C. ATP binding to myosin is a muscle-internal event and does not directly regulate presynaptic neurotransmitter release.
  • D. Ca2+ binds troponin based on Ca2+ availability, not ATP binding to myosin.
Passage 3

Researchers stimulate a single skeletal muscle fiber while imaging sarcomeres under high magnification. During contraction, they measure band lengths and observe that the A-band length does not change. However, the I-band and H-zone become progressively smaller with stimulation.

In a second trial, the same fiber is stimulated again, but at a higher frequency so that each stimulus arrives before the fiber fully relaxes. The measured tension increases with each successive stimulus until the trace becomes a sustained plateau with no relaxation between stimuli.

Finally, the researchers add a Ca2+ chelator to the preparation after stimulation and note that cross-bridge formation stops even though ATP is still present.

  • Use band behavior: constant A-band means thick filament length is unchanged; shrinking I-band/H-zone means increased overlap.
  • Translate band changes into geometry: increased overlap brings Z-lines closer (shorter sarcomere).
  • For repeated stimulation: if twitches add up, that is summation; if there is no relaxation at all, that is tetanus.
  • For Ca2+ removal: without Ca2+ bound to troponin, tropomyosin blocks actin binding sites.

C. Z-lines move closer together as the sarcomere shortens
D. Myosin filaments elongate to increase overlap
Correct ✅
Incorrect ❌
Explanation

Correct answer: C

Sliding of thin filaments toward the M-line shortens the sarcomere, bringing Z-lines closer together while A-band length remains constant.

Why the other choices are not ideal:

  • B. Thin filaments do not depolymerize during normal contraction; they slide.

23. A muscle fiber is stimulated repeatedly such that tension increases with each stimulus because the fiber does not fully relax between stimuli. If stimulation frequency increases further until no relaxation occurs, the fiber is experiencing:
A. A latent period
B. A single simple twitch
C. A decrease in motor unit recruitment
D. Tetanus
Correct ✅
Incorrect ❌
Explanation

Correct answer: D

At very high stimulation frequency, individual twitches fuse into a sustained contraction with no relaxation—tetanus.

Why the other choices are not ideal:

  • A. The latent period is the brief delay after a single stimulus before contraction begins.
  • B. A simple twitch includes relaxation; it is a single brief response.
  • C. Recruitment concerns multiple motor units; this describes frequency effects within a fiber.
24. A researcher chelates Ca2+ in a muscle fiber preparation after stimulation. Which regulatory outcome most directly prevents further cross-bridge formation?
A. Myosin heads lose the ability to hydrolyze ATP
B. Tropomyosin covers myosin-binding sites on actin
C. Intercalated discs break down and stop ion flow
D. Troponin remains bound to Ca2+ and keeps binding sites exposed

Incorrect ❌
Explanation

Correct answer: B

Without Ca2+ bound to troponin, tropomyosin re-covers actin’s myosin-binding sites, preventing cross-bridge binding.

Why the other choices are not ideal:

  • A. ATP hydrolysis capability is not the primary block; the binding sites are inaccessible.
  • C. Intercalated discs are a cardiac feature, not the key regulator in skeletal muscle fibers.
  • D. Chelation removes Ca2+, so troponin cannot keep sites exposed.
