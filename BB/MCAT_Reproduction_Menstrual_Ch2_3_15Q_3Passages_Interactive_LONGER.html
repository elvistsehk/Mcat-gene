A. He will express the disease because he is hemizygous for X-linked genes
B. He will be a carrier but unaffected because recessive alleles are silent in males
C. He will express the disease only if his Y chromosome also carries the allele
D. He will express the disease only if he undergoes lyonization of the X chromosome
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A.

Why: Males have only one X chromosome; with no second allele to mask a recessive mutation, an X-linked recessive allele is expressed.

Why the other choices are not the best fit:

  • C: The Y chromosome generally does not carry homologous alleles for most X-linked loci.
  • D: X-inactivation (lyonization) applies to females with two X chromosomes.

Passage 1: Puberty Signaling, Two-Cell Control in the Testis, and Feedback

A 15-year-old presents with delayed puberty, reduced facial hair growth, and decreased testicular volume for age. He reports normal childhood growth but minimal recent voice deepening. On exam, the testes are descended and anatomically normal in position.

Laboratory testing shows low serum testosterone, low inhibin B, and low-to-normal gonadotropins. A GnRH stimulation test is performed: after a single dose of GnRH, LH and FSH rise modestly but do not reach typical pubertal peaks. The clinician suspects impaired pulsatile GnRH release rather than complete pituitary failure.

Ultrasound confirms normal testicular architecture, including seminiferous tubules and surrounding interstitial tissue. A limited biopsy is obtained. Histology shows that spermatogonia are present and some primary spermatocytes appear, but later stages (spermatids/spermatozoa) are sparse. Interstitial tissue shows reduced steroidogenic activity compared with age-matched controls.

The clinician explains that two different testicular cell populations respond to different pituitary hormones: one population supports spermatogenesis within seminiferous tubules, while the other produces testosterone in the interstitium. Testosterone drives male secondary sexual characteristics and also provides negative feedback to both the hypothalamus and anterior pituitary to stabilize hormone output.

To localize the defect, the team compares two treatment strategies over eight weeks: (i) intermittent GnRH pulses delivered via a pump, and (ii) constant (nonpulsatile) GnRH infusion. Only the pulsatile regimen produces a meaningful rise in LH/FSH and downstream testicular function.

How to reason:

  1. Translate labs into axis-level problems: low testosterone + low-to-normal LH/FSH suggests upstream (GnRH) under-stimulation.
  2. Use testis cell division of labor: LH → Leydig → testosterone; FSH → Sertoli → sperm maturation (and inhibin-related support).
  3. Leverage the GnRH experiment: physiology depends on pulsatile GnRH; constant signaling can blunt gonadotropin release.
  4. Pick answers that match both the location (interstitium vs seminiferous tubules) and the direction of feedback effects.

Passage 2: Cycle Timing, Contraception, and Interpreting Hormone Patterns

A student tracks daily basal body temperature (BBT) and weekly serum hormone levels across two consecutive 28-day cycles. In Cycle A, BBT is relatively lower during days 1–13 and then increases by ~0.3–0.5°C beginning around day 15, remaining elevated until the next bleed. The student’s ultrasound report notes follicle growth during the first half of the cycle and a corpus luteum-like structure after midcycle.

Hormone measurements in Cycle A show that estrogen rises during the follicular phase and reaches a high level near midcycle. Shortly thereafter, LH shows a sharp, narrow spike, while FSH also increases but less dramatically. Progesterone remains low early but rises markedly during the latter half of the cycle before falling just prior to menstruation.

In Cycle B, the student starts a combined oral contraceptive pill (OCP) containing estrogen and progesterone. During that month, follicular development on ultrasound is minimal. Serum LH and FSH remain relatively low and do not exhibit a prominent midcycle surge. However, during the placebo week, the student experiences bleeding similar in duration to a typical menses.

The instructor emphasizes two principles: (1) exogenous estrogen/progesterone provide negative feedback to suppress GnRH and pituitary gonadotropins, preventing an LH surge and ovulation; and (2) bleeding can still occur when hormone levels drop, because the endometrium requires sustained steroid support to remain intact.

To test understanding, the instructor presents a third pattern: a patient with low progesterone in the late cycle and an endometrium that never thickens adequately, despite a normal midcycle LH surge.

How to reason:

  1. Use physiologic markers: an LH surge is the best clue that ovulation occurred; a sustained BBT rise supports luteal progesterone effects.
  2. Apply feedback logic: estrogen/progesterone (OCPs) → negative feedback → low GnRH → low LH/FSH → no ovulation.
  3. Separate ‘bleeding’ from ‘ovulation’: withdrawal bleeding is driven by hormone withdrawal, not by release of an egg.
  4. For unusual patterns, decide whether the defect is pre-ovulatory (follicle/estrogen) or post-ovulatory (corpus luteum/progesterone).

Passage 3: Fertilization Mechanics, Oocyte Arrest, and Receptor-Based Sex Development

In an assisted-reproduction clinic, clinicians retrieve a secondary oocyte from the fallopian tube shortly after ovulation. The cell appears unusually large and is surrounded by a thick glycoprotein-rich zona pellucida. An outer layer of adherent granulosa-derived cells—the corona radiata—remains attached, suggesting recent ovulation.

A semen analysis from the partner reveals many motile sperm. Microscopy highlights a prominent midpiece packed with mitochondria and a long flagellum. The embryologist explains that mitochondria supply ATP required for motility, especially as sperm travel from the cervix through the uterus toward the oviduct.

The embryologist further notes that the sperm head bears an acrosome, a Golgi-derived cap that contains hydrolytic enzymes. Upon contact with the zona pellucida, the sperm undergoes an acrosome reaction, releasing enzymes that help it penetrate the zona pellucida and ultimately fuse with the oocyte membrane.

The oocyte at retrieval is still arrested in meiosis and will not complete its final meiotic division unless fertilization occurs. Successful penetration and fusion trigger completion of meiosis II, producing a mature ovum and a polar body; only then can the haploid pronuclei from sperm and ovum merge to form a diploid zygote.

In a separate adolescent clinic case, a phenotypically female teenager presents with primary amenorrhea. Karyotyping reveals XY chromosomes. Serum testosterone is within or above the typical male range. Imaging reveals no uterus. The clinician suspects androgen insensitivity syndrome (AIS), in which androgen receptors are absent or defective, preventing testosterone from producing typical male differentiation despite normal androgen production.

How to reason:

  1. Recall arrest points: primary oocytes arrest in prophase I; ovulated secondary oocytes arrest in metaphase II.
  2. Connect structure to function: acrosome enzymes enable penetration of the zona pellucida/corona radiata; penetration is the trigger for meiosis II completion.
  3. Use endocrine logic in AIS: chromosomal sex (XY) can differ from phenotype when receptors fail to respond to hormones.
  4. Eliminate options that describe female-cycle timing (LH surge) when the question is about fertilization-triggered events.
