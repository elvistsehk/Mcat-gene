MCAT Biology — Skeletal System (Ch. 11.2)
Standalone Questions (Q1–Q15)
1. A biopsy from the shaft of a long bone shows thick, densely packed matrix with little internal space. Which macroscopic tissue type is most responsible for the bone’s characteristic strength?
A. Compact bone
B. Spongy (cancellous) bone
C. Articular cartilage
D. Periosteum
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A.

Why: Compact bone is dense and arranged to resist bending and torsion; it provides most of a long bone’s strength, especially in the diaphysis.

Other choices:

  • B: Spongy bone is a trabecular lattice optimized for dispersing forces and housing marrow; it is not the primary source of macroscopic strength.
  • C: Articular cartilage cushions joints; it is flexible and avascular, not a major contributor to bone strength.
  • D: The periosteum is a fibrous covering important for protection and muscle attachment, not the tissue type generating strength.
2. A teenager’s long bones stop increasing in length shortly after puberty. Which structure’s change most directly explains the halt in longitudinal growth?
A. Expansion of the Haversian canals
B. Conversion of red marrow to yellow marrow
C. Closure of the epiphyseal (growth) plate
D. Thickening of the synovial capsule
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: C.

Why: Longitudinal growth occurs at the epiphyseal plate (cartilage with mitotic cells). When the plate ossifies/closes during puberty, lengthening stops.

Other choices:

  • A: Haversian canals are vascular/nerve channels within osteons; they support bone health but do not determine growth in length.
  • B: Marrow composition affects hematopoiesis and fat storage, not the mechanism of growth plate-driven elongation.
  • D: The synovial capsule encloses movable joints; it is not the longitudinal growth site of long bones.
3. The major inorganic crystals that harden bone matrix contain calcium, phosphate, and hydroxide. Which compound best represents this mineral component?
A. Calcium carbonate
B. Calcium chloride
C. Calcium sulfate
D. Hydroxyapatite
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: D.

Why: Bone’s inorganic matrix is primarily hydroxyapatite crystals, a calcium phosphate hydroxide mineral that confers rigidity.

Other choices:

  • A: Calcium carbonate is important in shells and some stones, but it is not the primary mineral of human bone.
  • B: Calcium chloride is a salt; it does not form the structural crystals embedded in bone matrix.
  • C: Calcium sulfate is not the major mineralized component of bone.
4. A patient with hyperparathyroidism has elevated parathyroid hormone (PTH). Which change in bone activity is most directly promoted by this hormone?
A. Increased cartilage secretion by chondrocytes
B. Increased bone resorption leading to higher blood calcium
C. Increased synovial fluid production to lubricate joints
D. Reduced remodeling in response to mechanical stress
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: B.

Why: PTH is released when blood calcium is low; it promotes bone resorption (via osteoclast activity/signaling), releasing calcium and phosphate into the bloodstream.

Other choices:

  • A: Chondrocytes secrete cartilage matrix; PTH’s key systemic role here is calcium homeostasis via bone turnover.
  • C: Synovial fluid is produced by synovium at joints; it is not a primary endocrine target of PTH.
  • D: Bone remodeling in response to stress is a normal adaptive process; PTH does not “turn off” remodeling broadly.
5. Which bone region is the cylindrical shaft of a long bone and typically contains marrow within its interior?
A. Epiphysis
B. Metaphysis
C. Diaphysis
D. Perichondrium
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: C.

Why: The diaphysis is the long shaft of a long bone. It is primarily compact bone externally and contains marrow internally.

Other choices:

  • A: The epiphyses are the enlarged ends that participate in joints and disperse forces with spongy cores.
  • B: The metaphysis is the flared region between shaft and end; it is not the long cylindrical shaft.
  • D: Perichondrium surrounds cartilage, not the shaft region of bone.
6. A fracture patient heals slowly because the outer covering of the bone was severely damaged, reducing sites for muscle attachment and limiting local cells that can become bone-forming cells. Which structure was most likely injured?
A. Periosteum
B. Synovium
C. Articular cartilage
D. Volkmann’s canal
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A.

Why: The periosteum is a fibrous sheath around bone that protects it, provides muscle attachment, and contains cells that can differentiate into bone-forming cells—important for growth and repair.

Other choices:

  • B: Synovium lines movable joints and secretes synovial fluid; it does not surround long bone shafts as an external sheath.
  • C: Articular cartilage coats joint surfaces for cushioning; it is not the main repair-supporting outer covering of bone.
  • D: Volkmann’s canals are transverse channels in compact bone; injury to them affects vascular supply locally but does not match the described “outer covering” and attachment role.
7. Mature bone cells housed in small cavities within the lamellae are called:
A. Osteoblasts
B. Chondrocytes
C. Osteoclasts
D. Osteocytes
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: D.

Why: Osteocytes are mature bone cells that reside in lacunae within the mineralized matrix and help maintain bone tissue.

Other choices:

  • A: Osteoblasts build bone matrix; they are typically on bone surfaces during formation.
  • B: Chondrocytes produce cartilage matrix, not bone.
  • C: Osteoclasts resorb bone; they are large, multinucleated and act at bone surfaces.
8. A histology slide shows concentric rings of mineralized matrix surrounding a central channel containing blood vessels and nerves. Which structural unit is being observed?
A. Trabecula
B. Osteon (Haversian system)
C. Epiphyseal plate
D. Synovial capsule
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: B.

Why: Compact bone is organized into osteons: lamellae arranged in concentric circles around a central canal that carries vessels and nerves.

Other choices:

  • A: Trabeculae are spicules in spongy bone forming a lattice; they do not form concentric lamellae around a central canal.
  • C: The epiphyseal plate is cartilaginous and is the site of bone lengthening, not a concentric lamellar unit.
  • D: The synovial capsule surrounds a joint cavity; it is not the microscopic structural unit of compact bone.
9. Red bone marrow differs from yellow bone marrow primarily because red marrow:
A. Contains hematopoietic stem cells that generate blood cells
B. Contains only adipocytes and is metabolically inactive
C. Is located only in articular cartilage at joints
D. Lacks any vascular supply
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A.

Why: Red marrow is hematopoietic: it contains stem cells that produce RBCs, WBCs, and platelets.

Other choices:

  • B: Yellow marrow is primarily fat and is relatively inactive compared with red marrow.
  • C: Marrow is found within bone cavities; articular cartilage is avascular and does not house marrow.
  • D: Bone is vascular; marrow is supplied by vessels within the bone.
10. Which pairing correctly matches a cell type with its primary function in the skeletal system?
A. Osteoclast — secretes cartilage matrix (chondrin)
B. Chondrocyte — resorbs bone to release calcium
C. Osteoblast — builds bone by depositing matrix
D. Osteocyte — multinucleated macrophage that chews bone
✅Correct

Correct Answer: C.

Why: Osteoblasts are bone-forming cells that synthesize and deposit bone matrix during formation/remodeling.

Other choices:

  • A: Cartilage matrix is secreted by chondrocytes, not osteoclasts.
  • B: Bone resorption is performed by osteoclasts, not chondrocytes.
  • D: Osteoclasts are the large multinucleated resorptive cells; osteocytes are mature maintenance cells in lacunae.
11. An adult patient with high blood calcium is found to have elevated calcitonin. Which bone-level effect is most consistent with this hormonal state?
A. Increased activation of vitamin D to increase resorption
B. Promotion of bone formation, lowering blood calcium
C. Inhibition of osteoblast activity to prevent mineral deposition
D. Closure of the epiphyseal plate to stop growth
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: B.

Why: Calcitonin (from thyroid parafollicular cells) is released when blood calcium is high and shifts balance toward bone formation, decreasing blood calcium.

Other choices:

  • A: Vitamin D activation (often downstream of PTH) is associated with promoting resorption, which would raise blood calcium.
  • C: Calcitonin does not inhibit osteoblasts as its primary homeostatic purpose; it opposes hypercalcemia by reducing circulating calcium.
  • D: Epiphyseal closure is a developmental/puberty-related event, not an acute endocrine response to calcium levels.
12. A clinician suspects impaired diffusion of nutrients and wastes between bone cells and the vascular channels in compact bone. Which structure would most directly be involved in this exchange process?
A. Lamellae
B. Volkmann’s canals
C. Haversian canals
D. Canaliculi
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: D.

Why: Canaliculi are tiny channels connecting lacunae; they permit exchange of nutrients and wastes between osteocytes and the central/transverse canals.

Other choices:

  • A: Lamellae are concentric rings of matrix; they provide structure but are not the microchannels for exchange.
  • B: Volkmann’s canals are transverse channels that connect osteons; they carry vessels but are not the fine network linking osteocytes.
  • C: Haversian canals are central channels within osteons; they carry vessels and nerves but do not directly connect to every osteocyte without canaliculi.
13. A researcher compares skeletal systems across species. Which feature is an advantage of an endoskeleton over an exoskeleton for a growing organism?

✅Correct

Correct Answer: A.

Why: Exoskeletons often require molting (shedding/regrowth) to accommodate growth, whereas endoskeletons can enlarge internally and remodel as the organism grows.

15. A clinician distinguishes a tendon injury from a ligament injury. Which statement is most accurate?
A. Tendons connect bone to bone, while ligaments connect muscle to bone

D. Tendons secrete synovial fluid, while ligaments form cartilage

❌Incorrect

Passages (3 passages × 3 questions)
Passage 1: Bone Pain, Calcium Levels, and Remodeling Signals

A 62-year-old woman presents with progressive back pain and loss of height over the past two years. A DEXA scan shows reduced bone mineral density. A vertebral compression fracture is suspected after a minor fall. Laboratory testing reveals normal serum magnesium and potassium, but low-normal serum calcium. The clinician notes that her diet has been low in calcium for several months.

On follow-up, additional labs show elevated parathyroid hormone and increased markers of bone turnover. Imaging suggests that the internal architecture of vertebrae contains thin bony struts with enlarged spaces between them. Despite the low bone mass, the clinician emphasizes that the patient’s skeleton remains metabolically active and constantly undergoing remodeling.

How to reason:

  1. Translate the clinical clues: low bone mineral density + compression fracture suggests weakened matrix and altered remodeling.
  2. Use labs to infer endocrine direction: low calcium tends to increase PTH, shifting balance toward resorption.
  3. Link structure to function: vertebrae have a lot of spongy bone; thinning struts imply changes in trabeculae.
  4. For cell questions, classify by role: osteoblasts build, osteoclasts resorb.
  5. Choose answers that explain the data (labs + imaging) rather than simply naming a term.

A. Chondrocyte secretion of chondrin into cartilage
B. Osteoblast deposition of new hydroxyapatite without resorption
C. Synovial cell production of lubricating fluid in articular cavities
D. Osteoclast-mediated resorption releasing calcium and phosphate
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: D.

Why: Elevated PTH in the setting of low/low-normal calcium points toward increased bone resorption, which releases mineral into the bloodstream. That process is executed by osteoclasts.

Other choices:

  • A: Cartilage production is not the main homeostatic lever for correcting low serum calcium.
  • B: Bone formation can occur, but the endocrine signal described (high PTH) primarily promotes resorption to raise serum calcium.
  • C: Joint lubrication is not the mechanism addressing low serum calcium or DEXA findings.
17. The vertebral imaging in Passage 1 describes “thin bony struts with enlarged spaces between them.” Which bone feature is most directly being described?
A. Trabeculae within spongy bone
B. Osteons within compact bone
C. Periosteum surrounding the diaphysis
D. Epiphyseal plates at the ends of long bones

❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A.

Why: Spongy bone is a lattice of bony spicules called trabeculae, with spaces between them filled by marrow. Thinning of struts and enlarged spaces points to trabecular loss.

Other choices:

  • B: Osteons are the compact bone units with concentric lamellae around central canals, not “struts with spaces.”
  • C: The periosteum is an outer fibrous covering, not the internal lattice described.
  • D: Epiphyseal plates are cartilaginous growth regions in adolescents; vertebrae in an adult do not use epiphyseal plates for growth.
18. Suppose the clinician administers a therapy that increases calcitonin signaling. Which change would best be expected over time?
A. Higher serum calcium due to accelerated resorption
B. Shift toward bone formation with a tendency to lower serum calcium
C. Faster longitudinal growth of long bones by reopening growth plates
D. Conversion of compact bone into cartilage to increase flexibility
✅Correct
❌Incorrect

Correct Answer: B.

Why: Calcitonin is released in response to high blood calcium and promotes bone formation (relative to resorption), which lowers circulating calcium levels.

  • C: Epiphyseal plate closure is not reversed by calcitonin in adults; longitudinal growth does not restart.

Passage 2: A Joint Problem in an Athlete

A 19-year-old soccer player reports sharp knee pain that worsens during running and improves with rest. There is no evidence of ligament tear on initial testing, but the knee feels “gritty” with motion. The clinician suspects early damage to tissues that normally reduce friction during movement. On examination, the joint is stable, but there is mild swelling.

An MRI suggests thinning of the smooth tissue coating the ends of the femur and tibia. The clinician explains that movable joints contain a capsule enclosing a joint cavity, and that a soft lining within the capsule produces a lubricating fluid that allows low-friction movement. The athlete is advised that persistent wear could lead to bones rubbing more directly against each other, amplifying pain.

How to reason:

  1. Identify what kind of joint is described: pain with motion + lubrication cues → a movable synovial joint.
  2. Differentiate tissues: articular cartilage coats bone ends; synovium secretes lubricating fluid; ligaments stabilize bone-to-bone connections.
  3. Use the absence of instability to downplay ligament rupture and focus on friction-reducing structures.
  4. Predict consequences: less lubrication or cartilage → increased friction → pain from bone-on-bone contact.
  5. Pick the option that matches the mechanism, not the symptom description alone.
19. Based on Passage 2, which structure’s degradation most directly explains the “bones rubbing more directly against each other” over time?
A. Periosteum
B. Epiphyseal plate
C. Articular cartilage
D. Volkmann’s canal
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: C.

Why: Articular cartilage coats the articular surfaces of bones in movable joints, limiting impact and friction. Loss of this cartilage leads to bone-on-bone contact (as in osteoarthritis).

Other choices:

  • A: The periosteum is an outer covering of bone and is not the friction-reducing surface inside the joint.
  • B: The epiphyseal plate is a growth structure in adolescents; it does not serve as the smooth joint-surface coating.
  • D: Volkmann’s canals are vascular channels in compact bone; they are unrelated to joint surface friction.

Passage 3: Building Bone, Breaking Bone, and Building Again

A graduate student joins a rowing team and increases training volume over three months. Early in the season, she develops localized forearm pain that worsens with repeated loading. A sports medicine physician explains that bone is dynamic and can adapt to repetitive stress by altering where it deposits and removes matrix. The physician expects that, if training is managed appropriately, the skeleton can remodel to better handle the new forces.

To study the adaptation, researchers obtain a small sample from the painful region. They observe a well-organized series of concentric rings around central channels that contain vessels and nerve fibers, as well as small cavities that each house a mature bone cell. The sample also shows increased activity of bone-forming cells in regions that are predicted to bear the highest stress during rowing strokes.

How to reason:

  1. Recognize the big idea: bone responds to mechanical stress via remodeling (resorption + formation).
  2. Map microscopic structures: concentric rings + central channels → osteons; cavities with mature cells → lacunae with osteocytes.
  3. Translate “bone-forming cells” → osteoblasts; “resorbing cells” → osteoclasts.
  4. Use the passage to predict what changes would help: reinforce high-stress regions with more matrix deposition.
  5. Select answers that connect structure → function → adaptive outcome, not memorized labels alone.

A. The epiphyseal plates reopen, increasing bone length

D. The remodeled high-stress reinforcement may diminish as the mechanical stimulus is removed
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: D.

Why: Remodeling is driven by mechanical stress. Removing the repetitive load reduces the stimulus for maintaining extra reinforcement, so bone may remodel toward a less reinforced baseline over time.
