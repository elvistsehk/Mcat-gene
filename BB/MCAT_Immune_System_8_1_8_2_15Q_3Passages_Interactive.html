MCAT Biology — Immune System (Ch. 8.1–8.2)
Standalone Questions (Q1–Q15)
1. A patient develops symptoms within hours of cutting their hand while cooking. Immune cells in the tissue begin phagocytosis and release inflammatory cytokines without needing prior exposure to the microbe. Which description best matches the response?
A. Adaptive immunity; rapid and antigen-specific with immunologic memory
B. Humoral immunity; driven primarily by antibodies secreted by plasma cells
C. Innate immunity; rapid and nonspecific, poised to respond immediately
D. Cell-mediated immunity; slower and dependent on clonal expansion of T-cells
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: C.

Why: A rapid response in tissues (phagocytosis + cytokines) that does not require prior antigen exposure is characteristic of innate (nonspecific) immunity.

Other choices:

  • A: Adaptive immunity is antigen-specific and generates memory, but it is slower to start.
  • B: Humoral immunity is the antibody arm of adaptive immunity; antibodies are not produced immediately after a first exposure.
  • D: Cell-mediated immunity relies on T-cell activation/expansion and is not the fastest first-line response after a new breach.
2. Which statement most accurately describes the status of B-cells immediately after they exit the bone marrow?
A. They are mature but naïve because they have not yet encountered their specific antigen
B. They are already plasma cells actively secreting high-affinity IgG
C. They have completed maturation in the thymus and will kill infected cells directly
D. They can present only endogenous antigens via MHC class I
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A.

Why: B-cells develop in bone marrow. When they leave, they are mature (receptor formed) but naïve (no antigen exposure yet).

Other choices:

  • B: Plasma cells form after activation (often in lymph nodes/spleen) and subsequent differentiation.
  • C: Thymus maturation is for T-cells, not B-cells; cytotoxic killing is a T-cell function.
  • D: All nucleated cells present endogenous peptides on MHC-I; B-cells can also present exogenous antigen on MHC-II when acting as APCs.
3. A clinician describes an immune cell that matures in a gland anterior to the heart and later helps coordinate immune responses and can directly kill virally infected cells. Which division of adaptive immunity is most associated with this cell?
A. Humoral immunity
B. Complement-mediated immunity
C. Cell-mediated immunity
D. Barrier immunity
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: C.

Why: T-cells mature in the thymus (anterior to the heart). Their hallmark is cell-mediated immunity (coordination by helper T-cells, killing by cytotoxic T-cells).

Other choices:

  • A: Humoral immunity is antibody-driven (B-cells → plasma cells).
  • B: Complement is mainly a nonspecific blood-protein defense (even if activated by antibodies).
  • D: Barriers (skin/mucosa) are noncellular innate defenses, not adaptive T-cell functions.
4. Which leukocyte is classified as a granulocyte?
A. Monocyte
B. Neutrophil
C. Lymphocyte
D. Macrophage
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: B.

Why: Granulocytes contain prominent cytoplasmic granules and include neutrophils, eosinophils, and basophils (with mast cells closely related to basophils).

Other choices:

  • A: Monocytes are agranulocytes in blood.
  • C: Lymphocytes (B-cells, T-cells, NK cells) are agranulocytes.
  • D: Macrophages are tissue-resident derivatives of monocytes and are agranulocytes.
5. A blood-borne phagocytic cell migrates into tissues and differentiates into a long-lived resident immune cell. Which starting cell in the bloodstream most directly gives rise to that resident population?
A. Basophil
B. B-lymphocyte
C. Eosinophil
D. Monocyte
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: D.

Why: Monocytes circulate in blood and become macrophages in tissues (including specialized resident forms such as microglia, Langerhans cells, and osteoclasts).

Other choices:

  • A: Basophils are granulocytes involved in allergic responses and histamine release.
  • B: B-cells differentiate into plasma cells or memory B-cells, not macrophages.
  • D: Eosinophils target parasites and participate in allergic inflammation; they do not become macrophages.
6. Complement can be activated by a pathway that involves antibody binding to a pathogen. Despite this, complement is considered part of innate immunity because:
A. Complement proteins are produced only after B-cells class-switch to IgG
B. Complement requires antigen presentation on MHC-II to function
C. Complement only targets viruses inside host cells
D. Complement acts broadly as a fixed, nonspecific protein system rather than a customizable antigen-specific response
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: D.

Why: Complement is a set of preformed blood proteins that provide nonspecific defense (e.g., membrane attack on bacteria). Even when antibodies trigger the classical pathway, the system itself is not “tailored” like adaptive receptors.

Other choices:

  • A: Complement proteins are present in blood as part of innate defense, not made only after class switching.
  • B: Complement does not require MHC-II antigen presentation to punch holes in bacterial membranes.
  • C: Complement primarily targets extracellular microbes (especially bacteria), not viruses inside cells.
7. A patient with influenza reports fever, muscle aches, and malaise early in the illness. Nearby uninfected cells reduce protein synthesis and increase antigen presentation. Which mediator most directly contributes to this “flu-like” syndrome and antiviral state?
A. Interferons released by virally infected cells
B. Histamine released by mast cells
C. Defensins released by keratinocytes
D. Lysozyme secreted in tears and saliva
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A.

Why: Interferons are produced by virally infected cells and induce nearby cells to reduce protein synthesis, decrease permeability, and upregulate MHC I/II—improving detection and limiting viral spread. They also contribute to “flu-like” symptoms.

Other choices:

  • B: Histamine causes vasodilation and vascular leak in allergic/inflammatory responses, not the antiviral protein-synthesis shutdown.
  • C: Defensins are antimicrobial enzymes on skin; they do not create a systemic antiviral state.
  • D: Lysozyme breaks down bacterial cell walls (peptidoglycan), not viral replication.
8. A respiratory pathogen is trapped in mucus and moved upward by coordinated beating of epithelial structures toward the oropharynx to be swallowed. This protective mechanism relies primarily on:
A. Antibody class switching in B-cells
B. Cilia-driven clearance along mucous membranes
C. Cytotoxic T-cell–mediated apoptosis of infected epithelial cells
D. Alternative complement activation requiring no antibodies
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: B.

Why: The respiratory tract uses mucus + cilia to trap particulate matter/pathogens and move them toward the throat for removal—an innate, noncellular defense.

Other choices:

  • A: Class switching is an adaptive B-cell process that occurs after activation, not a mechanical barrier defense.
  • C: Cytotoxic T-cells act later and kill infected cells; the prompt describes prevention of entry and clearance.
  • D: Complement is a blood protein system and does not describe mucociliary transport.
9. A macrophage detects conserved microbial features and rapidly adjusts cytokine release to recruit the most effective set of immune cells for that category of invader. Which receptor system best enables this “category recognition”?
A. T-cell receptors that undergo somatic recombination
B. B-cell receptors that undergo affinity maturation
C. Fc receptors that bind antibody-coated targets
D. Pattern recognition receptors such as toll-like receptors
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: D.

Why: PRRs (including TLRs) recognize conserved pathogen-associated patterns and help innate cells decide which cytokines to release to recruit appropriate effector cells.

Other choices:

  • A: TCR recombination creates antigen specificity in adaptive immunity, not broad “category” recognition.
  • B: Affinity maturation is an adaptive process after B-cell activation.
  • C: Fc receptors enable recognition of antibody-tagged targets (opsonization), which depends on adaptive antibodies.
10. A hepatocyte infected with an intracellular parasite displays peptides derived from proteins synthesized within the cell. Which antigen-presentation mechanism is most consistent with this situation?
A. Exogenous pathway via MHC class II
B. Antigen display only by macrophages and dendritic cells
C. Endogenous pathway via MHC class I on the infected nucleated cell
D. Complement-mediated lysis requiring IgE to bind the pathogen
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: C.

Why: MHC-I is expressed on all nucleated cells and presents endogenous peptides (from inside the cell), allowing immune surveillance of infected or abnormal cells.

Other choices:

  • A: MHC-II presents exogenous peptides and is primarily on professional antigen-presenting cells.
  • B: While macrophages/dendritic cells are professional APCs, any nucleated cell can present via MHC-I.
  • D: Complement does not require IgE, and this option mixes unrelated concepts.
11. A macrophage engulfs a bacterium from the extracellular environment, digests it, and then displays bacterial peptides on its surface to communicate with adaptive immune cells. Which pairing best matches this process?
A. Endogenous pathway; MHC class I
B. Exogenous pathway; MHC class II
C. Exogenous pathway; MHC class I
D. Endogenous pathway; MHC class II
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: B.

Why: Antigens that originate outside the APC (engulfed microbes) are presented via the exogenous pathway on MHC-II.

Other choices:

  • A: MHC-I is endogenous; the stem describes engulfed extracellular bacteria.
  • C: MHC-I is not the primary route for phagocytosed extracellular peptides in classic MCAT framing.
  • D: “Endogenous + MHC-II” mismatches both the source and the molecule class.
12. A tumor cell avoids detection by cytotoxic T-cells by decreasing surface expression of MHC class I. Which immune cell can still recognize and eliminate the tumor cell based on this change?
A. Natural killer cell
B. Plasma cell
C. Basophil
D. Naïve B-cell
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A.

Why: NK cells can detect downregulation of MHC (“missing self”) and induce apoptosis in infected or cancerous cells, helping when T-cells can’t recognize the target.

Other choices:

  • B: Plasma cells secrete antibodies; they do not directly kill MHC-low tumor cells.
  • C: Basophils mediate allergic inflammation via histamine release.
  • D: Naïve B-cells require activation and mainly contribute through antibodies, not direct killing based on MHC levels.
13. A patient presents with wheezing and a suspected helminth infection after travel. Which leukocyte type would be expected to rise and to play a prominent role in this scenario?
A. Neutrophil
B. Monocyte
C. NK cell
D. Eosinophil
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: D.

Why: Eosinophils are strongly associated with invasive parasitic infections and allergic-type responses; they contain red-orange granules and can contribute to inflammation.

Other choices:

  • A: Neutrophils are most associated with acute bacterial infections and pus.
  • B: Monocytes become macrophages and are general phagocytes, but eosinophils are the classic “parasite” clue.
  • C: NK cells kill virus-infected and MHC-low cancer cells; they are not the main responders to helminths.

Why: Mast cells are basophil-like cells that reside in tissues (mucosa/epithelium) and release histamine in response to allergens, driving inflammatory changes in vessels.

  • A: Neutrophils are phagocytes targeting bacteria; histamine release is not their defining function.

Passage 1: Barriers, the Gut, and Early Defense

A 6-year-old child scrapes a knee on a playground. The parents rinse the wound, cover it, and assume it will heal. By evening, the skin around the abrasion is red, warm, and tender. Overnight, the child develops a low-grade fever and the bandage becomes soaked with thick yellow drainage.

In clinic, the physician explains that the skin is usually a strong physical barrier and that normal skin secretions can inhibit microbes, but a break in the barrier provides a direct entry point for pathogens. The physician notes that early in a first-time infection, the body can still contain microbes quickly, even before a highly specific response develops.

During history-taking, the parents mention the child has had multiple antibiotic courses for ear infections over the past year and now has intermittent diarrhea after antibiotics. The physician explains that the gastrointestinal tract normally eliminates many pathogens through acidity in the stomach and also gains protection from the large population of resident bacteria that compete with invaders; disrupting that normal flora can change susceptibility to certain infections.

Two weeks later, the child’s younger sibling is born. The family asks why newborns can develop gastrointestinal infections more easily than older children. A nurse explains that the newborn gut has not yet developed a stable community of normal bacteria and that components in breast milk help protect mucosal surfaces while the infant’s immune system matures.

In discussing prevention, the physician adds that some circulating proteins can damage bacterial membranes and help contain infection even though those proteins are not “custom-made” to recognize only one bacterial species. The physician emphasizes that these defenses can operate rapidly alongside cellular responses at the site of injury.

How to reason:

  1. Identify what is barrier-based (skin integrity, stomach acid, mucus/cilia, normal flora) vs what is cell-based (phagocytes, cytokines, inflammation).
  2. If you see thick yellow drainage and acute local warmth/redness, think acute bacterial inflammation and which cell type dies in large numbers to form pus.
  3. If antibiotics are followed by diarrhea, connect to disrupted gut flora and loss of competitive protection in the GI tract.
  4. For newborn GI vulnerability, combine underdeveloped immunity with lack of established microbiota and mucosal protection from milk.
  5. For “blood proteins that damage bacterial membranes,” map to complement and why it is nonspecific.

✅Correct

Correct Answer: B.

Why: Thick yellow drainage (pus) often reflects collections of dead neutrophils plus debris after acute bacterial infection. Neutrophils are abundant, short-lived phagocytes that target bacteria.

  • C: Naïve B-cells are adaptive cells; they do not form pus via short-lived phagocytosis.

Passage 2: Viral Evasion and Immune Surveillance

Researchers culture human airway epithelial cells and infect them with a respiratory virus at low multiplicity of infection. Within hours, the infected cells secrete signaling proteins that diffuse through the culture. Neighboring uninfected cells respond by reducing overall protein synthesis, which limits viral replication if the virus spreads into those cells.

The same early signals also increase expression of antigen-presentation molecules on nearby cells. The researchers note that, even before any antibodies appear in a primary infection, the tissue environment can become “primed” for immune detection.

In a follow-up experiment, the team engineers a viral strain that decreases the infected cell’s surface display of one class of major histocompatibility complex molecules. When this engineered strain spreads, cytotoxic T-cells in co-culture show reduced activation, consistent with fewer “danger” signals coming from infected epithelial targets.

Unexpectedly, another immune cell population kills infected targets more efficiently in the engineered-strain condition than in the wild-type condition. The researchers propose that the viral modification shifts which immune surveillance pathway is most effective by altering how infected cells are “seen.”

They ask which mediator best explains the early antiviral tissue state, which immune cell type would gain an advantage against infected cells with reduced MHC display, and which antigen pathway would normally alert cytotoxic T-cells to virally infected epithelial cells.

How to reason:

  1. Signals that create an antiviral state and increase antigen display point to interferons.
  2. Cytotoxic T-cells depend on MHC-I on nucleated cells to detect endogenous viral peptides.
  3. If a virus lowers MHC-I, predict cytotoxic T-cells work worse, but NK cells work better (missing self).
  4. Match “which pathway normally alerts CTLs” to endogenous presentation on MHC-I.
  5. Choose the answer that explains the shift in killing efficiency across immune mechanisms.

❌Incorrect

Why: The described antiviral state (reduced protein synthesis, decreased permeability, increased antigen presentation) is a classic interferon effect.

Other choices:

  • A: Histamine causes vasodilation and vascular leak; it does not directly shut down protein synthesis to block viruses.
  • B: Complement targets microbes in blood/extracellular space, especially bacteria, and does not explain the intracellular antiviral state.
  • D: Lysozyme targets bacteria by breaking peptidoglycan; viruses are not affected this way.

Passage 3: Lymphoid Organs and Communication Hubs

A college student presents with sore throat, fatigue, and a sensation of fullness under the jaw. A friend says, “Your glands are swollen,” but the clinician explains that the structures enlarging are lymph nodes responding to immune activation rather than endocrine glands. On exam, the nodes are tender and mobile, and the student also has enlarged tonsillar tissue.

The clinician reviews how immune defenses are coordinated across organs. Leukocytes are produced in bone marrow through hematopoiesis. Some immune cells then complete maturation in specialized sites before circulating. The clinician explains that lymph nodes filter lymph and provide a place for immune cells to communicate and mount a coordinated response, including activation of cells that can later produce antibodies.

Because the student reports recent travel and a brief episode of gastrointestinal symptoms, the clinician explains that the digestive tract is a common portal of entry for pathogens. Clusters of immune tissue positioned near the gut help sample potential invaders entering via food and water; these sites are grouped under gut-associated lymphoid tissue.

Finally, the student asks why they rarely get the same illness twice in the same season. The clinician notes that some immune responses leave behind long-lived cells that respond more rapidly and strongly when the same antigen is encountered again, which changes the speed and magnitude of future responses.

How to reason:

  1. Translate anatomy language: “swollen glands” here are lymph nodes (lymphadenopathy), which swell with immune activation.
  2. When a question asks “why they swell,” think cell activation + proliferation happening inside nodes.
  3. For immune tissue near the gut, map the location clue to GALT, especially Peyer’s patches and appendix aggregates.
  4. For “rarely get it twice,” look for immunologic memory as a defining feature of adaptive immunity.
  5. Pick answers that connect the clinical observation to an underlying mechanism, not just a label.
