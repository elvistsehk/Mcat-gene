MCAT Biology — Cell Cycle, Mitosis & Meiosis (Ch. 2.1–2.2)
Standalone Questions (Q1–Q15)
1. A human somatic cell in G0 is best described as:
A. Undergoing separation of sister chromatids
B. Replicating DNA for an upcoming division
C. Reducing chromosome number by half
D. Not actively preparing to divide and carrying out normal cellular functions
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: D.

Why: G0 is an offshoot of G1 in which cells are metabolically active but not progressing through the cell cycle toward division.

Why the other choices are not the best fit:

  • B: DNA replication occurs in S phase, not G0.
  • C: Sister chromatids separate during anaphase of mitosis or anaphase II of meiosis.
  • D: Reduction of chromosome number occurs in meiosis I, not in G0.
2. A cell spends ~90% of its time in interphase. Which stages make up interphase?
A. G1, M, and cytokinesis
B. G1, S, and G2
C. G0, S, and M
D. S, G2, and M
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: B.

Why: Interphase comprises G1 (growth), S (DNA synthesis), and G2 (pre-mitotic checks).

Why the other choices are not the best fit:

  • B: G0 is a non-dividing state and M is mitosis; interphase excludes M.
  • C: M is not part of interphase.
  • D: M and cytokinesis occur during M stage, not interphase.
3. A diploid human cell has completed S phase. Which statement is most accurate?
A. It is now haploid because DNA was replicated
B. It still has 46 chromosomes, but each chromosome now consists of two sister chromatids (92 chromatids total)
C. It has 23 chromosomes because homologous pairs have separated
D. It now has 92 chromosomes because the DNA amount doubled
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: B.

Why: Replication doubles DNA content and chromatids, but chromosome count (by centromeres) and ploidy remain 2n until homologs separate in meiosis I.

Why the other choices are not the best fit:

  • B: Chromosome count is defined by centromeres; sister chromatids remain joined, so chromosomes are still 46.
  • C: Ploidy does not change in S phase.
  • D: Homologous separation occurs in anaphase I of meiosis, not S phase.
4. Which checkpoint is most directly described by the cell ‘deciding whether the condition of the DNA is good enough for synthesis’?
A. Metaphase checkpoint (spindle assembly)
B. S/G2 checkpoint
C. G1/S checkpoint (restriction point)
D. G2/M checkpoint
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: C.

Why: The G1/S checkpoint, also called the restriction point, controls entry into S phase and responds to DNA damage (notably via p53).

Why the other choices are not the best fit:

  • B: There is no canonical S/G2 checkpoint emphasized here; key checkpoints are G1/S and G2/M.
  • C: G2/M focuses on readiness for mitosis after DNA replication.
  • D: Spindle assembly is later and checks chromosome attachment during metaphase.
5. A tumor sample shows loss-of-function mutation in TP53. Which outcome is most consistent with this change?
A. Cyclin levels will never fluctuate during the cell cycle
B. Homologous chromosomes will fail to undergo synapsis
C. The cell will be unable to replicate DNA in S phase
D. Cells with damaged DNA are more likely to proceed through the cell cycle without arrest and repair
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: D.

Why: p53 helps arrest the cell cycle at checkpoints (especially G1/S and also G2/M) when DNA is damaged; loss can allow mutation accumulation and cancer.

Why the other choices are not the best fit:

  • B: Cyclin oscillations can still occur; p53 is a checkpoint regulator, not the direct generator of cyclin cycling.
  • C: DNA replication machinery is not eliminated by TP53 mutation; the issue is quality control/repair.
  • D: Synapsis occurs in meiosis I and is not the direct role of p53.
6. Cyclin-dependent kinases (CDKs) are activated most directly by:

B. Increasing the number of centrioles
C. Binding the correct cyclin to form a CDK–cyclin complex
D. Dephosphorylating DNA polymerase

❌Incorrect

Correct Answer: C.

Why: CDKs require stage-specific cyclins; the complex phosphorylates targets (including transcription factors) to drive progression.

Why the other choices are not the best fit:

  • B: DNA polymerase regulation is not the defining activation mechanism for CDKs here.
  • C: Centriole duplication is a cell-cycle event but not the mechanism of CDK activation.
  • D: Crossing over is a meiotic recombination event, unrelated to CDK activation.
7. During prophase of mitosis, which event best enables spindle microtubules to access chromosomes?
A. Formation of chiasmata
B. Replication of DNA
C. Separation of homologous chromosomes
D. Dissolution of the nuclear envelope
✅Correct
❌Incorrect

Why: In prophase, the nuclear membrane breaks down so spindle fibers can attach to kinetochores on chromosomes.

Why the other choices are not the best fit:

  • B: Chiasmata form during prophase I of meiosis, not mitotic prophase.
  • C: Homologous chromosome separation occurs in anaphase I of meiosis.
  • D: DNA replication occurs in S phase, before mitosis begins.
8. A drug prevents microtubule depolymerization. Which mitotic phase is most directly impaired?
A. Telophase
B. G1
C. S phase
D. Anaphase
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: D.

Why: Anaphase requires shortening of kinetochore microtubules to pull sister chromatids to opposite poles; blocking depolymerization disrupts separation.

Why the other choices are not the best fit:

  • B: G1 is primarily growth and organelle synthesis, not microtubule-driven chromatid movement.
  • C: S phase is DNA replication, not spindle fiber shortening.
  • D: Telophase involves nuclear reformation and chromosome decondensation after separation has occurred.
9. Which structure is the attachment site for spindle fibers on chromosomes?
A. Kinetochore (protein complex at the centromere region)
B. Ribosome
C. Synaptonemal complex
D. Nucleolus
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A.

Why: Kinetochore fibers attach to kinetochores at centromeres to move chromosomes during division.

Why the other choices are not the best fit:

  • B: Synaptonemal complex holds homologs together in prophase I of meiosis, not spindle attachment in mitosis.
  • C: The nucleolus makes rRNA; it is not a spindle attachment site.
  • D: Ribosomes translate mRNA; they do not attach spindle fibers to DNA.
10. Which statement best distinguishes metaphase I of meiosis from metaphase of mitosis?
A. In meiosis I, chromosomes are invisible; in mitosis, chromosomes are visible
B. In meiosis I, sister chromatids separate; in mitosis, homologous chromosomes separate
C. In meiosis I, homologous chromosomes (tetrads) align as pairs across from each other; in mitosis, individual chromosomes align with sister chromatids attached to opposite poles
D. In meiosis I, DNA is replicated; in mitosis, DNA is not replicated
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: C.

Why: Meiosis I aligns homologous pairs (tetrads) so homologs segregate in anaphase I; mitosis aligns each chromosome so sister chromatids can separate in anaphase.

Why the other choices are not the best fit:

  • B: Sister chromatids separate in mitotic anaphase and meiosis II; homologs separate in meiosis I.
  • C: DNA replication occurs before both processes (S phase).
  • D: Chromosomes are condensed and visible during metaphase in both processes.
11. Crossing over most directly increases genetic diversity by:
A. Recombining alleles between homologous chromosomes, potentially unlinking linked genes
B. Producing identical sister chromatids for accurate mitosis
C. Preventing independent assortment
D. Doubling chromosome number in gametes
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A.

Why: Crossing over occurs at chiasmata between homologous chromatids in prophase I, creating new allele combinations and reducing linkage.

Why the other choices are not the best fit:

  • B: Identical sister chromatids are produced in S phase; crossing over changes (some) chromatids rather than making them identical.
  • C: Meiosis reduces chromosome number; it does not double it in gametes.
  • D: Crossing over complements genetic variation; it does not prevent independent assortment.
12. A human gamete produced after meiosis II is typically:

13. Nondisjunction during anaphase I most directly causes which immediate problem in meiosis?

14. A student claims 'a chromosome' always means two chromatids. Which correction is most accurate?
A. Before S phase, a chromosome can be a single chromatid; after S phase, a chromosome consists of two sister chromatids joined at the centromere
B. Chromosomes are always identical to homologous pairs
C. Chromatids only appear after meiosis II
D. Chromosomes only exist during metaphase
✅Correct
❌Incorrect

Correct Answer: A.

Why: The term chromosome can refer to one chromatid (pre-S) or the two-chromatid unit (post-S) as long as sister chromatids remain joined at one centromere.

Why the other choices are not the best fit:

  • C: Homologous chromosomes are similar but not identical; sister chromatids are identical copies.

15. Which statement best explains why meiosis I is called 'reductional division'?

Why: Meiosis I separates homologous chromosomes, reducing ploidy from diploid to haploid.

Passage 1: Flow Cytometry and Checkpoint Failure

A researcher uses flow cytometry to measure DNA content in a population of human epithelial cells. Under normal conditions, most cells cluster at a DNA-content peak labeled 2C, with a smaller group spanning between 2C and 4C and an additional cluster at 4C.

After exposure to a DNA-damaging chemical, the researcher expects cell-cycle arrest. Instead, the treated population shows a large increase in cells that remain between 2C and 4C, and a second experiment reveals a loss-of-function mutation in TP53.

Microscopy of the treated culture also shows many mitotic figures with condensed chromosomes, but several cells display missegregation events and later form a rapidly growing mass when implanted into mice.

How to reason:

  1. Translate DNA-content peaks: 2C ≈ G1/G0, between 2C–4C ≈ S, 4C ≈ G2/M.
  2. Connect DNA damage to checkpoints: damage should trigger G1/S arrest (restriction point) and sometimes G2/M, largely via p53.
  3. If cells accumulate mutations and still enter division, infer checkpoint failure and higher cancer risk.
  4. Use the experimental observations (flow cytometry + TP53 mutation + tumor growth) to choose the most causal explanation.
16. In the untreated population, the cells spanning DNA content between 2C and 4C are most likely in which stage?
A. S phase
B. G2
C. G1
D. M phase
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A.

Why: DNA content increases from 2C to 4C during S phase as replication proceeds.

Why the other choices are not the best fit:

  • B: G1 cells have 2C DNA content before replication.
  • C: G2 cells have completed replication and are at ~4C.
  • D: M phase cells generally remain ~4C until cytokinesis; the defining feature here is intermediate DNA content during replication.

B. p53 normally arrests the cycle at checkpoints to allow repair; without it, damaged cells can enter S phase and divide
C. p53 is the enzyme that cross-links peptidoglycan, so loss weakens the cell wall
D. p53 is the structural protein of kinetochores, so loss prevents spindle attachment
✅Correct
❌Incorrect

Correct Answer: B.

Why: p53 is a key checkpoint regulator (notably at G1/S and also G2/M); loss reduces arrest/repair and permits mutation accumulation, promoting cancer.

Why the other choices are not the best fit:

  • B: DNA polymerase can function without p53; p53’s role is quality control, not the polymerase itself.
  • C: Kinetochores are protein complexes at centromeres; p53 is not their structural component.

18. The rapid tumor formation after implantation is most directly supported by which chain of reasoning?
A. Cells entered G0 permanently, which increased division rate
B. Crossing over during mitosis created new allele combinations that drove cancer
C. Checkpoint failure allowed damaged cells to continue dividing, accumulating mutations that enabled uncontrolled growth
D. S-phase arrest prevented replication errors, so cells became more stable and proliferative
✅Correct
❌Incorrect

Correct Answer: C.

Why: Without proper checkpoint arrest, DNA damage can persist and compound, increasing the probability of oncogenic mutations and uncontrolled proliferation.

Why the other choices are not the best fit:

Passage 2: A Genetic Counselor’s Case—Where Did the Extra Chromosome Come From?

A couple seeks counseling after a pregnancy is diagnosed with trisomy 21. The counselor explains that aneuploidy can arise when chromosomes fail to separate properly during meiosis. To help the couple understand, the counselor uses a simplified model: a germ cell begins meiosis as a diploid cell with homologous pairs, replicates its DNA once, and then undergoes two divisions.

Genetic testing of the fetus shows that both copies of chromosome 21 inherited from the mother are not identical; at several loci, the fetus carries two different maternal alleles on chromosome 21. The father contributes a normal single copy.

The counselor also notes that crossing over can occur during prophase I, potentially exchanging segments between maternal and paternal homologs before homologs separate in anaphase I.

How to reason:

  1. Start with what 'trisomy' implies: the zygote received two copies from one parent and one from the other.
  2. Use allele patterns to locate the error: two different maternal alleles suggests both homologs traveled together (often anaphase I nondisjunction).
  3. If the two maternal copies were identical, that would more strongly suggest meiosis II nondisjunction (failure of sister chromatids to separate).
  4. Remember timing: DNA replicates once (S phase), then meiosis I separates homologs, meiosis II separates sister chromatids.
19. The observation of two different maternal alleles on chromosome 21 most strongly suggests nondisjunction occurred during:
A. Anaphase I of meiosis (failure of homologous chromosomes to separate)
B. Mitosis in a somatic cell after fertilization
C. Prophase I (failure of synapsis)
D. Anaphase II of meiosis (failure of sister chromatids to separate)
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A.

Why: If homologs fail to separate in anaphase I, the gamete can receive both maternal homologs, which are similar but not identical and thus can carry different alleles.

Why the other choices are not the best fit:

  • B: Synapsis failure affects crossing over/alignment but does not directly explain two different maternal homologs in one gamete.
  • C: Anaphase II nondisjunction tends to produce two identical copies (sister chromatids) from one homolog, more often yielding identical alleles at many loci.
  • D: Post-zygotic mitotic nondisjunction can cause mosaicism; the prompt emphasizes two maternal copies inherited at conception.
20. If nondisjunction had instead occurred in anaphase II, the counselor would more likely expect the two maternal chromosome 21 copies to be:
A. Completely unrelated because they come from different species

C. Absent because meiosis II eliminates chromosomes
D. Always opposite in allele identity because crossing over is impossible
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: B.

Why: Meiosis II separates sister chromatids; failure can send both sister chromatids of one homolog into the same gamete, making the two copies highly similar.

Why the other choices are not the best fit:

  • B: They are still maternal chromosomes, not from another species.
  • C: Crossing over can occur earlier in prophase I; sister chromatids can still be very similar even if not perfectly identical.
  • D: Meiosis II does not eliminate chromosomes; it separates chromatids to produce haploid gametes.
21. Which event must occur before either meiosis I or meiosis II can distribute chromosomes to daughter cells?

C. DNA replication during S phase to create sister chromatids
D. Integration of viral DNA into the host genome

❌Incorrect

Correct Answer: C.

Why: Both meiosis and mitosis require a prior replication step so chromosomes consist of sister chromatids before segregation events.

Why the other choices are not the best fit:

  • B: Separation is a later step and depends on prior replication/attachment.
  • C: Cleavage furrow is cytokinesis; it does not create chromatids/chromosomes to segregate.

Passage 3: Mapping Genes and Interpreting Recombination

In a model organism, a student studies two genes, A and B, located on the same chromosome. The student performs a testcross and counts offspring phenotypes to estimate how often recombination separates alleles that were originally inherited together (linked).

In one experiment, the student observes 8% recombinant offspring. In a second experiment using genes C and D, the recombinant frequency is 35%. The student learns that recombination frequency can be used to infer the relative distance between genes and is reported in centimorgans.

The instructor emphasizes that crossing over occurs during prophase I of meiosis between homologous chromosomes within tetrads, and that the farther apart two loci are, the more likely a crossover will occur between them.

How to reason:

  1. Recognize what recombination frequency measures: how often a crossover event separates linked alleles.
  2. Convert to distance logic: higher recombination % generally implies greater physical distance between loci (up to a practical limit).
  3. Keep timing straight: crossing over occurs in prophase I between homologous chromosomes, not between sister chromatids.
  4. Use these principles to infer which gene pair is closer and to predict effects of changing locus distance.
22. Based on the data, which gene pair is most likely farther apart on the chromosome?
A. Genes A and B
B. Neither pair is on the same chromosome
C. Both pairs are equally distant because recombination is random
D. Genes C and D
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: D.

Why: A higher recombinant frequency (35% vs 8%) indicates a greater likelihood of crossing over between the loci, implying they are farther apart.

Why the other choices are not the best fit:

  • B: Lower recombination suggests closer linkage (genes A and B are likely closer).
  • C: While crossover events are probabilistic, recombination frequency systematically increases with distance.
  • D: The passage states the genes are on the same chromosome for each pair.

Correct Answer: A.

Why: Crossing over that increases diversity occurs between homologs carrying different alleles; sister chromatids are copies of the same DNA sequence.

Why the other choices are not the best fit:
