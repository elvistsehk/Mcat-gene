MCAT Biology — Genetics (Ch. 12.2)
Standalone Questions (Q1–Q15)
1. A single-nucleotide substitution changes a codon from GAA (Glu) to GUA (Val). Which point mutation type best describes this change?
A. Silent
B. Missense
C. Nonsense
D. Frameshift
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: B.

Why: A missense mutation is a point mutation that changes one amino acid to another in the protein. Here, glutamate is replaced by valine.

Other choices:

  • A: Silent mutations do not change the amino acid sequence.
  • C: Nonsense mutations create a stop codon.
  • D: Frameshifts result from insertions or deletions that alter the reading frame, not single-base substitutions.
2. Which set correctly lists the three major categories of point mutations discussed in this chapter?
A. Insertion, deletion, translocation
B. Duplication, inversion, deletion
C. Frameshift, inversion, nonsense
D. Silent, missense, nonsense
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: D.

Why: Point mutations are single-nucleotide substitutions that can be silent (no amino-acid change), missense (amino-acid substitution), or nonsense (stop codon).

Other choices:

  • A: These are not point mutation categories; insertion/deletion can be frameshift; translocation is chromosomal.
  • B: These are chromosomal-level rearrangements.
  • C: Frameshift is not a point mutation; inversion is chromosomal; nonsense is a point mutation.
3. A single base substitution converts a sense codon into a stop codon early in the coding sequence. What is the most likely direct consequence at the protein level?
A. Premature truncation of the protein
B. No change due to wobble
C. A single amino-acid substitution without length change
D. A frameshift only if the mutation is in the third base of the codon
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A.

Why: A nonsense mutation introduces a stop codon, which ends translation early, producing a truncated protein that is often nonfunctional.

Other choices:

  • B: Wobble can allow silent mutations, but a stop codon is not “silent.”
  • C: That describes a missense mutation.
  • D: Frameshifts require insertion/deletion not divisible by 3; codon position does not define frameshift for substitutions.
4. A mutation inserts one nucleotide into the coding region of a gene. Which category best describes this mutation at the nucleotide level?
A. Silent point mutation
B. Missense point mutation
C. Frameshift mutation (insertion)
D. Chromosomal inversion
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: C.

Why: Frameshifts are caused by insertion or deletion of nucleotides that shift the triplet reading frame. A single-base insertion is the classic cause.

Other choices:

  • A/B: Point mutations are substitutions of one base for another, not insertions.
  • D: Inversion is a large-scale chromosomal rearrangement, not a one-base insertion.
5. Which best explains why many silent mutations occur at the third nucleotide of a codon?
A. The third base is not transcribed into mRNA
B. The third base is removed during RNA splicing
C. The third base always specifies the start codon
D. Degeneracy (wobble) of the genetic code often tolerates changes in the third base
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: D.

Why: Many amino acids are encoded by multiple codons that differ at the third base. A substitution there often does not change the amino acid, producing a silent mutation.

Other choices:

  • A: All three bases of the codon are transcribed and read during translation.
  • B: Splicing removes introns, not a specific codon base.
  • C: Start codon identity is not defined by “third base always”; start is AUG.
6. Which is the best example of a mutagen as described in this section?
A. Ultraviolet radiation damaging DNA
B. DNA polymerase proofreading a mismatch
C. A ribosome selecting a tRNA during translation
D. A chaperone helping a protein fold correctly
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A.

Why: Mutagens are agents that increase mutation rate by damaging DNA. UV (ionizing radiation) is a classic mutagen.

Other choices:

  • B: Proofreading reduces mutations.
  • C: Translation errors can cause faulty proteins without DNA mutation, but the ribosome itself is not a mutagen.
  • D: Chaperones help prevent misfolding; they do not cause DNA mutations.
7. A transposon inserts into the middle of a coding region. Which outcome is most likely?
A. Guaranteed silent mutation due to wobble
B. Disruption of the gene’s coding sequence, potentially producing a nonfunctional protein
C. Increase in independent assortment during meiosis
D. Restoration of wild-type allele by homologous pairing
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: B.

Why: Transposon insertion can interrupt an open reading frame, altering the mRNA and protein product, often severely.

Other choices:

  • A: Large insertions are not silent and do not rely on codon wobble.
  • C: Independent assortment is driven by chromosome orientation/segregation, not transposon insertion.
  • D: Homologous pairing does not automatically “repair” a disrupted gene in somatic cells.

13. Genetic drift is most likely to cause a noticeable change in allele frequency in which scenario?
A. A very large population with extensive outcrossing and no isolation
B. A population in which every individual has identical fitness for all traits
C. A small isolated population experiencing random survival and reproduction events
D. A population under strong directional selection favoring one allele
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: C.

Why: Genetic drift is random change in allele frequencies and is strongest in small populations, where chance events have larger proportional effects.

Other choices:

  • A: Large populations buffer random fluctuations.
  • B: Equal fitness does not eliminate drift; drift depends on chance and population size, but the question asks where it is most noticeable.
  • D: Directional selection is nonrandom and distinct from drift.
14. A small group becomes reproductively isolated after a natural disaster, and over generations a rare recessive disease becomes unusually common. Which explanation best matches the chapter’s population-genetics concepts?
A. Founder effect followed by inbreeding increases homozygosity for the recessive allele
B. Genetic leakage introduces the recessive allele from a different species
C. High mutation rate in the recessive gene due to wobble
D. Strong stabilizing selection eliminates heterozygotes from the population
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A.

Why: A small isolated founding group can, by chance, carry a higher frequency of a rare allele. Inbreeding in a small population increases homozygosity, which raises the prevalence of recessive diseases.

Other choices:

  • B: Leakage is gene flow between species via hybridization; the scenario is within one species.
  • C: Wobble explains silent point mutations, not population-level increases in a specific recessive disease allele.
  • D: Stabilizing selection does not specifically remove heterozygotes; removing heterozygotes would be selection against carriers, not a drift-based founder story.
15. Which statement best describes genetic leakage as defined in this chapter?
A. Random allele-frequency change in any population due to chance alone
B. DNA polymerase errors during replication that introduce new point mutations
C. Exchange of DNA segments between homologous chromosomes in meiosis I
D. Flow of genes between species through hybridization and backcrossing
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: D.

Why: Genetic leakage refers to gene flow between species, often through hybrid offspring that can reproduce with one parent species, transferring alleles across species boundaries.

Other choices:

  • A: That is genetic drift.
  • B: That is mutation via replication error.
  • C: That is recombination (crossing over) within a species.
Passages (3 passages × 3 questions)
Passage 1: A Mutation That Helps—Until It Hurts

In a malaria-endemic region, clinicians notice that a particular hemoglobin variant is unusually common. Genetic testing shows that affected individuals carry a single nucleotide change in the hemoglobin gene. Some individuals with two copies of the variant allele experience chronic anemia and painful crises, but individuals with only one copy typically have mild symptoms, if any. Epidemiologic data indicate that people with one copy of the variant allele are less likely to develop severe malaria.

A molecular biology lab sequences hemoglobin transcripts from a heterozygous individual and confirms that the altered codon changes the identity of one amino acid in the hemoglobin protein. The lab also identifies a subset of patients with a different hemoglobin allele in which an early stop codon appears near the beginning of the coding sequence, producing a very short polypeptide that is rapidly degraded.

Public health officials debate how the allele frequency might shift if malaria incidence decreases substantially over the next several decades due to improved prevention, while medical care continues to reduce childhood mortality from severe anemia.

How to reason:

  1. Classify each mutation by what it does to the protein: amino-acid substitution → missense; early stop codon → nonsense.
  2. Connect genotype to fitness in the environment: heterozygotes have selective advantage where malaria is common.
  3. Predict allele-frequency direction if environment changes: removing malaria reduces the heterozygote advantage, so selection pressure shifts.
  4. Distinguish selection from chance: environmental advantage is nonrandom selection, not drift.
  5. Choose answers that explain both the molecular mechanism and the population pattern described.

D. Chromosomal inversion

  • C: Frameshift deletions require nucleotide loss that shifts the reading frame, not a single substitution.

Passage 2: A New Island Population

After a severe storm, a small group of lizards is swept from a mainland to a nearby island. The island population remains isolated from the mainland for many generations because strong currents prevent migration. Within two decades, field biologists notice that a rare skin-color allele—uncommon on the mainland—has become much more frequent on the island. The island population also shows reduced variation at multiple genetic markers compared with the mainland population.

Genetic analysis suggests the founding individuals carried an unusually high fraction of the rare allele by chance. Later, as the population remained small, close relatives often mated. Over time, investigators document increased frequency of both homozygous genotypes at several loci and a higher incidence of a recessive developmental defect.

Conservation biologists debate whether introducing unrelated mainland lizards would improve the island population’s long-term viability, while also considering how such intervention might change allele frequencies.

How to reason:

  1. Ask: is the change driven by chance or by fitness? The passage emphasizes “by chance” in the founders → genetic drift.
  2. Small isolated group starting a new population → founder effect (an extreme drift case).
  3. Repeated mating among relatives → inbreeding, which increases homozygosity and can reveal recessive disorders.
  4. Reduced marker variation signals loss of genetic diversity, which can reduce population fitness (inbreeding depression).
  5. Introducing unrelated individuals is outcrossing, predicted to increase variation and reduce homozygosity.
19. Which concept best explains why the rare allele became common on the island even without evidence that it improves survival?
A. Genetic leakage
B. Directional selection
C. Founder effect (genetic drift in a new isolated population)
D. Increased mutation rate from UV radiation
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: C.

Why: The founder effect is genetic drift when a small founding group “samples” the gene pool nonrepresentatively. The allele can become common by chance alone.

Other choices:

  • A: Leakage is gene flow between species; this is within one species.
  • B: Selection requires differential fitness; the passage states no evidence of survival benefit.
  • D: Mutation could introduce new alleles, but the key story is that founders already carried the allele and it rose in frequency by chance.

A. Inbreeding within a small population
B. Codon wobble during translation
C. Chromosomal translocation between species
D. Increased penetrance caused by recombination
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A.

Why: Inbreeding increases the probability that related individuals share alleles identical by descent, raising homozygosity and revealing recessive disorders.

Other choices:

  • B: Wobble is a molecular translation concept, not a population-level driver of homozygosity.
  • C: Between-species gene flow would be leakage, not the mechanism described, and translocation is a chromosomal mutation not implied here.
  • D: Penetrance is about genotype-to-phenotype probability; recombination doesn’t “cause” penetrance increases in this manner.
21. Introducing unrelated mainland lizards into the island breeding group would most likely do which of the following over time?
A. Increase homozygosity and worsen recessive disease prevalence
B. Prevent any future mutation from occurring

D. Increase genetic variation and reduce inbreeding depression through outcrossing
✅Correct
❌Incorrect

Correct Answer: D.

Why: Outcrossing introduces unrelated alleles, increasing heterozygosity and genetic diversity, which can improve fitness and reduce inbreeding depression.

Other choices:

  • A: Outcrossing tends to decrease homozygosity, not increase it.
  • B: Migration does not stop molecular causes of mutation.
  • C: Mutation categories are not “converted” by introducing new individuals.
Passage 3: Chromosomes Under Stress

A cytogenetics lab studies workers exposed to a chemical that can damage DNA. In one sample, the karyotype shows that a large portion of one chromosome is missing. In another sample, a region from chromosome 8 appears twice in a row on the same chromosome, increasing copy number for multiple genes. A third sample contains a chromosome in which a segment’s orientation appears reversed relative to the reference karyotype.

In a separate case, a child has developmental delay and the lab identifies that a segment from chromosome 11 is now located on chromosome 22, while a segment from chromosome 22 appears on chromosome 11. Clinicians suspect that the rearrangement disrupted gene regulation at the breakpoints rather than changing individual codons.

The lab director emphasizes that these large-scale changes differ from single-nucleotide substitutions, but both types can ultimately change protein function and phenotype.

How to reason:

  1. Match each karyotype clue to a chromosomal mutation: missing segment → deletion; repeated segment → duplication; reversed orientation → inversion.
  2. If DNA moves between chromosomes, decide whether it’s insertion (moved segment) or translocation (swap between chromosomes). A swap in both directions → translocation.
  3. Use “breakpoints disrupt regulation” to avoid getting trapped in codon-level thinking—this is chromosomal scale.
  4. Eliminate point-mutation options when the evidence is karyotype-level rearrangement.
  5. Choose answers that directly explain the observed chromosome pattern, not just a definition.
