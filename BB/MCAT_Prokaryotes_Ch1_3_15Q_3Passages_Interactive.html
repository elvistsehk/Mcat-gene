Standalone Questions (Q1–Q15)
1. A microorganism has a single circular chromosome localized to a nucleoid region and lacks membrane-bound organelles. Which additional feature is most consistent with this organism?
A. A nucleus enclosed by a double membrane
B. 40S and 60S ribosomal subunits
C. A 30S and 50S ribosome (70S total)
D. Mitochondria that generate ATP via cristae
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: C.

Why: Prokaryotes lack a nucleus and membrane-bound organelles, and they have 70S ribosomes composed of 30S and 50S subunits.

Why the other choices are not the best fit:

  • A: A nucleus is a defining feature of eukaryotes.
  • C: Mitochondria are membrane-bound organelles found in eukaryotes.
  • D: 40S and 60S subunits are eukaryotic (80S) ribosomes.
2. A cell uses histones and initiates translation with methionine, yet divides by binary fission and contains a single circular chromosome. Which domain best fits these traits?
A. Viruses
B. Bacteria
C. Eukarya
D. Archaea
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: D.

Why: Archaea share several gene-expression features with eukaryotes (histones, translation initiation with methionine, similar RNA polymerases) but are prokaryotic in organization (circular chromosome; binary fission/budding).

Why the other choices are not the best fit:

  • B: Bacteria generally use histone-like proteins; true histones are characteristic of archaea (in this context).
  • C: Eukaryotes have linear chromosomes and divide by mitosis/meiosis, not binary fission.
  • D: Viruses are not cellular and do not divide by binary fission.

Why: Gram-positive bacteria have a thick peptidoglycan wall that traps crystal violet, producing a purple appearance.

Why the other choices are not the best fit:

  • C: Most bacteria have a cell wall; sterols are associated with eukaryotic membranes.
  • D: Mitochondria are eukaryotic organelles, not part of a bacterial envelope.

6. Penicillin becomes less effective when a bacterium acquires an enzyme that blocks peptidoglycan cross-linking inhibition. Penicillin’s primary target is best described as:
A. A microtubule motor that powers flagellar beating
B. An enzyme that catalyzes peptidoglycan cross-linking
C. A channel that transports DNA through the nuclear pore
D. A ribosomal subunit that binds tRNA in the A site
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: B.

Why: Penicillin interferes with the cross-linking of peptidoglycan, weakening the cell wall and causing osmotic lysis.

Why the other choices are not the best fit:

  • B: Many antibiotics do target ribosomes, but penicillin targets cell wall cross-linking enzymes.
  • C: Microtubule beating is eukaryotic cilia/flagella; bacterial flagella rotate via a basal body motor.
  • D: Bacteria lack a nuclear envelope and nuclear pores.
7. A bacterium has an outer membrane with LPS and a periplasmic space. Where is the thin peptidoglycan layer located?
A. Adjacent to the plasma membrane, within the periplasmic space
B. Inside the nucleoid region around DNA
C. Outside the outer membrane, exposed directly to the environment
D. Only at the base of the flagellum
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A.

Why: Gram-negative bacteria have a thin peptidoglycan layer located between the inner (plasma) membrane and outer membrane, in the periplasm.

Why the other choices are not the best fit:

  • B: In gram-negatives, the outer membrane is most external; peptidoglycan is not exposed outside it.
  • C: Peptidoglycan is a cell wall component, not a DNA-packaging structure.
  • D: Peptidoglycan surrounds the cell and is not restricted to flagellar structures.
8. Which statement correctly contrasts bacterial and eukaryotic flagella?
A. Eukaryotic flagella are powered by ATP synthase embedded in the flagellar membrane
B. Bacterial flagella rotate using a basal body motor; eukaryotic flagella beat via microtubule sliding in a 9 + 2 axoneme
C. Both are made of actin filaments and beat using myosin
D. Both are composed of flagellin and rotate at the same frequency
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: B.

Why: Bacterial flagella are flagellin-based rotary structures (filament–hook–basal body). Eukaryotic flagella/cilia are microtubule-based 9 + 2 structures that bend via motor-driven sliding.

Why the other choices are not the best fit:

  • B: Actin–myosin is not the core system for ciliary/flagellar motility.
  • C: Flagellin is bacterial; eukaryotic axonemes are tubulin-based and do not rotate like bacterial flagella.
  • D: ATP synthase is not the motor for eukaryotic flagellar beating; dynein is.

Passage 1: Choosing an Antibiotic in the Emergency Department

A 19-year-old patient presents with fever and rapidly worsening hypotension. Blood cultures grow a bacterium whose envelope includes an outer membrane rich in lipopolysaccharide (LPS). The clinical team suspects the patient’s symptoms are partly driven by an intense inflammatory response to components of the bacterial surface.

The organism is treated with a drug that inhibits peptidoglycan cross-linking. Within hours, microscopy shows swelling and lysis in a subset of the bacteria, but many survive and later regrow. Further testing reveals that surviving cells produce an enzyme that prevents the antibiotic from effectively disrupting the cell wall.

The team debates switching to an antibiotic that binds the bacterial ribosome. They prefer a target that differs substantially from the host’s protein synthesis machinery to minimize toxicity.

How to reason:

  1. Identify the envelope clue: LPS + outer membrane → gram-negative bacteria.
  2. Use mechanism clues: blocking peptidoglycan cross-linking weakens the wall → osmotic lysis; regrowth implies resistance in survivors.
  3. For ribosome selectivity, compare subunits: bacteria have 30S/50S vs eukaryotes 40S/60S.
  4. Pick answers that connect multiple passage clues (LPS + cell wall + resistance + ribosomes), not just one phrase.

B. Deep purple cells that retain crystal violet
C. Pink-red cells after counterstain with safranin
D. Green staining because LPS is a pigment
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: C.

Why: An outer membrane with LPS is characteristic of gram-negative bacteria, which do not retain crystal violet well and therefore take up the safranin counterstain (pink-red).

Why the other choices are not the best fit:

  • A: Purple retention is typical of gram-positive thick peptidoglycan walls.
  • C: Most bacteria have cell walls; the passage describes a complex envelope, not wall absence.
  • D: LPS is not a pigment that determines a ‘green’ Gram stain.

Passage 2: An Oxygen Gradient in a Wound

Researchers sample bacteria from different depths of an infected wound. Near the surface, oxygen levels are relatively high; deeper in the wound, oxygen becomes scarce. Two bacterial isolates are studied: Isolate X grows only in oxygen-rich culture, while Isolate Y grows both with and without oxygen but switches to a fermentation-like pathway when oxygen is removed.

When Isolate Z from the deepest region is briefly exposed to oxygen, its viable count plummets. Biochemical assays reveal rapid accumulation of reactive oxygen-containing radicals in Z after exposure.

To compare energy generation, investigators measure ATP production. In the oxygen-rich condition, X shows evidence of electron transport occurring at the cell membrane rather than within mitochondria.

How to reason:

  1. Translate growth patterns into categories: only with O₂ → obligate aerobe; with or without O₂ → facultative anaerobe; dies in O₂ → obligate anaerobe.
  2. Use the mechanistic clue: ROS accumulation after O₂ exposure supports oxygen toxicity in obligate anaerobes.
  3. For ETC location: prokaryotes lack mitochondria, so ETC proteins are in the plasma membrane.
  4. Select answers that integrate observations + mechanism, not just definitions.

Passage 3: Tracing the Spread of Resistance Genes in a Hospital Ward

A hospital microbiology lab tracks an outbreak of a multidrug-resistant bacterial strain. Sequencing shows that resistance genes are carried on a circular DNA element that is not required for basic survival but provides a clear advantage in the presence of antibiotics.

In one experiment, researchers mix resistant donor cells with sensitive recipient cells. After incubation, many recipients acquire resistance even when no bacteriophage is detected. Microscopy shows physical cell–cell contact structures connecting donors to recipients.

In a second experiment, cell-free supernatant from lysed resistant bacteria is added to a sensitive culture. Some sensitive cells become resistant without any cell–cell contact. Finally, when a bacteriophage that previously infected resistant bacteria is used to infect sensitive bacteria, resistance appears in a subset of the newly infected cells.

How to reason:

  1. Identify the DNA element: circular, nonessential, advantageous → plasmid.
  2. Match each experiment to mechanism: contact bridge → conjugation; cell-free DNA uptake → transformation; phage vector → transduction.
  3. Use what’s excluded: ‘no bacteriophage’ rules out transduction in the first experiment.
  4. Pick answers that reflect experimental design (how DNA moved), not just that resistance increased.
