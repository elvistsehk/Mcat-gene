Standalone Questions (Q1–Q15)
1. A eukaryotic cell is engineered so that transcription occurs in one compartment while translation occurs in another. Which structure is most directly responsible for creating these distinct environments?
A. The nuclear envelope (double membrane surrounding the nucleus)
B. The Golgi apparatus
C. The plasma membrane
D. The nucleolus
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A.

Why: The nuclear envelope separates nucleoplasm from cytoplasm, enabling transcription (and RNA processing) to be spatially separated from translation; nuclear pores then regulate exchange.

Why the other choices are not the best fit:

  • A: Plasma membrane separates cell from outside, not nucleus from cytoplasm.
  • B: Nucleolus synthesizes rRNA; it does not create the nucleus–cytoplasm boundary.
  • D: Golgi modifies/sorts proteins; it is not the boundary enabling transcription/translation compartmentalization.
2. A cell shows a prominent dark body within the nucleus and high rRNA output. Which structure is most consistent with this observation?
A. Chromatin (heterochromatin)
B. Nuclear pore complex
C. Mitochondrial matrix
D. Nucleolus
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: D.

Why: The nucleolus is a dense nuclear subregion where rRNA is synthesized and ribosomal subunits are assembled; it often appears as a darker spot.

Why the other choices are not the best fit:

  • A: Nuclear pores mediate transport across the nuclear envelope; they are not the rRNA synthesis site.
  • C: Heterochromatin is tightly packed DNA and is generally transcriptionally inactive.
  • D: The mitochondrial matrix is inside mitochondria, not in the nucleus.
3. A mutation reduces the surface area of the inner mitochondrial membrane without changing total mitochondrial volume. Which function is most directly impaired?
A. Ribosomal RNA synthesis
B. Glycolysis in the cytosol
C. Lysosomal hydrolysis of endocytosed cargo
D. Electron transport chain capacity for oxidative phosphorylation
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: D.

Why: ETC complexes are embedded in the inner mitochondrial membrane; cristae increase membrane surface area and therefore increase ETC and ATP synthase capacity.

Why the other choices are not the best fit:

  • A: Glycolysis occurs in the cytosol and does not depend on mitochondrial inner membrane surface area.
  • C: rRNA synthesis occurs in the nucleolus.
  • D: Lysosomal degradation depends on lysosomal enzymes, not mitochondrial membrane surface area.
4. A student uses endosymbiotic theory to predict a feature of mitochondria. Which finding would best support that prediction?
A. Mitochondria store hydrolytic enzymes to digest cellular debris
B. Mitochondria are assembled only during mitosis from Golgi-derived vesicles
C. Mitochondria replicate by binary fission and contain their own genetic material
D. Mitochondria are continuous with the nuclear envelope
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: C.

Why: Serial endosymbiosis proposes that mitochondria originated from engulfed prokaryotes, consistent with semi-autonomy (own genes + binary fission).

Why the other choices are not the best fit:

  • B: Golgi-derived vesicles are part of the secretory pathway, not mitochondrial biogenesis.
  • C: Hydrolytic enzymes are characteristic of lysosomes.
  • D: The ER is contiguous with the nuclear envelope; mitochondria are not.

Why: Mitochondria can trigger apoptosis by releasing pro-apoptotic factors that activate caspases and related pathways, leading to controlled cell dismantling.

Why the other choices are not the best fit:

  • C: Lipid storage is not a canonical trigger for apoptosis.
  • D: Cleavage furrow actin dynamics relate to cytokinesis, not initiation of apoptosis.
6. A toxic compound causes lysosomal membranes to rupture. Which immediate consequence best explains why this can lead to apoptosis?
A. ATP synthase reverses and hydrolyzes ATP

D. Microtubules depolymerize and block vesicle traffic
✅Correct
❌Incorrect

Correct Answer: C.

Why: Lysosomes sequester hydrolytic enzymes; membrane rupture releases these enzymes, causing autolysis and promoting programmed cell death.

Why the other choices are not the best fit:

  • B: ATP synthase behavior depends on proton gradient; it is not the key effect of lysosomal rupture here.
  • C: Microtubule disruption affects transport but does not directly explain enzyme-mediated autolysis.
  • D: Nuclear pore closure would impede transport but is not the primary lethal mechanism described for lysosomal rupture.

Passage 1: Very-Long-Chain Fatty Acids and Oxidative Stress

A pediatric clinic evaluates two infants with hypotonia and developmental delay. Blood testing in both infants reveals elevated very-long-chain fatty acids (VLCFAs). The clinician suspects a defect in an organelle responsible for β-oxidation of VLCFAs and for handling a reactive oxygen species that is routinely produced during cellular metabolism.

In fibroblasts from Infant A, electron microscopy shows normal mitochondria with prominent cristae, but few small, spherical vesicles normally present in the cytoplasm. In fibroblasts from Infant B, those vesicles appear normal in number, but the cells show increased markers of oxidative damage and an unusually high concentration of hydrogen peroxide.

To distinguish the two cases, the lab treats both cell lines with a compound that increases catalase activity specifically in the organelle that normally contains hydrogen peroxide. After treatment, oxidative damage markers decrease substantially in one cell line but not the other.

How to reason:

  1. First identify which organelle is being tested by the clues: VLCFA β-oxidation + hydrogen peroxide handling points to peroxisomes (catalase lives there).
  2. Then separate organelle absence (few vesicles seen) from organelle present but enzyme imbalance (high H₂O₂).
  3. Use the intervention: boosting catalase only helps if the organelle is present but the oxidative stress is due to insufficient breakdown of H₂O₂.
  4. Finally, predict downstream effects by matching metabolism to compartment: peroxisomes handle VLCFAs, while mitochondria focus on ATP production via ETC on cristae.

Passage 2: Airway Defense and Motility

A group of researchers studies recurrent respiratory infections in a cohort of patients. Nasal epithelial biopsies show that the lining cells are tightly packed, rest on a basement membrane, and display apical projections that normally move mucus toward the throat.

High-speed video microscopy reveals that the projections beat weakly and with poor coordination. Ultrastructural analysis identifies a defect in the motor apparatus required for sliding of cylindrical protein polymers arranged as nine outer pairs with two central components.

When fluorescently labeled vesicles are tracked in these epithelial cells, cargo that should move inward toward the centrosome accumulates near the plasma membrane, suggesting impaired transport along the same polymer system.

How to reason:

  1. Start with tissue ID: a lining on a basement membrane with tight junctions is epithelium; apical projections moving mucus are cilia.
  2. Recognize structure: 9 + 2 plus a motor defect points to microtubules and their motors (especially dynein for ciliary beating).
  3. Link intracellular transport: long-distance vesicle traffic uses kinesin/dynein on microtubules; inward (toward centrosome/minus end) is typically dynein.
  4. Choose answers that require connecting multiple cues (tissue + cytoskeleton + motor direction), not just naming a structure.

Passage 3: Secreted Proteins and Cellular Trafficking

An endocrine cell line is engineered to express a fluorescent version of a peptide hormone. Under normal conditions, fluorescence is observed first in a perinuclear reticular network, then in a series of stacked sacs, and finally in small vesicles that fuse with the plasma membrane to release the hormone.

A researcher adds a drug that disrupts vesicular trafficking from the reticular network to the stacked sacs. After treatment, the hormone fluorescence accumulates in the reticular network, secretion drops sharply, and the stacked sacs appear fragmented. Electron microscopy also shows that hydrolytic compartments in the cytoplasm receive fewer newly delivered enzymes.

In a separate experiment, another mutant cell line retains normal trafficking but shows failure to tag certain proteins with chemical groups needed for proper sorting; as a result, some enzymes that should be delivered to hydrolytic compartments are instead secreted.

How to reason:

  1. Map the pathway: perinuclear reticular network = rough ER (protein entry during translation), stacked sacs = Golgi, then secretory vesicles → membrane.
  2. Use the intervention: blocking ER→Golgi traffic causes buildup in ER and decreases secretion—so questions will test where proteins are made and how they’re routed.
  3. Hydrolytic compartments are lysosomes; they need enzymes that are processed/sorted by the Golgi (often via chemical tags/signals).
  4. When a sorting tag fails, predict misdelivery (e.g., lysosomal enzymes secreted) rather than a failure of protein synthesis.
