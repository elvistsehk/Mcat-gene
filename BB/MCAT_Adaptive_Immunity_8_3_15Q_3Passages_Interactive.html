MCAT Biology — Adaptive Immunity (Ch. 8.3)
Standalone Questions (Q1–Q15)
1. A B-cell is activated and differentiates into a plasma cell that secretes antibodies. Which antibody effect most directly prevents a virus from entering host cells?
A. Neutralization that blocks attachment or entry into tissues
B. Agglutination that forms large insoluble complexes for easier phagocytosis
C. Opsonization that tags pathogens for immediate phagocytosis
D. Induction of apoptosis by injecting cytotoxic granules into infected cells
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A.

Why: Neutralizing antibodies bind a pathogen (or its toxins) in a way that blocks invasion—often by preventing binding to host receptors or entry into tissues/cells.

Other choices:

  • B: Agglutination mainly improves clearance by clumping pathogens, not specifically blocking entry.
  • C: Opsonization marks pathogens for phagocytes, but does not necessarily stop entry directly.
  • D: Cytotoxic granule injection is a CD8+ T-cell/NK-cell killing mechanism, not an antibody effect.
2. In an antibody molecule, which region determines binding specificity for a particular antigenic sequence?
A. Constant region of the heavy chains
B. Fc portion that interacts with leukocyte receptors
C. Variable region at the tips of the Y-shaped molecule
D. Disulfide linkages that stabilize the overall structure
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: C.

Why: Antigen specificity comes from the variable (V) domains at the ends of the Y, where unique polypeptide sequences form the antigen-binding site.

Other choices:

  • A/B: The constant/Fc region mediates effector functions (binding Fc receptors, complement), not antigen specificity.
  • D: Disulfide bonds support structure but do not encode antigen recognition.
3. A thymocyte survives only if it can recognize antigen presented on MHC, but then is eliminated if it strongly reacts to self-proteins. Which pairing correctly matches these checkpoints?
A. Negative selection then positive selection
B. Isotype switching then clonal selection
C. Affinity maturation then somatic hypermutation
D. Positive selection then negative selection
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: D.

Why: Positive selection keeps T-cells that can respond to MHC (otherwise they are useless in the periphery). Negative selection deletes self-reactive T-cells to promote self-tolerance.

Other choices:

  • A: This reverses the order described for thymic education.
  • B: These are B-cell processes and do not describe thymic selection checkpoints.
  • C: These relate to refining antibody affinity, not MHC recognition and self-reactivity elimination.
4. A patient has recurrent oral thrush and Pneumocystis pneumonia. Laboratory testing reveals a profoundly reduced population of cells that coordinate immune responses by secreting lymphokines. Which lymphocyte subset is most directly reduced?
A. CD8+ cytotoxic T-cells
B. CD4+ helper T-cells
C. Plasma cells
D. Eosinophils
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: B.

Why: CD4+ helper T-cells coordinate adaptive responses via lymphokines/cytokines. Their loss (classically in HIV) leads to opportunistic infections.

Other choices:

  • A: CD8+ cells kill infected cells directly; reduced coordination is more CD4+ related.
  • C: Plasma cells secrete antibodies but do not serve as the primary “coordinators” via lymphokines.
  • D: Eosinophils are granulocytes linked to parasites/allergy, not the classic opportunistic infection pattern.
5. A patient is infected with a bacterium that remains primarily outside host cells. Which adaptive lymphocyte response is most directly optimized for clearing this extracellular pathogen?
A. CD8+ cytotoxic T-cell recognition of endogenous peptides on MHC-I
B. CD4+ helper T-cell activation by exogenous peptides on MHC-II
C. NK-cell killing triggered by decreased MHC expression
D. Interferon-driven shutdown of host translation in neighboring cells
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: B.

Why: Extracellular bacteria are processed and presented as exogenous antigens on MHC-II, which activates CD4+ helper T-cells. These cells coordinate macrophages and B-cells to clear extracellular infections.

Other choices:

  • A: CD8+ cells are best for intracellular (endogenous) antigens such as viral proteins.
  • C: NK cells respond to missing/low MHC, often in viral infection or cancer evasion.
  • D: Interferons are a hallmark of antiviral innate signaling, not primary anti-extracellular bacterial coordination.
6. A B-cell undergoes rapid mutations in its antigen-binding region, and only clones that bind the antigen with the highest affinity expand. Which term best describes the survival-and-expansion step that creates specificity?
A. Negative selection
B. Complement fixation
C. Isotype switching
D. Clonal selection
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: D.

Why: Clonal selection means only lymphocytes with receptors that best match the antigen proliferate, generating a specific high-affinity response.

Other choices:

  • A: Negative selection eliminates self-reactive clones (classically in thymus for T-cells).
  • B: Complement fixation is an effector function triggered by the antibody constant region.
  • C: Isotype switching changes antibody class (IgM→IgG/IgA/IgE, etc.) without changing antigen specificity.
7. A patient’s first exposure to an antigen produces antibodies after about a week, while later exposure produces a faster and larger antibody response. Which cell type most directly explains the improved later response?
A. Memory B-cells that rapidly differentiate into plasma cells upon re-exposure
B. Neutrophils that increase chemotaxis after repeated infections
C. Basophils that store antibodies in cytoplasmic granules
D. Epithelial cells that undergo positive selection to recognize MHC
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A.

Why: The secondary antibody response is faster and more robust because memory B-cells persist after the primary response and quickly generate plasma cells on re-exposure.

Other choices:

  • B: Neutrophils are innate cells and do not explain antigen-specific memory.
  • C: Basophils store histamine and mediate allergic inflammation; they are not the main memory mechanism.
  • D: Positive selection is a thymic T-cell maturation process, not an epithelial feature.
8. An antibody binds multiple bacteria at once, forming large insoluble clumps that are easier for phagocytes to ingest. Which antibody effect is illustrated?
A. Neutralization
B. Apoptosis induction
C. Agglutination
D. Positive selection
✅Correct

Correct Answer: C.

Why: Agglutination is antibody-mediated clumping of pathogens into larger complexes that can be cleared more efficiently by phagocytes.

Other choices:

  • A: Neutralization blocks invasion or toxin function rather than forming clumps.
  • B: Apoptosis induction is mainly a cytotoxic T-cell/NK mechanism.
  • D: Positive selection describes thymic T-cell education, not an antibody effector effect.

Correct Answer: B.

Why: Opsonization means “tagging” pathogens with antibodies so phagocytes with Fc receptors can bind and ingest them efficiently.

  • A: Agglutination is clumping, not specifically Fc receptor–mediated phagocytosis.
  • C: Isotype switching changes antibody class without directly describing phagocyte recognition in the moment.
  • D: Negative selection deletes self-reactive clones during maturation, not during pathogen tagging.

A. Passive natural immunity via placental antibody transfer
B. Passive artificial immunity via intravenous immunoglobulin
C. Innate immunity that improves through clonal selection
D. Active artificial immunity via vaccination

Correct Answer: D.

Why: Vaccines provide antigen exposure without true infection, stimulating the host to generate plasma cells and memory cells—active, artificial immunity.

Other choices:

  • C: Innate immunity does not become antigen-specific through clonal selection.
11. Which statement best differentiates passive from active immunity?
A. Passive immunity transfers antibodies without transferring the antibody-producing plasma cells, so protection is temporary
B. Passive immunity always generates memory cells faster than active immunity
C. Active immunity is mediated exclusively by innate granulocytes
D. Active immunity cannot be induced artificially
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A.

Why: Passive immunity gives the recipient ready-made antibodies (placenta, breast milk, immunoglobulin infusion). Because the recipient does not receive plasma cells or generate new memory cells from scratch, protection is transient.

Other choices:

  • B: Passive immunity does not produce memory; active immunity does.
  • C: Active immunity is mainly adaptive (B- and T-cells), not exclusively innate granulocytes.
  • D: Vaccination is the classic artificial induction of active immunity.
12. A virally infected cell displays viral peptides on its surface and is then killed by an adaptive lymphocyte. Which lymphocyte and MHC pairing best matches this event?
A. CD4+ helper T-cell recognizing exogenous peptide on MHC-II
B. Treg cell recognizing endogenous peptide on MHC-II
C. CD8+ cytotoxic T-cell recognizing endogenous peptide on MHC-I
D. Plasma cell recognizing endogenous peptide on MHC-I
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: C.

Why: Viral proteins are produced inside infected cells and presented on MHC-I. CD8+ cytotoxic T-cells (CTLs) recognize MHC-I–peptide complexes and kill infected cells.

Other choices:

  • A: CD4+ cells respond to MHC-II (exogenous) and coordinate responses rather than directly killing infected nucleated cells in this classic pathway.
  • B: Tregs suppress immune responses and are characterized by CD4 and Foxp3; MHC-II is not the primary endogenous presentation route.
  • D: Plasma cells secrete antibodies; they do not kill infected cells via MHC recognition.

Why: After thymic maturation/selection, T-cells are mature but naïve. They become activated and clonally expand only after encountering their antigen in the periphery.

D. Opsonization

Passage 1: Primary vs Secondary Responses in a Vaccine Clinic

A community clinic administers a vaccine containing purified protein from a respiratory bacterium. A subset of first-time recipients reports mild fever and soreness for a day, but no signs of true infection. The clinic collects blood samples at days 0, 3, 7, 10, and 21 to measure antibody levels specific to the vaccine antigen.

In first-time recipients, antigen-specific antibodies remain low at day 3, rise noticeably by about day 7, and peak near day 10. By day 21, antibody levels decline, but a stable population of antigen-specific lymphocytes can still be detected in lymph nodes using flow cytometry.

Six months later, the same individuals receive a booster dose containing the same protein antigen. This time, antigen-specific antibodies rise substantially by day 3 and reach higher peak levels than after the first injection. Many recipients report no symptoms at all.

In a separate scenario, a traveler exposed to tetanus is given an intramuscular injection of antibodies against tetanus toxin the same day as the injury. The traveler’s antibody levels become protective immediately but return toward baseline over the next several weeks if no additional intervention is performed.

Clinic staff discuss which immune features explain the delayed rise after first exposure, the rapid rise after boosting, and why antibody transfer can be protective right away but does not provide long-term immunologic memory.

How to reason:

  1. Look for timing: 7–10 days suggests a primary response requiring activation and expansion of specific clones.
  2. A faster, bigger rise after re-exposure points to memory cells driving a secondary response.
  3. Immediate protection after antibody injection is passive immunity (antibodies only, no new memory created).
  4. Vaccines usually create active immunity because the host generates plasma and memory cells.
  5. When protection fades quickly, infer the absence of long-lived plasma cells or memory cells in the recipient.
16. In the first-time vaccine recipients, the delay before antibody levels rise substantially is best explained by which requirement of adaptive immunity?
A. Immediate degranulation of mast cells in response to the antigen
B. Alternative complement activation that must assemble membrane attack complexes
C. Antigen-driven activation and clonal expansion of rare antigen-specific B-cells into plasma cells
D. NK-cell killing of vaccine antigen–presenting cells due to reduced MHC expression
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: C.

Why: The primary antibody response takes time because antigen-specific B-cells must be activated, proliferate, and differentiate into plasma cells that secrete high quantities of antibody.

Other choices:

  • A: Mast cell degranulation is more about allergic inflammation and can occur quickly but does not explain delayed antibody production.
  • B: Complement is innate and can act quickly; it does not account for a week-long delay typical of primary adaptive responses.
  • D: NK killing relates to “missing self” and is not the central explanation for antibody kinetics after vaccination.

  • C: Neutrophils are short-lived innate phagocytes and do not persist as antigen-specific memory.

Passage 2: Thymic Education, CD Markers, and Opportunistic Infection

A 32-year-old patient presents with persistent fever and weight loss, followed by an unusual fungal pneumonia. Flow cytometry of peripheral blood reveals markedly reduced CD4+ T-cell counts but relatively preserved CD8+ T-cell counts. The clinician explains that some T-cells normally coordinate immune responses by secreting signaling molecules that recruit and activate other leukocytes.

To teach trainees, the clinician reviews that T-cells mature in the thymus under the influence of thymic factors. During maturation, developing T-cells face two checkpoints: one that ensures they can respond to antigen presented on MHC, and another that deletes T-cells that react strongly to self-antigens. The clinician notes that failure of these processes can lead to either immunodeficiency or autoimmunity.

In a case conference, trainees debate why the patient struggles with infections that are normally controlled by coordinated immune responses. They also discuss why the immune system must avoid attacking self-tissues and how specialized T-cells can dampen immune activity after an infection is controlled.

As an exercise, the clinician asks which subset would be most impaired in responding to antigens presented on MHC-II, which thymic selection step prevents self-reactive T-cells from entering circulation, and which marker distinguishes regulatory T-cells that promote self-tolerance.

How to reason:

  1. Link CD markers: CD4 pairs with MHC-II (exogenous) and coordinates responses; CD8 pairs with MHC-I (endogenous) and kills infected cells.
  2. Opportunistic infections suggest loss of helper coordination rather than only loss of direct killing.
  3. Thymic checkpoints: ability to recognize MHC is positive selection; deletion of self-reactive cells is negative selection.
  4. Self-tolerance and “turning down” responses points toward Treg cells.
  5. Distinguish Treg from Th by the presence of Foxp3.

Passage 3: Antibody Regions, Effector Functions, and Specificity

A research group isolates B-cells from volunteers after exposure to a novel bacterial surface protein. Early after exposure, most antigen-binding antibodies in serum are low-affinity. Over the next week, the group detects a shift toward higher-affinity antibodies that bind the same antigen more tightly. Sequencing reveals changes clustered in the antigen-binding portions of the antibody genes.

The researchers also test how different antibody preparations influence clearance. In one assay, antibodies cause bacteria to form large clumps that settle out of solution. In another assay, bacteria remain dispersed, but macrophages ingest them more quickly when antibodies are present. In a third assay, bacteria fail to attach to epithelial cells when antibodies are added, even though macrophages are absent from the experiment.

Finally, the team engineers an antibody that still binds the antigen normally but cannot efficiently recruit complement proteins or interact with Fc receptors on leukocytes. Surprisingly, the engineered antibody still blocks epithelial attachment but is less effective at accelerating macrophage ingestion.

The team asks trainees to infer which antibody region determines specificity, which region mediates recruitment of complement and leukocyte receptors, and which antibody effect best matches each assay’s outcome.

How to reason:

  1. Changes that improve affinity while keeping the same target suggest refinement of the variable region (antigen-binding site).
  2. Recruiting leukocytes and complement depends on the antibody constant (Fc) region.
  3. Clumping bacteria is agglutination; faster macrophage ingestion is opsonization; blocking attachment is neutralization.
  4. If binding remains normal but effector recruitment fails, suspect the constant region is altered while the variable region is intact.
  5. Pick answers that explain the pattern across multiple assays, not just one observation.
22. The engineered antibody binds antigen normally but poorly recruits complement and Fc-receptor–bearing leukocytes. Which antibody region is most likely altered?
A. Antigen-binding site within the variable region tips
B. Disulfide bonds linking heavy and light chains
C. Hypervariable loops that directly contact antigen
D. Constant (Fc) region responsible for effector interactions
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: D.

Why: Complement recruitment and Fc receptor binding are mediated by the antibody constant (Fc) region. Preserved antigen binding implies the variable region remains functional.

Other choices:

  • A/C: Variable-region changes would typically disrupt antigen binding specificity/affinity.
  • B: Disulfide bonds stabilize structure; disrupting them would likely alter overall folding and binding, not selectively remove effector recruitment.
