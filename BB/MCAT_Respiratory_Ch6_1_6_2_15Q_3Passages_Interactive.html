MCAT Biology — Respiratory System (Ch. 6.1–6.2)
Standalone Questions (Q1–Q15)
1. During quiet inhalation, which pair of muscles is most directly responsible for expanding the thoracic cavity?
A. Internal intercostals and abdominal muscles
B. Sternocleidomastoid and scalene muscles
C. Diaphragm and external intercostals
D. Diaphragm and internal intercostals
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: C.

Why: Quiet inspiration is active and is driven primarily by contraction of the diaphragm (flattens downward) and the external intercostals (lift and expand the rib cage), increasing intrathoracic volume.

Other choices:

  • A: These are used for forced expiration, not quiet inspiration.
  • B: Accessory muscles help during labored breathing, not typical quiet breathing.
  • D: Internal intercostals assist forced expiration (pull ribs down), opposing inspiration.
2. Surfactant is most important because it:
A. lowers surface tension to reduce alveolar collapse during exhalation
B. increases mucus viscosity to trap pathogens
C. increases intrapleural pressure to inflate the lungs
D. chemically binds oxygen to facilitate diffusion into blood
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A.

Why: Surfactant acts like a detergent on the alveolar lining, lowering surface tension and preventing the alveoli from collapsing as the lungs recoil during expiration.

Other choices:

  • B: Mucus trapping is part of airway defense, but not surfactant’s primary role.
  • C: Lung inflation depends on negative intrapleural pressure created by expansion of the thorax.
  • D: Oxygen transport is largely via hemoglobin in blood; diffusion depends on partial pressure gradients.
3. A spirometry tracing shows tidal volume (TV) = 0.5 L, inspiratory reserve volume (IRV) = 3.0 L, and expiratory reserve volume (ERV) = 1.2 L. What is the vital capacity (VC)?
A. 3.7 L
B. 4.7 L
C. 5.2 L
D. 1.7 L
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: B.

Why: Vital capacity is the maximum exhaled after a maximal inhalation, which equals VC = IRV + TV + ERV. So VC = 3.0 + 0.5 + 1.2 = 4.7 L.

Other choices:

  • A: Misses one component of the sum.
  • C: Overestimates (likely double-counts a component).
  • D: Adds only two components instead of all three.
4. In negative-pressure breathing, which sequence best describes the pressure changes during the start of inhalation?
A. Intrapulmonary pressure rises, then intrapleural pressure rises
B. Intrapleural pressure rises, then intrapulmonary pressure falls
C. Intrapulmonary pressure falls first, causing the thorax to expand
D. Intrapleural pressure falls first, then intrapulmonary pressure falls
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: D.

Why: Contraction of the diaphragm/external intercostals increases thoracic volume, expanding the pleural space first. By Boyle’s law, pleural pressure falls. The lungs then expand into that lower-pressure space, decreasing intrapulmonary pressure and drawing air inward.

Other choices:

  • A: Pressures do not rise at the start of inspiration.
  • B: Pleural pressure falls (becomes more negative) during inspiration.
  • C: The thorax expands due to muscle contraction, not because lung pressure falls first.
5. Which structure sequence correctly traces airflow from entry to the site of gas exchange?
A. Nares → nasal cavity → pharynx → larynx → trachea → bronchi → bronchioles → alveoli
B. Nares → larynx → pharynx → trachea → bronchioles → bronchi → alveoli
C. Nares → nasal cavity → larynx → esophagus → trachea → bronchi → alveoli
D. Nares → nasal cavity → trachea → pharynx → larynx → bronchi → alveoli
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A.

Why: Air enters through the nares, passes the nasal cavity (filter/warm/humidify), then pharynx, larynx, trachea, bronchi, bronchioles, and finally reaches alveoli for gas exchange.

Other choices:

  • B: Swaps pharynx/larynx order and bronchi/bronchioles order.
  • C: Esophagus is a food pathway and is not part of the airway.
  • D: Places pharynx after trachea, which is anatomically incorrect.
6. A patient has a pneumothorax (air in the intrapleural space). Which immediate mechanical consequence best explains lung collapse?
A. The mucociliary escalator stops moving mucus
B. The pressure difference that normally keeps the lung expanded against the chest wall is lost
C. Alveolar partial pressure of oxygen abruptly becomes zero
D. Hemoglobin can no longer bind oxygen in pulmonary capillaries
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: B.

Why: Normally, a closed pleural space allows a negative intrapleural pressure that “couples” the lung to the chest wall. Air in the pleural space equalizes pressures and breaks that coupling, allowing elastic recoil to collapse the lung (atelectasis).

Other choices:

  • A: Ciliary function affects clearance, not the immediate mechanics of lung expansion.
  • C: Gas in the alveoli doesn’t instantly become zero; collapse is a mechanical issue.
  • D: Hemoglobin binding is intact; the problem is inadequate ventilation/expansion.
7. A person voluntarily holds their breath for an extended period. Which stimulus is most likely to override voluntary control and restart ventilation?
A. A small decrease in blood glucose
B. A small increase in blood calcium
C. Rising arterial partial pressure of carbon dioxide detected by brainstem chemoreceptors
D. A small increase in thyroid hormone levels
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: C.

Why: The medullary ventilation center contains chemoreceptors that are primarily sensitive to CO2 (and associated acidity). Rising CO2 is the dominant drive to breathe and can override voluntary control.

Other choices:

  • A/B/D: These are not the primary acute signals controlling ventilation.
8. During quiet exhalation, which statement best describes the main cause of airflow out of the lungs?
A. Elastic recoil decreases lung volume, raising intrapulmonary pressure above atmospheric
B. Internal intercostals contract, expanding thoracic volume
C. Diaphragm contracts to increase intrathoracic volume
D. Surfactant increases surface tension to push air out
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A.

Why: Quiet expiration is typically passive. When inspiratory muscles relax, lung/chest wall elastic recoil reduces volume, which raises intrapulmonary pressure above atmospheric and drives air out.

Other choices:

  • B: Internal intercostals assist forced expiration by pulling ribs down (reducing volume), not expanding volume.
  • C: Diaphragm contraction is for inspiration.
  • D: Surfactant lowers surface tension; increasing surface tension would worsen collapse risk.
9. Which respiratory defense mechanism best explains why paralysis of airway cilia can cause a prolonged cough and difficulty clearing mucus?
A. Decreased production of IgA on mucosal surfaces
B. Impairment of the mucociliary escalator that normally moves mucus toward the oral cavity
C. Increased surfactant breakdown in alveoli
D. Failure of the epiglottis to cover the glottis during swallowing

❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: B.

Why: Mucus traps particles/pathogens, and underlying cilia propel it upward to be expelled or swallowed. If cilia are paralyzed, mucus accumulates, provoking cough and persistent symptoms.

Other choices:

  • A: IgA is protective but does not directly “move” mucus.
  • C: Surfactant affects alveolar stability, not mucus clearance.
  • D: Epiglottis dysfunction causes aspiration risk, not a primary mechanism of mucus stasis.
10. A patient develops thick scarring of the alveolar-capillary membrane. Which immediate effect on gas exchange is most likely?
A. Increased diffusion of oxygen into blood due to larger concentration gradients
B. Increased oxygen binding to hemoglobin because hemoglobin becomes more abundant
C. Decreased diffusion rate of gases across the membrane, increasing risk of hypoxia
D. Increased carbon dioxide production by alveolar epithelial cells
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: C.

Why: Diffusion across the alveolar-capillary interface depends on a thin membrane. Fibrosis increases diffusion distance, reducing gas transfer efficiency and contributing to hypoxemia.

Other choices:

  • A: A thicker barrier lowers diffusion even if gradients exist.
  • B: Hemoglobin quantity is not an immediate consequence of membrane scarring.
  • D: The key limitation is diffusion across the barrier, not altered cellular CO2 production.
11. Which statement best describes the primary direction of gas diffusion at the alveolus under normal conditions?
A. Oxygen diffuses from alveoli to pulmonary capillaries, while carbon dioxide diffuses from capillaries to alveoli
B. Oxygen diffuses from capillaries to alveoli, while carbon dioxide diffuses from alveoli to capillaries
C. Both oxygen and carbon dioxide diffuse from capillaries to alveoli
D. Both oxygen and carbon dioxide diffuse from alveoli to capillaries
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A.

Why: Deoxygenated blood entering pulmonary capillaries has lower O2 and higher CO2 than alveolar air. Diffusion proceeds down each partial pressure gradient: O2 into blood, CO2 into alveoli for expiration.

Other choices:

  • B/C/D: These reverse or oversimplify the normal gradients.

Why: Forced expiration is active. Internal intercostals pull the rib cage down and abdominal muscles increase intra-abdominal pressure, pushing the diaphragm upward and decreasing thoracic volume, increasing pressure to expel air.

  • D: Relaxation alone is characteristic of quiet expiration, not forced expiration.

Why: Brainstem respiratory control is primarily sensitive to CO2, but oxygen sensing becomes especially important during significant hypoxemia. Severe low O2 can strongly stimulate ventilation even if CO2 is not markedly elevated.

  • B: Low CO2/alkalemia tends to reduce ventilatory drive.

14. Consider the bicarbonate buffer system in blood: CO2 + H2O ⇌ H2CO3 ⇌ H+ + HCO3−. If ventilation decreases and carbon dioxide is retained, what is the most immediate effect on blood pH?
A. pH increases because carbon dioxide is a base
B. pH stays constant because both H+ and HCO3− increase equally
C. pH decreases because the equilibrium shifts right, increasing hydrogen ion concentration
D. pH decreases because bicarbonate is consumed to form oxygen
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: C.

Why: Retaining CO2 increases dissolved CO2, pushing the buffer equilibrium toward carbonic acid and then toward H+ and HCO3−. Increased H+ lowers pH (respiratory acidosis physiology).

Other choices:

  • A: CO2 retention acidifies blood through carbonic acid formation.
  • B: Even if HCO3− rises, the rise in H+ (strong acid) shifts pH toward acidity.
  • D: Oxygen is not produced by this buffer reaction.

Passage 1: A Chest Injury and Rapid Breathing

A 19-year-old patient is brought to the emergency department after being struck in the chest during a soccer match. The patient reports sharp pain on one side of the chest and feels “short of breath.” On exam, one side of the chest expands less during inspiration, and breath sounds are reduced on that side. The patient’s respiratory rate is elevated.

A clinician explains that the lungs are not rigidly attached to the chest wall. Instead, each lung is surrounded by two pleural layers with a thin fluid film between them. This arrangement normally allows the chest wall to pull the lungs outward during inspiration. The clinician also notes that certain pathologic events can expand the pleural space with air or fluid, disrupting normal mechanics and causing partial or complete collapse of the affected lung.

Arterial blood gas testing shows the patient has an elevated partial pressure of carbon dioxide compared with expected values. The clinician points out that a specific brainstem control center adjusts ventilation rhythmically and responds strongly to rising carbon dioxide. The clinician also notes that oxygen sensing becomes most important when oxygen levels fall substantially.

How to reason:

  1. Identify the mechanical problem: asymmetric expansion + reduced breath sounds points to impaired ventilation on one side.
  2. Link pleural anatomy to mechanics: a sealed pleural space helps keep the lung expanded; adding air/fluid breaks that coupling.
  3. Use Boyle’s law logic: volume/pressure changes in the thorax drive airflow; if the lung can’t expand, ventilation drops.
  4. Interpret CO2: high CO2 implies hypoventilation relative to CO2 production; medullary chemoreceptors strongly increase respiratory drive.
  5. Pick options that explain the mechanism (pleural pressure relationship) and predict the direction of changes (ventilation/CO2).

B. Alveolar surface tension decreases dramatically
C. The trachea becomes obstructed, preventing airflow to both lungs
D. Intrapleural pressure moves toward atmospheric, eliminating the normal pressure gradient that keeps the lung expanded
✅Correct
❌Incorrect

Correct Answer: D.

Why: A pneumothorax introduces air into the pleural space, equalizing pleural pressure with atmospheric pressure. Losing the negative pleural pressure uncouples the lung from the chest wall; elastic recoil then collapses the lung.

Other choices:

  • A: Diaphragm injury could reduce ventilation, but the passage highlights pleural mechanics and lung collapse.
  • B: Lower surface tension would actually help keep alveoli open; collapse here is pleural-pressure related.
  • C: Tracheal obstruction would affect both lungs and does not match unilateral reduced breath sounds.

  • C: Epiglottis closure protects the airway during swallowing; it is not a ventilation control mechanism.

Passage 2: “Walking Pneumonia” and Airway Defense

A 22-year-old college student develops a persistent dry cough and low-grade fever that lasts for weeks. The student is still able to attend classes, but reports fatigue and worsening cough at night. On exam, lung sounds are mildly abnormal, but the student does not appear severely ill. A clinician suspects an atypical pneumonia and explains that certain infections can interfere with the normal clearance of airway secretions.

The clinician reviews the layered defenses of the respiratory tract. The upper airway filters and conditions incoming air, while mucus in the internal airways traps particles and microbes. Normally, cilia propel mucus upward toward the oral cavity, where it can be expelled or swallowed. The clinician also notes that immune protections are present on mucosal surfaces and within alveoli, including antibody-mediated defenses and phagocytic cells that can engulf pathogens and signal to other immune cells.

During the visit, the student also reports having seasonal allergies. The clinician explains that in some people, immune cells in the airways release inflammatory chemicals in response to common environmental triggers, causing bronchial irritation and contributing to symptoms like cough and wheeze even without infection.

How to reason:

  1. Separate “mechanical clearance” from “immune killing”: mucus/cilia move material; antibodies/macrophages neutralize or ingest it.
  2. When cough is prolonged, ask which defense was disrupted: impaired clearance leads to mucus retention and irritation.
  3. Use location logic: upper airway filters; internal airway uses mucus + cilia; alveoli rely more on macrophages/IgA at surfaces.
  4. For allergy clues, identify which immune cell releases inflammatory mediators after antigen binding (think immediate hypersensitivity).
  5. Choose answers that connect a mechanism to the symptom pattern rather than restating the passage.

Passage 3: Panic, Hyperventilation, and Acid–Base Shifts

A 28-year-old patient arrives at an urgent care clinic feeling dizzy and tingling in the hands after a stressful event. The patient is breathing very rapidly and reports a sensation of “not getting enough air,” even though a pulse oximeter reading is normal. The clinician suspects the patient is hyperventilating.

The clinician explains that the brainstem ventilation center is highly sensitive to carbon dioxide. When carbon dioxide is exhaled faster than it is produced, its blood level falls, and this can shift acid–base balance through the bicarbonate buffer system. The clinician writes the buffer relationship on a whiteboard and emphasizes that changes in carbon dioxide can rapidly change hydrogen ion concentration and pH.

To help the patient understand why the tingling occurs, the clinician explains that symptoms can result from a physiologic response to altered blood chemistry rather than from a drop in oxygen delivery. The clinician also notes that if the patient were to slow breathing enough to retain carbon dioxide, the buffer system would shift in the opposite direction.

How to reason:

  1. Use the vital clue: normal oxygen reading + rapid breathing suggests the primary issue is CO2, not O2 delivery.
  2. Translate “hyperventilation” into chemistry: CO2 falls → buffer shifts left → H+ falls → pH rises.
  3. Predict the feedback: low CO2 reduces respiratory drive, but anxiety can temporarily override this.
  4. For interventions, pick what reverses the chemistry (retain CO2 or reduce CO2 loss), not what adds oxygen.
  5. Eliminate choices that confuse metabolic vs respiratory causes; here the driver is ventilation affecting CO2.
22. Based on the buffer relationship CO2 + H2O ⇌ H2CO3 ⇌ H+ + HCO3−, the patient’s hyperventilation would most immediately cause which change in blood pH?
A. Decreased pH because oxygen rises and forms acid
B. No change in pH because H+ and HCO3− change together
C. Increased pH because carbon dioxide decreases, shifting the equilibrium left and lowering hydrogen ion concentration
D. Increased pH because bicarbonate is converted to glucose
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: C.

Why: Hyperventilation “blows off” CO2, lowering CO2 in blood. By Le Châtelier’s principle, the buffer shifts left, reducing H+ and raising pH (respiratory alkalosis physiology).

Other choices:

  • A: Oxygen level changes do not directly create acid in this buffer system.
  • B: H+ is a strong acid; lowering H+ increases pH even if bicarbonate changes too.
  • D: Bicarbonate is not converted to glucose in this context.
