Glycogen Metabolism
Timer 12:00
Passage 1 (Questions 1–7)

A physiology group compares glycogen handling in liver and skeletal muscle during a fed state, a short fast, and intense exercise. The investigators focus on how branching changes the speed of glycogen use and why the same glycogen granule serves different purposes in different tissues.

Figure 1. Glycogen contains linear α(1→4) linkages and branch-point α(1→6) linkages.

In the fed state, excess intracellular glucose is trapped as glucose 6-phosphate. Phosphoglucomutase can shift that phosphate to carbon 1, allowing formation of UDP-glucose. Glycogen synthase then extends a growing chain by creating α(1→4) bonds, whereas branching enzyme cuts a short terminal segment and reattaches it through an α(1→6) bond. The researchers note that branching does not merely change appearance. By creating many nonreducing ends, branching increases the number of sites at which synthesis or degradation can proceed at the same time.

During glycogen breakdown, glycogen phosphorylase removes residues from the outer ends of the granule by cleaving α(1→4) bonds with inorganic phosphate rather than water, releasing glucose 1-phosphate. However, phosphorylase cannot pass directly through an α(1→6) branch point. Debranching enzyme therefore acts in two steps: first, a transferase shifts a small terminal oligosaccharide to a nearby chain; second, an α(1→6)-glucosidase hydrolyzes the remaining branch-point residue, releasing one molecule of free glucose.

Figure 2. Glycogen can supply glucose 6-phosphate for glycolysis; in liver and kidney, glucose 6-phosphatase can also generate free glucose for export.

The team then measures metabolites under two perturbations. In fasting liver, glucagon activates glycogen phosphorylase and inhibits glycogen synthase. Glucose 1-phosphate from glycogen becomes glucose 6-phosphate, and hepatocytes can convert that intermediate to free glucose for release into blood. In exercising skeletal muscle, epinephrine and AMP also favor glycogen phosphorylase, but the glucose 6-phosphate is used locally in glycolysis because myocytes lack glucose 6-phosphatase.

The investigators predict that highly branched glycogen supports faster synthesis and breakdown. They also predict that glucose 6-phosphatase deficiency would still allow glycogenolysis, but would block liver from raising blood glucose efficiently between meals. By contrast, debranching-enzyme deficiency would cause phosphorylase to stall near branch points and slow glycogen mobilization.

How to reason

  • Start by separating structure from pathway flow. Figure 1 explains which bonds enzymes can and cannot handle; Figure 2 explains where each product can go next.
  • Track the product at each step: glycogen phosphorylase → glucose 1-phosphate, then phosphoglucomutase → glucose 6-phosphate, then either glycolysis or free-glucose release in liver/kidney.
  • For branch questions, focus on the phrase many nonreducing ends. More ends means more simultaneous enzyme access, so branching affects rate rather than only storage capacity.
  • When a question compares liver with muscle, ask which tissue can perform the last step: muscle can break glycogen down, but only liver/kidney can use glucose 6-phosphatase to export glucose.
  • When an enzyme defect is introduced, identify exactly where the block occurs. A late block still allows earlier intermediates to form; a branch-point block causes phosphorylase to stall before complete degradation.
  • Avoid answer choices that switch phosphorylase with phosphatase, or that assume every released residue is immediately free glucose.
A mutant glycogen granule is synthesized with far fewer α(1→6) branches than normal, but all other enzymes remain functional. During sudden epinephrine stimulation, which immediate effect is most likely?
Explanation
The best answer is B. The passage states that branching creates many nonreducing ends. Those ends are the entry points used during both synthesis and degradation. With fewer branches, the granule has fewer simultaneous access points, so early glycogenolysis is slower even if phosphorylase itself works normally.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • A: Phosphorylase releases glucose 1-phosphate, not mainly free glucose.
  • C: Reduced branching does not remove glycogen synthase activity; synthase still forms α(1→4) bonds.
  • D: Fewer branches would give less branch-point glucose release, not more.
A liver homogenate is supplied with glycogen, inorganic phosphate, and active glycogen phosphorylase, but no debranching enzyme. As the reaction proceeds, which pattern would most likely be observed?
Explanation
The best answer is C. Phosphorylase can remove residues only along α(1→4) linkages and stops when it approaches an α(1→6) branch point. Without debranching enzyme, glycogen breakdown stalls after outer chains are shortened, leaving a limit-dextrin-like remnant.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • A: Complete consumption cannot occur because branch points block continued phosphorylase action.
  • B: UDP-glucose is a synthesis intermediate, not a glycogenolysis product.
  • D: Phosphoglucomutase acts on the glucose 1-phosphate produced, but it cannot bypass the structural block at branches.
A researcher measures a rise in hepatic glucose 6-phosphate after glucagon treatment but finds little increase in blood glucose output. Which defect best fits the passage?
Explanation
The best answer is A. The clue is that glucose 6-phosphate accumulates, meaning earlier glycogenolysis steps are still working. The missing ability is the final conversion of glucose 6-phosphate to free glucose for export, which requires glucose 6-phosphatase in liver.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • B: A branching enzyme defect changes glycogen structure but does not directly explain accumulated glucose 6-phosphate after glucagon stimulation.
  • C: Without phosphorylase, glucose 1-phosphate and therefore glucose 6-phosphate would not rise normally from glycogen.
  • D: Without phosphoglucomutase, glucose 1-phosphate would accumulate instead of glucose 6-phosphate.
Which finding would most strongly support the claim that branching accelerates both glycogen synthesis and glycogen degradation?
Explanation
The best answer is B. The passage argues that extra branches create extra nonreducing ends. More ends allow multiple synthase or phosphorylase molecules to work at the same time, which is a direct mechanistic explanation for faster flux in either direction.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • A: Energy cost is not the central mechanistic claim made here.
  • C: Synthase extends α(1→4) chains; it is not restricted only to branch points.
  • D: Debranching enzyme does not serve as the hormonal activator of glycogen synthase.
One residue at an α(1→6) branch point is ultimately released as free glucose during glycogenolysis. Which enzyme activity directly produces that free glucose?
Explanation
The best answer is C. Most residues are removed by phosphorylase as glucose 1-phosphate. The special exception is the single branch-point residue, which is hydrolyzed from the α(1→6) linkage by the glucosidase function of debranching enzyme, yielding free glucose.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • A: Phosphorylase generates glucose 1-phosphate, not free glucose.
  • B: Phosphoglucomutase only rearranges phosphate position.
  • D: Glycogen synthase is a synthesis enzyme and does not perform this hydrolysis step.
Myocytes and hepatocytes are both exposed to hormones that favor glycogen breakdown. Which result would most clearly distinguish the liver response from the muscle response?
Explanation
The best answer is B. Both tissues can perform glycogenolysis up to glucose 6-phosphate. The important tissue-specific difference in the passage is that liver has glucose 6-phosphatase and can therefore generate free glucose for export, whereas muscle typically retains glucose 6-phosphate for glycolysis.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • A: Both tissues produce glucose 1-phosphate from glycogen.
  • C: Both tissues need phosphoglucomutase to interconvert glucose 1-phosphate and glucose 6-phosphate.
  • D: Epinephrine can favor glycogen breakdown in both tissues, though the physiological purpose differs.
A patient has normal hepatic glycogen phosphorylase and normal glucose 6-phosphatase, but a defective debranching enzyme. After an overnight fast, which outcome is most consistent with the passage?
Explanation
The best answer is C. Early glycogenolysis along outer α(1→4) segments can still occur because phosphorylase is normal. But without debranching enzyme, phosphorylase cannot clear branch points efficiently, so liver cannot fully mobilize glycogen during fasting even though the final phosphatase step remains intact.
Why the other choices are not as good
  • A: Some glucose 1-phosphate can still be generated from outer chain residues.
  • B: Glucose 6-phosphatase acts after glucose 6-phosphate is formed and does not solve the structural block at branches.
  • D: UDP-glucose is used in glycogen synthesis, not export during glycogen breakdown.
