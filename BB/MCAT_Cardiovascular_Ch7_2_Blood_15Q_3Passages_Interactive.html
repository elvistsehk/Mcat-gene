Standalone Questions (Q1–Q15)
1. Blood plasma is best described as which of the following?
A. The cellular fraction of blood containing erythrocytes, leukocytes, and platelets
B. Plasma with added clotting factors and fibrinogen removed
C. A lipid-rich fraction that increases during fasting
D. An aqueous solution containing nutrients, salts, respiratory gases, hormones, and proteins
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: D

Plasma is the liquid portion of blood and contains dissolved solutes and proteins (including clotting factors).

Why the other choices are not best:

  • Cellular fraction: describes the formed elements, not plasma.
  • Clotting factors removed: describes serum, not plasma.
  • Lipid-rich fraction: does not define plasma; lipids can be present but are not the defining feature.
2. Serum differs from plasma primarily because serum:
A. Lacks clotting factors (and fibrinogen) that are present in plasma
B. Contains more erythrocytes after centrifugation
C. Is the buffy coat layer between plasma and red cells
D. Contains platelets that promote coagulation
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A

Serum is essentially plasma with clotting factors removed after coagulation; it is often preferred for certain laboratory tests.

Why the other choices are not best:

  • More erythrocytes: erythrocytes sediment into the packed cell layer, not serum.
  • Buffy coat: contains leukocytes and platelets, not serum.
  • Contains platelets: platelets are part of the formed elements; serum is cell-free.
3. Which red blood cell feature most directly increases gas exchange efficiency?
A. Presence of numerous mitochondria for aerobic ATP production
B. Biconcave shape that increases surface area-to-volume ratio
C. A nucleus that allows rapid cell division in circulation
D. Thick cell wall rich in peptidoglycan
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: B

Biconcave morphology increases surface area and helps erythrocytes traverse capillaries efficiently, improving diffusion of O2/CO2.

Why the other choices are not best:

  • Mitochondria: mature RBCs lack mitochondria; they rely on glycolysis.
  • Nucleus/division: mature RBCs are anucleate and cannot divide.
  • Peptidoglycan: is bacterial cell wall material, not present in RBCs.
4. Why do mature erythrocytes rely primarily on glycolysis rather than oxidative phosphorylation?
A. They lack mitochondria, preventing aerobic ATP production
B. They are in a hypoxic environment inside arteries
C. Oxidative phosphorylation would produce too much CO2 for transport
D. Glycolysis allows hemoglobin to bind more oxygen by generating ATP
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A

Mature RBCs lose mitochondria, so they cannot perform oxidative phosphorylation and must generate ATP via anaerobic pathways (glycolysis).

Why the other choices are not best:

  • Hypoxic inside arteries: arterial blood is oxygen-rich; oxygen availability is not the limiting factor.
  • Too much CO2: CO2 production is not the core reason; the absence of mitochondria is.
  • ATP improves binding: ATP generation does not directly increase hemoglobin’s oxygen binding in this way.
5. Hematocrit is most accurately defined as:
A. The mass of hemoglobin per deciliter of plasma
B. The number of leukocytes per microliter of blood
C. The percentage of whole blood volume that is composed of red blood cells
D. The concentration of platelets per milliliter of blood
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: C

Hematocrit quantifies the fraction of blood volume occupied by erythrocytes and is expressed as a percentage.

Why the other choices are not best:

  • Hemoglobin per dL: describes hemoglobin concentration (g/dL), not hematocrit.
  • Leukocyte count: is a white blood cell measurement.
  • Platelet count: is a thrombocyte measurement.
6. A CBC reports hemoglobin concentration in which unit most commonly?
A. Millimeters of mercury (mmHg)
B. Milligrams per milliliter (mg/mL)
C. Grams per deciliter (g/dL)
D. Percent (%)
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: C

Hemoglobin is typically reported as mass per volume, classically in g/dL in clinical practice.

Why the other choices are not best:

  • mmHg: is a pressure unit used for blood pressure.
  • mg/mL: is a valid mass/volume unit, but the standard for hemoglobin is g/dL.
  • %: is used for hematocrit, not hemoglobin.
7. Which blood cell type is typically anucleate in circulation?
A. Erythrocytes
B. Neutrophils
C. Lymphocytes
D. Monocytes
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A

Mature red blood cells lose their nucleus (and organelles) to maximize space for hemoglobin.

Why the other choices are not best:

  • Neutrophils: have a multilobed nucleus.
  • Lymphocytes: are nucleated immune cells.
  • Monocytes: are nucleated and can differentiate into macrophages.
8. Platelets are best described as:
A. Nucleated cells that mature in the thymus and kill infected cells
B. Cell fragments released from megakaryocytes in the bone marrow
C. Granulocytes that release histamine during allergic responses
D. Plasma proteins that directly catalyze fibrin formation
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: B

Platelets (thrombocytes) are cytoplasmic fragments derived from megakaryocytes and function in hemostasis and clot formation.

Why the other choices are not best:

  • Thymus-maturing killers: describes T-cells (lymphocytes).
  • Histamine-releasing granulocytes: describes basophils/mast cells, not platelets.
  • Plasma proteins catalyzing fibrin: refers to clotting factors; platelets are cellular fragments.
9. Which hormone most directly stimulates red blood cell production in response to low oxygen levels?
A. Thrombopoietin secreted by the thymus
B. Erythropoietin secreted by the kidney
C. Insulin secreted by pancreatic β-cells
D. Aldosterone secreted by the adrenal medulla
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: B

Erythropoietin (EPO) is produced by the kidney and promotes erythrocyte development in the bone marrow, especially under hypoxic conditions.

Why the other choices are not best:

  • Thrombopoietin: stimulates platelet production and is mainly from liver/kidney, not thymus.
  • Insulin: regulates glucose, not erythropoiesis.
  • Aldosterone: is from adrenal cortex and regulates salt/water balance, not RBC production.
10. Which leukocyte category contains cytoplasmic granules visible by microscopy and is strongly associated with acute inflammation?
A. Agranulocytes (e.g., lymphocytes, monocytes)
B. Erythrocytes
C. Granulocytes (e.g., neutrophils, eosinophils, basophils)
D. Platelets
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: C

Granulocytes have visible granules containing antimicrobial/inflammatory mediators and commonly participate in acute inflammatory responses.

Why the other choices are not best:

  • Agranulocytes: do not have the same type of visible exocytosed granules.
  • Erythrocytes: are oxygen carriers, not immune cells.
  • Platelets: are clotting fragments, not leukocytes.
11. A patient has 8,000 leukocytes per microliter of blood. This value is best interpreted as:
A. Severely low because leukocytes normally make up 45% of blood volume
B. Abnormally high because leukocytes should be absent from blood
C. Equivalent to hematocrit and therefore indicates anemia
D. Within the typical reference range for leukocyte count
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: D

Normal leukocyte counts are commonly cited around 4,500–11,000 per microliter; 8,000/µL falls in that range.

Why the other choices are not best:

  • 45% of blood volume: refers to formed elements overall (mostly RBCs), not leukocytes.
  • Leukocytes absent: leukocytes normally circulate in blood.
  • Equivalent to hematocrit: hematocrit is a percentage of RBC volume, not WBC count.
12. Which ABO genotype(s) could produce blood type A?
A. IAIA or IAi
B. IBIB or IBi
C. IAIB only
D. ii only
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A

A antigen expression occurs with at least one IA allele; O (i) is recessive, so IAi still produces type A.

Why the other choices are not best:

  • IBIB/IBi: produce type B.
  • IAIB: produces type AB (codominance).
  • ii: produces type O (no A/B antigens).
13. Why can type O red blood cells often be used as ABO “universal donor” packed RBCs?
A. They contain both anti-A and anti-B antibodies that neutralize recipient antibodies
B. They express both A and B antigens, so they are recognized as self by all recipients
C. They lack A and B antigens, reducing the chance of recipient anti-A or anti-B antibodies binding donor RBCs
D. They are Rh-negative by definition
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: C

For packed RBC transfusions, donor RBC surface antigens are key. Type O RBCs have neither A nor B antigens, minimizing ABO antigen-triggered hemolysis.

Why the other choices are not best:

  • Contains antibodies: antibodies are in plasma; packed RBCs minimize donor plasma.
  • Express both antigens: that is AB, not O.
  • Rh-negative by definition: O can be Rh+ or Rh−; ABO and Rh are separate.
14. Which statement about Rh factor is most accurate?
A. Rh positivity (D antigen presence) is inherited in an autosomal dominant manner
B. Rh-negative individuals naturally have anti-Rh antibodies without prior exposure
C. Anti-Rh antibodies are typically IgM and readily cross the placenta
D. Rh factor refers to the A and B antigens on RBCs
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A

Presence of the D antigen (Rh+) behaves dominantly. Anti-Rh antibodies usually form only after exposure (sensitization) and are often IgG (placenta-crossing).

Why the other choices are not best:

  • Naturally present antibodies: anti-Rh antibodies generally require exposure to Rh+ blood.
  • IgM crossing placenta: IgM does not readily cross the placenta; IgG does.
  • A/B antigens: are ABO system, not Rh.
15. In erythroblastosis fetalis due to Rh incompatibility, fetal red blood cells are most directly destroyed by:
A. Fetal anti-A IgM antibodies crossing into maternal blood
B. Maternal anti-Rh IgG antibodies crossing the placenta and binding fetal Rh+ erythrocytes
C. Maternal anti-Rh IgM antibodies rapidly crossing the placenta
D. Maternal complement proteins in fetal plasma recognizing ABO antigens
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: B

In Rh− mother with Rh+ fetus, sensitization can lead to anti-Rh IgG production. IgG crosses placenta and can hemolyze fetal RBCs in later pregnancies.

Why the other choices are not best:

  • Fetal IgM: IgM does not cross placenta well, and fetal antibodies are not the usual driver.
  • Maternal IgM: IgM is large and poorly crosses placenta; IgG is the key.
  • Complement in fetal plasma: mechanism centers on maternal IgG binding fetal RBCs.
Passages (3 passages × 3 questions)
Passage 1: A Centrifuge Tube and a Marathon Training Plan

A 21-year-old endurance athlete presents for a routine sports physical before starting an altitude-training program. The clinician orders a blood sample and spins it in a centrifuge. After separation, the top layer is a clear straw-colored fluid, a very thin middle layer is barely visible, and a large, dense red layer occupies the bottom of the tube.

Laboratory results show a reduced hematocrit compared with the athlete’s prior test. The athlete reports switching to a very low-calorie diet to “cut weight,” and also reports increased fatigue during long runs. The clinician explains that oxygen delivery depends on the number of circulating red blood cells and the amount of hemoglobin they contain, not simply on how fast the athlete breathes.

To help the athlete understand the physiology, the clinician describes why mature red blood cells have unusual cellular features and why their energy metabolism differs from most other cells.

How to reason:

  1. Start by mapping the centrifuge layers: top = plasma, thin middle = buffy coat, bottom = packed red cells; then connect a reduced red layer to a lower hematocrit.
  2. Translate the lab into function: lower hematocrit means fewer RBCs per blood volume, so oxygen-carrying capacity can drop even if ventilation is normal.
  3. Recall RBC specializations: loss of nucleus/organelles maximizes hemoglobin; loss of mitochondria forces reliance on glycolysis and prevents the cell from consuming its own oxygen cargo.
  4. Look for an inference question that links structure to function (shape, organelles, metabolism) rather than a direct definition.
  5. Eliminate choices that confuse plasma versus serum, or that mix up what hematocrit measures.
16. In Passage 1, which centrifuge layer corresponds to the “very thin middle layer” that can increase during infection?
A. Packed red blood cells
B. Plasma
C. Serum
D. The buffy coat (leukocytes and platelets)
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: D

In a centrifuged tube, the middle buffy coat contains leukocytes and platelets and is normally thin; it can appear larger if leukocytes rise.

Why the other choices are not best:

  • Packed RBCs: form the dense bottom layer.
  • Plasma: is the top liquid layer when anticoagulated blood is centrifuged.
  • Serum: is plasma without clotting factors; it is not a distinct centrifuge layer in whole blood separation.
17. Based on Passage 1, which red blood cell feature most directly helps prevent the cell from using the oxygen it transports?
A. Loss of the nucleus, preventing transcription of hemoglobin
B. Loss of mitochondria, preventing oxidative phosphorylation
C. Biconcave shape, which increases oxygen consumption
D. Presence of granules that store antimicrobial enzymes
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: B

Without mitochondria, RBCs cannot perform aerobic metabolism, so they do not consume the O2 bound to hemoglobin before delivery to tissues.

Why the other choices are not best:

  • Nucleus loss: limits cell division and gene expression, but oxygen consumption is prevented by absent mitochondria.
  • Biconcave shape: increases diffusion surface area; it does not increase oxygen consumption.
  • Granules: characterize granulocytes, not RBCs.
18. In Passage 1, the clinician links fatigue to reduced hematocrit. Which inference best explains why a lower hematocrit can impair endurance performance?
A. It increases plasma viscosity, reducing diffusion across alveoli
B. It increases the number of leukocytes that compete for oxygen binding sites
C. It directly prevents ventilation from increasing during exercise
D. It lowers blood oxygen-carrying capacity because fewer RBCs are available per unit blood volume
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: D

Hematocrit reflects RBC fraction. With fewer RBCs, total hemoglobin available to carry oxygen decreases, reducing delivery to working muscle.

Why the other choices are not best:

  • Plasma viscosity: is not the central consequence of lower hematocrit.
  • Leukocytes competing for binding: leukocytes do not bind oxygen via hemoglobin.
  • Prevents ventilation increase: ventilation is controlled by respiratory centers, not hematocrit directly.
Passage 2: Fever, Pus, and a Spike in White Blood Cells

A 58-year-old patient comes to an urgent care clinic with fever, localized redness, and a painful skin lesion that is draining thick fluid. A complete blood count shows a marked increase in total leukocytes compared with the patient’s baseline. The clinician explains that some white blood cells contain granules packed with antimicrobial molecules and inflammatory mediators.

The clinician also notes that other white blood cells are better known for targeted immune responses and for maintaining long-term memory of prior pathogens. The patient asks why vaccines can protect years later, even when the initial exposure was brief.

Finally, the clinician cautions that the thin middle layer seen in centrifuged blood is normally small, but can become more prominent during infection when leukocyte counts rise.

How to reason:

  1. Identify the evidence: pus and acute inflammation with high WBC suggests a granulocyte-heavy response; connect visible granules to granulocytes.
  2. Separate innate versus adaptive clues: rapid antimicrobial response versus long-term memory points to different leukocyte types (neutrophils vs lymphocytes).
  3. Use the centrifuge clue: a larger buffy coat implies more leukocytes/platelets relative to red cells.
  4. Choose options that require you to infer which cell type fits the described role, not ones that simply restate ‘infection increases WBC.’
  5. Rule out distractors that describe platelets or red blood cells when the prompt is about immune defense and memory.
19. In Passage 2, which leukocyte type best matches cells that contain cytoplasmic granules with antimicrobial molecules and commonly dominate acute bacterial inflammation?
A. Lymphocytes
B. Neutrophils
C. Monocytes
D. Erythrocytes
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: B

Neutrophils are granulocytes with prominent granules and are classic first responders in acute bacterial infection, contributing to pus formation.

Why the other choices are not best:

  • Lymphocytes: are key for specific immunity and memory, not primary pus-forming granulocytes.
  • Monocytes: become macrophages in tissues and are phagocytic but are agranulocytes in this classification.
  • Erythrocytes: carry oxygen and are not immune cells.
20. Passage 2 notes that some white blood cells maintain long-term memory and help vaccines work. Which cell type is most directly responsible for producing antigen-specific antibodies after activation?
A. B lymphocytes (B-cells)
B. Basophils
C. Platelets
D. Neutrophils
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: A

B-cells differentiate into plasma cells that secrete antibodies and can also form memory B-cells, supporting long-term protective immunity.

Why the other choices are not best:

  • Basophils: are granulocytes involved in allergy/inflammation, not primary antibody producers.
  • Platelets: are clotting fragments.
  • Neutrophils: are innate phagocytic responders, not antibody factories.
21. In Passage 2, a larger-than-usual buffy coat would be most consistent with which change in a CBC?
A. Decreased platelet production by megakaryocytes
B. Decreased plasma protein concentration due to liver failure
C. Increased red cell mass due to erythropoietin suppression
D. Increased leukocyte count relative to baseline
✅Correct
❌Incorrect

Correct Answer: D

The buffy coat contains leukocytes and platelets; during infection, leukocyte numbers often increase, making the buffy coat more prominent.

Passage 3: Transfusion Planning and a Second Pregnancy

A patient with chronic anemia is scheduled for surgery and may require packed red blood cells. The patient’s chart lists blood type B-negative. The blood bank prepares compatible units and explains that transfusions focus on matching the donor’s red blood cell antigens to avoid rapid hemolysis.

Separately, the patient mentions being pregnant and recalls that a prior pregnancy involved a baby with Rh-positive blood. The obstetric team explains that exposure to fetal blood can sensitize an Rh-negative parent, leading to antibody production that may affect later pregnancies if another fetus is Rh-positive.

The team discusses why this issue is different from typical ABO mismatch during pregnancy, and they outline a medication strategy that prevents the parent’s immune system from becoming strongly sensitized.

How to reason:

  1. First, handle transfusion compatibility: for packed RBCs, think donor RBC antigens and recipient antibodies; type B recipients have anti-A antibodies, so avoid donor RBCs with A antigen.
  2. Add Rh: Rh-negative recipients should avoid Rh-positive donor RBCs to prevent sensitization; match both ABO and Rh for safety.
  3. For pregnancy, recognize the timing: sensitization during the first Rh-mismatched pregnancy usually affects subsequent Rh+ pregnancies because IgG antibodies can cross the placenta.
  4. Explain the ABO vs Rh difference using immunology: anti-AB antibodies are typically IgM (poor placental transfer), while anti-Rh can be IgG (placenta-crossing).
  5. Choose the answer that follows the chain: exposure → sensitization → antibody class → placental transfer → fetal hemolysis, and note how prophylaxis (passive antibodies) prevents sensitization.
22. In Passage 3, which packed red blood cell unit is the safest ABO match for a B-negative recipient if the goal is to avoid ABO hemolysis?
A. A-negative packed red blood cells
B. AB-negative packed red blood cells
C. B-negative or O-negative packed red blood cells
D. B-positive packed red blood cells
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: C

Type B recipients have anti-A antibodies, so donor RBCs must not carry A antigen; B or O RBCs are compatible. Rh-negative recipients should ideally receive Rh-negative blood.

Why the other choices are not best:

  • A-negative: donor RBCs carry A antigen and can be targeted by recipient anti-A.
  • AB-negative: donor RBCs carry A antigen and can trigger hemolysis in a B recipient.
  • B-positive: ABO is compatible, but Rh-positive can sensitize an Rh-negative recipient.
23. Passage 3 contrasts ABO mismatching in pregnancy with Rh incompatibility. Which immunologic property best explains why Rh disease can threaten later pregnancies?
A. Maternal anti-A antibodies are IgG and always cross the placenta in large amounts
B. Fetal IgM antibodies cross into maternal circulation and destroy maternal RBCs
C. Maternal anti-Rh antibodies can be IgG and cross the placenta to hemolyze fetal Rh+ RBCs
D. Rh-negative parents naturally produce anti-Rh antibodies without prior exposure
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: C

Rh sensitization can lead to anti-D IgG. IgG crosses placenta and can attack fetal red cells in subsequent Rh+ pregnancies, causing erythroblastosis fetalis.

Why the other choices are not best:

  • Maternal anti-A always IgG: anti-AB antibodies are commonly IgM (poor placental transfer) in this context.
  • Fetal IgM: IgM is large and poorly crosses placenta; fetal antibodies are not the usual driver.
  • Naturally produced anti-Rh: anti-Rh typically requires exposure to Rh+ blood.
24. In Passage 3, the medication strategy described to prevent sensitization in an Rh-negative parent most closely reflects which mechanism?
A. Active immunization that stimulates memory B-cells against Rh antigens
B. An antibiotic that kills Rh+ red blood cells in the placenta
C. A diuretic that lowers maternal blood volume to reduce antigen exposure
D. Passive immunization that binds fetal Rh+ cells and prevents the parent from mounting an active anti-Rh response
✅Correct
❌Incorrect
Show explanation

Correct Answer: D

Rh-immunoglobulin (RhoGAM) provides preformed antibodies that neutralize Rh+ fetal cells in maternal circulation, reducing immune activation and preventing long-term sensitization.

Why the other choices are not best:

  • Active immunization: would increase, not prevent, memory formation.
  • Antibiotic: does not target human RBC antigens.
  • Diuretic: does not specifically prevent antigen recognition or antibody production.
